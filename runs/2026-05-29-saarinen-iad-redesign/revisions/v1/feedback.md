# Reader feedback — Revised v1

The analysis should be revisited in light of a new terminal development strategy, which includes a physical connector between the Main Terminal and the A/B Concourse, approximately 84 narrow-body equivalent gates accessible from a central processing node through a redevelopment of the A/B Concourse and the Main Terminal, and a future center-spine tunnel system connecting additional concourses beyond A/B while retaining AeroTrain service. This fundamentally changes the question from whether Saarinen would have pursued distributed concourses versus a centralized terminal, to how he might have designed a highly connected, multi-modal passenger circulation system around a dominant central node. The report should explore whether the emerging Dulles concept—an iconic terminal serving as the primary processing and orientation space, supported by multiple layers of connectivity including walking corridors, tunnels, and automated transit—may actually align more closely with Saarinen’s original emphasis on clarity, legibility, and passenger experience than previous analyses suggest. In addition, the report should examine the long-term implications of these investments on the post-2036 airport evolution strategy: specifically, which design decisions should be optimized for the ultimate end-state rather than the intermediate 2036 condition, how the connector and center spine influence future concourse expansion, and whether future capital investments should prioritize passenger circulation, operational flexibility, and network connectivity over additional terminal processing capacity.
