# Airport COO Brief: IAD Operational Reality as the Consequence of Design Decisions, 1962–2026

**Stage 1 Research Brief — Airport COO Perspective**
**Prepared for:** Transform Airports AI Council, Saarinen/IAD Run
**Date:** May 2026
**Voice:** COO chair, 25 years in airport operations, former airline side

---

## Key Findings

1. **Saarinen's "no walking" principle was architecturally coherent but operationally brittle.** The mobile lounge concept worked when a single fleet of vehicles served a single building to a dispersed ramp. It broke progressively as passenger volume scaled, as connecting traffic grew relative to terminating traffic, and as post-9/11 sterile-area requirements created hard new constraints on when passengers could be airside versus landside. The failure mode was not the concept itself; it was that the concept required a controllable, closed system, and every subsequent decade opened a new hole in that system. [Source: https://www.thetransportpolitic.com/2010/01/26/dulles-airport-replaces-distinctive-mobile-lounge-system-with-aerotrain/]

2. **The 1996 SOM expansion solved the footprint problem and created the coherence problem.** Doubling the terminal's length to 1,240 feet tripled nameplate passenger-handling capacity. It also meant the building became long enough that Saarinen's fundamental spatial logic — enter from the curb, traverse 200 feet, board your vehicle — no longer read from either end of the annex. You now have passengers entering at the east end of a building they cannot see the west end of, with three security checkpoints distributed across that length and no single organizational spine that makes sense. [Source: https://www.som.com/projects/washington_dulles_international_airport__main_terminal_expansion]

3. **The AeroTrain retrofitted a solution to the wrong generation of problem.** The 2010 AeroTrain was designed for a world where midfield concourses were the primary airside, mobile lounges were a legacy liability, and a fast underground connection would rationalize the whole system. What opened was a question-mark-shaped track that serves three stations, leaves Concourse D entirely mobile-lounge-dependent, and has a C-gate station located 500 feet away from the concourse building it serves — accessible only via underground moving walkway — because funding ran out before the loop could close. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AeroTrain_(Washington_Dulles_International_Airport), https://www.thetransportpolitic.com/2010/01/26/dulles-airport-replaces-distinctive-mobile-lounge-system-with-aerotrain/]

4. **The international arrivals experience is the single most operationally consequential failure of the inherited layout.** International terminating passengers at IAD today are transported by mobile lounge to the International Arrivals Building — a structure added in 1991 that sits in limbo: not the main terminal, not a concourse, reached by a surface vehicle, the last major US hub still routing international terminations through a separate physical building on the airfield. In fiscal year 2024 that building processed 3.8 million passengers. During the April 2022–March 2023 period, CBP data documented 348 days with peak wait times exceeding 60 minutes and 98 days exceeding two hours. [Source: https://ryanbourne.substack.com/p/the-travesty-of-dulles-airport-passport, https://www.flydulles.com/Customs]

5. **Concourse C/D is the COO's most operationally distressed asset.** Built in 1983 as a temporary structure, nominally refurbished in 2006, still operating in 2026 with United's 777s and connecting bank traffic. The HVAC was replaced in 2006; the sanitary system had to be reconfigured after repeated backup and flooding events. The facility generates persistent cost: mobile lounge trips to and from the main terminal average 630 per day from C/D gates alone, at a diesel burn of 260,000 gallons per year across the retained lounge fleet. Every day I run this building I am managing an asset whose designers assumed would be demolished in the 1990s. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/washington-dulles-prepares-to-finally-replace-temporary-1980s-era-united-airlines-concourses/, https://www.theautopian.com/these-bizarre-bus-like-mobile-lounges-tried-to-revolutionize-airports-now-theyre-relics-of-the-past/]

6. **The Tier 2 / Concourse E program is operationally correct in its diagnosis and incomplete in its cure.** The 435,000-square-foot Concourse E, opening September 2026 with 14 United gates and direct AeroTrain access, addresses the most urgent gate-count constraint. But it opens before the AeroTrain is extended to new Concourse D (2028–2033 at best), before the C/D temporary structure is demolished, and before the international arrivals mobile-lounge-to-IAB chain is resolved. The $22 billion revitalization concept announced May 2026 would close these gaps by 2034 — if the bonds price, if no scope creep, and if federal funding appears. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/, https://www.futuretravelexperience.com/2025/09/mwaa-announces-new-concessions-for-dulles-concourse-e-opening-in-autumn-2026/]

7. **The mobile lounge fleet is now a safety liability, not just an operational anachronism.** November 10, 2025: a mobile lounge struck the Concourse D dock at an angle at 4:30 PM, injuring 18 passengers, one of whom lost consciousness from a concussion. A second collision occurred later that same month. The fleet's original manufacturers — Budd and Chrysler — no longer supply parts. Annual operating cost is $2.5 million for the original PTVs alone. The $16.4 million refurbishment contract for two prototype Plane-Mates is a holding action; it is not a solution. [Source: https://wtop.com/virginia/2025/11/18-injured-after-mobile-lounge-crashes-at-dulles-airport/, https://www.theautopian.com/these-bizarre-bus-like-mobile-lounges-tried-to-revolutionize-airports-now-theyre-relics-of-the-past/]

8. **Latent capacity in the IAD footprint exists but is distributed wrong.** The main terminal today is running well below its nameplate check-in and circulation capacity because checked baggage volumes have compressed — most United hub traffic is carry-on. But the concourse gate count is constrained, the international capacity is constrained by IAB throughput, and the AeroTrain's J-shaped track creates asymmetric load on B and C stations. Concourse E adds 14 gates to the AeroTrain spine. Concourse D mobile-lounge gates remain the hard ceiling until the AeroTrain extension opens, which is now nominally 2033. That seven-year gap is the operational gap the $22B program is trying to close with bonds rather than patience. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/]

## Evidence Section

**Saarinen's design intent — the documentary record:**
Saarinen's core principle was not aesthetic but operational: "How do we solve the passengers walking for miles." His answer was to bring the vehicle to the passenger rather than the passenger to the plane, accepting the mobile lounge as infrastructure in exchange for a compact, single-building terminal. Walking distance from curb to mobile lounge door was approximately 200 feet in the original configuration. The mobile lounge's acceptance "allowed us to make the terminal a single compact building" — his own words. [Source: https://www.pbs.org/wnet/americanmasters/eero-saarinens-revolutionary-design-dulles-airport/7853/]

The 1962 terminal was 600 feet long and 200 feet wide. Saarinen designed it to extend 320 feet in either direction, anticipating growth. The expansion he anticipated arrived 34 years later. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dulles_International_Airport_Main_Terminal]

**The 1996 expansion — what it built and what it lost:**
SOM's 1996 expansion added 300 feet to each end, faithfully replicating the catenary structure and concrete finish. Terminal length reached 1,240 feet; floor area grew from 500,000 to 1.1 million square feet. Passenger handling capacity grew to a nameplate 40 million per year. The expansion respected the aesthetic but changed the experiential geometry fundamentally: what was a 600-foot building with legible ends became a 1,240-foot building where the east and west ends are 12 minutes of walking from each other and the entry experience differs depending on which curb drop-off lane the taxi knows. [Source: https://www.som.com/projects/washington_dulles_international_airport__main_terminal_expansion]

The 1996 annexes also absorbed the additional mobile lounge docking positions that the expansion required. What the expansion did not address was the relationship between the main terminal and the midfield concourses, which were already operating as a separate world accessible only by vehicle or a surface-level pedestrian tunnel. That relationship would not be rationalized for another 14 years.

**The AeroTrain retrofit — what it solved and what it deferred:**
The AeroTrain opened January 26, 2010, at a total project cost of $1.4 billion including tunnels, stations, and the security mezzanine at the main terminal. The system operates 29 Mitsubishi Heavy Industries Crystal Mover vehicles at up to 42 mph, completing trips to concourses in approximately 72 seconds between stations. It replaced mobile lounges to Concourses A, B, and C. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AeroTrain_(Washington_Dulles_International_Airport)]

What it did not do: it did not close the loop to Concourse D, leaving those gates permanently mobile-lounge-served. The C-gate station is located 500 feet away from the existing C/D concourse building, connected by underground moving walkway, because the station was designed for a future permanent Concourse C location that funding did not reach in 2010. The AeroTrain "won't help customers attempting to get from one end of a terminal to another" — meaning long concourse walks persist for many passengers regardless of transit time. [Source: https://www.thetransportpolitic.com/2010/01/26/dulles-airport-replaces-distinctive-mobile-lounge-system-with-aerotrain/]

SOM's AeroTrain station design preserved Saarinen's terminal above ground by placing the station entirely underground, with a faceted concrete-beam roof, skylights, and terrazzo flooring intended to reference the original terminal's interior. The design decision was architecturally respectful. Its operational consequence was a second basement level that further extended the security-to-gate walk for passengers using the main terminal checkpoints. [Source: https://www.som.com/projects/washington-dulles-international-airport-main-terminal-aerotrain-station/]

**The international arrivals chain — the most unresolved Saarinen legacy:**
IAD's international terminating passenger today boards a mobile lounge at the gate, rides to the International Arrivals Building (opened 1991), processes through CBP, and then either exits or — if connecting — re-bags, re-checks, transits back through TSA, and takes the AeroTrain or another mobile lounge to their domestic departure gate. The minimum connection time for international arrivals at IAD is among the longest of any US hub. [Source: https://www.flydulles.com/Customs]

The IAB processed 3,812,781 passengers in fiscal year 2024. The 2022–2023 period documented 348 days where peak wait times exceeded 60 minutes, and 98 days exceeding two hours. CBP staffing discrepancies — official data claiming booth coverage that observers in the hall counted as materially lower — compounded the physical layout problem. The layout problem is a 1962 decision (no international arrivals building was contemplated in the original Saarinen program because international volume at a greenfield US airport in 1962 was a fraction of what it is today) compounded by a 1991 add-on that created a separate facility rather than integrating arrivals into the main terminal. [Source: https://ryanbourne.substack.com/p/the-travesty-of-dulles-airport-passport]

**The Concourse C/D condition — infrastructure deferral as daily operational reality:**
Concourse C/D was constructed in 1983 as a temporary structure; it remains the primary United Airlines hub concourse in 2026. A rehabilitation in 2006 replaced HVAC, lighting, ceiling tiles, and renovated restrooms. The sanitary system was subsequently reconfigured by Burns Engineering after repeated flooding incidents from years of growth and concession additions. Passenger boarding bridges received HVAC, plumbing, and electrical relocation under a $100 million capital improvements program. The concourse has received more capital investment in sustained-life work than many permanent structures. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/washington-dulles-prepares-to-finally-replace-temporary-1980s-era-united-airlines-concourses/]

Passenger experience: "the concourse was a frightful old O'Hare low ceilings nightmare from the 80s." That is what the COO manages as United's hub. United operates approximately 220 daily flights from IAD, holding approximately 44% of local traffic. [Source: https://upgradedpoints.com/travel/airports/washington-dulles-international-iad-airport/]

**The January 2018 IROPS event — the stress test:**
Winter Storm Grayson forced JFK to close in January 2018, diverting 32 international flights to IAD and delivering approximately 6,000 passengers to a facility not equipped to receive them. Many diverted airlines had no ground support agreements, no staff, and no established procedures at Dulles. Mobile lounge operators deplaned all arriving passengers in bitter cold; maintenance staff manually unloaded approximately 1,000 pieces of luggage from stranded aircraft. CBP processed every arriving international passenger. Passengers spent the night in the terminal. [Source: https://www.flydulles.com/winter-storm-causes-unexpected-6000-passengers-land-dulles]

The IROPS gap that event exposed: mobile lounges are the only way to get international arrivals from the gate to CBP. When 32 additional flights arrive simultaneously, the lounge dispatch system — already running the normal daytime cycle — cannot surge. There is no backup routing. The CBP facility is in a separate building. Stranded passengers cannot self-navigate from their aircraft to anywhere useful because the airfield is the only way to connect them. This is a structural vulnerability that the $22B plan eliminates only if the AeroTrain extension and new concourse program actually complete by 2034.

**The November 2025 mobile lounge crashes — safety reaching decision threshold:**
Two separate mobile lounge collisions at Dulles in November 2025. The November 10 incident: a Plane-Mate struck the Concourse D dock at an angle at 4:30 PM, injuring 18 passengers. One passenger sustained a concussion and lost consciousness. The airport remained operationally open. MWAA confirmed an investigation. A second collision followed later the same month. [Source: https://wtop.com/virginia/2025/11/18-injured-after-mobile-lounge-crashes-at-dulles-airport/]

The fleet's manufacturers no longer supply parts. MWAA had approved a $16.4 million contract in 2023 for two prototype refurbished Plane-Mates, with a $143 million option to refurbish the full fleet. The refurbishment program and the crash series are now running simultaneously, which is not an acceptable operational posture for a system that makes 800 trips per day. [Source: https://www.theautopian.com/these-bizarre-bus-like-mobile-lounges-tried-to-revolutionize-airports-now-theyre-relics-of-the-past/]

**The Tier 2 / $22B revitalization — what it commits and what it defers:**
Concourse E (Tier 2 Phase 1): 435,000 square feet, 14 gates, direct AeroTrain, opening September 2026. Budget: $900 million. Federal grant contribution: $187.4 million. This is the right project and it is arriving roughly 15 years later than the gate-count pressure began demanding it. [Source: https://www.futuretravelexperience.com/2025/09/mwaa-announces-new-concessions-for-dulles-concourse-e-opening-in-autumn-2026/]

The $22 billion May 2026 program adds: AeroTrain extension to new Concourse D ($3.75 billion, 2028–2033); new Concourse B replacing C/D ($2.26 billion); new Concourse D for non-United domestic flights ($3.7 billion); Concourse A/B renovation ($6.2 billion); main terminal extensions of 300 feet east and west. Funded entirely by $21.8 billion in new bonds plus $1.1 billion in airport fees. No confirmed federal contribution as of May 2026. The DOT RFI that triggered the acceleration is a political frame, not a funding commitment. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/]

HOK is the design architect for the expanded program: Concourse E Tier 2 and the new Tier 3 concourse, totaling more than 2.8 million square feet, 22 international gates, and a new Federal Inspection Services facility. HOK's stated intent is to honor Saarinen's main terminal legacy while positioning for contemporary growth. [Source: https://www.hok.com/news/2025-03/hok-to-design-major-expansion-at-dulles-international-airport-adding-more-than-2-8-million-square-feet/]

## The Operational Case For and Against the Thesis

**The thesis as I understand it from the operator's chair:** Saarinen's 1962 design encoded a set of principles separable from its visual signature — modal clarity, the compact single-arrival-experience, midfield flexibility, civic monumentality — and subsequent expansions have violated those principles while sometimes crediting Saarinen for the outcome. A 2026 reinterpretation faithful to the philosophy, not the aesthetic, would make different choices than the current MWAA master plan.

**Where the thesis is operationally correct:**

The current MWAA plan does not honor Saarinen's modal clarity principle. It adds concourses sequentially, each of which creates a new connection type — AeroTrain, mobile lounge, underground walkway, surface-level tunnel — without resolving which mode is authoritative. When Concourse E opens in September 2026, I will be running five distinct passenger-to-aircraft connection types simultaneously: jet bridge from main terminal Z gates, AeroTrain to A and B, AeroTrain to C via the 500-foot underground walkway, AeroTrain to new Concourse E, and mobile lounge to Concourse D and IAB. That is not an operating system; that is sedimentary accumulation. Saarinen would have recognized this as the exact problem he was solving, now recreated in modern materials.

The thesis is also correct that the main terminal as a civic arrival experience remains one of the strongest assets in US aviation. The suspended catenary roof, the interior volume, the transparency from curb to concourse — these are not nostalgia. They are things that function. The problem is that the main terminal now serves predominantly as a security-screening funnel rather than the single coherent gateway Saarinen designed. You check in (if you check in at all), you find one of three checkpoint queues, you descend to the AeroTrain mezzanine, and you leave the Saarinen building. The civic experience is experienced for roughly 400 feet before the operational machine takes you underground and keeps you there.

A Saarinen-philosophy redesign in 2026 would address the modal fragmentation directly. It would make a single decision about how passengers get from terminal to aircraft and build infrastructure to honor that decision completely — which is what the $22B program's AeroTrain extension is trying to do for 2033, and what the mobile lounge system did (imperfectly) for 1962.

**Where the thesis runs into operational resistance:**

Saarinen's "single compact building" principle is obsolete as a prescriptive model. IAD handles approximately 23 million annual passengers, with United's hub requiring the scheduling flexibility of a multi-bank connecting operation across several concourses simultaneously. A truly compact single-building model cannot sustain a 220-daily-flight hub without gate counts that would require a building of a different kind of scale than Saarinen contemplated. The original design was calibrated to 8 million annual passengers — which IAD exceeded in the late 1970s. The principle behind the design (minimize walking, maximize coherence) remains valid. The specific form (a single building with mobile lounge service) is not recoverable at 2026 demand levels.

Post-9/11 sterile-area architecture is a hard constraint that Saarinen could not have anticipated and that fundamentally changes the spatial logic of his design. The pre-2001 terminal had a much more permeable boundary between landside and airside — the mobile lounge concept in its original form assumed passengers could move between zones with less physical separation. Today's sterile-area requirements mean that the international arrivals passenger who must re-clear TSA to make a domestic connection is not experiencing an operational failure; they are experiencing the regulatory framework. Any "Saarinen-philosophy" redesign that does not grapple with this constraint at full strength is not a serious plan.

The international arrivals chain is the place where Saarinen's principles and 2026 TSA/CBP requirements are most in conflict. Saarinen wanted a single arrival experience — one building, one sequence, one spatial clarity. The post-9/11 regime requires separate international arrivals processing, secondary screening of connecting passengers, and physical separation of sterile and non-sterile populations. You cannot satisfy both simultaneously with the IAB-plus-mobile-lounge architecture. The new FIS facility in the Tier 2 expansion program — located in the midfield concourses rather than a separate IAB — is actually a Saarinen-philosophy move: it brings arrivals processing to where the aircraft are, rather than moving passengers to a separate building. That is the correct direction.

## Specific Examples That Frame the Thesis

**Example 1: The C-Gate Platform Offset — Capital Handoff Failure**
The AeroTrain's C-gate station was built 500 feet away from the existing Concourse C/D building, connected by underground moving walkway, because the station was sized and positioned for a permanent replacement concourse that did not get funded. The 2010 capital program closed out with a station serving a building it does not connect directly. Every passenger connecting through IAD on a C-gate itinerary today walks 500 feet underground from the AeroTrain platform to the concourse — which was the exact problem the AeroTrain was supposed to eliminate. This is the canonical example of a design decision made by people optimizing for a future state that never arrived, handing operations a daily liability in the present state. [Source: https://www.thetransportpolitic.com/2010/01/26/dulles-airport-replaces-distinctive-mobile-lounge-system-with-aerotrain/]

**Example 2: Concourse C/D — The Permanent Temporary**
Built in 1983 as a temporary structure. Refurbished 2006. Sanitary system reconfigured after flooding. HVAC replaced. Jet bridges re-engineered. Still hosting United's hub 43 years later, with 777s at gates designed for narrowbodies and a ceiling height that makes a Monday-morning push feel like a submarine. The $22B program proposes demolishing C/D once the new Concourse B replacement opens in the 2028–2030 timeframe. I have heard this before. Until the wrecking ball is in the photograph, C/D is my problem. The design lesson: "temporary" in airport capital language means "for as long as the permanent solution takes to get funded," which at IAD has historically been measured in decades. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/washington-dulles-prepares-to-finally-replace-temporary-1980s-era-united-airlines-concourses/]

**Example 3: The International Arrivals Building — Separate Is Not Equal**
The IAB is a 268,000-square-foot building opened in 1991, the year before the midfield concourses opened, solving the CBP-facility problem for a moment in time. It was the right answer for 1991 volume and 1991 security architecture. In 2024 it processed 3.8 million passengers through a mobile-lounge-accessible separate building while ranking as the second-fastest US hub for international arrival processing — which says more about how bad the US system is overall than it does about IAD's performance. When 32 additional international flights diverted to IAD in the 2018 winter storm, the IAB became the system's critical failure point: the only way to connect 6,000 unexpected passengers from their aircraft to CBP was through a surface-vehicle fleet that was already fully tasked. Saarinen's single-arrival-experience principle would have demanded that CBP processing be inside, or continuously connected to, the primary terminal — which is precisely what the Tier 2 FIS facility proposes to accomplish 35 years later. [Source: https://www.flydulles.com/winter-storm-causes-unexpected-6000-passengers-land-dulles, https://ryanbourne.substack.com/p/the-travesty-of-dulles-airport-passport]

**Example 4: The Mobile Lounge Fleet in November 2025 — Asset Condition Reaching Operational Limit**
Two collisions in one month. Eighteen passengers injured. An investigation underway. Manufacturers who no longer support the fleet. A refurbishment contract for two prototype vehicles. Eight hundred daily trips that cannot be suspended. This is not a design philosophy question; this is a safety question that became a design philosophy question because the mobile lounge is both an operational necessity and an architectural icon. If these vehicles were ordinary buses, MWAA would be procuring replacements in a standard fleet cycle. Because they are Saarinen's mobile lounges, the decision tree includes preservation, refurbishment, and heritage arguments in addition to the operational and safety calculus. The building's operational DNA is now at tension with the building's operational safety. [Source: https://wtop.com/virginia/2025/11/18-injured-after-mobile-lounge-crashes-at-dulles-airport/]

**Example 5: Concourse E Opening 2026 — What the Tier 2 Program Gets Right and Leaves Incomplete**
Concourse E opens September 2026: 435,000 square feet, 14 United gates, LEED Silver, direct AeroTrain, modern FIS provision in Phase 2, natural light, proper ceiling height. This is the right project. It delivers what the COO needs — gate count, AeroTrain connectivity, modern passenger experience — and it honors Saarinen's midfield flexibility principle: the 1962 design explicitly anticipated expansion into the midfield, and a new AeroTrain-served concourse is exactly the kind of midfield growth Saarinen's original system was designed to accommodate (though he imagined mobile lounges, not a fixed transit system, as the connection). What Phase 1 does not deliver: resolution of Concourse D mobile-lounge service, demolition of C/D, the FIS facility for international arrivals, or the AeroTrain western extension. Concourse E in 2026 is a first payment on a debt that has been accumulating since 1983. [Source: https://www.futuretravelexperience.com/2025/09/mwaa-announces-new-concessions-for-dulles-concourse-e-opening-in-autumn-2026/, https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/20/dulles-airport-lands-41m-federal-grant-for-concourse-e-construction/]

## The COO's Honest Assessment of Where Saarinen Was Right and Where He Wasn't

**Where he was right and where current design has lost it:**
Saarinen understood that the terminal building should read as a single coherent thing — not a sequence of independently designed concourse fingers attached to a central headhouse. The midfield concourse model that dominates current US airport design creates exactly the problem he was avoiding: passengers spend their experience in a gate area that has no relationship to the arrival building, navigating corridors that could be anywhere. The Saarinen main terminal still reads as a place. Most of what gets built in concourses today does not. HOK's stated intent to "honor the legacy" of the Saarinen terminal while adding 2.8 million square feet of concourse is an architectural commitment that the operational record suggests is genuinely difficult to sustain at that scale.

The compact-terminal-plus-distributed-aircraft principle is also correct in ways the industry has not absorbed. The push to build ever-longer concourse fingers, adding walking distance in exchange for direct gate access, trades one problem (vehicle reliability) for another (passenger fatigue and connection time). The AeroTrain is a better answer than the mobile lounge. But a 14-gate concourse reached by two-minute AeroTrain is also a better answer than a 14-gate finger requiring an 8-minute walk.

**Where he was wrong and the industry has correctly moved on:**
The mobile lounge concept assumed a controllable, low-volume, high-dwell-time passenger. Modern hub traffic is high-volume, low-dwell-time, and carrying enormous connecting-passenger fractions. At IAD, connecting passengers are a substantial share of United's 220 daily flights. A mobile lounge system for connecting traffic is not a design choice; it is a delay machine. The vehicle adds 10–15 minutes to every connection, creates a dependency chain (vehicle availability, weather, airfield sequencing) that the passenger and the airline have no visibility into, and fails non-gracefully in IROPS. The AeroTrain, for all its incomplete routing, is the right architecture for connecting-hub traffic. Saarinen's vehicle was right for 1962 terminating traffic. It was wrong by 1990 for the connecting hub IAD had become.

The "single arrival experience" principle also requires letting go of one of its corollaries: the idea that the terminal should be the spatial center of gravity for the entire passenger journey. At a hub serving 23 million passengers annually, the gate area — wherever it is — is where passengers spend most of their airport time. The main terminal is a 400-foot walk-through on departure and a baggage-claim visit on arrival. Saarinen designed for a world where the terminal was the experience. Today's passengers experience the concourse. A 2026 reinterpretation has to reckon with that honestly: if you want to honor Saarinen's experience principles, you invest them in the concourse, not just in the main terminal.

**What a 2026 Saarinen-philosophy redesign would look like from this chair:**
Five specific moves, each grounded in operational reality rather than architectural romanticism:

1. Resolve the mode question completely. Pick one vehicle system — the AeroTrain — and build to it. Stop layering mobile lounges, moving walkways, surface tunnels, and underground trains as parallel systems. The $22B program does this if it completes. Every year it does not complete, the layered system accumulates cost and complexity.

2. Bring international arrivals processing into the AeroTrain system. The FIS facility in Phase 2 of Concourse E does this. Accelerate it. The IAB-plus-mobile-lounge chain for international terminating passengers is the single largest operational failure at IAD, and it is a solvable problem with the infrastructure already being built.

3. Demolish C/D on a fixed schedule tied to Concourse E and the new Concourse B opening. "Temporary" facilities persist because the demolition date is never locked before the replacement opens. Lock the date.

4. Extend the main terminal experience into the AeroTrain station rather than treating the underground as a functional interlude. SOM's AeroTrain station does this with the terrazzo and the concrete beam skylights. Extend that intent into the platform dwell spaces. The station is where the passenger transitions between the Saarinen building and the rest of the airport; it should honor that transition, not deny it.

5. Design the new concourses for dwell, not throughput efficiency alone. Saarinen's lounges were comfortable because he understood passengers would spend time in them. Modern concourses optimize for throughput. At a hub with scheduled connecting banks, some passengers have 90 minutes at the gate. The facility should be worth being in. That is not nostalgia; it is customer retention.
