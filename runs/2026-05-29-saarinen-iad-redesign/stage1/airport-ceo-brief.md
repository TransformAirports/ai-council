## Key Findings (Board-Chair Read)

1. **The $22B revitalization has no secured federal funding mechanism as of May 2026.** The December 2025 DOT RFI was an information-gathering exercise, not a procurement. Six P3 consortia submitted proposals ranging from $14.4B to $50B. MWAA's current approved plan is a $7B capital program; the $22B figure includes financing costs and assumes bond issuance on top of the $4.9B in existing outstanding debt. Introducing an "architectural philosophy" frame into this environment costs political capital before a single dollar is committed. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/]

2. **MWAA's ratings are solid but the coverage trajectory is deteriorating.** Moody's Aa3 / S&P AA- / Fitch AA- (all stable, affirmed May 2025) reflect a 2024 DSCR of approximately 3.16x. But Moody's projects coverage declining toward 1.3x over the next five years as MWAA executes approximately $5.5B in new bond issuances between 2025 and 2028. Any discretionary scope addition that is not AIP-eligible, PFC-fundable, or airline-cost-neutral will be scrutinized in the next rating briefing. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/financial-statistics/investor-relations/credit-ratings]

3. **United holds effective veto authority.** United accounts for approximately 70% of landed weight at IAD. The new Airport Use and Lease Agreement runs 2025–2039 under a hybrid residual structure with an Extraordinary Coverage Protection provision triggering at 125% of combined senior and subordinate debt service. Under the Agreement's capital review framework, any project outside pre-approved scope or safety exemptions requires majority-in-interest approval. United's 70% landed-weight share makes its approval functionally determinative. The 15-year agreement window is now locked; the next renegotiation opens circa 2038. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/airports-authority-and-united-airlines-execute-long-term-lease-dulles-international]

4. **The $41M Concourse E FAA grant (May 2026) illustrates what the federal funding system actually rewards.** Capacity expansion, ADA compliance, multimodal connectivity, replacement of aging infrastructure — these are the scoring criteria under the BIL/IIJA Airport Terminal Program. "Architectural philosophy as a binding constraint" scores zero against any of those criteria. The path to federal money runs through capacity, safety, and modal integration framing, not through design intent. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/20/dulles-airport-lands-41m-federal-grant-for-concourse-e-construction/]

5. **The 2012 OIG audit and the AeroTrain cost history define the institutional risk tolerance at MWAA specifically.** The 2012 DOT OIG audit — conducted at congressional request and resulting in permanent federal oversight legislation in 2014 — cited weak procurement controls, mismanagement, and a lack of accountability. The AeroTrain itself opened in January 2010 following scope and cost challenges. A CEO at this institution contemplating ambitious architectural reinterpretation does so against a specific documented history of what congressional scrutiny of MWAA ambition looks like. [Source: https://www.oig.dot.gov/library-item/28647]

6. **The board governance structure creates a long shadow from Washington.** MWAA's 17-member board includes three presidential appointees (who must come from states other than VA/MD/DC and no more than two of the same party), seven Virginia gubernatorial appointees, four DC mayoral appointees, and three Maryland gubernatorial appointees. No member is compensated beyond expenses. The bistate-compact-plus-federal-oversight structure means that any strategy visible enough to appear in a position paper has a direct line to the Hill. The 2012 oversight episode is institutional memory, not ancient history. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Washington_Airports_Authority]

7. **CEO Jack Potter's public posture in 2025–2026 is relentlessly operational.** His public statements from March 2025 through the revitalization announcement emphasize managed airline costs, growth projections, the China market recovery, and Concourse E delivery. "I'm still kind of bullish on where we're going" is not a quote from a CEO who is opening a conversation about design philosophy. The revitalization framing MWAA is using publicly — "awaiting an official announcement of the government's decision" — is the managed distance of an institution that did not choose this fight and is not sure yet whether to embrace it. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2025/03/20/despite-recent-headwinds-mwaa-ceo-says-future-is-bright-at-dulles/]

8. **The Saarinen building is listed on the National Register of Historic Places.** Any material alteration triggers Section 106 review under the National Historic Preservation Act, with a mandatory multi-party consultation process (36 C.F.R. Part 800) that can add 12–18 months to project timelines. This is a constraint in both directions: it complicates demolition or major alteration, but it also gives the CEO a procedural mechanism to slow or shape proposals arriving from outside the institution. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

## Evidence Section

### Bond-Rating Lens

Moody's February 2023 methodology for U.S. Airport Revenue Bonds uses a three-factor scorecard: air traffic demand and market position, carrier base quality and concentration, and financial metrics including coverage ratios and liquidity. S&P applies a two-dimensional enterprise risk/financial risk profile. Both methodologies penalize single-carrier concentration — airports where the dominant carrier represents more than 50–60% of landed weight receive lower "carrier base quality" scores even when that carrier is investment grade. United at 70% landed weight is at the high end of what rating agencies consider in their concentration assessments.

The forward coverage math is the most important number in this brief. MWAA's 2024 DSCR of 3.16x looks healthy in isolation. Moody's projection of approximately 1.3x coverage over the 2025–2030 window — as the $5.5B in planned new bonds layer onto the existing $4.9B debt portfolio — means MWAA will be defending its Aa3 rating in successive briefings with declining headroom. The additional bonds test in MWAA's bond indenture runs at 1.4x–1.5x; the Extraordinary Coverage Protection in the Use and Lease Agreement activates at 125% of debt service. MWAA is not in distress, but the next five years of rating-agency conversations will be about coverage trajectory, not architectural vision. [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-coming-to-market-with-829-million-municipal-bond-deal]

What rating agencies want to hear in a MWAA briefing in 2026: enplanement trajectory through 2030 (favorable, 30%+ growth projected), the Concourse E delivery schedule, the 2025–2039 Use and Lease Agreement security, the hybrid residual structure's coverage floor, and management's plan for the coverage compression during the peak debt-service years. What they do not want to hear: that the capital program includes scope driven by a 1962 architect's design intent, that Section 106 consultations may extend timelines, or that the airport is in a philosophical debate with TSA about sterile-area architecture.

Moody's explicit scorecard factor: "Capital planning and financial management." An airport that embeds discretionary design constraints into its capital program signals lower management quality on that factor, all else equal.

### Airline Use-and-Lease Structure

The January 2025 Airport Use and Lease Agreement — 15 years through 2039 — is the defining document for any capital program discussion at IAD for the foreseeable future. It runs as a hybrid residual: airlines collectively guarantee airport cost recovery, with a settlement mechanism requiring airlines to cover any deficiency within 120 days of fiscal year close. The Extraordinary Coverage Protection provision ratchets debt service coverage support up to 40% (from 25%) if needed to hit at least 1.4x coverage.

The MII structure within this agreement is the specific constraint that a Saarinen-philosophy capital proposal would encounter. Pre-approved projects and safety/regulatory/FAA-funded work are exempted from airline capital review. "Architectural philosophy as a binding constraint" is not a safety project, not FAA-funded, and not in the pre-approved scope. It would land in the Additional Projects category, requiring majority-in-interest approval from carriers representing the majority of landed weight — which is United, functionally.

United's strategic calculus at IAD is not complicated: the airline wants gates, connecting-bank slot efficiency, premium cabin product space, and baggage system capacity. It does not have a stated preference for or against Saarinen's design principles. But it has a strong preference against any capital program uncertainty that raises its landed cost during the period when MWAA is already managing coverage compression. A CEO who frames the Saarinen conversation as "this changes our approach to terminal programming" is asking United to approve scope ambiguity during a period when United's financial team is already modeling MWAA cost exposure through 2039. That is a conversation United will not have without exacting something in return. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-completes-negotiations-new-use-and-lease-agreement]

The prior United lease extension through 2024 — and the successful negotiation of the 2025–2039 agreement — represent the most important financial achievement of the Potter era. A CEO who has just completed a 15-year agreement is not going to reopen the cost calculus on the airline side to accommodate a design philosophy conversation.

### Federal Funding Stack

PFC cap remains $4.50 per enplanement — unchanged since 2000, frozen again in the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (P.L. 118-63). ACI-NA continues to advocate for an increase to $8.50+. In constant-dollar terms, the PFC has lost more than 40% of its purchasing power since 2000. For MWAA, which currently collects PFC revenue as part of its capital program funding mix, the frozen cap means every dollar of capital programming competes harder for PFC eligibility — and PFC-eligible projects are defined by FAA, not by architectural philosophy.

The BIL/IIJA Airport Terminal Program — $1B annually from 2022 through 2026 — scores projects on capacity increase, passenger access, aging infrastructure replacement, ADA compliance, and multimodal connectivity. The May 2026 $41.8M Concourse E grant demonstrates that MWAA can win ATP grants when the project narrative aligns with the scoring rubric. A Saarinen-philosophy reinterpretation of terminal approach — modal separation, arrival experience integration, reduced hard physical barriers — has no natural ATP scoring alignment unless bundled into a capacity or ADA framing. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/iija/airport-terminals]

The federal calendar complication specific to MWAA: the DOT owns the airport under a lease running through 2100. Any significant capital program or P3 structure requires DOT engagement that runs on a different clock than MWAA's board calendar. The December 2025 RFI's January 2026 deadline — with no funding mechanism confirmed five months later — illustrates that federal decision-making on Dulles is operating outside MWAA's scheduling control. The CEO's job in this environment is to maintain optionality, not to commit program scope to a philosophy that has no federal constituency.

### MWAA-Specific Governance Overlay

The bistate compact structure creates a board that is unusually exposed to political cross-currents. Seven Virginia gubernatorial appointees, four DC mayoral appointees, three Maryland gubernatorial appointees, three presidential appointees — each with their own principal whose political interests may not align. The 2012 OIG audit episode is the relevant precedent: congressional attention to MWAA's capital management practices resulted in a DOT monitor installation and eventually permanent oversight legislation. A CEO who wants to put architectural philosophy into the capital program as a binding constraint is creating a surface area for exactly that kind of scrutiny.

The AeroTrain is the institutional reference point. Opened January 26, 2010 after significant cost and scope challenges, it replaced the mobile lounges for midfield concourse access — and is now itself being discussed as the backbone of the $22B revitalization's underground circulation concept. The lesson the institution absorbed from AeroTrain is not architectural; it is programmatic control. MWAA's subsequent investment in Oracle construction management systems and program controls reflects that learning. A CEO introducing a new philosophical frame into capital programming is fighting that institutional memory.

The Section 106 dynamic is the one place where Saarinen's legacy is already a legal reality, not an aspirational frame. The main terminal's National Register listing means any material alteration — including the proposed 300-foot east/west extensions in the current revitalization concept — triggers mandatory consultation with the Advisory Council on Historic Preservation, the State Historic Preservation Offices of Virginia and DC, and potentially interested parties including preservation organizations. This is not a constraint the CEO can waive. It is also, from the CEO's chair, a procedural tool: Section 106 timelines can be used to slow externally-driven proposals while MWAA develops its own preferred approach.

## The Financial-and-Political Case: For and Against

### The Case For Putting Saarinen-Philosophy in the Room

The honest version of the affirmative case is not romantic. It is strategic.

The $22B revitalization, as currently framed by the DOT and the six P3 respondents, is a scope problem looking for a governing framework. The RFI responses range from $14.4B to $50B — a spread that reflects the absence of any agreed program definition. Into that vacuum, MWAA must insert something. A Saarinen-philosophy frame — properly stripped of aesthetic nostalgia and recast as a set of operational and experiential principles — could serve as MWAA's internal program definition tool before external pressure defines it for them.

Specifically: the principle of modal clarity (a single arrival experience, no mixing of departing and arriving passengers in curb-side circulation), the principle of mid-field expansion flexibility (the original design's intent to accommodate future capacity without compromising the main terminal's organizational logic), and the principle of the airport as civic space (not as a retail strip with gates attached) — these are defensible as contemporary operational principles without requiring anyone to invoke Saarinen's name. The CEO does not need to say "Saarinen." The CEO needs a program framework that distinguishes MWAA's approach from a generic hub-expansion-as-fast-as-possible narrative.

The Section 106 process makes Saarinen's intent legally present in any case. If Section 106 consultation is mandatory, MWAA might as well shape the "no adverse effect" determination by having a documented, principled position on what the building's design intent was, rather than letting preservation advocates or external consultants define that record.

There is also a political-capital argument. The Trump administration's interest in making Dulles "the international gateway our nation's capital deserves" is a monumental-civic-architecture argument, not an operational efficiency argument. MWAA can make common cause with that framing — translating Saarinen's civic-gateway intent into contemporary design language — without surrendering financial or programmatic control to a federal timeline that has no funding mechanism.

Cost of putting it in the room: approximately six months of internal alignment effort, a Section 106 pre-consultation with the ACHP, and one difficult conversation with United about what "design philosophy as binding constraint" means for their gate programming assumptions. United 70% landed weight; United's concern is schedule and cost, not aesthetics. If the CEO can demonstrate that the Saarinen-philosophy reinterpretation does not raise United's per-enplanement cost or compress its connecting-bank efficiency, United's opposition softens from active veto to passive skepticism — which is manageable.

### The Case Against

The honest version of the negative case is also not romantic. It is probabilistic.

MWAA's coverage trajectory declines toward 1.3x through 2030. Rating agencies are watching. The next bond issuance — part of the $5.5B planned 2025–2028 program — will go to market with rating briefings that need clean, straightforward program narratives. "We are executing a $7B capital plan against a 2039 airline use agreement that covers our cost structure; here is our enplanement trajectory; here is our coverage model" is a briefing that gets Aa3 affirmed. "We are also reconsidering our capital philosophy in light of our founder architect's design intent, which requires us to engage with TSA about sterile-area architecture and with ACHP about historic consultation" is a briefing that generates questions the CEO cannot answer in a ratings call.

The MII risk is not theoretical. United at 70% landed weight has effective veto over Additional Projects. A Saarinen-philosophy frame, if it generates any scope uncertainty — changes to terminal programming, gate flexibility assumptions, connecting-concourse layouts — puts the CEO in a conversation with United about whether the additional scope requires MII approval. That conversation, once opened, takes months. And it happens during the period when MWAA has just executed a 15-year agreement that United signed. United did not sign a 15-year agreement to have the program philosophy renegotiated in year two.

The AeroTrain history and the 2012 OIG audit are not just institutional memory — they are documented congressional record. A position paper arguing for Saarinen-philosophy as a binding capital constraint will be read by the Hill staff who oversee MWAA under the 2014 permanent oversight legislation. That audience does not read it as architectural sophistication. They read it as scope risk on a program that is already the subject of an unsolicited administration revitalization initiative with no funding mechanism.

The cleanest version of the negative case: the right CEO move is to acknowledge Saarinen's design intent as context (legally necessary under Section 106 anyway), use it to resist the most operationally damaging elements of any externally-driven revitalization proposal (linear concourses that violate the mid-field flexibility principle, for instance), and continue executing the $7B approved plan while the $22B proposal finds its funding mechanism. That is not capitulation; it is sequencing. The CEO who defends institutional program control while a federal initiative with no money finds its footing is in a much stronger position at the 2039 lease renegotiation than the CEO who consumed political capital on a design philosophy conversation and generated rating-agency questions in the process.

## Specific Data Points and Precedents for the Strategist

**1. MWAA coverage compression: 3.16x in 2024, projected ~1.3x by 2028–2030.** This is the single most important financial constraint on any discretionary capital scope. The next three rating-agency briefings will be about coverage trajectory, not program ambition. Any scope addition that is not AIP-eligible, PFC-fundable, or airline-cost-neutral narrows the already-compressing coverage margin. The Aa3/AA- ratings are not guaranteed through the debt-service peak years. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-bond-ratings]

**2. United's 70% landed weight vs. the 65% AIPP approval threshold.** Under the Airport Investment Partnership Program framework (49 U.S.C. § 47134), any P3 concession structure requires 65% airline approval by landed weight. United at 70% clears that bar alone. Any capital program discussion — whether framed as Saarinen philosophy or not — has to clear United as a practical matter before it can proceed. The CEO who has not had the United conversation privately before making any public statement about the capital program is the CEO who loses the next board vote. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

**3. DFW's "drive-to-the-gate" design failure as the canonical negative precedent.** DFW opened in 1974 with a design philosophy premised on origin-and-destination traffic. By 1981, American and Delta had converted it to a hub; by the late 1980s, the majority of passengers were connecting traffic, and the drive-to-gate concept was structurally obsolete — generating billions in remediation through the TRIP program in the 2010s. The lesson is that design philosophy embedded in capital decisions has a 30-year amortization schedule, and the industry context that makes it correct can change within a decade. Saarinen's design principles face the same test: which of them are operationally durable for the 2030–2060 period, and which are specific to a regulated-era aviation context that no longer exists? [Source: https://airporthistory.org/dfw-rebuild-1.html]

**4. Denver's $400M baggage system as the canonical cost-overrun cautionary tale.** DEN's automated baggage handling system — a $193M contract that exceeded $400M and delayed opening by 16 months — is the precedent every airport capital program review invokes when an innovative concept enters the capital plan. The DEN baggage system was not architectural philosophy; it was technology. But its failure pattern — scope driven by a vision rather than operational validation, cost escalation during execution, timeline compression under political pressure — maps directly onto the risk profile of a Saarinen-philosophy frame embedded in a $22B program that has no confirmed funding mechanism and an eight-year completion target. [Source: https://medium.com/@ketan.keshav7/the-400-million-lesson-what-the-denver-airport-baggage-fiasco-taught-us-about-escalation-and-project-failure]

**5. Section 106 as a two-directional tool.** The National Register listing of the Saarinen terminal means Section 106 consultation is mandatory for any material alteration — including the current revitalization proposal's 300-foot extensions and new linear concourses. This is not optional. The precedent value for the CEO: Section 106 timelines (12–18 months for meaningful consultation) can function as a managed brake on externally-driven scope changes, while simultaneously creating the official documentary record through which MWAA defines Saarinen's design intent on its own terms. The CEO who engages proactively with the ACHP and the State Historic Preservation Offices shapes that record; the CEO who treats Section 106 as a compliance obstacle cedes that shaping to preservationists whose interests may not align with operational needs. The TWA Hotel's successful adaptive reuse at JFK — completed under a Section 106 process that required genuine engagement — is the positive precedent. The Oculus at the World Trade Center — where preservation arguments and architectural ambition combined to produce a $4B structure with $11B in actual cost — is the cautionary one. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

## CEO-Chair Synthesis

The Strategist should understand the IAD CEO's position in May 2026 this way: the institution has just executed the most important financial instrument of its generation (the 2025–2039 Use and Lease Agreement), has an approved $7B capital plan, has a Concourse E under construction with a federal grant in hand, and is watching an administration-driven $22B revitalization proposal with no funding mechanism develop on a timeline outside MWAA's control. The CEO is simultaneously managing a coverage trajectory that is tightening toward its covenant floor during the peak debt-service years.

Into that environment, a position paper arguing that 1962 design philosophy should be a binding consideration in the capital program lands as follows: it is intellectually interesting, it has legal force through Section 106 whether or not the CEO chooses to engage with it philosophically, and it has zero natural constituency in the three systems — bond indentures, airline use agreements, and federal grant rubrics — that actually govern capital decisions.

The CEO's honest move is neither to dismiss the thesis nor to champion it. It is to use it.

Saarinen's principles — recast as program-definition tools, not as aesthetic constraints — give MWAA a basis for asserting institutional control over a program that is currently being defined by a DOT RFI, six P3 consortia, and a political framing the airport did not initiate. The CEO who walks into the next DOT conversation with a documented, principled position on what the Dulles design intent was — grounded in the Section 106 record, stripped of nostalgia, translated into contemporary modal-clarity and civic-experience language — is in a stronger negotiating position than the CEO who has only a $7B plan and a polite statement that MWAA looks forward to working collaboratively with the administration.

That is the narrow lane in which Saarinen-philosophy serves the CEO's interest. Outside that lane, it is an expensive conversation that a board whose primary lenses are bond rating, airline relationship management, and federal funding pursuit does not need to have.

The piece this run produces should make that lane visible and specific — or honestly conclude that the lane does not exist.
