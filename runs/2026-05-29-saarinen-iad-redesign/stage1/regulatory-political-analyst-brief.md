# Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief
## Stage 1 — Saarinen IAD Redesign: The Constraint Architecture

**Run:** If Saarinen Were Designing Dulles Today
**Date:** May 2026
**Agent:** Regulatory-Political Analyst

---

## Key Findings

1. **The sterile-area paradigm enacted post-9/11 under 49 CFR Part 1542 is the single most consequential regulatory constraint on any Saarinen-philosophy reinterpretation.** The regulation does not prescribe architectural form, but it requires that access to the sterile zone be controlled through TSA-supervised screening — meaning the "walk from curb to aircraft without a hard barrier" concept central to Saarinen's 1962 design cannot be revived in any legally compliant form. The question is not whether a barrier exists but where it sits and how it is designed.

2. **The TSA Checkpoint Design Guide (CDG) translates the regulatory minimum into an architectural standard that presumes a post-9/11 hub terminal.** The guide mandates 8-foot solid walls separating sterile from non-sterile zones, specific lane-footprint dimensions, queue management areas, and exit-lane controls — a geometry that is fundamentally incompatible with Saarinen's original single-level, open-floor-plate concept. CT scanner installations in 2024–2025 are deepening the physical equipment footprint.

3. **The ADA framework (49 CFR Parts 37 and 38; 14 CFR Part 382; 2010 ADA Standards for Accessible Design) did not exist in 1962 and applies directly to any revived mobile-lounge-style modal access.** Any new modal-access vehicle system at IAD must provide level or near-level boarding (horizontal gap ≤3 inches, vertical gap ≤5/8 inch), accessible routes throughout, and full compliance with DOT transportation-vehicle accessibility specifications. Saarinen's original mobile lounges, designed without these requirements, cannot be replicated in new construction without fundamental redesign of the boarding interface.

4. **The NRHP listing of the Saarinen main terminal (1997) and the 1987 Programmatic Memorandum of Agreement create a mandatory Section 106 consultation process for any federal undertaking at IAD.** The PMOA governs all federally funded projects at the airport. Any major renovation or demolition of the 1962 building is an adverse effect requiring FAA consultation with the Virginia SHPO and ACHP. The constraint runs both ways: it limits destructive change but also limits how aggressively a Saarinen-philosophy reinterpretation can depart from the inherited fabric.

5. **The MWAA governance structure — a bistate compact with a 17-member board, federal DOT lease to 2100, permanent DOT IG oversight established by 2014 legislation, and a track record of OIG findings about procurement failures — creates a political environment deeply skeptical of design-driven capital.** After the 2012 OIG audit and the Silver Line cost overrun controversy, the institutional reflex is toward demonstrable utilitarian outcomes (gates added, bags per hour, TSA wait times) rather than architectural ambition. Any Saarinen-philosophy argument must arrive in a language auditors and airline executives can defend to appropriators.

6. **The $22B revitalization concept (December 2025 DOT RFI, presented to airlines May 2026, no confirmed federal funding mechanism) is the live decision context.** The plan — $21.8B in new bonds plus $1.1B in airport fees, targeting 2034 completion — preserves the Saarinen main terminal shell with 300-foot extensions on each side while eliminating mobile lounges via expanded AeroTrain. The plan leaves Saarinen's philosophical legacy largely unexamined: it retains the aesthetic object while systematically eliminating the modal-access concept and the single-terminal clarity that made the philosophy coherent.

7. **CBP's Airport Technical Design Standards (ATDS, current version 2021) for Federal Inspection Services space impose a secondary constraint on international gate design.** The required corridor system — separate arrivals routing from gate to primary inspection, primary inspection, baggage claim, secondary inspection, and transfer-passenger recheck — drives gate and concourse geometry that Saarinen's program brief never addressed. The ATDS update cycle (every three years) means these requirements shift faster than airport capital programs.

8. **The preservation framework constrains both directions simultaneously.** The 1987 PMOA and the NRHP listing mean MWAA cannot simply demolish and rebuild the 1962 building without a finding of adverse effect and a mitigation agreement — but they also do not prevent it. A project-specific Programmatic Agreement (already determined appropriate by VDHR/VASHPO for the current terminal redevelopment EA) can memorialize mitigation measures and allow construction to proceed. Historic preservation law does not veto; it requires consultation and documentation.

## Evidence

### 49 CFR Part 1542 — The Sterile Area and What It Forecloses

The foundational definition: "Sterile area means a portion of an airport defined in the airport security program that provides passengers access to boarding aircraft and to which the access generally is controlled by TSA, or by an aircraft operator under part 1544 of this chapter or a foreign air carrier under part 1546 of this chapter, through the screening of persons and property." [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/49/1540.5]

Part 1542 Subpart C (Operations) requires airport operators to control access to sterile areas and ensure that all individuals entering through a screening checkpoint are screened for weapons, explosives, and incendiaries. Individuals escorted into a sterile area without screening must remain under continuous escort. [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-B/chapter-XII/subchapter-C/part-1542/subpart-C]

The regulatory text itself does not specify wall heights, lane dimensions, or checkpoint placement. Those architectural specifics are carried in TSA's Checkpoint Design Guide (CDG). The CDG (Revision 4.0, 2012) requires that all walls adjacent to the non-sterile side of a checkpoint be at least 8 feet high to prevent the passage of prohibited items, that checkpoint modules conform to TSA-specified lane widths, and that exit-lane control devices (sensor-activated one-way doors or turnstiles) be installed to prevent reverse flow. [Source: https://airportscouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Checkpoint_Design_Guide_CDG_Rev_4_0.pdf]

TSA's Checkpoint Requirements and Planning Guide (CRPG, May 2025) is the current operational document. It specifies that checkpoint placement may occur at or near the terminal entrance (making the full landside sterile) or deep in the terminal — but that massing passengers in public areas before screening must be avoided if entrance placement is used. [Source: https://www.tsa.gov/sites/default/files/checkpoint-requirements-and-planning-guide-read-me-first-crpg-may-2025.pdf]

**The Saarinen conflict is specific:** The 1962 design placed all arrivals, departures, and the pre-aircraft zone on a single open floor plate. Passengers moved from ticket counter to mobile lounge to aircraft without encountering a fixed, walled barrier. This geometry is irreconcilable with the 8-foot wall requirement, the lane footprint, and the queue management areas required by the CDG — regardless of where the checkpoint is placed. Moving the checkpoint to the terminal entrance (perimeter screening) would make the entire building sterile but would create exactly the unsecured mass-gathering problem the CDG cautions against. Positioning it deep in the terminal replicates the current arrangement and preserves the problem Saarinen-philosophy seeks to solve. There is no geometrically simple escape from this constraint.

The CT scanner rollout at more than 250 checkpoints as of 2024–2025 adds equipment depth: CT units have a slightly smaller tunnel opening than conventional X-ray and require all carry-on items in bins, increasing the footprint of each screening lane. [Source: https://www.tsa.gov/computed-tomography]

### ADA Standards and Modal-Access Requirements

14 CFR Part 382 Subpart D requires that intra-terminal transportation systems — "moving sidewalks, shuttle vehicles and people movers" — comply with 49 CFR Parts 37 and 38. [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-II/subchapter-D/part-382/subpart-D]

49 CFR Part 38 (Accessibility Specifications for Transportation Vehicles) specifies that where horizontal gaps exceed 3 inches or vertical gaps exceed 5/8 inch between vehicle floor and boarding platform, a bridge plate, ramp, or equivalent device must be provided. For key stations and new construction, the horizontal gap must be no greater than 4 inches and the vehicle floor must be within plus or minus 2 inches of platform height. [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-A/part-38]

The 2010 ADA Standards for Accessible Design (adopted by DOJ for Title II and Title III entities) require accessible routes throughout airport facilities, including to and from all passenger services areas. [Source: https://www.ada.gov/law-and-regs/design-standards/2010-stds/]

**The Saarinen conflict is specific:** The original IAD mobile lounges were not designed for level boarding. They were vehicle-to-aircraft boarding platforms that worked because the aircraft door height and the lounge loading bridge were calibrated to narrow jet types common in 1962. A 2026 revival of mobile-lounge-style modal access — where passengers board a vehicle at terminal level and the vehicle drives to the aircraft — requires either: (a) a boarding interface that achieves 49 CFR Part 38 gap and height tolerances at every possible aircraft parking position (geometrically difficult for remote stands), or (b) a permanently stationed lift mechanism at the vehicle-to-passenger interface (which transforms the lounge into a jetway-equivalent and defeats the modal-flexibility argument). Neither option is architecturally trivial, and neither was a constraint Saarinen's design team faced.

### FAA Advisory Circular 150/5360-13A — Terminal Planning Assumptions

AC 150/5360-13A (2018, the current governing document) addresses outbound baggage processing, sterile-area security integration, concourse gate planning, and curb-to-gate passenger flow. The AC was developed in a post-9/11, post-hub-and-spoke world and presumes: (a) a separation between landside (public) and airside (screened) that requires dedicated checkpoint zones; (b) multi-concourse configurations at large airports with consolidated screening locations feeding concourse gates; (c) inline baggage screening systems integrated into the baggage handling system architecture. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/resources/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.current/documentNumber/150_5360-13]

**The Saarinen conflict is specific:** The AC's hub-terminal planning framework assumes that gates are organized in linear or finger-pier concourses served by a central screening node. Saarinen's design assumed no fixed concourse at all — the midfield position of aircraft was flexible, with mobile lounges as the connective tissue. The AC does not prohibit remote-stand configurations, but its planning guidance for gate-to-checkpoint walking distances, level changes, and baggage system architecture all presume the connected-concourse model. Designing against these assumptions is not prohibited — the AC is guidance, not regulation — but it means the airport cannot rely on the AC's planning nomographs and must conduct original analysis. That is a capital-budget line item and an FAA review burden that the current program does not appear to be carrying.

Baggage handling is a secondary but important constraint: TSA requires 100% checked-baggage screening (49 CFR Part 1544.205), and the current inline EDS (explosive detection system) architecture embeds screening equipment inside the baggage handling conveyor system. This architecture presumes a terminal building deep enough and structurally capable of housing inline belts. The Saarinen building's basement levels (added in later renovations) can accommodate this; a new remote-stand or remote-terminal configuration would require replication of the inline system at each location.

### CBP Federal Inspection Services Requirements

CBP's Airport Technical Design Standard (ATDS, 2021 version) establishes space programming for FIS facilities: sterile arrival corridor, primary inspection hall, baggage claim, secondary inspection, and transfer-passenger recheck. The ATDS specifies that arriving international passengers must be kept physically separate from all non-FIS passengers until they clear all inspections. [Source: https://www.sskies.org/images/uploads/subpage/PARAS_0052.PlanningConsiderationsInternationalArrivalsFacilities_.FinalReport_.pdf]

Each FIS project also requires a Program of Requirements (POR) issued by CBP's Office of Field Operations — a project-specific document that governs spatial programming and is mandatory. CBP's three-year ATDS update cycle means the FIS requirements applicable to a 2026 design decision may differ from those applicable in 2030 during construction, a risk that must be carried in the design contingency.

**The Saarinen conflict:** IAD's international operations — roughly 10% of operations but a disproportionate share of revenue — require FIS space that the 1962 design did not address at all (international service came later). Any reinterpretation of Saarinen's modal-access concept that touches international arrivals must accommodate the full CBP sterile corridor and inspection sequence. This is not a philosophical constraint; it is a mandatory operational separation that CBP enforces through the POR. No design argument overrides it.

### MWAA Governance and the Political Environment

MWAA was created by the Metropolitan Washington Airports Act of 1986, transferred from FAA under a 50-year lease (extended in 2003 and again in 2024 to 2100). The authority is governed by a 17-member board: 7 appointed by Virginia's governor, 4 by the D.C. mayor, 3 by Maryland's governor, and 3 by the President. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Washington_Airports_Authority]

The 2012 DOT OIG audit — requested by Representatives Frank Wolf and Tom Latham — found "questionable procurement practices, mismanagement, and a lack of overall accountability." MWAA used categorical exceptions to limit competition for almost two-thirds of contracts exceeding $200,000. [Source: https://www.oig.dot.gov/library-item/28647] The 2014 Silver Line financial management audit found MWAA's controls "not sufficient to ensure eligibility of expenses on FTA's Dulles Rail Project grant." These findings produced permanent statutory DOT IG oversight in 2014 legislation. [Source: https://www.oig.dot.gov/library-item/28646]

**The political economy reading:** MWAA has been in oversight recovery mode for over a decade. Its board members serve six-year terms across three jurisdictions with different political priorities. Any capital initiative that can be characterized as architect-driven rather than operationally justified — and that does not produce immediately legible metrics (gates per dollar, TSA throughput per lane) — arrives into a governance environment already primed for skepticism. The $22B plan's presentation to airlines in May 2026 leads with gate counts, AeroTrain extension, and mobile-lounge elimination, not design philosophy. That is not accidental.

The AeroTrain precedent matters: the $1.4–$1.5B underground people-mover opened in January 2010 after absorbing what MWAA described as a "D2 program" of $5B in capital improvements. The system opened six months late, was truncated from its originally planned circular route (9/11-era traffic drop forced cutbacks), and still does not serve all concourses — which is precisely why the $22B plan now proposes to extend it. [Source: https://airportimprovement.com/article/dulles-debuts-15-billion-train-system/] The AeroTrain is the canonical example of a capital program where engineering ambition met revenue reality and lost. Any 2026 design argument that revives Saarinen's mobile-lounge philosophy — however technologically updated — will be read against this precedent by MWAA's board, its lenders, and every congressional staffer who attended the 2012–2014 oversight hearings.

### The $22B Revitalization as Live Political Backdrop

The DOT published its Request for Information in December 2025 following Secretary Duffy's announcement. The FAA maintains a dedicated page for the initiative. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/dulles] MWAA presented a detailed plan to airlines in May 2026: $6.2B for main terminal and Concourse A work (2027–2034), $3.75B for AeroTrain extension and underground connector (2028–2033), $4B for new Concourse C (2026–2030), $3.7B for Concourse D east gates (2027–2033), and $2.26B for Concourse B (2028–2034). Total: $21.8B in bonds plus $1.1B in airport fees. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/]

The plan preserves the Saarinen main terminal and extends it 300 feet on each side. It eliminates mobile lounges via AeroTrain expansion. It adds linear concourses in the midfield. It does not, in the public presentation, engage the question of whether these choices honor or violate Saarinen's design philosophy; it is a throughput-optimization program dressed in the architectural preservation of the 1962 shell.

Critically: no federal funding mechanism is confirmed as of May 2026. The DOT RFI invited ideas from developers, architects, and engineers; it did not commit federal capital. The $21.8B bond structure makes the airport's cost per enplanement untenable for low-cost carriers at projected traffic levels — estimated at $90.64 per enplanement by 2035 — and cannot proceed without United Airlines' endorsement. United represents approximately 70% of landed weight at IAD. Under FAA privatization rules (which would require at least 65% carrier support by landed weight), United's approval is not just influential; it is determinative. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/dulles-airport-could-finally-kill-its-mobile-lounges-but-the-rebuild-plan-now-costs-22-billion/]

**The window for philosophical intervention:** The program scope is not yet locked. MWAA is "awaiting an official announcement of the government's decision" before proceeding. The design documents for the main terminal extension and Concourse A are at a stage where a Saarinen-philosophy frame could still be introduced into the program of requirements. Once MWAA's board approves a full capital budget and bond issuance, the design philosophy question closes into an interior-design problem.

### Historic Preservation — The 1987 PMOA and NRHP Listing

The Saarinen main terminal was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1997 under Criteria A and C (significance in transportation history and architecture). [Source: https://www.docomomo-us.org/register/washington-dulles-international-airport-main-terminal]

The FAA executed a Programmatic Memorandum of Agreement (PMOA) on May 29, 1987 — at the time of the DOT-to-MWAA lease transfer — with the Advisory Council on Historic Preservation and the Virginia State Historic Preservation Officer (VASHPO). MWAA inherited the PMOA as part of the lease. All federally funded or federally approved undertakings at IAD since 1987 must be conducted in consultation with VASHPO pursuant to this agreement. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/public-notice-iad-terminalconcourse-redevelopment-and-associated-development-draft-programmatic]

For the current terminal/concourse redevelopment EA, VDHR/VASHPO has determined that a project-specific Programmatic Agreement is appropriate. This agreement will "outline processes for continued design review and include mitigation measures to address any future adverse effects." [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/public-notice-iad-terminalconcourse-redevelopment-and-associated-development-draft-programmatic]

Section 106 of the National Historic Preservation Act (54 U.S.C. § 306108) requires federal agencies to take into account the effect of any undertaking on historic properties and afford the ACHP a reasonable opportunity to comment. For airport projects, FAA is the lead federal agency. The process produces a Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) or Programmatic Agreement (PA) — a legally binding document, not an advisory opinion. It does not give the SHPO a veto; it requires consultation and documented mitigation.

**The constraint runs both ways:** The NRHP listing prevents MWAA from demolishing or materially altering the 1962 building without a finding of adverse effect and a documented mitigation strategy. But it does not prevent demolition — it requires process. The 1990s expansion (the east and west annexes designed by SOM) was conducted without formal NRHP listing at the time; preservation considerations shaped design but did not control it. The current Programmatic Agreement framework for the $22B program will likely impose design-review requirements on whatever is done to the 1962 shell — but the shell's 300-foot extensions and ticket-counter renovation described in the May 2026 plan do not appear to trigger automatic rejection.

For a Saarinen-philosophy reinterpretation: the PMOA/Section 106 process is an asset as much as a constraint. If MWAA were to pursue design moves that honor Saarinen's spatial intent — preserving the open floor plate, limiting internal subdivision of the main hall, maintaining visual continuity between landside and terminal volume — the SHPO consultation process provides an institutional mechanism to enforce those choices against value-engineering pressure. Conversely, the same process would scrutinize any decision to significantly alter the 1962 handrails, the catenary roof geometry, or the interior column arrangement.

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: The AeroTrain as Precedent for Modal-Philosophy Programs

The AeroTrain (opened January 2010, cost $1.4–1.5B) is the most instructive precedent for any revived modal-access philosophy at IAD. It was conceived in the 1990s as a full circular underground people mover that would have replaced all mobile lounges. The post-9/11 traffic crash forced MWAA to cut the system to a J-shaped partial circuit serving Concourses A, B, and C only. Concourse D and the international arrivals concourse retained mobile lounges. The system opened six months behind schedule.

The lesson is not that modal-access infrastructure fails — it is that modal-access infrastructure planned at one traffic level and built at a different one gets truncated in exactly the ways that matter most. The $22B plan now proposes to complete what the D2 program started, 15 years later, at costs that have inflated severalfold. A design philosophy that relies on a network-effect modal-access system (the system must serve all gates to be coherent) is particularly vulnerable to this truncation dynamic.

### Case Study 2: The 1990s Annexes and the Failure of Preservation-by-Style

When MWAA expanded the main terminal in the mid-1990s with SOM-designed east and west annexes, the design brief was to match Saarinen's aesthetic language. The result was criticized by preservation advocates (and retrospectively by most observers) as producing additions that referenced Saarinen's visual grammar without honoring his spatial logic. The building got longer but the single unified arrival hall — Saarinen's core spatial argument — was diluted by the added concourse space.

This is the canonical example of what happens when "preserve the building" is substituted for "preserve the philosophy." The annexes passed muster with the historic preservation process because they did not alter the 1962 fabric directly. They were architecturally respectful in material and form. But they violated the operational and spatial principles that gave the original building its coherence.

The $22B plan's 300-foot terminal extensions risk repeating this pattern. Preserving the Saarinen shell while extending it without engaging the question of what the shell was actually doing is a design decision that looks like preservation and functions as erasure.

### Case Study 3: Section 106 as Delay Mechanism at Chicago O'Hare

FAA Section 106 consultation for the O'Hare modernization program (Terminal Area Plan) produced a full environmental assessment with Appendix H dedicated to historic properties. The process required FAA to identify historic resources in the area of potential effect, consult with the Illinois SHPO, and document findings before approving the undertaking. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/TAP_Final_EA_Appendix_H_0.pdf]

The IAD analogue: the 1987 PMOA was specifically designed to streamline Section 106 for a complex, multi-decade airport with ongoing federal-approval needs. It replaced case-by-case Section 106 consultation with a standing agreement framework. The project-specific Programmatic Agreement now being developed for the terminal/concourse redevelopment EA is the functional equivalent for the $22B program. Both streamline the process; neither eliminates it. At IAD, the existence of the standing PMOA means Section 106 is not a veto but it is a mandatory process step that adds time and documentation burden to any design change affecting the 1962 fabric.

## Direct Quotes and Data Points for Strategist Use

**1. The sterile-area definition (verbatim regulatory text):**
"Sterile area means a portion of an airport defined in the airport security program that provides passengers access to boarding aircraft and to which the access generally is controlled by TSA, or by an aircraft operator under part 1544 of this chapter or a foreign air carrier under part 1546 of this chapter, through the screening of persons and property." — 49 CFR § 1540.5 [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/49/1540.5]

Use this to anchor the argument that the sterile-area requirement is definitional, not discretionary. The regulation defines the concept; TSA's Airport Security Program defines where the boundary sits in each specific terminal. There is design flexibility in where the line is drawn; there is no flexibility in whether the line exists.

**2. The checkpoint wall height (verbatim from CDG):**
"All walls adjacent to the non-sterile side need to be at least 8'-0" high to prevent the passage of prohibited items from the non-sterile area to the sterile area." — TSA Checkpoint Design Guide, Revision 4.0 (2012) [Source: https://airportscouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Checkpoint_Design_Guide_CDG_Rev_4_0.pdf]

Use this to make the architectural conflict concrete: an 8-foot wall through Saarinen's open floor plate is not a minor modification. It is a categorical restructuring of the spatial logic.

**3. The $22B cost-per-enplanement figure:**
Projected cost per enplanement of $90.64 by 2035 under the $22B plan. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/dulles-airport-could-finally-kill-its-mobile-lounges-but-the-rebuild-plan-now-costs-22-billion/]

Use this to frame the political economy: no design philosophy discussion survives a number that makes the airport uncompetitive for low-cost carriers. Any Saarinen-philosophy argument that adds cost must explain what operational or revenue outcome justifies it against this baseline.

**4. The MWAA OIG audit finding (2012):**
"MWAA used categorical exceptions to limit competition for almost two-thirds of MWAA's contracts that exceeded $200,000." — DOT OIG Audit Report, October 31, 2012 [Source: https://www.oig.dot.gov/library-item/28647]

Use this not as a current indictment (MWAA has reformed) but as institutional memory: the oversight environment around MWAA has been structured by this finding. Any design-driven capital initiative that cannot be defended in terms of competitive procurement, measurable outcomes, and lender-grade financial metrics arrives into an institutional environment that was specifically reformed to be suspicious of discretionary executive spending.

**5. The PMOA continuity:**
"MWAA has undertaken extensive renovations and expansion of the airport, conducted in consultation with the Virginia SHPO pursuant to the PMOA." — FAA Section 106 Progress Report context [Source: https://www.achp.gov/sites/default/files/2018-09/2014%20FAA%20Section3ProgressReport.pdf.pdf]

Use this to establish that the historic preservation process is not a new risk for the $22B program — it is a standing obligation that has been operational since 1987. The question is not whether Section 106 applies (it does) but whether MWAA's project-specific PA for the terminal redevelopment incorporates design-quality obligations or merely documents mitigation of physical alteration.

## What Can and Cannot Move, and on What Timeframe

**Cannot move on any airport-project timeline:**

- **49 CFR § 1540.5 (sterile-area definition) and Part 1542 (airport security program requirements):** These are post-9/11 statutory and regulatory constructs. Changing them requires TSA rulemaking with notice-and-comment — a multi-year process that would require DHS to conclude that the security rationale has changed. There is no political constituency for weakening the sterile-area concept. Airports that have tried to negotiate alternative configurations (perimeter screening, integrated biometric corridors) have achieved modifications in their Airport Security Programs, not changes to the regulation. The design space is at the level of ASP negotiation, not rulemaking.

- **ADA requirements under 49 CFR Parts 37 and 38:** These are statutory (ADA, 1990; Air Carrier Access Act, 1986) and regulatory. The specific technical tolerances (3-inch horizontal gap, 5/8-inch vertical gap) for people-mover boarding are from DOT rulemaking. Any new modal-access system at IAD must comply. The question is engineering — how to achieve compliance — not whether to.

- **CBP ATDS requirements:** These are CBP administrative standards updated every three years. They can be modified in project-specific PORs, but the basic requirement for physical separation of international arrivals cannot be negotiated away. It is a Customs statute (19 U.S.C. § 1459 et seq.) implemented through CBP operational policy.

**Can be negotiated at the Airport Security Program level (multi-year, not permanent):**

- **Checkpoint placement within the terminal:** Where the sterile-area boundary sits is defined in each airport's TSA-approved Airport Security Program. Airports have negotiated entrance-level perimeter screening (making the whole terminal sterile) and other configurations. This requires TSA regional approval and carries operational tradeoffs. It is not a quick change, but it is a lever that exists.

- **CT scanner deployment configuration:** TSA's rollout of CT scanners has proceeded checkpoint by checkpoint through funded procurement. An airport with design influence over its checkpoint renovation can shape how CT equipment is integrated, including the lane count, placement, and queue geometry. This is a design opportunity, not a constraint.

**Can be shaped through Section 106 and PMOA process (project-timeline, but requires intentional use):**

- **Design-quality obligations in the Programmatic Agreement:** The project-specific PA being developed for the terminal/concourse redevelopment EA can include provisions requiring that design changes to the 1962 fabric be reviewed against spatial and experiential criteria, not merely material and visual criteria. This is the mechanism by which a Saarinen-philosophy frame can be embedded in a legally binding document. It requires MWAA to negotiate for it and FAA to agree — neither of which is automatic.

- **Interior organization of the 300-foot terminal extensions:** The current plan describes extensions of 300 feet on each side for ticket counter renovation and new concourse access. The spatial organization of those extensions — whether they honor or subvert the open-floor-plate logic — is a design decision not yet made. This is the most accessible entry point for a Saarinen-philosophy argument in the live program.

**Cannot be changed and should be let go:**

- **The mobile lounge as literal object:** The original mobile lounge — a detached vehicle that drives across the apron — is functionally incompatible with: (a) 49 CFR Part 38 boarding-gap tolerances at remote aircraft positions, (b) modern wingtip clearance and foreign object debris requirements on active taxiways, (c) the passenger-experience baseline that competing international airports have established. The ADA constraint alone requires engineering that transforms the lounge into something other than what Saarinen designed. A 2026 modal-access philosophy should look for the principle (separation of passenger and aircraft movement, modal clarity between landside and airside) rather than the vehicle.

- **The single-terminal, all-gates-from-one-building geometry:** Dulles's traffic volume and gate count requirements — projected 30% growth by 2030 over 2024 figures per MWAA's own projections — cannot be served from a single midfield terminal of any physically achievable size. The principle worth preserving is not the single building but the legibility and clarity of the passenger sequence.
