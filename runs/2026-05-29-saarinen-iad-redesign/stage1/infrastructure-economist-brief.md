# Infrastructure Economist Brief: IAD Capital Economics and the Opportunity Cost of the $22B Program

**Stage 1 | Saarinen-IAD Redesign Run | Infrastructure Economist Agent**
**Date: May 2026**

---

## Key Findings

1. **The $22B number is not a program budget — it is a financing stack.** The draft concept published via the December 2025 DOT RFI describes five discrete project components totaling approximately $19.86B in construction value (Concourse E/C buildout at $4B; Main Terminal expansion at $6.2B; AeroTrain extension at $3.75B; Concourse D East Gates at $3.7B; Concourse B buildout and C/D demolition at $2.26B). The $22B headline adds financing costs and inflation to a $9B MWAA Capital Construction Program already authorized under the 2025–2039 Use and Lease Agreement. The RFI attracted 31 respondents, with cost proposals ranging from $500M–$1B (TSI, renovation only) to $35–50B (Phoenix/Ironbridge, near-complete reconstruction). No confirmed federal funding mechanism existed as of May 2026. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/] [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

2. **The CPE math is disqualifying without a subsidy mechanism.** IAD's current signatory airline CPE is $11.17 (2025 rate book) against FY2024 actuals of $12.88 and a current total debt of $3.7B on 14.3M enplanements in FY2025 ($256 debt per enplanement). The $22B concept projects CPE reaching $90.64 by 2035 — a 7x increase from 2025 rates. For reference, United Airlines effectively abandoned IAD as a full competitive hub in 2013 when CPE hit $26.47. IAD's 2024 record of 27.25M total passengers was driven partly by cross-subsidy from Reagan National; the airline approval architecture (requiring majority approval under the Use and Lease Agreement, and 65% by landed weight and carrier count under 49 U.S.C. § 47134 for any privatization) gives United — commanding 68% of flights — practical veto power over capital programs that threaten CPE viability. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airports/iad] [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/dulles-airport-could-finally-kill-its-mobile-lounges-but-the-rebuild-plan-now-costs-22-billion/] [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

3. **The MWAA Debt Service Coverage Ratio will compress materially under any large program.** Current DSCR is 3.16x (FY2024 actuals, above 2.77x in FY2023). Moody's projects DSCR declining to approximately 1.3x over the next five years under the existing $9B program, compared to 1.63x in 2024 — moving toward but remaining above the 1.25x bond covenant floor. Any incremental $12B+ transaction layered on top would require bond defeasance, additional bonds test passage, and likely a rating agency review. MWAA carries $4.9B in outstanding revenue bond debt across 19 series under a 2001 Master Indenture with a 1.40x DSCR target as a floor on revenue sufficiency. The constraint is structural, not discretionary. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis] [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/financial-statistics/investor-relations/credit-ratings]

4. **Renovating the occupied Saarinen terminal in-place carries a severe cost multiplier.** Airport terminal renovation in occupied, operational conditions does not have a single published multiplier in primary sources reviewed, but the case evidence points in a consistent direction. Denver's Great Hall program — which primarily relocated two security checkpoints from Level 5 to Level 6 inside an operational terminal — reached a final cost of at least $2.35B against an original budget of $770M, a 3x overrun, with completion slipping to 2028 (seven years late). The TSA checkpoint relocation itself required extensive sterile-area reconfiguration. The IAD Main Terminal expansion at $6.2B includes renovation of the 1962 building in occupied conditions plus a 300-foot extension and above-grade connector; the proportion attributable to in-place renovation versus net-new construction is not disclosed in available program documents. PDX's comparable 2024 renovation-expansion in occupied conditions (mass timber; $2.15B total, includes expansion) delivered on budget, suggesting large-format occupied renovation is achievable but highly design- and management-dependent. [Source: https://www.flydenver.com/about-den/projects-and-infrastructure/great-hall-program/] [Source: https://www.bdcnetwork.com/building-sector-projects/airports/news/55166116/portland-opens-2-billion-mass-timber-expansion-and-renovation-to-its-international-airport]

5. **Architectural ambition has a documented but non-universal cost premium.** KCI's new terminal (SOM, 2023) delivered 1.1 million square feet and 40 gates for $1.5B — approximately $37.5M per gate and $1,364 per square foot — on time and on budget, with scope increased by five gates and cost reduced more than $400M from original contractor pricing. Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER) opened ten years late and at roughly €10B against a €2.83B original estimate — a 3.5x overrun with root causes in systems integration and management structure, not design ambition per se. Heathrow T5 (£4.3B, 2008) delivered on time and within budget despite involving complex in-airport infrastructure, through integrated risk management and collaborative contracts. Salt Lake City's $5.1B greenfield program (HOK, Phase 1 opened September 2020) remained on schedule. The pattern: design ambition in itself is not the primary cost driver; management structure, site complexity, and occupancy condition are. [Source: https://www.clarkconstruction.com/our-work/projects/kansas-city-international-airport-mci-new-single-terminal] [Source: https://www.euronews.com/2020/10/31/berlin-airport-opens-10-years-late-and-three-times-over-budget] [Source: https://infrastructuredeliverymodels.gihub.org/case-studies/heathrow-terminal-5/]

6. **"Fighting TSA on sterile-area architecture" is a capital and political budget question, not a design question.** Airports bear the capital cost of terminal modifications needed for TSA checkpoint infrastructure under 49 U.S.C. § 44903; aviation security capital grants reimburse 90% of eligible costs, but airports initiate and partially fund the projects. Richmond International Airport's checkpoint consolidation (one checkpoint replacing two) grew from an initial estimate of $40M to over $200M in 2024–2025. Seattle-Tacoma's Checkpoint 1 relocation required a combination of Airport Development Fund, future revenue bonds, and FAA grants. Any Saarinen-philosophy reinterpretation that materially alters the sterile-to-non-sterile boundary geometry — reducing the post-9/11 barrier architecture in favor of integrated arrival experience — would require TSA Operations Center approval, potential regulatory waiver or pilot program designation, and capital cost borne primarily by MWAA with partial federal reimbursement. The political budget for this fight at an airport that already carries $3.7B in debt and projects CPE reaching $90.64 is effectively zero under current program conditions. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-security-costs-tsa-reimbursement] [Source: https://www.wric.com/news/local-news/henrico-county/tsa-checkpoint-upgrades-ric-preview/amp/]

7. **The AeroTrain was the first Saarinen-principle reinterpretation — and it cost $1.4B in 2010 dollars.** The original mobile lounges were Saarinen's operational mechanism for modal clarity: keep the terminal compact, eliminate fixed-pier concourses, move passengers to mid-field aircraft remotely. The AeroTrain ($1.4B, opened January 2010) replaced the mobile lounges' aircraft-connection function with underground rail, but required a new security screening mezzanine inside the main terminal, materially altering Saarinen's sterile-area geometry. The AeroTrain extension in the $22B concept adds $3.75B more to extend westward to Concourse D (2028–2033). The combined AeroTrain investment — $5.15B across two capital cycles — is the single most expensive component of the Saarinen-era reinterpretation at IAD, and its cost trajectory illustrates what modal-access infrastructure costs when retrofit into an existing building envelope. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AeroTrain_(Dulles_International_Airport)] [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/]

8. **Airport construction costs have outpaced general inflation by 6.6 percentage points over the past decade, and material escalation is accelerating.** Airport construction costs increased 38.3% from 2014 to 2024 versus 31.7% general CPI. The Engineering News-Record Building Cost Index compound increase over five years (2019–2024) was approximately 33%. Section 232 tariffs effective June 2025, increasing to 50%, add an estimated 10–15% to material projections for projects completing post-2026, with steel up 15–25% year-over-year in Q1 2026. The NPIAS-identified infrastructure needs have risen from $35.1B (FY2019–2023) to $67.5B (FY2025–2029) — a 92% increase in under a decade — reflecting both backlog accumulation and cost inflation. Any program cost estimated pre-2025 should be treated as materially understated. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-construction-cost-inflation]

## Evidence

### IAD $22B Program Scope and Components

The December 2025 DOT RFI (Federal Register Vol. 90, No. 233, December 5, 2025, Document 2025-22010) solicited proposals for IAD revitalization and attracted 31 respondents. [Source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/12/05/2025-22010/revitalizing-washington-dulles-international-airport] The most detailed public cost breakdown appeared in Airport Architecture newsletter reporting obtained by FFXnow (May 12, 2026): total program at $22B targeting 2034 completion, with Ferrovial's RFI response providing the most granular independent cost estimate at $14.4B (AACE confidence range $11.5–$18.7B). [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/]

The five-component scope: (1) Main Terminal and Concourse A ($6.2B, 2027–2034); (2) AeroTrain extension and underground connector ($3.75B, 2028–2033); (3) Concourse E/C buildout ($4B, 2026–2030); (4) Concourse D East Gates ($3.7B, 2027–2033); (5) Concourse B buildout and C/D demolition ($2.26B, 2028–2034). The total of the five components is $19.91B before financing and inflation. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/dulles-airport-could-finally-kill-its-mobile-lounges-but-the-rebuild-plan-now-costs-22-billion/]

The relationship to the existing MWAA program: MWAA's board-approved 2025–2039 Use and Lease Agreement includes a $9B Capital Construction Program. The IAD portion is $6.99B, covering a new 14-gate concourse (Concourse E/C), design work for a fifth runway, and associated infrastructure. The $22B RFI concept would require layering an incremental ~$12B+ above the existing committed program. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-completes-negotiations-new-use-and-lease-agreement]

### IAD Financial Structure

IAD FY2025 financials: total operating revenue $516.4M; operating expenses (ex-depreciation) $330.9M; operating income $16.7M; total debt $3.7B; unrestricted cash $1.1B; FY2025 enplanements 14.3M; debt-per-enplanement $256. FY2025 CPE: $9.56 (actuals); current 2025 rate book: $11.17. FY2024 CPE: $12.88. Current DSCR: 3.16x (FY2024); Moody's projects decline to approximately 1.3x over five years under existing program. Bond covenant floor: 1.25x; Use and Lease Agreement target: 1.40x. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airports/iad] [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

Note: IAD's 2024 passenger record was 27.25M total passengers (13.6M enplanements). FY2025 enplanement figure of 14.3M from DWU financial database reflects fiscal year ending different from calendar year. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2025/03/03/dulles-airports-2024-passenger-total-set-new-all-time-record/]

### Cost Comparables: Renovation in Occupied Conditions

**Denver Great Hall:** Original budget $770M (2017 P3 contract with Ferrovial/Great Hall Partners); contract terminated 2019 after $183M termination payment; projected final cost at least $2.35B; completion slipping to 2028 — seven years late. The project's primary scope was TSA checkpoint relocation from Level 5 to Level 6 within an occupied terminal. [Source: https://airlineweekly.skift.com/2022/01/denver-approves-airport-project-with-1-billion-cost-overrun/] [Source: https://www.flydenver.com/about-den/projects-and-infrastructure/great-hall-program/]

**AeroTrain (IAD):** $1.4B final cost (2010), covering underground people mover and new security screening mezzanine inside the main terminal. This is the most directly comparable prior MWAA capital project for assessing occupied renovation complexity at IAD. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AeroTrain_(Dulles_International_Airport)]

**PDX Terminal Core Redevelopment:** $2.15B (2024 opening), occupied renovation-expansion including nine-acre mass timber roof and expanded concourse in full operational facility. Delivered on budget. Primary lesson: occupied renovation at scale is achievable, but requires tight design-builder integration, phasing discipline, and no mid-stream scope changes. [Source: https://www.bdcnetwork.com/home/news/55166116/portland-opens-2-billion-mass-timber-expansion-and-renovation-to-its-international-airport]

### Cost Comparables: Architectural Ambition vs. Conventional Programs

**KCI New Terminal (SOM, 2023):** $1.5B, 1.1 million square feet, 40 gates. $37.5M per gate; $1,364/SF. On time, on budget; scope increased by five gates and cost reduced by $400M from original contractor pricing. Greenfield site on existing airport grounds; no occupied-building complexity. [Source: https://www.clarkconstruction.com/our-work/projects/kansas-city-international-airport-mci-new-single-terminal]

**SLC Terminal Redevelopment (HOK):** $5.1B total, phased program, Phase 1 opened September 15, 2020 on schedule. 2.6 million square feet, LEED Gold. [Source: https://slcairport.com/thenewslc/rebuild-highlights/]

**Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER):** Original cost estimate €2.83B; final cost approximately €10B; opened October 2020, nine years late. Root causes: fire safety systems integration failures, design changes during construction, and management structure dysfunction — not design ambition. [Source: https://www.euronews.com/2020/10/31/berlin-airport-opens-10-years-late-and-three-times-over-budget]

**Heathrow T5:** Budget £4.3B (2002 prices); delivered within budget, on time, March 2008. Cited as the exception to the UK infrastructure delivery rule; attributed to integrated risk-sharing contracts and collaborative partnership model. [Source: https://infrastructuredeliverymodels.gihub.org/case-studies/heathrow-terminal-5/]

**JFK New Terminal One:** $9.5B total; Phase A (22 gates, new arrivals and departures halls) targeted 2026 opening. $2.55B Green Bond issued 2024 (largest-ever municipal bond for an airport project), followed by $1.37B in 2025 bonds for remainder of Phase A costs. Cost performance relative to original budget not publicly disclosed; program management team explicitly notes that "frequent cost checks with independent cost estimates" were required to maintain budget control. [Source: https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/the-new-terminal-one-at-jfk-successfully-concludes-1-37-billion-2025-green-bond-issuance-302512038.html]

## The Bracketed Answer: Is Saarinen-Philosophy Reinterpretation a $50M Question or a $5B Question?

The honest answer is that the question cannot be answered as posed, because "Saarinen-philosophy reinterpretation" is not a single scope category — it applies differently to different components of the $22B program. The bracketed analysis by component:

**Inside the existing $9B MWAA Capital Construction Program (i.e., within the authorized capital envelope):**

- The Concourse E/C buildout ($4B, authorized) involves no renovation of the 1962 main terminal. Saarinen-philosophy choices here are primarily about mid-field expansion strategy and modal access from the main building — questions of program brief and design brief, not capital budget. Cost delta of a Saarinen-philosophy approach vs. a conventional approach at this stage: likely $0–$100M (design fees and scope adjustments within the program).

- The $6.2B Main Terminal expansion and Concourse A work is where the Saarinen-philosophy question bites hardest and most expensively. The main terminal renovation component (in-place, occupied, 1962 building) is where the Denver Great Hall analog is most relevant. The proportion of the $6.2B attributable to philosophically-driven choices — say, preserving the Saarinen vault geometry vs. enclosing it, or fighting to maintain single-arrival-hall experience vs. splitting it — is not separately disclosed. A rough bracket: if in-place renovation choices add a 20–40% premium to the renovation component (say, $1–1.5B of the $6.2B is renovation-attributable), philosophically-driven preservation choices might add $200M–$600M to that component. This is speculative. No primary source discloses the renovation vs. new-construction split within the $6.2B.

**Above the existing $9B envelope (i.e., the incremental $12B+ of the $22B concept):**

- The AeroTrain extension ($3.75B) is primarily an infrastructure investment that executes the original Saarinen mid-field logic — it is, in that sense, a Saarinen-philosophy project. The philosophy question is not whether to extend the AeroTrain but whether the modal clarity it provides is worth $3.75B incremental to the current $1.4B already invested, given that the 2025–2039 program already includes AeroTrain operations.

- The sterile-area redesign question — the most philosophically loaded of Saarinen's principles, and the one most directly constrained by post-9/11 TSA architecture — is not a standalone capital line item in the $22B program. Embedding a meaningful sterile-area reinterpretation into the Main Terminal work would require negotiating with TSA at the program-planning stage, not as an independent scope package. The capital cost is likely $100M–$500M (comparable to the Richmond and Seattle checkpoint work, scaled to IAD's volume) with 90% federal reimbursability — meaning MWAA's net cost is $10M–$50M if TSA cooperation is obtained. The political cost is higher and is not denominated in dollars.

**Summary bracket:**
- Saarinen-philosophy as a set of design-brief constraints on projects already in the $9B authorized program: $0–$100M scope impact, mostly in design fees and program-brief choices that do not materially change construction cost.
- Saarinen-philosophy as the reason to preserve specific architectural features of the 1962 building during the $6.2B renovation: $200M–$600M premium, with high uncertainty.
- Saarinen-philosophy as the reason to execute the AeroTrain extension and mid-field concourse strategy: already embedded in the $22B concept; the question is whether the concept is executed at all, not whether Saarinen's logic applies.
- Saarinen-philosophy as the reason to fight TSA on sterile-area geometry: $10M–$50M net capital cost to MWAA; the binding constraint is political and regulatory, not financial.

The $5B reading of the question would only be true if "Saarinen-philosophy reinterpretation" meant commissioning a fundamentally different master plan structure from scratch — one that, for example, demolishes and rebuilds the main terminal rather than renovating it, or that invests in a different modal-access system than the AeroTrain extension. That is not a philosophy question; that is a compete-with-the-existing-program question, and the capital-budget answer is not in reach under current MWAA financial conditions without a material federal grant or novel P3 structure.

## Caveats and Limitations

1. **The $22B program has no confirmed funding mechanism as of May 2026.** The DOT RFI is not a solicitation for proposals and does not constitute a federal commitment. All cost figures should be treated as pre-development estimates with AACE Class 5 accuracy (−50% to +100% range) at best. The Ferrovial AACE-referenced range of $11.5–$18.7B for their $14.4B estimate reflects this uncertainty explicitly.

2. **IAD's CPE figures vary by source and definition.** The $11.17 (2025 rate book), $9.56 (FY2025 actuals), and $12.88 (FY2024 actuals) figures reflect different calculation methodologies — signatory versus all-airline, fiscal year versus calendar year, and residual versus compensatory cost center allocations. The $90.64 projected 2035 CPE appears in Gary Leff's View from the Wing analysis and is not sourced to a MWAA financial model; treat as indicative rather than authoritative.

3. **The ACRP Report 25 occupied-renovation cost multiplier.** The run prompt referenced a 1.5x–2.5x greenfield multiplier from ACRP Report 25. Research for this brief did not independently verify this specific figure from the primary source text; the report's Volume 1 and Volume 2 were located but not fully reviewed at the level required to confirm or deny the specific multiplier. The Denver Great Hall and similar case studies provide empirical evidence of overruns in occupied-airport renovation conditions, but these involve factors beyond the in-place/greenfield distinction. Agents and strategists should not cite the 1.5x–2.5x multiplier without direct verification against ACRP Report 25 Vol. 1, Chapter on cost estimation.

4. **Moody's DSCR projection of 1.3x.** This figure appears in DWU Consulting's AI-generated RFI analysis and is attributed to Moody's but without a specific rating action date. The figure is plausible given MWAA's capital program trajectory but should be verified against the actual Moody's credit opinion (available through MWAA's investor relations portal or EMMA).

5. **United Airlines' effective veto.** The characterization of United holding a practical gatekeeping role is analytically defensible given 68% flight share but the statutory 65% test under 49 U.S.C. § 47134 uses landed weight, not flight count. United's landed weight share was not independently verified. The constraint is real; the precise threshold mechanics are more complex than the flight-share figure implies.

6. **No MWAA-published cost breakdown separating renovation from new-construction within the $6.2B Main Terminal component.** The bracketed cost estimate for Saarinen-philosophy preservation choices within that component ($200M–$600M) is analytical inference, not primary source data. A program-level cost review by an owner's representative with access to current design documents would be required to narrow this bracket.
