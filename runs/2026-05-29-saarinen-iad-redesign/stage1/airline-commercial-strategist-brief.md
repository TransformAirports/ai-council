## Key Findings

1. **United controls roughly 58% of enplanements at IAD by market share and roughly 47% of local origin-and-destination traffic.** With approximately 6.4 million enplaned passengers annually and 250–280 daily departures as of 2025, United's dominance at Dulles exceeds the threshold at which a carrier effectively holds veto power over any airport capital program through the Majority-in-Interest mechanism — especially under the new 15-year MWAA use and lease agreement signed effective January 1, 2025. [Source: https://simpleflying.com/where-united-airlines-washington-dulles-passengers-go/] [Source: https://crankyflier.com/2026/04/16/united-continues-to-gain-local-traffic-in-its-hubs-even-without-buying-american/]

2. **IAD's current CPE of $12.88 (FY2024) sits at the competitive middle of the large-hub range — well below the $29–30 range at ORD and LAX but above ATL's $3.94.** This position is not cost-peril territory for United, but the $22 billion revitalization program carries debt service implications that will push CPE materially higher. A program of that magnitude, financed by $21.8 billion in new bonds, will affect the airline rate base and United's appetite for signing long-dated preferential-use agreements. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airports/iad] [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/large-hub/cpe]

3. **Saarinen's 1962 program brief was written for a CAB-regulated, point-to-point airline system with no connecting traffic architecture.** The program assumed passengers arrived at the terminal building, checked in, and rode a mobile lounge to their aircraft — a linear, non-branching passenger journey. Hub-and-spoke requires the opposite: a branching, time-compressed, multi-leg journey in which the minimum connecting time (35 minutes domestic-to-domestic at IAD, 65 minutes international-to-domestic) drives gate adjacency requirements that Saarinen's compact pavilion-plus-remote-aircraft model structurally cannot satisfy without radical modification. [Source: https://www.flyertalk.com/forum/united-airlines-mileageplus/2147294-consolidated-iad-connection-time-logistics-domestic-international-archive.html]

4. **United's $500 million investment in Concourse E — 14 gates, 40,000 square feet of lounge space, full jetbridge contact stands — is an explicit rejection of Saarinen's modal logic.** The new concourse moves passengers directly from the secure concourse environment to the aircraft without any intermediate modal step. The AeroTrain alignment with Concourse E represents a decade-delayed correction of the fundamental design error in the 1990s midfield concourse program: parking a transit stop where a concourse was planned rather than where passengers actually fly. The gap between planned and actual infrastructure forced United passengers into the longest walks in the network on connecting itineraries. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/billions-spent-at-dulles-but-despite-new-gates-and-lounge-united-flyers-to-still-be-stuck-using-40-year-old-temporary-concourse/]

5. **Premium-product real estate is now a non-negotiable line item in United's hub economics, not an amenity.** United's MileagePlus loyalty program generated $2.9 billion in partner revenue in 2024 — the Chase co-branded credit card being the dominant contributor — and premium cabin revenue grew 14% year-over-year in 2024. The lounge infrastructure at hubs is directly tied to credit card acquisition, premium cabin yield management, and loyalty program economics. A terminal design that reduces United's ability to control and brand its lounge real estate at IAD is a design that attacks a specific, quantified revenue stream. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/united-is-building-two-of-the-worlds-largest-lounges-internal-presentation-shows-how-hub-spending-will-drive-its-next-profit-leap/]

6. **IAD's connecting passenger ratio is approximately 40% (6.6 million passengers of 16.6 million total), with European connections — particularly Germany, UK, and Spain — representing 27% of the connecting base.** This ratio is lower than pure funnel hubs like Atlanta or Charlotte but high enough that any terminal configuration that degrades connecting-itinerary times will materially reduce United's hub contribution margin. The connecting traffic directly supports the transatlantic joint venture with Lufthansa and Air Canada, which is the highest-yield traffic category at IAD. [Source: https://simpleflying.com/where-united-airlines-washington-dulles-passengers-go/]

7. **The $22 billion MWAA revitalization program (May 2026) requires United's effective consent under the MII process in the new 15-year use agreement.** The plan calls for four new linear concourses, AeroTrain extension to all concourses, demolition of the temporary C/D concourse, and a 300-foot east-west extension of the Saarinen main terminal — financed by $21.8 billion in new bonds. United's signatory status and traffic dominance give it affirmative MII leverage. No airline reactions were on record as of the plan's May 2026 disclosure, but the absence of public comment from United is itself a signal that negotiations are live. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/]

## Evidence Section

### United's Hub Position at IAD

United operates IAD as a banked international hub structured around four primary departure waves. The morning domestic bank has grown fastest since 2022; the two transatlantic departure banks — the primary commercial purpose of IAD within United's network — have strengthened with new destinations including Dakar, Nice, and Venice (summer 2025), and Reykjavik (May 2026). [Source: https://crankyflier.com/2025/02/11/dulles-is-on-the-rise-at-united/]

The hub's structural character is different from United's other primary hubs in one important way: DCA's slot controls redirect short-haul domestic traffic away from IAD, making IAD skewed toward medium- and long-haul flying. As Cranky Flier has documented, "IAD is the long-haul airport for Washington DC, has a role with plenty of O&D for transcon and international, but is limited in O&D for short haul due to DCA." [Source: https://www.airliners.net/forum/viewtopic.php?t=1497861] This structural characteristic has direct architectural implications: the connecting traffic flowing through IAD is disproportionately international, with longer minimum connection times and higher baggage complexity than purely domestic connecting traffic. Saarinen's terminal, conceived for a world without domestic hub connections at all, was designed for neither.

In 2024, IAD set an all-time passenger record at 27.25 million total passengers. United's share of roughly 6.4 million enplaned passengers represents approximately 47% of local O&D traffic, with connecting passengers adding to United's total through-put contribution. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2025/03/03/dulles-airports-2024-passenger-total-set-new-all-time-record/]

### The Premium-Product Revenue Connection

United's "United Next" strategy, formalized in 2021 and accelerated through 2024–25, made the physical product at hub airports a central variable in the airline's financial model. The strategy targets a 30% increase in seats per departure over 2019 levels through higher-gauge aircraft and cabin densification, with premium cabins carrying a disproportionate share of the yield gain. [Source: https://markets.financialcontent.com/stocks/article/finterra-2026-3-18-united-airlines-ual-the-premiumization-pivot-and-the-united-next-era]

The lounge is the physical manifestation of that premium strategy on the ground. United describes its new 40,000-square-foot United Club at Concourse E as increasing IAD's total United Club square footage by 70% — from roughly 23,000 square feet across three existing locations to a combined figure that will rank the IAD installation among the nine largest airport lounges globally. The Houston IAH club, at 55,000 square feet, will be the largest. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/united-is-building-two-of-the-worlds-largest-lounges-internal-presentation-shows-how-hub-spending-will-drive-its-next-profit-leap/]

The commercial logic linking lounge investment to revenue is direct: United's Chase MileagePlus co-branded credit card portfolio drives $2.9 billion in annual partner revenue. Access to United Club locations is one of the primary acquisition and retention levers for card spend. An airport design that reduces United's ability to locate, brand, and scale lounge infrastructure on its terms is a design that introduces friction into a $2.9 billion revenue channel. [Source: https://www.pointswithacrew.com/the-curious-case-of-delta-and-cvg/]

### Gate Architecture Requirements: What Changed Between 1962 and 2026

Saarinen's original terminal processed aircraft at remote stands. The mobile lounges served as the modal link. The geometry of the aircraft on the ramp was irrelevant to the terminal building's architecture — planes could be parked anywhere within lounge range. This was design logic appropriate to a regulated era in which all aircraft at a given airport were similarly sized (predominantly Boeing 707s and Douglas DC-8s at IAD's opening) and connections between flights were rare rather than the primary commercial purpose of the airport.

A 2026 hub terminal requires the following from its gate architecture that Saarinen's program brief did not contemplate:

**Contact stands for every gate.** Remote stands at hub airports impose a boarding time penalty of 15–20 minutes relative to jetbridge gates — a penalty that is unacceptable when domestic minimum connection times are 35 minutes. United's new Concourse E specifies jetbridges at all 14 gates. The old temporary C/D concourse, built in the 1980s, still relies on mobile lounges and bus operations for some positions, creating the connect-time vulnerability that the new concourse explicitly corrects. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/billions-spent-at-dulles-but-despite-new-gates-and-lounge-united-flyers-to-still-be-stuck-using-40-year-old-temporary-concourse/]

**Gate adjacency that supports banking choreography.** A hub bank is a choreographed simultaneous arrival-and-departure sequence in which feeder flights arrive within a compressed window, connecting passengers transfer, and a second wave departs. At IAD, United's 5–7 PM bank serves 81 destinations; the 10 PM bank serves 53. The physical distribution of gates across concourses determines whether those 81 departures can be reached by passengers arriving in the prior bank. When connecting passengers must transit between concourses — currently requiring the AeroTrain or a long walk — the connecting window compresses and United must either extend MCTs (losing competitive connecting itinerary options) or absorb misconnect costs. The $22 billion plan's AeroTrain extension to all concourses addresses this directly, but that solution is itself a rejection of Saarinen's concept: it is a rail system threading through a building designed to need no rail system. [Source: https://crankyflier.com/2025/02/11/dulles-is-on-the-rise-at-united/]

**Widebody-capable gate infrastructure.** IAD's transatlantic operation requires gates capable of simultaneously servicing Boeing 777-200ERs, 787-9s, and 787-10s alongside A319s and B737-MAX narrow-bodies. Gate sizing — pavement, jetbridge reach, wingtip clearances — must accommodate this mix without reassigning aircraft or displacing connecting flows. Saarinen's mobile lounge system is, paradoxically, theoretically more flexible on this dimension: the lounge can reach any aircraft on any stand. But the turn-time and connect-time penalties make that flexibility commercially irrelevant.

**Inline baggage screening integrated into the concourse spine.** Post-9/11 TSA requirements for checked baggage screening mandated inline explosive detection systems (EDS) integrated into the baggage conveyor architecture. Saarinen's terminal has been through multiple baggage handling system upgrades, including the IAD East/West Baggage Handling project that reconfigured the conveyor system and added a three-level below-grade concrete addition to the east basement. [Source: https://www.clarkconstruction.com/our-work/projects/iad-eastwest-baggage-handling-concourse-cd-rehabilitation] The inline EDS requirement creates a physical constraint that Saarinen's original building did not account for and that the $22 billion plan must resolve for each new concourse through dedicated below-grade screening infrastructure.

### The MWAA Use Agreement and MII Leverage

The 15-year use and lease agreement signed effective January 1, 2025 governs the financial relationship between MWAA and the airlines at both IAD and DCA. The agreement covers a $9 billion capital construction program: $6.99 billion at IAD (including Concourse E and fifth runway design) and $2.39 billion at DCA. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-completes-negotiations-new-use-and-lease-agreement]

The agreement required approval by airlines representing a majority of flights before taking effect. Given United's approximately 58% market share by enplanements, United's signature was the agreement. United's signature was also structurally necessary — no other signatory combination could have reached majority without United's participation.

The MII mechanism works in two forms relevant to the $22 billion revitalization proposal. Under an affirmative MII, MWAA must receive positive approval from the majority before proceeding with a capital project. Under a negative MII, the project proceeds unless the majority actively disapproves. The typical threshold at large hub airports requires consensus from carriers representing more than 50% of enplaned passengers — a threshold United alone can control at IAD. [Source: http://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/articles/airport-capital-review]

The $22 billion plan's May 2026 disclosure was met publicly with MWAA stating they are "awaiting an official announcement" from the Department of Transportation — language that suggests the plan's financial structure has not yet been agreed with the airlines. At $21.8 billion in new bond debt, the CPE impact is potentially transformative. If that debt service were to push IAD's CPE from its current $12.88 toward the $25–30 range of ORD or LAX, United's interest in holding preferential-use gates at IAD rather than migrating connecting traffic to a cheaper alternative hub would shift materially.

The CVG case is the relevant precedent: at its 2005 peak, Delta controlled 75% of CVG flights and used that monopoly position to accumulate effective veto power over airport spending "even down to the purchase of new airport police equipment." When CVG's cost structure became uncompetitive relative to Delta's other hubs — a function precisely of the capital-heavy infrastructure the hub had built — Delta reduced operations from more than 600 daily flights to fewer than 180 by 2013. The warning signs at CVG were visible 18 months before the operational cuts in the gap between Delta's public rhetoric about CVG's importance and its actual new-route decisions. [Source: https://simpleflying.com/what-happened-delta-air-lines-hub-cincinnati/]

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: SFO Harvey Milk Terminal 1 — Civic-Experience Meets Common-Use Architecture

The $2.5 billion Harvey Milk Terminal 1 at SFO, completed with the final section opening June 17, 2024, represents the most direct comparable to the question embedded in a Saarinen-philosophy reinterpretation at IAD: can civic-experience-focused, common-use terminal architecture coexist with airline commercial preferences for branded space? [Source: https://simpleflying.com/san-francisco-harvey-milk-terminal-1-game-changer/]

The partial answer is: airlines will adapt when they have no structural alternative, and then they will invest in brand adjacency within the civic framework. Alaska Airlines moved from Terminal 2 to Harvey Milk Terminal 1 in June 2024, completing a two-year, $30 million relocation project. Alaska committed to that investment despite (or because of) the common-use framework — noting proximity to oneworld alliance partners as a network advantage. Alaska's investment was in the elements it could brand within the common-use shell: the airline-specific club location, the branded check-in zones, the gate-area presentation.

The constraint at SFO Harvey Milk Terminal 1 was this: Alaska and the other Terminal 1 carriers are not hub carriers at SFO. United dominates SFO from Terminal 3 and the International Terminal. The civic-experience framework was applied to carriers whose commercial model did not depend on the lounge ecosystem in the same way United's does. A similar common-use design applied to United's primary hub concourse would face a fundamentally different commercial pressure. [Source: https://news.alaskaair.com/guest-experience/alaska-airlines-unveils-new-state-of-the-art-location-at-harvey-milk-terminal-1-reinforcing-its-position-as-sfos-second-largest-carrier/]

The SFO case teaches a specific principle: civic-experience terminal design is commercially tolerable when it is applied to non-hub carriers in a multi-carrier terminal. The question of whether it is tolerable when applied to a hub carrier's primary operational infrastructure has not been tested at a US airport of IAD's scale.

### Case Study 2: Atlanta's Plane Train Spine — Hub-Friendly Architecture at Scale

Atlanta Hartsfield-Jackson's architecture is the functional template that United's terminal program at IAD is working toward, not Saarinen's. ATL's design — a central domestic terminal at the head, international at the tail, concourses arranged as ribs off a subterranean spine, Plane Train running the length with departures every two minutes — achieves Delta's minimum connecting time of 35 minutes at the world's busiest airport. [Source: https://thepointsguy.com/airline/hartsfield-jackson-atlanta-international-airport-plane-train/]

The ATL spine works because it solves the problem Saarinen's design never had to solve: moving 35-minute connecting passengers reliably between concourses at a 100-million-passenger-per-year hub. Walking distance from any Plane Train platform to a midpoint gate is 350–400 feet including vertical transition — a number designed to the MCT constraint, not derived from architectural principles.

The $22 billion IAD plan's AeroTrain extension replicates ATL's subterranean spine logic: underground people mover, concourses above, timed to MCT requirements. The plan effectively concedes that Saarinen's surface-level, mobile-lounge-mediated modal architecture cannot scale to the connecting-time requirements of a 2026 hub. The underground spine is not a refinement of Saarinen's concept — it is a replacement of its most distinctive operational feature.

### Case Study 3: The CVG Dehubbing — What Happens When Airport Capital and Airline Economics Diverge

CVG at its 2005 peak had Delta operating more than 600 daily flights and 22.7 million annual passengers. The airport had built infrastructure commensurate with Delta's operations. Delta's contractual position gave it veto power over most major airport capital expenditures.

The dehubbing was not caused by a single event but by a widening gap between CVG's cost structure — driven by the capital intensive infrastructure built to serve the hub — and the route economics of Delta's connecting traffic. When Delta evaluated whether to maintain connecting flows through CVG or route them through Atlanta, the CPE comparison made CVG uncompetitive. By 2013, fewer than 180 daily departures served the airport. [Source: https://simpleflying.com/what-happened-delta-air-lines-hub-cincinnati/]

The IAD/CVG comparison has limits: United's transatlantic joint venture with Lufthansa and Air Canada creates gateway dependencies at IAD that CVG never had — Dulles is a designated international gateway for that joint venture in a way CVG was not for any of Delta's alliances. But the structural lesson holds: when CPE diverges from the airline's network economics, capital program scale becomes a liability, not an asset. The $22 billion plan's financing structure warrants explicit CPE modeling before United's MII position is engaged.

## Strategic Assessment: Would United Fight a Saarinen-Philosophy Reinterpretation?

The honest answer is conditional: United would not fight a reinterpretation that preserved its operational requirements; it would effectively veto a reinterpretation that threatened them.

Saarinen's principles that United would accept in a 2026 reinterpretation:
- Monumental civic arrival experience in the landside terminal — United has no commercial interest in the curb-to-security zone
- Modal clarity in landside vehicle processing — faster curb access reduces misconnect risk
- Architectural legibility and efficient wayfinding — both reduce customer service costs
- Flexible mid-field expansion geometry — if the expansion framework accommodates preferential-use gate additions for United, the flexibility principle serves United's growth agenda

Saarinen's principles that United would resist in a 2026 reinterpretation:
- **The "single arrival experience" that suppresses branded terminal differentiation.** United's lounge infrastructure — 40,000 square feet planned at Concourse E — is a commercial asset, not a passenger amenity. Common-use architecture that prevents United from locating, sizing, and branding that lounge on its own terms eliminates the physical expression of a revenue-generating product tier.
- **Remote-stand operations in the name of modal purity.** Any reinterpretation that resurrects mobile-lounge-mediated boarding — even nominally, as an interpretive gesture to Saarinen's original concept — fails the 35-minute domestic MCT constraint and the 65-minute international-to-domestic MCT constraint. This is not a fight worth having. The MCT numbers are not negotiable.
- **Terminal geometry that degrades gate-bank adjacency.** A "faithful" Saarinen reinterpretation that returned to a compact single-terminal building with remote aircraft stands would require United to abandon banked hub operations at IAD entirely. That is not a design philosophy choice — it is a route-network restructuring event that United's board would not approve.
- **Capital programs that raise CPE without proportional network benefit.** The $22 billion plan's aesthetic choices — the 300-foot Saarinen terminal extension, the preserved civic forecourt — are commercially neutral only if they do not increase the bond debt load beyond what the network economics support. If "faithful to Saarinen" means additional capital expenditure on civic-architectural elements that do not reduce airline operating costs or increase yield, the MII process is the mechanism through which United expresses that judgment.

The Majority-in-Interest clause in the 2025 use agreement is the institutional mechanism through which this negotiation happens. Under an affirmative MII structure, MWAA cannot proceed with capital programs without United's effective consent. The $22 billion plan as disclosed does not document airline reactions — and that absence is the most strategically significant data point in the public record as of May 2026.

## Secondary Carrier Positions at IAD

United's dominance renders secondary carriers commercially secondary, but their positions matter to the MII arithmetic. The airport's non-United signatory carriers — American, Southwest, and the remaining international operators — collectively do not reach the 50% threshold needed to block a program United supports, nor to approve one United opposes.

International carriers at IAD (British Airways, Lufthansa, Air Canada, Emirates, Turkish, others) operate in the A/B concourses and rely on mobile lounges or the AeroTrain for aircraft access. These carriers are direct beneficiaries of the AeroTrain extension under the $22 billion plan, since the extension would reach Concourse A (renamed for international operations in the plan) and eliminate the mobile lounge dependency for their operations as well. Their commercial interest aligns with the plan's infrastructure logic even if they cannot drive the MII vote.

## The Architectural-Commercial Incompatibility Question

The question the report must answer directly: is Saarinen-philosophy terminal design compatible with 2026 airline-commercial requirements?

The answer has three parts.

**What is compatible:** Saarinen's insistence on legible, non-fragmented passenger experience from curb to gate is exactly what every hub carrier wants from the landside and security-to-airside transition. The failure of post-Saarinen expansion at IAD was not that it rejected his principles — it was that it added concourses in a geometry that forced passengers between buildings in ways Saarinen's original compact design never required. A 2026 reinterpretation that achieves airside continuity through the AeroTrain spine, rather than through mobile lounges, is more faithful to Saarinen's operational intent (unbroken passenger journey) than the current arrangement.

**What requires modification:** The "single arrival experience" as a design principle is compatible with hub operations if it is read as applying to the landside zone — curb, check-in, security. In the airside zone, a hub carrier's brand segmentation (Polaris vs. Premium Plus vs. Economy boarding, United Club vs. general concourse) is a commercial requirement, not an aesthetic preference. Saarinen's program brief, written for a world without loyalty programs, premium cabin stratification, or credit-card-linked lounge access, simply did not contemplate this. A 2026 Saarinen-philosophy reinterpretation must acknowledge that the airside experience is commercially differentiated in ways that the 1962 brief was not asked to accommodate.

**What is obsolete:** The remote-stand, mobile-lounge modal architecture is obsolete for hub operations. This is not a close call. The 35-minute MCT at a 280-flight-per-day hub is incompatible with 15–20 minute mobile lounge boarding penalties. The mobile lounges were a brilliant solution to the problem Saarinen was solving — how to handle aircraft that were not yet jet-bridge compatible at scale — and they are an operational liability for the problem that exists today. The $22 billion plan eliminates them. This is the correct decision.
