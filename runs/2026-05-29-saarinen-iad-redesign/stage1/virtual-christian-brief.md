# Virtual Christian Brief — Stage 1
## Saarinen at IAD: The Reframe Nobody Is Bringing

---

### THE REFRAME

Here is the thing that I do not think any of the structured agents will say, and it is the thing that has been bothering me about this whole conversation the moment it landed in my head: **the real cautionary tale at Dulles is not about Saarinen's building being ruined — it is about Saarinen's idea being slowly replaced by the correct idea, and the correct idea turning out to be uglier and worse.** The mobile lounge was stupid. The AeroTrain that replaced it cost $1.5 billion and left Concourse D unserved and deposited passengers 500 feet from the building into an underground moving walkway that dumps them into baggage claim. The Silver Line station is 1,150 feet from the terminal and arrived via an above-grade structure that the Virginia Historic Preservation Office flagged for visual impact — chosen specifically because the underground option was $640 million more. Every operational fix at IAD has been cheaper and more pragmatic than what it replaced and has produced a worse experience. The $22B plan proposes four new linear concourses and wants to turn Dulles into, and I am quoting directly from the reporting, something that "functions like Atlanta's Hartsfield-Jackson." That is the aspiration. Hartsfield. If that does not make you stop and think about what we are doing and who we are doing it for, I do not know what will. What Saarinen was doing at IAD was not just designing a building. He was arguing that airports should feel like something. The structured arguments about his principles being viable or obsolete are mostly right about the specifics and completely wrong about what is actually at stake in 2026, because what is at stake is not whether mobile lounges were efficient — they weren't — but whether the $22B program produces an airport that a person arrives in and feels something, or produces an airport that processes people at the rate Hartsfield does and feels like a mall in Tyson's Corner with gate numbers.

---

### THREE TO FIVE CONNECT-THE-DOTS ARGUMENTS

**1. The Penn Station Arc — and Where IAD Is On It**

Penn Station was not demolished in a fit of ignorance. It was demolished by people who had looked carefully at the economics and concluded that the building was not earning its keep. The railroad needed the revenue from Madison Square Garden. The math was real. The mistake was not the math — it was the failure to understand what the building was doing for the city that could not be captured in the railroad's revenue model. The loss only became fully legible in 1965 when New York created its Landmarks Preservation Commission, in 1968 when Ada Louise Huxtable wrote "we will probably be judged not by the monuments we build but by those we have destroyed," and again in 2021 when SOM opened Moynihan Train Hall — which is a good building, a genuinely moving space, but which exists because a generation later we reached back for what we could still reach for and found we could have the light and the ceiling height but not the original hall, not the full civic gravity, only the memory of it dressed in a new coat.

The IAD main terminal is on that arc. The question is where. My read — and this is the operator's gut talking, not the preservationist's — is that it is somewhere around 1958 Penn Station. The building is beloved in the architectural press, designated eligible for the National Register, revered by architects and not by airlines. The people who make the capital decisions are thinking about gate counts and connecting-bank flexibility and the operational gap between Dulles and the hub airports it is trying to compete with. The $22B plan will extend the main terminal east and west — SOM already doubled it in 1999 — and build four linear concourses and run the AeroTrain everywhere. At that scale the main terminal becomes a gateway to a system that looks nothing like Saarinen's intent, and the original building survives the way the original Baths of Caracalla survive: as a destination for people who know what they are looking at, not as the civic heart of the thing. The tragedy is not that this happens. The tragedy is that by the time it fully registers, the decision will already have been made. That is exactly what happened at Penn Station.

**2. GM Tech Center as the Missing Context for What Saarinen Actually Thought He Was Doing**

Everyone talks about the TWA terminal when they talk about Saarinen and the IAD building. But the right comparator for understanding what Saarinen thought he was doing at Dulles is not TWA at JFK. It is the GM Technical Center in Warren, Michigan, which he began in the late 1940s and completed in 1956. At GM Tech Center, Saarinen's thesis was: modularity, adaptability, and the idea that you design for the institution's *changing needs over time*, not just for its current program. He said the campus should be "based on steel — the metal of the automobile." The whole thing was organized as a modular grid that anticipated future buildings without fixing their forms. The architecture expressed the idea of the place — a center for research and engineering — not just the current requirements of the client.

At Dulles, Saarinen applied the same logic to an airport: the building should express the idea of flight and arrival, and it should be organized to accommodate aircraft that do not yet exist — hence the mobile lounges, which were not the failure they look like in retrospect. The mobile lounges were the modular, adaptable part of the system. The fixed terminal was the monumental, permanent part. He was applying the GM Tech Center logic: permanent civic monument plus flexible operational infrastructure. The problem was not the concept. The problem was that the flexible operational infrastructure turned out to be the wrong flexible operational infrastructure — you need gates at the terminal, not vehicles that carry you to remote aircraft stands — but the underlying idea, that you separate the monumental from the operational, is genuinely Saarinen and genuinely worth pulling forward into the 2026 conversation. The $22B plan does not do this. It proposes to solve the operational problem with more fixed infrastructure — four new linear concourses, extended AeroTrain, terminal extensions — that will be expensive to change when the next set of aircraft or the next set of security requirements make today's assumptions look as wrong as mobile lounges look now.

**3. Moynihan as the Proof That You Cannot Fully Come Back**

Moynihan Train Hall opened January 1, 2021. It is genuinely moving. The skylights are real, the ceiling height is real, the sense of civic arrival is partly restored. The architects — SOM for the hall, the wider team — did serious work. But here is the thing about Moynihan: the actual train platforms are still the same 1960s tunnels under the old station. You come down from the Moynihan light and civic dignity into the same dark, low-ceilinged, confusing Penn Station underbelly that everyone hates. The civic arrival is decorative in a specific sense: it is real as far as it goes and it stops exactly where the irreversible decisions were made. You can restore the roof. You cannot restore the spatial sequence. You cannot restore the original great room on 34th Street where the concourse met the street level and the light came down through the clerestory windows and a person arriving in New York understood that they had arrived somewhere that knew they were coming.

What does this tell the IAD conversation? It tells you that whatever gets built in the next eight years at Dulles is also largely irreversible at the scale of a generation. The four new linear concourses, if they get built, will define the airport's operational geometry for 30 to 40 years. The extended AeroTrain system will be the circulation infrastructure for the building's useful life. You can renovate the main terminal skin in 2055. You cannot undo the decision about where the concourses go and how they connect to the curb. The Moynihan lesson is not "you can always come back." The Moynihan lesson is "you can come back about 40 percent of the way, and the 40 percent you recover will be beautiful and important and will not fix the fundamental thing you lost." MWAA needs to hear that before the shovels go in.

**4. The Yamasaki Comparison — Two Architects, Two Kinds of Civic Failure**

Saarinen and Minoru Yamasaki are the two great mid-century Americans who thought hardest about what civic-monumental architecture should feel like in the age of corporate modernism. Yamasaki's WTC concourse was an enormous underground civic space, a pedestrian network connecting towers, subway stations, and PATH under a plaza designed at human scale to mitigate the towers above. It was also destroyed, obviously, in a different way and by a different force. But Yamasaki's old TWA terminal neighbor at JFK — he didn't build at JFK, wrong airport, he built Lambert St. Louis — and his WTC concourse demonstrate the same instinct Saarinen was working from: that large-scale transportation infrastructure should *perform civically*, should make a person feel they are moving through something built for them rather than for the flow optimization model.

Both architects got criticized for the same thing — Yamasaki at the WTC for designing a "Gothic" plaza that felt alien to Manhattan, Saarinen at Dulles for designing an arrival experience that depended on a vehicle concept that never scaled. Both were, in a specific way, right about the destination and wrong about the path. The current airport design generation has largely abandoned the aspiration and solved the operational problem. KCI's new single terminal is a genuinely good building — consolidated, clear, modal, well-reviewed — and it has none of Saarinen's civic ambition. It is the correct answer to a smaller question. The question it is not answering is: should arriving at Washington's primary international gateway feel like something? Not aesthetically. Operationally and civically.

**5. The Silver Line Arrival — The New Sequence Nobody Talks About**

Here is the thing that actually bothers me most as an operator, and I want to be specific because I have walked this sequence. The Silver Line station at IAD opened November 2022 and it is 1,150 feet from the main terminal. The station is above grade, next to Parking Garage 1, connected to the terminal by an existing pedestrian tunnel to the baggage claim level. The underground option was $640 million cheaper — I have that reversed, the above-grade option was $640 million cheaper, which is why they chose it. The Virginia Historic Preservation Office raised concerns about visual impact on the Saarinen building. So we have a situation where the most consequential change in the IAD arrival sequence in a generation — the introduction of transit access to a building designed for the car — was routed through an existing baggage-claim tunnel because it saved $640 million, and landed in a station that is nearly a quarter-mile from the front door of Saarinen's main terminal.

Saarinen's original sequence was: drive from downtown Washington (35 miles), pull up to the elevated roadway, enter the main terminal at grade, experience the full volume of the building, board a mobile lounge. That sequence was designed to make the arrival at the terminal feel earned — a dedicated drive, a monumental building, a specific vehicle. The 2022 sequence for transit riders is: board Metro in downtown DC, travel 35 miles, emerge above grade next to a parking garage, walk through a baggage-claim tunnel, enter the terminal from below. The experience of the building is never encountered from the front. You arrive via the utility side. This is not a small thing. It is a complete inversion of Saarinen's arrival logic, executed for $640 million in savings, and nobody in the design press has called it what it is: the moment IAD stopped being a building you arrive at and became a building you surface inside of.

### THE OPERATOR'S GUT CHECK

Okay, here is the bar version. If I'm having a beer with a smart colleague in the IAD employee lot, here is what I say:

The building is unambiguously great. Saarinen was right that airports should feel like something, and he was right that a single compact terminal with flexible airside connections was the correct geometry. He was wrong about mobile lounges and he was wrong in a way that has cost this airport a generation of operational disadvantage relative to every hub that built jetway gates when it was still cheap to do so. Those two things are both true simultaneously.

What worries me about the $22B plan is not that it is wrong about the operational fix. It is probably right about the operational fix. Four linear concourses, AeroTrain everywhere, mobile lounges gone — if you are trying to compete with United's hub connecting bank and move 40 million passengers a year, that is approximately correct. What worries me is that the operational fix and the design aspiration are being treated as unrelated questions, and in my experience they are the same question. The buildings that feel like something are the ones where somebody at the capital-decision level said "the experience of this place is a program requirement, not a design preference" and held that line through the value-engineering cycles and the TSA change orders and the airline negotiations and the FAA certification process. The buildings that feel like nothing are the ones where that conversation happened at the design-intent stage and then got traded away for gate flexibility or terminal square footage or AeroTrain station placement.

The Penn Station people did not know they were making the Penn Station mistake. They thought they were making a rational capital decision. That is what the Penn Station mistake looks like from the inside.

### PRODUCTIVE TANGENTS

**Worth following:**

- *Hospital surge capacity and the modular-vs-fixed infrastructure debate.* Saarinen's mobile lounge concept is actually closer to hospital surge planning than people realize — the idea that you separate permanent infrastructure from flexible response capacity. Modern hospital planners argue for "capacity in reserve" rather than fixed beds: you build the shell for surge, you fill it when needed. Saarinen built the terminal as the fixed monument and the mobile lounges as the flexible response to aircraft of various sizes and positions. The concept aged out not because it was wrong in principle but because the airline-commercial model demanded faster turns and gate-contact boarding. Worth thinking about whether any version of that logic survives in the 2026 reinterpretation — particularly given what biometric processing at the gate might do to gate-contact requirements.

- *LA Union Station and what "civic arrival" means in a car-first built environment.* The LA Union Station renovation and the Metro expansion have been trying since 2011 to make that building feel like a civic anchor again. It partly works. The Travertine and the gardens and the volume still carry weight. But it is ringed by freeways and the pedestrian connection to downtown is still broken. The parallel to Dulles is sharp: both buildings were designed for an arrival sequence that assumed a specific mode (train, car) and both have been retrofitted for transit access that arrives in the wrong place and from the wrong direction.

- *The Oculus at the World Trade Center as a cautionary tale about civic-monumental aspiration without operational intelligence.* Calatrava's Oculus cost $4 billion, opened in 2016, and is primarily experienced as a shopping mall with a spectacular ceiling. The civic-arrival language is all there — the soaring ribs, the light, the sense of emergence — and the building is largely used to buy things at a Banana Republic. The lesson is not that civic-monumental airports are doomed to become malls. The lesson is that civic-monumental design without a strong operational program that keeps the civic experience primary will be colonized by retail revenue. Dulles is not immune. The $22B plan's revenue assumptions are not published, but every large terminal program in the last 20 years has been partly financed by concession revenue projections that require heavy retail in the sterile zone. Saarinen's single-compact-terminal concept produces exactly the kind of compressed, high-traffic sterile zone that concession operators want. That is not a Saarinen principle. That is a coincidence that the $22B planners should not mistake for intent.

**Tangents I went down that did not fully pay off, but I am leaving them in:**

- *Saarinen's MIT chapel and Kresge Auditorium* as the other set of civic-religious works from the same period. I wanted to argue that his IAD design philosophy connects to how he thought about the spiritual dimension of public space. It might be there. But the evidence is thin enough that I did not want to load-bear on it. Worth the architectural historian checking.

- *The Dulles Access Road as civic infrastructure.* The whole arrival sequence depended on the Dulles Access Highway, which was built specifically for airport access and for decades prohibited local traffic. There is something interesting about a publicly funded dedicated road as the "arrival experience" design — you are prepared for the terminal by 35 miles of intentional corridor. I lost the thread on how to connect this to the 2026 reinterpretation in a way that was not just romantic. The Silver Line is partly on that corridor now. Maybe the architectural historian can work with it.
