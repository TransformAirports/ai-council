# Chief Engineer Brief: IAD Engineering and Construction Realities
## Stage 1 — Saarinen IAD Redesign Run

---

## Key Findings

1. **The 1962 building is a structural type with no precedent in modern renovation practice.** The catenary cable roof — 1,792 precast concrete panels, each 800 lbs, suspended on 1-inch cables spaced 10 feet apart between 65-foot inward-sloping concrete piers at 40-foot centers — is not a substrate you can renovate around freely. The structural logic is monolithic: the cables are pre-stressed by the panel dead load, the piers resist lateral load through their tapered geometry and deep foundations, and the interior is column-free precisely because the perimeter carries everything. Every interior intervention touches that logic indirectly. The 1996 SOM annexes proved this is replicable at the ends; they did not prove the interior can be freely reorganized.

2. **The 1996 SOM expansion was a structural act of faithfulness, not an architectural one.** SOM added 320 feet to each end — extending the terminal from 600 to 1,240 feet — by replicating the column spacing, cable geometry, precast panel system, concrete finishes, terrazzo floors, and glass patterns of the original. The cables were pre-stressed by crane rather than by sandbag (the 1962 method), but the structural geometry was not modified. What this means for a 2026 reinterpretation: the structural envelope of the terminal is fixed at its established geometry. You cannot introduce intermediate columns, you cannot change the roof line, and you cannot reconfigure the perimeter without breaking the pre-stress equilibrium. The inherited Saarinen envelope both permits and forbids renovation in specific, non-negotiable ways. [Source: https://www.som.com/projects/washington-dulles-international-airport-main-terminal-aerotrain-station/]

3. **The AeroTrain retrofit was a $1.5 billion proof that building under a live Saarinen terminal is feasible and brutally expensive.** Turner Construction excavated 220,000 cubic yards of soil beginning October 2002, working 20 feet below and 40 feet into the existing terminal with elaborate underpinning and shoring. The main terminal station — 50 feet deep, 140 feet wide, 1,300 feet long — required severing the terminal's structure at the foundation plane and rebuilding it from below while the building remained operational above. The project took more than seven years of active construction, opened in January 2010, and delivered 32 TSA screening lanes (nearly double the pre-construction capacity) relocated to the underground level. The engineering lesson: any future program that touches the terminal's subgrade — including AeroTrain extension, utility relocation, or new below-grade connections — starts from a known cost and complexity baseline that the 2026 $22B plan must clear, not beat. [Source: https://airportimprovement.com/article/dulles-debuts-15-billion-train-system/]

4. **The mobile lounge concept was constructible in 1958 because it solved a problem specific to that regulatory and operational moment.** Saarinen's own statement of design intent is direct: "The soundest system seemed to be one which brought the passenger to the plane rather than the plane to the passenger." The mobile lounges — 54 feet long, 16 feet wide, 17.5 feet tall, capacity 102 passengers, 26 mph maximum speed — were built by Chrysler and Budd, using a scissor-lift mechanism to match aircraft door heights from 8 to 16 feet. The concept eliminated jetbridge-driven gate density requirements and permitted a compact terminal. It failed operationally not because the vehicles were poorly engineered, but because the airport grew to a traffic level (approaching 4 million annual passengers by 1984) where the modal throughput of the lounge system could not match aircraft turn requirements. Jet bridges attached directly to concourse buildings — which Saarinen explicitly rejected — handled the same aircraft faster and with less apron congestion. The 2010 AeroTrain replaced lounges for most operations but did not eliminate them entirely; as of the current program, mobile lounges still serve international overflow and remote stands. [Source: https://www.pbs.org/wnet/americanmasters/eero-saarinens-revolutionary-design-dulles-airport/7853/; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_lounge]

5. **The TSA sterile-area boundary is not a design choice — it is a hard architectural constraint that forces decisions Saarinen's program never faced.** The post-9/11 security framework requires physical separation of sterile and non-sterile areas with walls a minimum of 8 feet high extending continuously from the ticket document checker position through the screening equipment into the sterile zone. Checkpoints require defined queuing depth, lane widths accommodating Advanced Imaging Technology equipment, and exit lanes with access control preventing re-entry. The 2004-2010 AeroTrain program relocated TSA screening to the first basement level of the terminal, below grade, using the excavated station volume as the checkpoint floor. This was the engineering workaround that preserved Saarinen's open main hall. Any 2026 reinterpretation faces the same constraint: the sterile boundary must exist somewhere between the terminal entrance and the aircraft boarding position. There is no operational workaround that eliminates it, only architectural choices about where it lands and how it is expressed. [Source: https://airportscouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Checkpoint_Design_Guide_CDG_Rev_4_0.pdf; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dulles_International_Airport_Main_Terminal]

6. **"Faithful reinterpretation of Saarinen's design philosophy" in 2026 is not a renovation question — it is a new-building question.** The existing building is on the National Register of Historic Places (listed 1997, following a 1978 NRHP eligibility determination). Any federal action affecting the terminal triggers Section 106 review under the National Historic Preservation Act, requiring consultation with the Advisory Council on Historic Preservation and the Virginia SHPO. Structural modification of the original catenary system — changing column positions, altering the roof geometry, introducing new vertical elements — is not a regulatory negotiation; it is the kind of undertaking that Section 106 was designed to put before a public record. More fundamentally, the structural logic of the building does not accommodate internal reorganization. A genuinely new Saarinen-philosophy building — one that reinterprets the principles of modal separation, compact terminal, mid-field flexibility, and civic arrival experience — is architecturally and regulatorily a new building, not a modification of the 1962 one. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis; https://www.achp.gov/sites/default/files/2018-09/2014%20FAA%20Section3ProgressReport.pdf.pdf]

7. **The $22B MWAA revitalization program has a 2034 target completion date that assumes parallel execution of NEPA, Section 106, airline agreement, bond defeasance, and construction — a sequencing that has never been accomplished at this scale on an operating airport.** The plan calls for four new linear concourses, main terminal expansion 300 feet in each direction, AeroTrain extension, and demolition of Concourse C/D. Phase 1 (main terminal expansion, new terminal concourse, Concourse A/B renovation) is targeted to begin in April 2027. Independent analysis places the realistic timeline to financial close alone at 42-48 months from legal clarification, with NEPA review potentially adding 12-24 months beyond that. The Denver Great Hall — a far simpler renovation of a single terminal building — ran from 2017 to a projected 2028 completion, three times its original budget, with one contractor termination along the way. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/; https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis; https://simpleflying.com/denver-international-airport-1-billion-great-hall-renovations/]

## Evidence

### The Structural System: What the 1962 Building Actually Is

The Dulles main terminal is a cable-suspension structure. Reinforced concrete piers — 65 feet tall on the north (curb) elevation and 40 feet tall on the south (airside) elevation — are spaced 40 feet apart and slope outward. Between them, 1-inch-diameter suspension cables spaced 10 feet apart carry 1,792 precast concrete panels, each 7.5 inches thick (including a 1.5-inch styrofoam layer), each weighing 800 pounds. The cables are encased in 14-to-16-inch concrete stiffening ribs that prevent cable bounce. The result is a roof that curves upward from both sides toward the center — a "hanging hammock" in Saarinen's phrase — creating 150 feet of column-free interior space over a plan that was originally 600 feet long. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dulles_International_Airport_Main_Terminal]

The structural system has two non-negotiable properties for any renovation program. First, the pre-stress in the cables is maintained by the weight of the roof panels. Removing panels — for skylights, for penetrations, for new MEP routing — changes the load distribution across the cable array and must be analyzed against the original cable pre-stress design. Second, the piers resist lateral loads through their tapered geometry and through deep foundations connected to a compression slab that runs transversely between each pier pair. There is no redundant lateral system — no shear walls, no braced frames. The piers are the lateral system. This is not a building you open up structurally without a full structural engineering reassessment that will take 18-24 months to complete to the level of confidence required for a construction permit. [Source: https://user.eng.umd.edu/~austin/ence353.d/lecture-material2021/Dulles-Airport-Cable-Structure-Eero-Saarinen.pdf]

Each of the tall concrete piers contains 20 short tons of reinforcing steel; the shorter piers contain 16 short tons. This is mid-century reinforced concrete, cast between 1958 and 1962, with 1950s cover standards and whatever chloride exposure has occurred in 63 years of operation in a mixed climate with de-icing chemical use on the apron side. The exterior preservation work documented on the historic air traffic control tower (a separate structure of the same era and construction type) involved concrete cleaning, joint sealant replacement, and membrane roofing replacement — the maintenance signature of a concrete building in its seventh decade. The cables themselves are embedded in concrete encasement, which protects against direct corrosion but also makes inspection of the cables for section loss effectively impossible without destructive investigation. [Source: https://wmschlosser.com/dulles-airport-main-terminal-historic-air-traffic-control-tower-exterior-preservation/]

The 1996 SOM expansion added two 320-foot annexes (east and west) and was dedicated September 6, 1996. SOM replicated the column spacing, cable geometry, and material palette without modification. The annexes include an additional basement level not present in the original structure — an engineering decision that foreshadowed the 2010 AeroTrain excavation program. The expansion brought the terminal to its current 1,240-foot length and approximately 1.1 million square feet. The critical engineering point: the 1996 expansion proved the structural system can be extended longitudinally. It did not prove anything about the interior — the 150-foot clear span remains clear, the perimeter columns remain the only vertical structural elements, and the column grid has not been modified in any direction. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dulles_International_Airport_Main_Terminal; https://sah-archipedia.org/buildings/VA-01-NV33]

### The Mobile Lounge: Engineering Proposition and Operational Failure

Saarinen's mobile lounge concept was an engineering response to the specific conditions of 1958: large jet aircraft (707, DC-8) arriving at a terminal that needed to accommodate them without fixed jetbridges, in an era when the FAA expected Dulles to serve perhaps 10 million annual passengers at build-out. The Flxible (later Chrysler/Budd) vehicles were purpose-built with scissor-lift mechanisms to match the varying door sill heights of different aircraft types — ranging from 8 to 16 feet above apron grade — while the terminal-side docking bays were engineered to receive the vehicle at a fixed height. The terminal's airside bays are not general-purpose ground-floor openings; they are designed specifically for mobile lounge docking. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_lounge; https://www.mightytravels.com/2024/10/the-rise-and-fall-of-mobile-lounges-a-look-back-at-dulles-airports-unique-passenger-transport-system/]

The system's operational failure was a capacity problem, not a mechanical one. As aircraft operations grew and connecting banks tightened, the throughput limit of lounge boarding — one vehicle per gate position, loading time constrained by vehicle capacity of 102 passengers, transit time across the apron, and scissor-lift cycle time at the aircraft — could not match the gate turn requirements of high-frequency narrow-body operations. Jet bridges, attached to fixed concourse buildings, eliminated the vehicle from the gate cycle entirely and handled the same 180-passenger aircraft faster. MWAA considered lounge replacement from at least the early 1990s. The 9/11 traffic decline delayed that decision by nearly a decade. The AeroTrain, opened January 26, 2010, replaced lounges for most operations to Concourses A, B, and C, at a system cost of approximately $1.4-1.5 billion. Mobile lounges remain in service for international overflow and remote gates as of 2026. [Source: https://www.thetransportpolitic.com/2010/01/26/dulles-airport-replaces-distinctive-mobile-lounge-system-with-aerotrain/; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AeroTrain_(IAD)]

### The AeroTrain: Engineering Below a Live Saarinen Terminal

The AeroTrain station under the main terminal is the most detailed available evidence of what it costs and what it takes to do major engineering work inside the Saarinen terminal envelope. Turner Construction began excavation in October 2002. The station occupies four underground levels: departures level, arrivals level, security mezzanine, and train platform. It is 50 feet deep, 140 feet wide, and 1,300 feet long — approximately the full footprint of the original 1962 building. [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrpwebresource2/dulles-international-airport-automated-people-mover-system-design-and-construction-of-the-main-terminal-and-tier-2-stations/; https://www.schnabel-eng.com/projects/dulles-international-airport-automated-people-mover/]

The geotechnical conditions are documented by Schnabel Engineering: mudstone, sandstone, and siltstone at 32-48 MPa unconfined compressive strength. Three distinct construction methods were employed — cut-and-cover where airfield operations allowed, New Austrian Tunneling Method under active taxiways and concourses, and tunnel boring machines for longer runs. Two Single Shield TBMs (6.4-meter diameter) completed four separate drives. The machines navigated 125-meter radius curves approaching the main terminal. The rock characterization, blasting evaluation, dewatering design, and instrumentation program were all pre-design work items — not construction-phase discoveries. [Source: https://www.schnabel-eng.com/projects/dulles-international-airport-automated-people-mover/; https://www.robbinstbm.com/projects/dulles-airport-train-system/]

The critical datum from the HNTB project narrative: "Most airport tunnels have traditionally been built before the airside goes operational." Building under a live airport required three distinct mining methods, staged access, and instrumented monitoring of construction impacts on the terminal's foundation system throughout. Project executive John McManus described the need to "severe the structure in order to build this new terminal beneath the existing building" while preserving integrity above. Total project cost: approximately $1.4-1.5 billion for what is fundamentally an automated people mover system in a relatively short 3.8-mile guideway. The premium for building under a live airport, in this structural context, is embedded in that number — it is not separable from the baseline. [Source: https://airportimprovement.com/article/dulles-debuts-15-billion-train-system/; https://www.hntb.com/projects/washington-dulles-international-airport-tunnel/]

### TSA Sterile-Area Architecture: The Immovable Constraint

The post-2001 security framework requires that a sterile area — physically separated from the non-sterile public zone by continuous barriers — exist between the checkpoint and the aircraft boarding position. TSA Checkpoint Design Guide (CDG) Rev. 4.0 specifies that walls adjacent to the non-sterile side must be at least 8 feet high from the ticket document checker position through to the sterile egress, with no gaps that could allow passage of prohibited items. Exit lanes require access control preventing unauthorized re-entry. Checkpoint lanes require sufficient depth for Advanced Imaging Technology equipment, divestiture tables, and recombination space. [Source: https://airportscouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Checkpoint_Design_Guide_CDG_Rev_4_0.pdf]

Saarinen's 1962 design created an open arrivals hall through which all passengers — arriving, departing, and connecting — flowed in a single undifferentiated space. That spatial concept is incompatible with the sterile-area paradigm as currently implemented. The 2010 AeroTrain program relocated TSA screening underground, preserving Saarinen's main hall above grade as a non-sterile departures and arrivals space. This was an elegant engineering resolution: the basement volume created by the station excavation became the checkpoint floor. Any future program that proposes to "restore" the Saarinen arrivals experience — unified, monumental, undivided — must either locate the checkpoint in a position that does not interrupt that experience (underground or in a separate structure upstream of the terminal), or propose a modification to the sterile-area architecture that requires formal TSA engagement and will not resolve quickly.

The FAA Advisory Circular 150/5360-13A (Airport Terminal Planning) frames the checkpoint siting question: the more common design locates the checkpoint "deep in the terminal" to avoid concentrating crowds in public areas. At IAD, deep placement means underground. Any reinterpretation of Saarinen's open-arrival philosophy that relocates the checkpoint aboveground — back into the Saarinen hall — redistributes the screening crowd into the most monumental civic space in the building. That is not a design improvement; it is a security and crowd-management regression. [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrpwebresource2/faa-advisory-circular-150-5360-13a/]

### The Historic Preservation Constraint

The Dulles main terminal was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1997. The FAA executed a Programmatic Memorandum of Agreement with the Advisory Council on Historic Preservation and the Virginia SHPO in May 1987, establishing a consultation framework for MWAA renovations. Section 106 review under the National Historic Preservation Act (36 C.F.R. Part 800) applies to any federal action affecting the terminal. Because MWAA's capital program relies on federal grants and FAA oversight, virtually all significant structural or exterior modifications to the 1962 building are federal actions. [Source: https://www.achp.gov/sites/default/files/2018-09/2014%20FAA%20Section3ProgressReport.pdf.pdf; https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

The practical planning implication: Section 106 consultation for modifications to a nationally significant mid-century modern building is not a 30-day review. It involves the SHPO, ACHP, preservation organizations (four separate preservation submissions were filed in response to the 2025 DOT RFI), and potentially Congressional stakeholders for a building of this profile. Multiple respondents to the 2025 RFI proposed adaptive reuse of the Saarinen shell (most notably Ferrovial/Grimshaw, who proposed converting the terminal interior to airside concessions while building a new terminal processor). Proposals that require structural modification to the original building face a different consultation burden than those that treat the shell as inviolable.

### The $22B Program: What the Phasing Actually Looks Like

The MWAA $22B revitalization (funded by $21.8B in new bonds and $1.1B in airport fees, targeting 2034 completion) proposes four concurrent or near-concurrent construction phases starting in mid-to-late 2027:

- Phase 1 (April 2027, $6.2B): Main terminal expansion, new terminal concourse, Concourse A/B renovation with above-ground connector
- Phase 2 (January 2028, $2.26B): New Concourse B (33 regional gates for United)
- Phase 3 (June 2027, $3.7B): New Concourse D (non-United domestic)
- Phase 4 (January 2028, $3.75B): Underground AeroTrain extension with subterranean central spine, target completion December 2033
- Concourse E/C (accelerated, $4B full build-out, target December 2030)

[Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/]

Independent analysis of the RFI responses places Ferrovial's Class 5 cost estimate at $14.4 billion (range $11.5-18.7B) and Phoenix/Ironbridge's estimate at $35-50 billion for a more ambitious scope. The variance between $14.4B and $50B for what is nominally the same airport is not a rounding difference — it reflects radically different assumptions about scope, phasing risk, and the cost of building under live operations. The realistic critical path to financial close — from legal clarification through Section 106 consultation, airline agreement, formal RFP, preferred bidder selection, and regulatory review — is 42-48 months, with NEPA potentially adding 12-24 months beyond that. An April 2027 construction start date assumes that clock starts now and runs without slippage. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

The Denver Great Hall is the relevant domestic comparator for program management risk on a live terminal renovation. A project originally framed as a $1.8B long-term concession, with the city on the hook for $770M, reached a final price tag of at least $2.35B, suffered a contract termination in August 2021, and will not wrap until 2028 — seven years after the original projected completion and during a period when the terminal served 69 million annual passengers. The IAD program is an order of magnitude larger, involves multiple simultaneous construction programs on an active international airport, and must maintain airline use agreement compliance throughout. [Source: https://simpleflying.com/denver-international-airport-1-billion-great-hall-renovations/]

### The Substrate Question: Renovation or New Building?

ACRP Report 68 (Guidebook for Evaluating Terminal Renewal Versus Replacement Options) establishes that the renewal-versus-replacement decision turns on lifecycle cost, capacity adequacy, facilities obsolescence, regulatory compliance, operational constraints, and airline needs — assessed jointly, not singly. A facility with "solid bones but functional obsolescence" is a renovation candidate; one with systemic infrastructure decay and strategic repositioning needs is a replacement candidate. The Dulles main terminal's structural bones are not in systemic decay — the 1962 catenary system has performed for 63 years. But the functional obsolescence is real: the building was designed for a modal access concept (mobile lounges) that no longer functions as designed, the baggage system has been rebuilt in the basement, TSA screening has been relocated underground, and the original 600-foot footprint has been extended to 1,240 feet by annexes. [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrpwebresource4/acrp-report-68-guidebook-for-evaluating-terminal-renewal-versus-replacement-options/]

The honest engineering answer to "is the existing building the right substrate for a faithful reinterpretation of Saarinen's design philosophy" is: it depends entirely on what "faithful reinterpretation" means. If it means preserving the catenary roof, the 40-foot column grid, and the open main hall while reorganizing what happens below and around that envelope, then the existing building is the substrate. If it means implementing Saarinen's *principles* — modal separation, compact terminal, mid-field flexibility, civic arrival experience — in a form that actually works under 2026 TSA, ADA, and airline-commercial requirements, then the existing building is a constraint, not a substrate. A building designed around mobile lounges cannot be operated around jetbridges without demolishing and rebuilding its airside face. A terminal designed for an undifferentiated passenger flow cannot implement post-2001 sterile-area architecture without underground screening or a checkpoint building that reads architecturally as a separate structure. These are not renovation questions; they are new building questions in principle, even if they are answered in the context of the existing envelope.

## Engineering Judgments Stated Explicitly (Practitioner Experience, Not Sourced to Public Documents)

The following reflect practitioner assessment of engineering conditions where no published source provides an equivalent statement:

**On the cable condition:** The 1-inch suspension cables at IAD are encased in concrete stiffening ribs. Concrete encasement protects against direct atmospheric corrosion but also makes non-destructive inspection of the cable cross-section practically impossible. At 63 years of age, with deicing chemical exposure on the apron side of the southern elevation, the cable condition is unknown without a destructive investigation program. Any renovation permit application that involves changes to roof loading — skylight installations, MEP penetrations, rooftop equipment — will require a structural engineer of record to certify the cable system's remaining capacity. That certification requires inspection data that does not currently exist in any public document. Budgeting 18-24 months for structural assessment before design development begins is not conservative; it is the minimum.

**On the column grid as renovation constraint:** The 40-foot column spacing at the perimeter defines the bay module for the entire building. Every interior program — check-in islands, security dividers, retail, signage structures, mechanical distribution — must work within or around a grid that cannot be modified without structural consequence. For a 2026 reinterpretation of Saarinen's design philosophy, this is simultaneously an asset (the column-free interior is the civic gesture the philosophy depends on) and a constraint (everything in the interior has to be freestanding or hung from the roof structure, because there is no other structure to attach to). The architectural implications of this fact are usually understated in design discussions of the terminal.

**On the phasing multiplier:** Construction at a live Class B airport (FAA Part 139, Index E based on serving widebody international aircraft) requires FAA-approved construction safety plans under AC 150/5370-2 for every stage that affects movement areas. Every utility outage that crosses an airline service circuit requires advance coordination with the airline use agreement counterparties. Every temporary structure within the OFA of a runway or taxiway requires an obstruction evaluation. At IAD, with United Airlines holding approximately 70% of domestic passenger market share, the airline use agreement counterparty relationship is not diffuse — United has both the incentive and the legal standing to slow down any capital program that degrades its operating environment. Phasing assumptions that treat airline coordination as a parallel track rather than a serial gating item are wrong, and every overrun in the history of live-airport construction has some version of that assumption embedded in it.
