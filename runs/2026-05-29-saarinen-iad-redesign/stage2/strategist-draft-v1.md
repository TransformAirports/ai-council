# The Stopwatch and the Hammock: Saarinen's Dulles, Read Honestly for 2026

*A position paper on what a faithful reinterpretation of Saarinen's design philosophy would, and would not, ask of MWAA's $22 billion revitalization program.*

---

## I. Chantilly, 1959

In the year before he drew the curve that would become the most photographed roof in American aviation, Eero Saarinen carried a stopwatch. So did his staff. They flew to other airports and timed themselves walking from curb to gate. They timed the boarding sequence. They timed how long it took for a passenger to find a restroom, a phone, a friend. The Civil Aeronautics Administration, which had selected Saarinen's office in May 1958 alongside Ammann & Whitney, had calculated that a conventional terminal sized to handle the projected build-out at Chantilly would require concourses extending more than 8,000 feet — a mile and a half of finger pier, end to end, just to bring passengers within walking distance of a jet. The team set out to disprove the premise. Over more than a year, they measured. Saarinen's own statement on the project, published the year he died: "Sometimes the problem and the times are right for an entirely new functional approach to a problem. No one asked us to grapple with the problem of the jet age terminal beyond the question of pure architecture, but I believe the architect has to assume that kind of responsibility."

The catenary roof — 1,792 precast concrete panels, each weighing 800 pounds, suspended on inch-thick cables strung between 65-foot inward-sloping piers — was not the argument. It was the conclusion. The argument was the stopwatch.

That distinction is the one the present conversation about the $22 billion revitalization of Dulles has lost. The thesis of this paper is narrow and specific. **Saarinen's design philosophy at Dulles was a problem-solver's philosophy, not a sculptor's, and three of its four load-bearing principles are still operationally correct in 2026. The fourth — that a single compact terminal can be the organizing element of the passenger journey — is obsolete, and any honest reinterpretation must say so by name. The $22 billion plan now in front of MWAA is, in its operational core, more faithful to the rescuable Saarinen than the aesthetic preservationists recognize; in its architectural execution, it is poised to repeat the precise pattern of every IAD expansion since 1996 — preserve the form, abandon the intent.**

What follows is an attempt to give MWAA's program staff five or six specific moves they can hold against the master plan, and an honest account of which constraints are worth fighting and which are not. The piece does not begin with renderings and does not end with them. It begins with a man and a stopwatch, and it ends with a question about what kind of building a person should arrive in when they land at the international gateway of the American capital.

---

## II. Executive Summary

1. **Saarinen's IAD design encoded four principles, not one.** Three remain operationally correct: modal clarity (a single, legible passenger sequence from curb to aircraft); midfield apron flexibility (the airside organized so aircraft can park wherever operations require, rather than where fixed jetbridges happen to be); and grade-separated passenger movement (passengers do not walk on ramps, breathe jet fumes, or share space with vehicles). The fourth principle — that the entire passenger sequence can be organized around a single compact terminal building — is obsolete at the volume IAD now serves, and must be released. (Architectural Historian; Operations Analyst)

2. **The mobile lounge failed as a vehicle, not as a philosophy, and the distinction is the entire argument.** Saarinen's own writing makes it explicit: "The acceptance of the mobile lounge concept allowed us to make the terminal a single compact building." Three independent design principles rode the lounge — compact terminal, flexible apron, grade-separated boarding. When critics describe the lounge as a "failure," they conflate execution with intent. The lounge collapsed under hub-bank arithmetic by 1985, when IAD crossed 5 million annual passengers. The principles it expressed did not. (Architectural Historian; Operations Analyst; Contrarian)

3. **The AeroTrain is ironically more faithful to Saarinen's modal-clarity principle than the current mobile-lounge-plus-jetbridge-plus-tunnel arrangement.** A $1.4 billion underground people mover, opened January 2010, replaced lounges for Concourses A, B, and C. It did not close the loop to D. It did not eliminate the 500-foot underground walk from the AeroTrain's C-gate station to the actual concourse it serves — a station built for a future concourse that never got funded. The architecture of the current operational system is sedimentary, not designed: five distinct passenger-to-aircraft modes operating simultaneously at IAD as of Concourse E's September 2026 opening. (Airport COO; Aviation Historian)

4. **The November 10, 2025 mobile lounge collision at Concourse D ended the heritage argument.** A Plane-Mate struck the dock at 4:30 PM and injured 18 passengers, one of whom lost consciousness. A second collision followed later that month. The fleet's original manufacturers no longer supply parts; the airport is mid-way through a $160 million refurbishment of vehicles whose architectural significance now sits in direct tension with their operational safety. The 2026 question is not whether to retire mobile lounges. It is what the principle they served looks like, executed in 2026 idiom. (Airport COO; Contrarian)

5. **The $22 billion plan preserves Saarinen's shell while completing the operational logic he was solving for — and it owes MWAA's board a direct engagement with the PIT comparison.** Pittsburgh International built a $1 billion hub terminal in 1992 designed around US Airways' 80% market share. US Airways dehubbed in 2004; enplanements fell 57% by 2013; Concourse E became a parking lot. United holds 70% of landed weight at IAD. The $22 billion program designs four linear concourses specifically for United's connecting banks. The structural risk is identical. The case that IAD will not repeat PIT is a case the master plan owes the reader and has not yet made. (Aviation Historian)

6. **The financial constraint is harder than the architectural one.** MWAA's debt service coverage ratio sat at 3.16x in 2024; Moody's projects it compressing toward 1.3x against a 1.25x covenant floor through the peak debt-service years. Independent analysis projects cost per enplanement reaching $90.64 by 2035 under the $22 billion plan, against a current $12.88 and a level — $26.47 — at which United walked away from IAD as a hub in the early 2000s. No design philosophy survives a CPE that makes the airport uncompetitive for the carrier whose 70% landed-weight share gives it functional veto authority. (Airport CEO; Regulatory-Political Analyst)

7. **The TSA sterile-area wall, not technology, is the binding architectural constraint biometrics will not dissolve in the time horizon that matters.** TSA's Checkpoint Requirements and Planning Guide (May 2025 revision) still requires walls adjacent to non-sterile areas to be a minimum of 8 feet high. Biometric identity verification has been deployed at 65 US airports through TSA PreCheck Touchless ID; CLEAR eGates run at 37; CBP biometric arrival and exit operate at every US international airport. None of these has changed the physical footprint of any US checkpoint. IATA's domestic-international integration thesis exists as a modeling exercise with anonymized case studies and no operational deployment anywhere. (Technology Scout)

8. **The Section 106 Programmatic Agreement is the live lever.** The National Register-listed Saarinen main terminal is governed by a 1987 Programmatic Memorandum of Agreement; the Virginia State Historic Preservation Officer has determined that a project-specific Programmatic Agreement is appropriate for the current redevelopment. That PA can embed design-quality obligations — spatial, experiential, and procedural — in a legally binding document, if MWAA chooses to negotiate for it. The 300-foot interior organization of the planned terminal extensions is the most accessible entry point in the live program. The window closes when MWAA's board commits a bond issuance to a fixed scope. (Regulatory-Political Analyst; Airport CEO)

## III. The Argument

### The stopwatch is the philosophy

Begin with the thing that most often gets missed. Saarinen did not approach airport design from architecture. He approached it from measurement. The Saarinen office spent more than a year before the first drawing studying passenger flow at airports across the country. Staffers timed every step of their own travel. The team documented that passengers at comparable airports walked an average of 900 feet from terminal to aircraft. The mobile lounge was an engineered substitution of vehicle-time for that walk. It was the conclusion of empirical research, not a formal experiment imposed on a reluctant client. The catenary roof followed the operational diagram. The diagram did not follow the roof.

This matters because the entire public account of Saarinen — the Saarinen of the TWA terminal at JFK, the Saarinen who told a reporter in April 1961 that if construction stopped at TWA the result would make "a beautiful ruin, like the Baths of Caracalla" — is an account of an architect who optimized for emotional experience and trusted that operations would catch up. That Saarinen built TWA. That building closed entirely in 2001, thirty-nine years after it opened, having been operationally disqualified almost from the start: the design was completed before the 707 entered service, the terminal was too small by the time it was finished, and it limped through four decades of incompatibility with the aircraft it was supposed to serve.

The Saarinen who built Dulles was a different architect on the same payroll. He built the same year. He worked from the same office. But Dulles was the building where he submitted his sketches to the empirical test before he submitted them to the client. He produced two terminals simultaneously, embodying opposite theories of what an airport is for, and one of them has operated continuously for sixty-four years while the other became a hotel. The hagiographic literature has not done the work of separating them. The reader of this paper must do that work before any of what follows can land.

What the stopwatch produced, in the form that has the best claim on 2026, was a building that took three operational principles seriously and built physical infrastructure to express them. First, the terminal would be compact — small enough that a passenger would not lose orientation inside it. Saarinen wrote: "The acceptance of the mobile lounge concept allowed us to make the terminal a single compact building." Note the dependency. The compact terminal was not the principle. It was the consequence of choosing to bring the vehicle to the passenger rather than route the passenger through a finger pier. Second, the apron would be flexible — aircraft could park anywhere within lounge reach, accommodating any size aircraft at any position, without the gate geometry constraining the operations. Third, the passenger would never walk on the ramp. No fumes, no weather, no aircraft taxi paths to cross.

Each of these principles was independently defensible. Each was an engineering response to a documented problem. None of them required the specific vehicle Saarinen and the FAA settled on. When subsequent generations of operators concluded that the vehicle was wrong, they did not — they could not — repeal the principles. They replaced the vehicle and inherited the principles whether they recognized them or not. The AeroTrain is the cleanest evidence of this. An underground automated people mover that brings the vehicle to the passenger, separates passengers from the ramp, and frees the apron from fixed gate geometry is, principle-for-principle, a Saarinen design. It is a better Saarinen design than the lounge ever was, because it scales to hub volumes the lounge could not.

### The vehicle was wrong by 1985 and nobody could fix it

The numbers are pitiless. Saarinen's original 1962 fleet of nineteen Chrysler/Budd vehicles, each carrying 102 passengers, could sustain throughput for a point-to-point international gateway handling 2 to 3 million passengers per year — Dulles's actual traffic through 1983. The failure threshold was not gradual; it was a cliff. When New York Air launched hub operations at Dulles in 1985 and traffic crossed 5 million, the connecting bank structure required simultaneous multi-gate arrivals and departures that the lounge fleet could not serve without prohibitive queuing. By 1987, at 10 million passengers, midfield Concourses C and D had opened with conventional jetbridges and were absorbing the connecting bank traffic. The Saarinen operating diagram had been abandoned within twenty-five years of opening.

The cliff was structural, not mechanical. Each lounge served one departure or one arrival at a time, then returned to base for the next load. In a point-to-point operation with scheduled departure windows spaced across the day, this worked. In a hub bank with twenty or thirty simultaneous arrivals or departures, the fleet math collapsed. The introduction of the wide-body in 1971 — and the larger 150-passenger Plane Mate built specifically to handle the 747 — bought another decade. Then it ran out. The "temporary" Concourse C/D, opened in 1983 as a stopgap, is still hosting United's hub in 2026. It is forty-three years into its temporary life, with HVAC replaced, the sanitary system reconfigured after repeated flooding, and ceilings low enough to make a Monday-morning push feel, as one operator put it, like a submarine.

The deeper point: the lounge was a brilliant solution to the problem Saarinen had set himself, which was how to handle the aircraft mix and passenger volumes of 1962 without an 8,000-foot finger pier. It was an operational liability for the problem that existed by the time he died — connecting hub traffic at deregulated volumes. He was not asked to solve that problem, and his program brief did not contemplate it.

The Aviation and Transportation Security Act of November 2001 added the second incompatibility. The post-9/11 sterile area, defined under 49 CFR § 1540.5 as "a portion of an airport defined in the airport security program that provides passengers access to boarding aircraft and to which the access generally is controlled by TSA," is a regulatory construct that did not exist when Saarinen drew his first sketch. TSA's Checkpoint Design Guide requires walls adjacent to non-sterile areas to be a minimum of eight feet high, with continuous perimeter enclosure from the document checker through the screening lanes. Saarinen's terminal was, definitionally, undifferentiated public space. Departing, arriving, and connecting passengers shared a single floor plate. The post-9/11 paradigm broke that geometry. MWAA's engineering response — a 121,700-square-foot security mezzanine inserted between the main hall and the AeroTrain platform in 2009, excavated into the basement to preserve the catenary roof above it — was elegant under constraint. It was also the moment the curb-to-aircraft sequence Saarinen had organized the entire building around stopped being a coherent spatial experience.

The mobile lounge failed by arithmetic. The single floor plate failed by statute. The compact-terminal principle, which depended on both, has to be released.

### Where the philosophy still holds

What survives is more interesting than what does not.

The first surviving principle is the one most violated by every IAD expansion since 1996: modal clarity. From the operator's chair, this is a question with a precise, measurable answer. As of Concourse E's opening this September, MWAA will be running five distinct passenger-to-aircraft connection types simultaneously. Jet bridge from the main terminal Z gates. AeroTrain to Concourses A and B. AeroTrain to Concourse C via the 500-foot underground moving walkway from the C-gate platform to the concourse building it is supposed to serve. AeroTrain to new Concourse E. Mobile lounge to Concourse D and to the International Arrivals Building. Five systems. Five passenger experiences. Each accumulated as the airport responded to immediate pressure, none designed against any organizing logic. Saarinen would have recognized this as the exact condition his terminal was built to prevent.

The second surviving principle is midfield flexibility — but read carefully. Saarinen's flexibility lived in the apron: aircraft could park anywhere because the lounge would find them. Once jetbridges replaced lounges, that specific kind of flexibility evaporated. But the underlying principle — that the building should accommodate the operational form the airline industry will demand in 2055, not just the form it demands in 2026 — has never stopped being correct. The 1996 SOM expansion proved that the catenary structure can be extended longitudinally. The 2010 AeroTrain proved that subgrade engineering can be done under a live Saarinen terminal, at known cost. The 1958 program brief explicitly anticipated extensions of 320 feet at each end of the terminal — extensions that were cut in original construction and finally built by SOM thirty-four years later, to Saarinen's specified length, with replicated structural geometry and column spacing.

The third surviving principle is the civic-gateway argument. Saarinen wrote in 1962: "Federal architecture is static, but a jet-age airport should be essentially non-static, expressing the movement and the excitement of travel." The terminal was conceived as the international gateway to the American capital, not as a commercial concession platform. This sounds romantic. It is not. Civic-gateway architecture, properly defined, is a program-of-requirements decision: the building's spatial sequence is treated as load-bearing, not as a value-engineering casualty. The Saarinen building still reads as a place. Most of what gets built in airport concourses today does not. The Kansas City new single terminal, opened February 2023 by SOM, demonstrates that the Saarinen diagram — compact, vertically organized, legible — can be executed in 2026 idiom without any of his specific 1962 implementation choices. KCI is the existence proof. Foster's Hong Kong International, opened 1998 and now extended through Terminal 2 to 100 million annual passengers, demonstrates that the principle scales to genuine hub volumes if the building is designed as a concourse from the start, rather than designed as a terminal and then connected to concourses.

The fourth principle — the compact single building as the organizing element — is the one to release. IAD handled 29 million passengers in 2025. The master plan targets 45 million by 2045. No physically achievable single terminal can serve 154 gates. The principle worth preserving is not the building. It is the legibility and clarity of the passenger sequence.

### The $22 billion plan, read against Saarinen rather than around him

What the May 2026 MWAA plan does, stripped of the politics that surround its disclosure: it eliminates mobile lounges by extending the AeroTrain to every concourse; it builds four new linear concourses with jet-bridged gates; it demolishes the temporary Concourse C/D; it preserves the Saarinen main terminal and extends it 300 feet east and 300 feet west, replicating the SOM-replicated catenary; it commits $21.8 billion in new bonds and $1.1 billion in airport fees against a 2034 target completion.

Read against Saarinen's actual operational principles rather than his aesthetic, the plan is more faithful than aesthetic-Saarinen partisans recognize. The AeroTrain extension finally resolves the modal fragmentation he was solving for sixty-four years ago. The elimination of the surface-vehicle dependency closes the safety gap exposed in November 2025. The linear concourses with contact gates serve hub-bank operations that Saarinen's 1962 brief did not contemplate but that are not optional in 2026 — the 35-minute domestic minimum connection time at a 280-flight-per-day hub is not negotiable.

Where the plan violates the philosophy — and this is where MWAA's program staff can usefully hold it accountable — is in the interior organization of the 300-foot terminal extensions, the design of the AeroTrain station dwell spaces, the spatial expression of the sterile-area boundary, the relationship between the preserved main terminal and the new concourses, and the question of whether the building still does any civic work beyond serving as a screening funnel for departing passengers and a baggage claim hall for arriving ones.

The 1996 SOM expansion is the canonical precedent for what goes wrong here. SOM replicated the catenary structure, the column spacing, the concrete finishes, the terrazzo floors, the precast panel system. It extended the terminal from 600 to the originally specified 1,240 feet. By every formal and material measure, the work was faithful. But during the same period, MWAA built Concourses A, B, C, and D in the midfield, with fixed jetbridges, abandoning the modal-clarity diagram entirely. The geometry was preserved. The operating logic was abandoned. The building looked like Saarinen. The airport began to operate like Atlanta.

The risk in the $22 billion plan is the same pattern at fifteen times the cost. Preserve the Saarinen shell, extend it 300 feet on each side in homage, run an underground spine to four new linear concourses, and accept that the operational airport of 2034 will be — to borrow the language from the May 2026 reporting — something that "functions like Atlanta's Hartsfield-Jackson." That is the stated aspiration. Hartsfield. A reader who has any feeling for what Saarinen was arguing about at Chantilly in 1959 should stop and consider what it means that a 2026 master plan for the international gateway of the American capital aspires to operate like Hartsfield, and what kind of building a person will arrive in when the plan completes.

### The Silver Line moment

There is a specific moment, datable to November 2022, when IAD stopped being a building you arrive at and became a building you surface inside of. The Silver Line station opened that month, 1,150 feet from the front door of the main terminal, above grade, next to Parking Garage 1, connected to the terminal by an existing pedestrian tunnel that surfaces in the baggage claim level. The underground option that would have brought the station directly into the Saarinen sequence was $640 million more expensive. The Virginia State Historic Preservation Office raised concerns about the visual impact of the above-grade alignment on the Saarinen building. MWAA chose the cheaper option.

The Saarinen-designed arrival sequence was: drive from downtown Washington, exit at the elevated curb, enter the main terminal at grade, experience the full volume of the building, board the mobile lounge. The sequence was designed to make the arrival at the terminal feel earned — a dedicated thirty-five-mile drive, a monumental building, a specific connection vehicle. The 2022 transit sequence is: board Metro downtown, travel thirty-five miles, surface above grade next to a parking garage, walk through a baggage-claim tunnel, enter the terminal from below. The experience of the building is never encountered from the front. A passenger arrives via the utility side. This is not a small thing. It is a complete inversion of Saarinen's arrival logic, executed for $640 million in savings, and the design press has largely not registered it as what it is.

The point is not that the Silver Line should have been routed differently. It is that the most consequential change in the IAD arrival sequence in a generation happened without anyone in the capital decision having had the conversation about what the arrival sequence is for. The conversation about modal clarity, civic threshold, and the experience of arrival at a federal gateway happened — to the extent it happened at all — as a Section 106 process about visual impact on the historic terminal, not as a program-of-requirements decision about what the building is doing for the city. The $22 billion plan is poised to repeat that conversation at a scale where the consequences will define IAD for the next forty years.

### The PIT warning

The structural risk that any honest reading of the $22 billion plan owes the reader is the Pittsburgh comparison. The numbers are uncomfortable. Pittsburgh International opened its $1 billion hub terminal in 1992, designed at US Airways' specifications for a connecting hub. US Airways carried approximately 80% of PIT's traffic at the time. United carries 70% of IAD's landed weight and 58% of enplanements today. US Airways began dehubbing in 2004. Enplanements at PIT fell from 9.15 million in 2002 to 3.97 million in 2013 — a 57% decline. The airport boarded up 12 of 25 gates in Concourse B; Concourse E, which had 22 gates, became surface parking. PIT ran a $40 million annual deficit within two years of the hub collapse.

The same pattern played out at CVG. Delta peaked there in 2005 at more than 600 daily flights to nearly 150 destinations and 22.7 million annual passengers. By 2013, CVG had fewer than 180 daily departures, fewer than 6 million passengers, and a credit rating that had dropped from A1 to Baa1.

In both cases the structural logic was identical. The airport built to hub scale for one anchor carrier. The anchor carrier's network strategy changed — merger, bankruptcy, competitive repositioning. The infrastructure built for one airline's banked operations became a permanent liability with no plausible alternative user base. IAD's $22 billion plan designs four linear concourses specifically around United's connecting-bank requirements. It makes IAD more United-dependent, not less. The PIT comparison is not a counsel against the plan. It is the question the plan owes the reader: what is the explicit case that IAD will not become the next PIT?

The honest answer involves Washington's larger and more diversified traffic base, the transatlantic joint venture with Lufthansa and Air Canada (a gateway dependency CVG never had), the slot constraints at DCA that direct medium- and long-haul flying to IAD, and the international gateway role that no plausible alternative US hub can take from Dulles within a single capital cycle. These are real arguments. But they need to be made on the record, with the PIT numbers in the room. The Saarinen-philosophy frame is not nostalgia here. It is the discipline of asking what the building is for at the level of operations, not at the level of imagery.

## IV. The Counter-Case, Honestly Presented

The strongest case against everything that has just been argued is straightforward and serious, and it deserves to be made at full strength before being answered.

The case begins with a refusal of the entire framing. The thesis claims to separate "design philosophy" from "aesthetic." But Saarinen's design philosophy *was* his aesthetic. The catenary roof at Dulles is not decoration. It is structural, thermal, and psychological simultaneously. The argument is the curve. To say one can take the philosophy without the look is to say one can take Miles Davis's musical thinking without the silences. The silences are the music. "Design philosophy separable from aesthetic" is the definitional move of sophisticated nostalgia — it launders a preference for the 1962 building through the language of principle, and it permits the writer to continue admiring the building while disclaiming the admiration.

The case continues with the operational record. The mobile lounge was the structural expression of Saarinen's modal-clarity principle. Remove the vehicle and you have removed the mechanism of the philosophy, not just its packaging. Saarinen had been warned. Contemporary congressional hearings in 1962 challenged the $232,000 per-vehicle cost (roughly $2.36 million in 2025 dollars). Representative Boland called the lounges "one of the ugliest" elements ever constructed. Architectural reviews called them "lumbering beasts." The vehicles topped out at 26 mph, carried 90 to 102 passengers depending on type, and required multiple units to load a single jumbo jet. The system broke at 5 million passengers. By 1987 it had been operationally relegated. The AeroTrain replaced it for most operations at a cost of $1.4 to $1.5 billion. The remaining fleet has been involved in at least sixteen collisions or mishaps since 2007, including the November 2025 incident that injured eighteen passengers. The $160 million refurbishment program keeps alive a system that has been in remediation since 1985. This is not a design philosophy that has aged well. It is a design concept in a sixty-three-year holding pattern.

The case continues with the deregulation gap. Saarinen designed for a CAB-regulated, point-to-point world. The Airline Deregulation Act of 1978 restructured the entire industry around hub-and-spoke networks, sixteen years after the airport opened. The thesis asks what Saarinen's principles mean in 2026 as if principles exist independent of the operational model they were designed to serve. Modal clarity made sense when a passenger arrived, was received, boarded a lounge, and flew. It does not translate to an airport where roughly 40% of United's traffic is connecting and must transit between concourses, banks, modes, and carriers within forty-five-minute windows. Applying a 1962 design philosophy to a 2026 connecting hub is like applying a dinner-party design philosophy to a cafeteria feeding ten thousand people. The scale and operational model changed the building type entirely.

The case continues with the redundancy of the Saarinen frame. Foster's Hong Kong delivered monumental civic scale, wayfinding clarity, modular expansion, and a legible passenger sequence without mobile lounges, without mid-century romanticism, and without requiring a design-philosophy argument rooted in a specific architect. Kansas City's 2023 single terminal resolved the modal-clarity problem on time and under budget, with no reference to Saarinen. Munich's modular Terminal 1 expansion has demonstrated needs-based growth for two decades. The principles the thesis attributes to Saarinen exist independently of him. Attaching them to a specific architect is biographical, not philosophical. The most parsimonious explanation for why these airports do not invoke Saarinen is that they do not need to.

The case continues with Saarinen's own self-description, which is the thesis's most uncomfortable enemy. Saarinen explicitly refused to develop a portable design philosophy. He insisted that every problem required a unique solution, that no style or method should be transported from project to project. "Sometimes the problem and the times are right for an entirely new functional approach," he wrote, and then he went and spent a year with a stopwatch. If one takes Saarinen at his word, the *Saarinenian* response to IAD in 2026 is not to reinterpret his 1962 design principles. It is to spend a year with a stopwatch at the current IAD, study every passenger touchpoint as it actually exists under TSA, CBP, United's bank choreography, the AeroTrain platforms, the IAB, the curb chaos at peak, and design something utterly new that responds to the problem as it exists now. The thesis's argument for Saarinen philosophy may be the most anti-Saarinenian thing one could do. The 2005 Journal of Architecture paper "Heroicisation, compensation and coronation or the risks of reevaluating Eero Saarinen" makes the academic version of this point. The design community has been wary of exactly this kind of revival for two decades.

The case continues with the regulatory floor. The post-9/11 sterile-area paradigm under 49 CFR Part 1542 is not a design parameter to be worked around. It is a security-critical statutory framework enacted in response to actual failures. ADA requirements under 49 CFR Parts 37 and 38 mandate accessible routes that the original mobile lounge concept could not satisfy without redesign. CBP's Airport Technical Design Standards impose corridor systems for federal inspection services that Saarinen's program brief never addressed. Treating these constraints as obstacles to design ambition that should be fought is not philosophy. It is wishful thinking dressed up in the language of intent.

The case ends with the political economy. MWAA has been in oversight recovery mode for over a decade following the 2012 DOT OIG audit, which found "questionable procurement practices, mismanagement, and a lack of overall accountability." Permanent statutory DOT IG oversight was established in 2014 legislation. The board is bistate-compact-plus-federal, exposed to political cross-currents from three jurisdictions. MWAA's coverage ratio is compressing toward 1.3x against a 1.25x covenant as the airport executes $5.5 billion in new bond issuances. The projected CPE of $90.64 by 2035 under the $22 billion plan would put IAD well above the level at which United left as a hub the first time. Any capital initiative that can be characterized as architect-driven rather than operationally justified arrives into a governance environment specifically reformed to be suspicious of discretionary executive spending. The CEO who walks into the next ratings briefing with a Saarinen-philosophy argument as a binding capital constraint is the CEO who generates questions they cannot answer in a ratings call.

That is the strongest version of the counter-case. It is not a strawman. It is what a competent skeptic would say, and a reader who has not internalized it should not be persuaded by anything that follows.

## V. Why the Counter-Case Is Insufficient

What must be conceded, by name:

Saarinen's design philosophy is not freely separable from his aesthetic. The catenary roof is structural. The compact terminal is a consequence of the lounge. The argument is the curve. To pretend otherwise is to misread the building. Conceded.

The mobile lounge as a specific vehicle is operationally obsolete and was warned to be obsolete from opening day. It cannot be revived. Any 2026 reinterpretation that proposed to revive it would fail the 35-minute minimum connection time, the ADA boarding tolerances, the FOD requirements on active taxiways, the safety record of the existing fleet, and the basic patience of any operator who has had to coordinate a hub bank around vehicle availability. Conceded.

The single compact building as the organizing element of the passenger journey is obsolete at IAD's current and projected traffic levels. Conceded, by name, at full strength. The principle worth preserving is the legibility of the passenger sequence, not the literal compactness of the building.

The Saarinen frame is not the only entry point to the principles the thesis cares about. Foster, KCI, Kansai, and Munich all do related work without invoking Saarinen. The principles exist independently. Conceded.

Saarinen's own self-description is the thesis's enemy. He refused portable philosophies. The most Saarinenian response to IAD 2026 is to spend a year with a stopwatch, not to revive 1962 principles. Conceded — and the concession is load-bearing, because it points at what the thesis is actually arguing.

The thesis is not that MWAA should hire Cesar Pelli's ghost. The thesis is that the *method* Saarinen used at Dulles — measurement before form, operations before aesthetics, the empirical test of each design move against actual passenger experience — is the method that has been progressively lost in the airport-design conversation, including the conversation around the $22 billion plan, and that recovering the method is more important than preserving any specific 1962 form. The stopwatch is the philosophy. The mobile lounge was the conclusion of the stopwatch's work in 1959. A different conclusion is appropriate in 2026, and the test of whether the conclusion is faithful is whether it was reached by the same kind of empirical patience.

This is why the counter-case is insufficient, not wrong. It is correct that Saarinen's specific implementations have aged out, that the design philosophy cannot be cleanly extracted from his aesthetic, that competent contemporary airports achieve modal clarity and civic scale without invoking him. What it does not address is what is happening, right now, inside the program of requirements for a $22 billion capital decision that will define the international gateway of the American capital for the next forty years.

What is happening is that the program of requirements is being defined by political pressure, by the constraints of bond markets, by the residual leverage of a single anchor carrier, by the throughput optimization preferences of TSA and CBP, and by the procurement timelines of a federal RFI with no confirmed funding mechanism. None of those forces is illegitimate. All of them are real. What is missing — what was present in 1959 in a way it is not present in 2026 — is anyone in the capital-decision conversation whose role is to ask, at full empirical seriousness, what the building is for at the level of human experience, and to defend that answer through the value-engineering cycles, the airline negotiations, the TSA change orders, and the FAA certification process.

The Saarinen frame is useful in 2026 not because Saarinen had the right answer for 2026. He did not, and would not have claimed to. The frame is useful because it names the absence of the role he played in 1959. The architect with the stopwatch. The person who treated the experience of arriving at an airport as a program requirement rather than a design preference. The person who held that line when the airline wanted more gates, the FAA wanted faster checkpoint flow, the contractor wanted simpler geometry, and Congress wanted lower cost. The Calatrava Oculus at the World Trade Center — $4 billion, primarily experienced as a shopping mall with a spectacular ceiling, "infamously nightmarish when it comes to maintenance" — is what civic-monumental ambition looks like without the stopwatch. The $22 billion IAD plan is not destined to become an Oculus. But it has not yet shown the work that would prevent it from becoming one.

What conditions, what caveats, what implications:

The thesis holds *if* MWAA reads Saarinen as a method, not as a form. The catenary stays where it is, the SOM extensions stay where they are, the 300-foot extensions go where the plan puts them. What changes is that the interior organization of those extensions, the dwell experience in the AeroTrain stations, the spatial expression of the sterile-area boundary, and the relationship between the preserved main terminal and the four new concourses are treated as program requirements with measurable success criteria — passenger sequence legibility, dwell quality, intuitive wayfinding without signage redundancy, civic threshold experience at the moment of arrival — and not as design preferences to be traded for gate flexibility or construction schedule.

The thesis holds *if* the Section 106 Programmatic Agreement now being developed for the terminal/concourse redevelopment is used to embed those criteria in a legally binding document. The VDHR/VASHPO has already determined that a project-specific PA is appropriate. The agreement will memorialize mitigation measures. Whether it memorializes design-quality obligations — spatial, experiential, procedural — depends on whether MWAA negotiates for them. The instrument exists. The window is open. It closes when the board approves the bond issuance.

The thesis holds *if* MWAA can frame the philosophical question in operational and financial language that survives the bond-rating briefing and the United MII conversation. "We are reconsidering our capital philosophy in light of our founder architect's design intent" is a sentence that loses both of those conversations. "We are defining the program of requirements with measurable spatial and experiential criteria that will reduce passenger-experience risk on a $22 billion asset, and we are documenting those criteria in the Section 106 PA so they survive value engineering" is a sentence that wins both. The Saarinen frame is shorthand inside MWAA. It is not the framing used outside.

The thesis holds *if* the Hartsfield comparison is taken seriously as an unintended consequence to be designed against, not as the aspiration to be optimized for. Hartsfield is a great hub. It is not what Dulles should aspire to be. The international gateway role, the diplomatic-arrival role, the federal-civic role at the capital are program elements that have no equivalent at ATL. Designing IAD to function like Hartsfield is leaving those roles unprogrammed.

The thesis holds, finally, *if* the obsolete principle — the single compact building as the organizer of the journey — is released with full clarity. The 2026 IAD passenger spends almost no time in the Saarinen main terminal. They check in (or do not, having checked in remotely), they screen, they descend to the AeroTrain, they emerge in a concourse, and they spend ninety minutes at the gate before boarding. The experience of the airport is overwhelmingly the experience of the concourse. The Saarinen-philosophy investment in 2026 is in the concourses, the AeroTrain platforms, the sterile-area boundary expression, and the way the building's parts read as a single sequence — not in the main terminal as a self-sufficient civic gesture. That is the move the $22 billion plan has not yet made and could still make.

If those conditions hold, the thesis is correct. If they do not, the counter-case is correct, and the right move for MWAA is to execute the $22 billion plan as a competent infrastructure program, accept that the international gateway of the American capital will operate like Hartsfield in 2034, and stop pretending otherwise. The hardest question this paper asks the reader is which of these outcomes seems more likely on the current trajectory, and what the conversation would have to look like in the next twelve months to bend the trajectory toward the first.

## VI. Implications for MWAA

What follows is specific. It is calibrated to MWAA's current capital program, its institutional posture in May 2026, its bond and airline constraints, and the Section 106 process now in development. It is not a list of generic recommendations.

**Move 1: Define the program of requirements for the 300-foot terminal extensions before the design is procured, and document the requirements in the Section 106 Programmatic Agreement.**

The 300-foot east and west extensions of the main terminal are the most accessible entry point for a Saarinen-philosophy frame in the live $22 billion program. The structural envelope is fixed by the inherited catenary geometry; SOM has already proven the extension can be done with formal fidelity. What has not been defined is the interior organization. Is it a check-in hall, replicating the existing function eastward and westward? Is it a sterile-side concourse extension feeding the AeroTrain spine? Is it a flexible mixed-use volume? The default trajectory, absent a positive intervention, is that each extension becomes whatever the value-engineering cycles produce when the bond pricing closes. The intervention is to define spatial and experiential criteria — sightline legibility from each curb entrance to the AeroTrain platform, single-decision wayfinding without signage redundancy, dwell quality benchmarks at the transitions, no more than one mode change between entry and security — and embed those criteria in the project-specific Programmatic Agreement now being negotiated with the Virginia State Historic Preservation Office. Section 106 is not a veto, but a PA is a legally binding document. Criteria embedded in it survive the value engineering. Criteria not embedded in it do not.

**Move 2: Treat the AeroTrain platform dwell spaces as Saarinen-philosophy real estate, not as transit interludes.**

The SOM AeroTrain station under the main terminal, opened January 2010, already does this work at the design-intent level: faceted concrete-beam roof, skylights, terrazzo flooring referencing the original terminal. The intent is correct. The execution stops at the architecture and does not extend into the passenger dwell experience on the platform itself. A passenger waiting two minutes for an AeroTrain in 2026 is in a transit station that could be anywhere. The same is true at the concourse platforms. The 2028–2033 AeroTrain extension creates the opportunity to define dwell quality, spatial generosity, natural light penetration where possible, and architectural continuity with the Saarinen idiom as program-of-requirements line items. Cost: marginal against the $3.75 billion budgeted for the extension. The platforms are the building's most-used space for the connecting passenger, who is most of United's hub traffic. The current plan does not engage them at this level.

**Move 3: Lock the demolition date for Concourse C/D before Concourse E opens.**

"Temporary" facilities persist at IAD because the demolition date is never locked before the replacement opens. Concourse C/D was built in 1983 as a temporary structure. It has now been temporary for forty-three years. It has received $100 million in capital improvements as sustained-life work. It is United's primary hub concourse and a daily operational liability — low ceilings, replaced HVAC, sanitary system reconfigured after flooding, jet bridges retrofitted for aircraft larger than the original gate design. The $22 billion plan proposes demolishing C/D once the new Concourse B opens in 2028–2030. The institutional history of IAD says this will slip. The Saarinen-philosophy move is to lock the demolition date in the Use and Lease Agreement amendment that will accompany the $22 billion bond issuance, with United as the affected party formally signed up to the schedule. The CEO who does this protects the operational coherence of the entire program. The CEO who does not inherits a permanent temporary structure for the next two decades.

**Move 4: Use the FIS facility in the new concourses to resolve the international arrivals chain Saarinen never designed for and the IAB has not solved.**

The single most operationally consequential failure of the inherited IAD layout is the international arrivals chain: gate, mobile lounge, International Arrivals Building, CBP processing, baggage, re-bag, re-check, re-screen TSA, AeroTrain or second mobile lounge, domestic departure gate. The IAB processed 3.8 million passengers in fiscal year 2024 and ran 348 days with peak wait times exceeding 60 minutes during the 2022–2023 reporting period, including 98 days exceeding two hours. The $22 billion plan locates the new Federal Inspection Services facility in the midfield concourses rather than in a separate IAB building. This is the correct direction and it is, ironically, the most Saarinenian move in the entire plan: it brings arrivals processing to where the aircraft are, rather than moving passengers across the apron to a separate facility. The risk is that the FIS facility is treated as a CBP procurement requirement satisfied at minimum spec, rather than as the single most important design move in the entire program for the passenger experience of arriving at the American capital from abroad. The Saarinen-philosophy frame holds MWAA to the second standard.

**Move 5: Negotiate the design-quality obligations into the Use and Lease Agreement amendment, not just into the Programmatic Agreement.**

United at 70% landed weight has functional MII veto over Additional Projects under the January 2025 Use and Lease Agreement. The $22 billion plan, as scope additions beyond the pre-approved $7 billion program, will require United's effective consent. The current MWAA approach is to negotiate program scope and CPE impact with United separately from the design conversation. The Saarinen-philosophy move is to bring the design-quality criteria into the United negotiation as a joint commitment — the airline, after all, has a direct commercial interest in passenger sequence legibility, dwell quality at the gate areas, and the operational coherence of the connecting bank. United's 2024 commitment to a 40,000-square-foot Concourse E United Club is itself an investment in the same passenger experience the Saarinen frame argues for. The conversation is not "design philosophy versus airline economics." The conversation is what specific design commitments serve United's commercial interest in retaining premium passengers who are also the passengers most sensitive to passenger-experience quality.

**Move 6: Make the explicit case, on the record, that IAD will not become PIT — and design the program against the risk that it might.**

The structural risk that the $22 billion plan repeats Pittsburgh's anchor-carrier-dependence pattern is the question MWAA's board owes its rating-agency briefings, its airline counterparties, and the federal funders whose money it is seeking. The honest answer involves the international gateway role, the transatlantic joint venture dependencies, the DCA slot constraint, the Washington traffic base — but those arguments need to be made on the record, with the PIT and CVG numbers in the room, and the design of the four new concourses needs to be flexible enough to accommodate a meaningful non-United operator should United's network strategy change at any point during the forty-year asset life. This is not a Saarinen-philosophy move in the narrow sense. It is the most important program-of-requirements decision the entire $22 billion program will make, and it is currently being deferred. The Saarinen-philosophy frame, in its most useful application to the CEO chair, is the discipline of asking the deferred question now.

These six moves do not require MWAA to invoke Saarinen by name in any public document. They do not require fighting the TSA on sterile-area architecture (a fight not worth having on the time horizon that matters). They do not require revisiting the 2025–2039 Use and Lease Agreement (which represents the most important financial achievement of the Potter era). They do not require slowing the $22 billion program (which the airport's growth trajectory will not permit).

They require treating the program of requirements as a program of requirements, rather than as a procurement specification. They require defining experiential criteria with the same rigor as throughput criteria. They require documenting those criteria in instruments — the Section 106 PA, the Use and Lease Agreement amendment, the airline use commitments — that survive value engineering. They require asking the deferred question about anchor-carrier dependency on the record, before the bond issuance commits the scope.

They require, in short, a stopwatch.

## VII.

The man who carried the stopwatch died in August 1961, age fifty-one, before the building opened. The terminal has operated for sixty-four years, has been doubled in length to the dimensions he originally specified, has had its operating diagram replaced and partially restored, has buried a 121,700-square-foot security mezzanine in its basement and an automated people mover under that, has watched its modal-clarity principle accumulate five parallel passenger-to-aircraft systems where it once contained one, and is now the live framing for a $22 billion capital decision being made by people for whom the architect's stopwatch is an artifact in a documentary. The question is not what Saarinen would do. He would not do anything. He is dead. The question is what kind of building a person arrives in when they land at the international gateway of the American capital in 2034, and whether anyone in the capital-decision conversation in 2026 is asking that question with the empirical seriousness he brought to a smaller version of the same question in 1959. The answer is currently no. It does not have to be.
