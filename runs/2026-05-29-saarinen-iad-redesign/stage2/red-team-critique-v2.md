# Red Team Critique — Strategist Draft v2
## Run: Saarinen IAD Redesign | Stage 2 | 2026-05-28

The Strategist took the v1 critique seriously. The biggest structural items moved: the four-principles-to-three concession is made (Section II.1), the 23/40 framing is at the top of Section III, the 1975 utilization gap is in the counter-case lead, the PIT verdict is committed to ("moderately lower than PIT was, but the difference is one of degree rather than kind, and the $22 billion plan moves IAD in the wrong direction"), the compact-building obsolescence gets its own structural-type subsection, the Oculus has its full paragraph in Section V, the Heroicisation paper is engaged by its three nouns rather than name-dropped, the AeroTrain "more faithful" claim now leads with the qualifier, and Section VI Move 5 explicitly names the asymmetry in United's interests. The closing question-asked-three-times is reduced to one. The PA is named as cross-cutting and used in Moves 1, 2, and 4. Most of the Section II parenthetical "(Architectural Historian; Operations Analyst)" attributions are gone.

That is real work, and v2 is materially better than v1. But the new prose introduced new problems, some of the "fixes" softened where they should have hardened, and a few high-priority items from v1 are still load-bearing and unfixed. What follows is ten items. The Strategist should treat them as binding for v3.

---

### 1. The "Implications" section now proposes a 60-to-90 day study but buries it as a footnote rather than naming it as a precondition for the other five moves

- **Location:** Section VI, final paragraph ("One additional move, suggested by the slacker brief and named here as a precondition rather than as a numbered intervention...")
- **Issue:** The v1 critique (item 34) flagged that if the Strategist truly believes Saarinen's method is the thesis, the position paper should be calling for an empirical study before scope lock. The Strategist responded — but then placed the empirical study below the six numbered moves, in a paragraph that opens "One additional move...named here as a precondition rather than as a numbered intervention because it is the empirical-method requirement that everything else assumes." If it is the empirical-method requirement that everything else assumes, it is Move 1, not the asterisk after Move 6. The current sequencing tells the MWAA staffer: "do six things, and also commission a study that all six depend on." The study should anchor the section, not trail it. As placed, it reads as the Strategist hedging — wanting credit for engaging the slacker brief's most threatening question without actually building the structure of the implications around the answer.
- **Recommendation:** Promote the 60-to-90-day passenger-experience study to Move 1 and renumber. Frame the existing Move 1 (the PA criteria for the 300-foot extensions) as Move 2, with explicit language that the spatial criteria in the PA are specifiable only after the study's output. This is a structural fix, not a cosmetic one — and it is the move that makes the Saarinen-method claim of the paper operationally consistent with its recommendations rather than rhetorically gestural.

### 2. The stopwatch is still doing rhetorical work in the closing paragraphs without doing analytical work

- **Location:** Section VII, paragraph 1 ("a $22 billion capital decision being made by people for whom the architect's empirical method is an artifact in a documentary"); Section V ("yes, the most Saarinenian thing to do in 2026 is to do the empirical work")
- **Issue:** V1 flagged the stopwatch as overplayed across five mentions. V2 reduced the count but the motif still anchors the close — "the architect's empirical method is an artifact in a documentary" is the same elevated register the v1 critique flagged ("the architect with the stopwatch"). The phrase "empirical method" appears in Section V, Section VI's footnote-move, and Section VII without ever being defined operationally. What does Saarinen's "empirical method" specifically include beyond stopwatch-timed walks? Did the office produce passenger surveys, observation protocols, structured interviews with airline operations staff? The architectural-historian brief says "more than a year of stopwatch-timed passenger-flow studies." The slacker brief says "spend a year with a stopwatch." That is what the documentary record supports. "Empirical method" as a phrase implies a methodology the briefs do not document at that level of specificity.
- **Recommendation:** Either define what the Strategist means by "empirical method" with specifics from the briefs (the 900-foot baseline, the more-than-100 proposals the FAA reviewed, the year of observation, the team composition) or replace "empirical method" with the more honest "stopwatch-timed observation of passenger behavior" wherever it appears. The current phrase has the texture of a defined term without the content of one.

### 3. The new "two-axis" frame resolves the v1 contradiction but the resolution is asserted rather than argued

- **Location:** Section III, subsection "The $22 billion plan, read on two axes"
- **Issue:** V1 item 40 flagged that the plan was simultaneously called "more faithful" and called violating the philosophy on five dimensions. V2 fixes this by naming two axes — operational-coherence (where the plan is faithful) and interior-experience (where the plan is at risk). The frame works. But the subsection is now 150 words and asserts the two-axis frame without arguing why these are the right two axes, why they are independent, or how the reader can tell when an item belongs on one axis versus the other. Modal clarity, for example: is "modal clarity" an operational-coherence question (number of modes the passenger encounters) or an interior-experience question (how legible the encounters are)? The Strategist treats it as both in different sentences. The frame is doing real work but the frame itself needs about 100 more words to land as analysis rather than as label.
- **Recommendation:** Add a short paragraph defining the two axes operationally, with one example of an element that sits cleanly on each axis and one element that requires choice between them. This is the kind of analytical move the piece is supposed to deliver and currently substitutes a label for.

### 4. The "Saarinenian response" engagement in Section V is rhetorically responsive but logically inconsistent with the implications section

- **Location:** Section V, paragraph beginning "The Saarinen frame is useful in 2026 not because Saarinen had the right answer for 2026..."
- **Issue:** V1 item 34 said the Strategist needed to either add an empirical-study Implications move or be honest about the gap. V2 says "the implications section recommends that MWAA commission exactly that kind of work." But the implications section only commissions a 60-to-90 day study, which is explicitly *not* a year with a stopwatch. The Strategist is asserting that the implications section answers the slacker's question 2; the implications section is recommending a study one-quarter the length of Saarinen's original work, by an independent firm rather than by an architect, that feeds into Moves 1, 2, and 4 rather than producing a new design. That is reasonable as a practical matter (the bond schedule will not wait). But the Strategist should not claim the 60-to-90 day study is "exactly that kind of work." It is a compressed, scoped version of that kind of work, justified by the bond schedule. The honesty the run prompt demands is to name the compression rather than to claim equivalence.
- **Recommendation:** Rewrite the Section V paragraph to say: "The implications section recommends a compressed version of Saarinen's empirical work — 60 to 90 days rather than a year, scoped to inform program-of-requirements decisions rather than to drive a new design, because the bond schedule does not permit the full method. The compression is itself a concession to the political-economic environment, and the reader should weigh it as such." This is the kind of honest engagement the run prompt's anti-hagiography clause requires.

### 5. The "$2.9 billion in evidence" number is now in the counter-case but the response in Section V to that specific number is missing

- **Location:** Section IV, paragraph 1 (deploys the $2.9B aggregate); Section V (concessions list does not engage the $2.9B specifically)
- **Issue:** The Strategist correctly moved the contrarian's $2.9B "evidence of progressive override" framing to the head of the counter-case (v1 item 31). Good. But in Section V, the concession list addresses the 1975 utilization gap and the AeroTrain replacement separately — and does not engage the contrarian's aggregate framing. The aggregate is the contrarian's strongest single move because it captures the *pattern* of override, not just two override events. The Strategist's concession that "The $2.9 billion in subsequent override capital is the financial record of the institutional realization that the design did not solve the problem the industry actually presented" is in the concession block — but the next move, which the v1 critique asked for, is to say what the Strategist's response to the pattern is. Why does this paper's thesis not simply concede the contrarian's stronger claim — that the design is a 63-year capital sink? The Strategist's implicit answer is that the override capital was spent preserving form rather than restoring intent; the explicit version of that answer is not in the text.
- **Recommendation:** Add 2-3 sentences after the $2.9B concession in Section V that name the Strategist's actual response: the $2.9B was capital spent overriding the operational concept while preserving the architectural envelope. The argument of this paper is that the next $22B is on track to do the same thing at scale, and that the Saarinen-philosophy frame is the discipline that distinguishes "override capital that preserves intent" from "override capital that preserves only form." Without this move, the counter-case's strongest aggregate sits in the concession block undefended.

### 6. The PIT verdict commits but the hedging move in Implications (Move 6) is structurally suspect

- **Location:** Section VI, Move 6, second paragraph (the three specific elements of the hedge)
- **Issue:** The verdict is committed (v1 item 21 fixed). The hedge moves are concrete (good). But: the second element of the hedge — "write into the Use and Lease Agreement amendment a covenant that no more than 70 percent of new gate capacity created by the program may be held under preferential use by a single carrier at any point in the asset life" — is a covenant that the airline-commercial-strategist brief specifically says United would resist. The brief is explicit: United resists "anything that suppresses branded terminal differentiation," "common-use mandates," and "anything that raises CPE without proportional network benefit." A covenant capping United's preferential use at 70% of new gates is the exact category of move the brief said United would resist. Move 5 explicitly carves United-resisted criteria out of the U&L Agreement and puts them in the PA. But Move 6 puts a United-resisted criterion into the U&L Agreement. The Strategist did not catch the inconsistency between Move 5 and Move 6. The third element of Move 6 (ATP grant scope language) is fine; the first element (common-use design for Concourse D) is plausible; the second element undercuts the careful asymmetry the Strategist built into Move 5.
- **Recommendation:** Either move the 70%-cap covenant from the U&L Agreement to the PA (consistent with Move 5's logic), or argue explicitly why this particular United-resisted item belongs in the U&L Agreement when others do not. The second option is harder but more honest — there is a case that a structural concentration cap is too important to leave to a non-binding instrument. Either way, the Strategist needs to address the inconsistency rather than leave it for the reader to find.

### 7. Move 2's "marginal cost" estimate is the kind of invented-number move the v1 critique warned against

- **Location:** Section VI, Move 2 ("Marginal cost against the $3.75 billion budget, based on industry-standard premium for APM station finishes versus base spec, is in the range of $30 to $60 million — analyst judgment derived from finish premium ratios of 0.8 to 1.6 percent typical of public-realm transit projects")
- **Issue:** The Strategist responded to v1's complaint about "marginal" being unquantified by inventing a number range with a methodology footnote ("analyst judgment derived from finish premium ratios"). The brief base does not contain finish-premium-ratio data for APM stations. The 0.8 to 1.6 percent range is a Strategist construction, not a brief-cited fact. Same for Move 6's "5 to 10 percent based on industry benchmarks" for common-use vs preferential-use gate cost differential — "industry benchmarks" is not in any brief. Both are flagged as "analyst judgment," which is more honest than v1's silence — but the practice of inventing a numeric range and labeling it analyst judgment is the v1 invented-numbers problem in a slightly more transparent form.
- **Recommendation:** Replace the numeric ranges with either (a) a verbal range ("low tens of millions against the $3.75 billion budget; the relevant comparison is with the SOM 2010 station's finish premium over standard APM stations of that period") that does not invent a percentage, or (b) a direct citation to a source the Strategist has confirmed during Stage 4 fact-checking. As written, the numbers will not survive fact-checking and the Strategist should not put a fact-checker in the position of having to flag them.

### 8. The "civic-gateway" term is used three times in v2 and still not grounded

- **Location:** Section III subsection "Where the philosophy still holds," third paragraph (defines it via Saarinen quote); Section V, "civic threshold experience at arrival"; Section VII, "civic ambition without empirical discipline"
- **Issue:** V1 item 24 said "civic-gateway architecture" was invoked as a principle and never grounded. V2's response is to anchor the term in the Saarinen 1962 quote ("Federal architecture is static, but a jet-age airport should be essentially non-static") and to use it more carefully. The grounding is partial: the Strategist now ties the term to Saarinen's own framing rather than to an analyst's coinage, but does not specify what the operational test is. The Strategist asks "does the spatial sequence of the building still produce the experience the design was organized to produce, or has the building become primarily a screening funnel for departing passengers and a baggage claim hall for arriving ones?" — this is a rhetorical-question move, not an operational test. What specifically distinguishes a building that does civic work from one that does not? Foster Hong Kong is named as a contemporary parallel. What specific design moves at Hong Kong does the Strategist identify as the civic work? "Clear lines of sight, minimal level changes, a vaulted roof that provides directionality" — this is the architectural-historian's language about wayfinding, not about civic threshold. The Strategist has imported a wayfinding observation and labeled it civic-gateway work.
- **Recommendation:** Either commit to a specific operational definition of "civic-gateway" that names 3-4 testable criteria (e.g., spatial threshold legibility, building's appearance in non-aviation cultural reference, expression of the arrival as a moment of transition between systems of governance, etc.) or replace "civic-gateway" with "spatial-threshold" or another more analytically precise term throughout. The current use is improved from v1 but still does load-bearing rhetorical work without load-bearing analytical content.

### 9. The new "Saarinen who built Dulles was a different architect on the same payroll" paragraph is sharp but the closing of Section VII reverts to elevated register

- **Location:** Section III, subsection "The stopwatch was the method, not the motif," paragraphs 2-3 (TWA-vs-IAD split); Section VII, final paragraph
- **Issue:** The TWA/IAD split is now done explicitly in Section III — this addresses v1's anti-hagiography concern at the place the architectural-historian brief required. Good. But Section VII reverts: "The question this paper leaves with MWAA is whether the next twelve months will be spent answering that problem at empirical strength or deferring it to the value-engineering cycles that have, by the documentary record, produced every other miss in the airport's sixty-four-year operational history." This is rhetorical-question close redux — v1 item 39 flagged this exact pattern. The v2 close has one rhetorical question rather than three, which is progress, but the question is still the close. The "every other miss" phrasing also overreaches: the documentary record includes both misses (1996 SOM extending without engaging concourse logic, Silver Line above-grade, IAB) and hits (the SOM AeroTrain station's design intent, the Concourse E AeroTrain alignment). The Strategist asserted the SOM 2010 station "does design-intent work at the architectural level" in Move 2. So "every other miss" is not what the documentary record shows. It is what the rhetorical close requires.
- **Recommendation:** End Section VII on a verdict, not a question. The piece has earned a verdict — name the specific binary: by date X (the bond issuance), MWAA either does Y (the six moves, anchored by the empirical study) or accepts Z (the Hartsfield default). Frame it as a deadline the institution faces, not as a question the paper asks. Also: replace "every other miss" with a more honest "most of the major capital decisions" or similar — the absolute framing is rhetorical, not documentary.

### 10. Section IV is still bloated relative to Section VI, and the rebalance v1 asked for did not happen at the word-count level

- **Location:** Sections IV (counter-case) and VI (Implications)
- **Issue:** V1 item 42 said Section IV should be cut by 30-40% and Section VI expanded by the same. V2's Section IV is approximately the same length as v1's Section IV (the counter-case got the $2.9B addition, the layering pattern from the technology-scout, and the sharper CEO-brief framing — all additions, no cuts). V2's Section VI is somewhat longer due to the precondition footnote and the Move 6 hedge expansion. So the gap closed slightly, but the structural rebalance the v1 critique asked for did not happen. The piece is now approximately 9,800 words (within the 8,000-10,000 range the run prompt specified) but the proportional weighting is still wrong: the counter-case still competes with the implications for length, and the implications are the deliverable. A MWAA staffer reading this paper to extract the actionable content has to wade through a long counter-case to get to the six moves.
- **Recommendation:** Cut Section IV's deregulation paragraph and the political-economy paragraph each by roughly 30% (both have made their point in fewer words elsewhere in the piece), redistribute the cuts to expand Move 4 (the FIS facility — currently the most under-developed of the six, despite the Strategist calling it "the most aligned move in the entire plan") with specifics about what the architecturally-defended version of FIS placement looks like vs. the CBP-minimum-spec version. The piece will read more like a position paper and less like a counter-case-with-implications-attached.

## Cross-cutting verdict

V2 fixed most of the highest-priority items from v1. The TWA/IAD anti-hagiography split is done. The PIT verdict is committed. The compact-building obsolescence has its structural-type argument. The Heroicisation paper's three nouns are engaged. The Oculus gets its paragraph and ties to the concession-revenue argument the slacker brief raised. The four-vs-three principles taxonomy is honestly attributed. The 1975 utilization gap leads the counter-case. The 23/40 framing is at the top of Section III. Section VI Move 5 names the asymmetry in United's interests. The PA is used across multiple moves rather than just Move 1.

What remains broken is mostly second-order: the empirical-study move is buried when it should anchor (item 1), "empirical method" is asserted without definition (item 2), the two-axis frame is labeled without being argued (item 3), the slacker-question engagement claims more than it delivers (item 4), the $2.9B aggregate is conceded without a response (item 5), Move 6 has an internal inconsistency with Move 5 (item 6), the "analyst judgment" cost ranges are invented numbers in slightly more honest dress (item 7), "civic-gateway" is still under-defined (item 8), the Section VII close reverts to rhetorical question and overreaches with "every other miss" (item 9), and Section IV/VI proportions are still wrong (item 10).

None of these is a structural rewrite. Items 1, 6, and 9 are the highest priority — item 1 because it makes the thesis's method-claim operational rather than gestural, item 6 because it removes an internal inconsistency that a careful reader will catch immediately, and item 9 because the close is the last thing the reader sees and currently undercuts the verdicts the piece has earned. Items 2, 3, 5, 7, 8 are 1-3 paragraph fixes. Item 10 is a redistribution. All ten are achievable in v3 without compromising the architecture of the piece.

The piece is publishable. v3 should make it the piece the run prompt asked for.
