# The Filter Nobody Removed: BHS Failure Is a Governance Problem, and MWAA's Capital Program Is the Place to Prove Otherwise

**Council Run:** Baggage Handling Best Practices
**Stage 2 — Strategist Draft v1**
**Date:** June 3, 2026

---

## I. Opening

At about 4:30 a.m. on Thursday, March 27, 2008, the first British Airways flight cleared the gate at Heathrow Terminal 5 and pushed back toward the runway. It carried no checked bags. None.

This is not the ordinary shorthand of an aviation disaster — "the bags didn't make it." The first aircraft departed Terminal 5 with zero bags in the hold because a single line of code, written to keep test messages from contaminating the live operating system during integrated trials, had never been switched off when the trials ended. The filter that protected the system during testing was still protecting it on opening morning. When the first real bags arrived at the first real induction stations, the message system refused to acknowledge them as legitimate. The bags were routed to manual sorting. Manual sorting overflowed within hours.

By Day Five, 500 flights had been cancelled, 28,000 bags were stranded in temporary storage, and British Airways was preparing a £10 million invoice to BAA. The Vanderlande system was rated at 12,000 bags per hour. It had been designed for twenty years. It had been built. It had been tested. The filter cost almost nothing to remove, and no one had removed it. The £4.3 billion building it was installed in lost five days because of an artifact from rehearsal.

Heathrow T5 is the most expensive single configuration management failure in the history of the airport industry, and it is not unique. It sits inside a pattern so consistent that treating it as coincidence has become a form of professional malpractice. Denver in 1995. Hong Kong's Chek Lap Kok in 1998. Kuala Lumpur the same year. Berlin Brandenburg in 2020. Four continents. Four vendors. Four owners. Three decades. The same failure mode, dressed in different uniforms.

**The thesis of this report.** First, the canonical baggage handling system failures of the last thirty years are not technology failures and have not been for some time. They are governance failures — owners who committed to opening dates before the system was ready, integrated tests cut to relieve schedule pressure, and operational accountability that died at the boundary between airport, airline, ground handler, and vendor. Second, the technology of bag sortation is now mature. Munich's Terminal 2 has run an Independent Carrier System since 2003 at 99.9% availability with no measurable wear on its original carriers; the architecture choices that determine BHS performance for thirty years have working examples to draw from. Third, MWAA's Concourse E opening at IAD in autumn 2026 — and the larger $7 billion Dulles capital program that surrounds it — should be evaluated against the governance threshold this report establishes, not against any vendor's throughput claim. The question is not whether MWAA can build a good baggage system. It is whether MWAA can build one and avoid the institutional failure that built every bad one that preceded it.

The history is unambiguous. Every owner of every failed BHS since 1995 knew it was risky. None of them delayed.

## II. Executive Summary

The reader who reads only this section should leave with the following claims, each grounded in the evidence developed below.

1. **The failure taxonomy is short and stable.** Across DEN '95, HKG '98, T5 '08, and BER '20, three causes recur: integrated end-to-end testing that was compressed or cancelled under schedule pressure; throughput and operational-readiness assumptions written to satisfy a business case rather than engineering reality; and an owner with no real ability — institutionally, financially, or politically — to push the opening date back when the system signaled it wasn't ready. The same pattern produced 16 months of delay in Denver (GAO RCED-95-35BR), £102 million in economic damage at Chek Lap Kok (Hong Kong Financial Secretary's estimate), 28,000 stranded bags at T5 (House of Commons HC 543, 2008), and 75,000-plus catalogued defects at BER (TÜV inspection, 2014).

2. **The technology layer is mature; the marketing layer is not.** Munich T2's ICS has operated 22 years with original carriers showing no measurable wear, 99.9% availability, and a mean time to repair of 8–10 minutes (BEUMER Group operational reference). Changi T3 ran more than 1,000,000 test bags through its BHS in a 90-day ORAT period and opened without incident. The current state of the art at scale is a known quantity. What is not mature is the distance between vendor read-rate claims and field performance: IATA documents barcode read rates in actual operations at 75–85% against vendor specs of 99%+; Amsterdam Schiphol's operational RFID read rate was 94.1% against a 99% target — a 2.9 million-bag-per-year shortfall at Schiphol's volume.

3. **CapEx dominates procurement decisions but does not dominate the cost.** ACRP Research Report 252 (2023) places initial purchase and installation at 15–20% of the 20-year BHS lifecycle cost and capital broadly defined at 30–40%. Operations, maintenance, energy, and labor consume the remaining 60–70%. Procurement that optimizes the smallest fraction of the lifecycle bill while accepting risk to the largest fraction is the standard institutional behavior. It is also the behavior that produces deferred-maintenance tails large enough to require a second capital cycle to cure.

4. **The integrated testing window is the single most consistent precursor to failure.** Munich Airport advised Denver in 1992 that a system Munich had taken two years to build and six months of 24/7 to test could not be safely commissioned in Denver's compressed schedule. The advice was ignored. At T5, a 2007 BHS team paper acknowledged in advance that limited testing time would compromise integration. The advice was logged and ignored. Changi T4 ran 100-plus trials with 4,000 participants and over a million test bags before its 2017 opening; Newark Terminal A ran 30 trials over six months with 3,000 trial bags before its 2023 opening. The airports that ran the trials opened the way they planned. The airports that compressed the trials produced the case studies.

5. **A 10-minute sortation outage generates roughly 6,000 unresolved bags. Resilience is a governance and design choice, not a technology feature.** BEUMER's published operational analysis is plain: the difference between an airport that recovers in hours and one that recovers in days is pre-staffed manual fallback, defined escalation authority for degraded-mode operations, and sufficient makeup-area buffer to absorb the backlog without re-injecting it into the live loop. These cost almost nothing to implement and require almost zero capital. They require governance documents, drills, and a ramp duty manager with the authority to redirect labor without checking with three carriers.

6. **MWAA's debt trajectory makes the schedule pressure at Concourse E a structural risk, not a project management worry.** Debt service coverage stood at 3.16x in 2024 and is projected to decline toward 1.3x by 2028 as approximately $5.5 billion in new bonds enter the rate base. Moody's, S&P, and Fitch all hold stable outlooks but Moody's changed the U.S. airport sector outlook to negative in May 2025. Debt per O&D enplanement is projected to rise from $223 in 2024 to roughly $400 by 2028. Every month of delay in a revenue-generating facility while debt service accumulates is the exact financial pressure that drove the Denver mayor to commit to an opening date the system could not honor.

7. **United's 62% share at IAD makes BHS performance during construction a commercial event, not an operational footnote.** Connecting transatlantic bags carry the highest direct liability exposure in the U.S. industry — Montreal Convention caps near $2,175 per passenger, plus rebooking and reputational costs. A BHS commissioning failure at Concourse E does not first hit MWAA's income statement; it hits United's, the same way T5 first hit BA's. The carrier's commercial pressure to open Concourse E early is the most predictable risk in the program and the most familiar to anyone who has read the T5 record.

8. **The capital decision is not whether to invest, but how to govern the investment.** Industry practice places BHS capital at roughly 5% of total airport capital deployment — implying a $300–350 million BHS exposure inside MWAA's $6.99 billion IAD program. That is a number large enough to move credit metrics if it slips, large enough to require rating-agency disclosure, and large enough to lock IAD's connection performance for the next thirty years. The right discipline at procurement is not selecting a system; it is selecting an integrated commissioning protocol, an explicit stop/go gate above the COO level, and a tunnel-and-makeup-room geometry sized for flow rather than for static storage. Each of these is an architectural decision made in schematic design, not a recovery move made at commissioning.

## III. The Argument

### a. The pattern is older than most of the people running it

Walk through the four canonical cases as the engineers themselves saw them at the time, and the line through the dots is not subtle.

Denver in 1992 hired BAE Automated Systems to deliver, in just over two years, the largest automated BHS ever attempted: 17 miles of track, 4,000 destination-coded vehicles, 100 networked computers, 5,000 photo eyes, 400 radio receivers, 56 barcode scanners. A delegation from Munich Airport — whose own substantially smaller BHS had taken two years to build and six months of 24/7 testing before commissioning — told the city the schedule was a fantasy. Denver pressed forward. Mid-project, the scope was expanded from a single United concourse to all twenty carriers on three concourses, without rebaselining schedule, budget, or risk assessment. By February 1995, when the airport opened sixteen months late, the GAO had documented approximately $360 million in direct delay costs: $230 million in operating deficits, $37 million in foregone income, $86 million for the parallel conventional system Denver had to build as a backup, and $8.3 million in emergency bond issuance fees. The City of Denver had carried $1.1 million per day in debt service while the building sat empty. The automated system limped along for ten more years at $1 million per month in maintenance — serving only United's outbound bags on one concourse — before United decommissioned it in 2005. Total spend across a system that never functioned to specification: about $520 million. United's spokesman, Jeff Green, explained the 2005 shutdown with the kind of plain language carriers use when they have run out of patience: "Using this system simply doesn't allow us to provide the level of quality customer service that we want to provide." It was a sentence the system had earned ten years earlier.

The post-mortems on Denver are often misread as a technology cautionary tale — "automation was too ambitious" — but the contemporaneous record does not support that reading. The GAO documented, in flat prose, that "the city of Denver initially planned to open DIA with a conventional baggage system to meet the scheduled October 1993 opening date, but later decided to accept the risk associated with building an automated baggage system." No risk assessment accompanied the decision. The Munich engineers had told them how this would end. The decision was made anyway.

Hong Kong's Chek Lap Kok opened July 6, 1998, on a politically immovable date — the first anniversary of the territory's handover to the People's Republic. Within the first week, the integrated airport management system collapsed across baggage, flight information displays, aircraft parking, and cargo. HACTL, the cargo terminal operator, shut its facility on July 7 and the government temporarily reactivated the closed Kai Tak cargo facility until August. Up to 10,000 pieces of checked baggage went to wrong destinations. The Hong Kong Financial Secretary estimated £102 million in economic damage to the territory. The Legislative Council inquiry concluded that the TAMS consortium had not completed commissioning before the politically locked handover-anniversary opening. The systems worked individually. They had not been tested together under real operational load. They had not been tested together because there was no time, because the calendar had decided that July 6 would happen no matter what the calendar of testing said.

Kuala Lumpur opened five days before Hong Kong with the same pathology. Heathrow T5 opened ten years after that, with the most experienced owner, the largest budget, and the longest planning window in the catalogue — twenty years from concept to ribbon. The Vanderlande BHS was eleven miles of conveyor designed for 12,000 bags per hour. Construction delays at the building, however, had compressed BA's familiarization program. BA's on-site training was deferred by six weeks. Willie Walsh later told the House of Commons Transport Select Committee, in language unusually direct for an airline CEO under oath, that "we made some mistakes and in particular had compromised on the testing regime as a consequence of delays in the building of the terminal." The Transport Committee documented what happened then. Staff arrived at gates they could not find in a building still partly under finish-out. The first BA flight pushed back with no bags. The message filter from testing had not been removed. By the end of the opening weekend, 28,000 bags were in storage. The estimated financial damage to BA was £16 million in direct costs; total industry-side damage including BAA settlements and reputational costs was substantially higher. A 2007 paper by the T5 BHS project team had said the quiet part out loud before opening day: "the challenge of testing the integrated software is related not only to its size and complexity but also to the limited time that will be available to test the software in its actual environment." The warning was on the record. The testing time was still cut.

Berlin Brandenburg is the partial exception. The primary failure mode at BER was fire suppression — a smoke extraction system designed by a man whose business card claimed an engineering credential he did not hold, drawn up to draw smoke downward against the physics of hot gas. The 2014 TÜV inspection identified more than 75,000 individual defects. The airport opened October 31, 2020, nine years late, during a pandemic, at €6.5 billion against an initial €2.8 billion estimate. The BHS itself was tested with 15,000 trial bags before opening. The structural lesson from BER for this report is not BHS-specific; it is the governance one. A supervisory board stocked with politicians who had no mechanism to override sunk-cost escalation cannot, in practice, hold an opening when the project is in trouble. The board structure decided the opening cadence. The board structure had no engineering judgment to apply.

Four cases. Three continents. Multiple decades. The same structure: an opening date set ahead of operational readiness, integrated testing compressed under schedule pressure, warnings logged and ignored, and an owner without the authority — or the willingness to exercise it — to stop the train.

This is not a coincidence. It is what an org chart looks like when nobody owns the operational mission after commissioning ends.

### b. The technology is the mature variable. The marketing layer isn't.

If the technology were really the failure mode, you would expect the industry's worst BHS performance to track its newest deployments. It does not.

Munich T2's Independent Carrier System has run since 2003. Twenty-two years on, the original carrier belts show no measurable wear; the system runs at 99.9% availability with mean time to repair of 8–10 minutes; sorting capacity has scaled from the initial commissioning level to over 20,000 bags per hour as passenger volume grew from 25 million to 41 million annually. Munich did not redesign the architecture as it grew. The architecture was sized for growth and grew. Frankfurt's first-generation ICS has been operating continuously since 1972 — fifty-plus years. Changi T3 opened with no first-week failure of consequence because the team ran more than a million test bags through the system over a 90-day ORAT period. Changi T4 added emulation before any physical testing — running the control software faster than real-time to validate behavior under peak demand. Newark Terminal A in 2023 ran 30 trials over six months, with 3,000 test bags and 700 trial passengers, and opened without becoming a case study.

The available sortation architectures — tilt-tray, cross-belt, ICS, DCV — are each well-understood. The architecture decision matters and it is irreversible once tunnel geometry is fixed in concrete. Tilt-tray achieves 5,000–6,000 bags per hour per loop with closed-loop topology that means a segment failure can affect the entire loop. Cross-belt is gentler on fragile items but speed-limited at 2.5 m/s, capping at 4,000–4,500 bags per hour. ICS handles 90-degree vertical lifts that tilt-tray cannot — useful for long below-grade runs of the type IAD requires — but the tote is roughly 48 inches against a bare bag's 30, which reduces EDS throughput by about 25% unless EDS capacity is sized up at programming. DCVs are useful at the long underground distances that defined Denver's concept and that also define IAD's concourse layout, but they are expensive per lane-mile and complex to maintain at scale. Each of these is a real engineering choice with real trade-offs. None of them is mysterious. Each has reference installations operating today at the throughput and reliability they were specified for. The technology is not the variable that drives whether a $300 million capital project ends as Munich or as Denver.

The marketing layer is a different story, and the gap between marketing and operations is where capital planners get fooled. RFID vendors and the airlines that have deployed them — primarily Delta — report 99.9% read accuracy. Delta's claim is credible at Delta's installation, where it has been audited internally. It is not a universal claim. Amsterdam Schiphol, running a substantial RFID trial, achieved 94.1% actual read rates against a 99% target — a six-point shortfall that translates, at Schiphol's volume, to roughly 2.9 million mis-tracked bags per year. IATA's industry data places average barcode read rates in actual operations at 85%; some practitioners place the field average at 75%. Vendor specifications for the same scanners routinely claim 95–99%. Published performance standards — the Maryland Aviation Administration's PEGS Chapter 16.2, for example — accept a 5% misread rate during normal operations. At an 1,800-bag-per-hour induction line, 5% misread is 90 bags an hour routed to manual encoding, which is the labor capacity of multiple staffed workstations.

These gaps are not failures of the technology. They are the field's normal performance, and they are what a competent BHS architecture is built to absorb. The mistake is asking RFID to do something it was never engineered to do — increase sortation capacity. A bag tracked at 99.9% accuracy that is sitting in an undersized Early Bag Storage or circling a saturated makeup carousel is still a late bag. The Contrarian brief makes this point sharply and correctly: tracking is a data layer on top of the physical system; it does not substitute for physical capacity. The Operations Analyst brief makes the same point from a different angle: airports converged on the wrong measurement when they made read rate the headline metric for BHS performance. The harder, less legible question is what the system does with the bags it loses track of.

### c. The lifecycle math says the procurement is upside down

ACRP Research Report 252, published in 2023, is the National Academies' formal framework for total cost of ownership analysis of airport BHS. Its central finding is unambiguous and inconvenient: initial purchase and installation accounts for 15–20% of the 20-year BHS lifecycle cost. Capital broadly defined — major mid-life replacements included — runs 30–40%. The remaining 60–70% is operations, maintenance, energy, and labor. Annual maintenance alone runs approximately 10% of initial construction cost. A $200 million BHS carries a $20 million annual maintenance obligation; over the typical 15-year conveyor lifespan, that is $300 million in maintenance against $200 million in construction.

Most airports do not procure BHS this way. The Chief Engineer's brief documents what is closer to the standard: airports specify and select on installed cost — dollars per linear foot of conveyor, dollars per induction point, dollars per CTX machine — because that is what the procurement file is built to compare. The OPEX consequence shows up in year three as a maintenance line that drifts upward, in year five as a jam rate that starts climbing past one per 10,000 bags, and in year eight as a manual labor cost the ground handler invoices to the carrier rather than the airport. The structural distortion is that BHS failure costs migrate to the parties that did not specify the system. The airport optimizes the smallest fraction of the lifetime bill while assigning the largest fraction to operators who had no role in the specification.

Two practical consequences follow. The first is that Early Bag Storage is almost universally undersized at the procurement stage because airports specify EBS for static capacity — bags held — and operators run it for flow. The difference is not subtle. Frankfurt, Amsterdam, and Atlanta size EBS for the rate at which the system can intake bags and release them in batches to match a departure wave, not for the number of bags that can sit on a rack. The COO's brief puts it concisely: a static-capacity EBS holds bags; a flow-sized dynamic bag store releases them on schedule at the rate the sortation loop needs. The under-sized EBS does not fail on a normal Tuesday morning. It fails on the morning a sortation loop is degraded and four delayed departure waves arrive at once with international inbound connect bags layered on top. That morning is not theoretical at a hub airport like Dulles. It is a regularly occurring stress condition.

The second consequence is that maintenance, when underfunded, is not a small problem that compounds slowly. It is the mechanism that converts a 1-jam-per-10,000-bags system into a 1-jam-per-1,000-bags system. That is a tenfold degradation, and it lands not as a single dramatic failure but as a steady increase in manual intervention that absorbs ramp labor and eats schedule margin every push period. The airport's income statement does not see most of it. The carrier's mishandled bag rate does, and so does the gate scoreboard at every airline's operating center.

### d. Integrated testing is the risk management mechanism, and the schedule pressure is structural

Every documented failure in the modern record includes a specific moment when integrated testing was shortened, rescoped, or deferred because the construction schedule had slipped and the opening date had not. The construction schedule slips because construction schedules at this scale always slip. The opening date does not slip because, by the time the slippage matters, the bond covenants and the airline move-in commitments and the political ribbon-cutting have made it immovable. The compression goes where the institutional resistance is weakest, which is testing.

The empirical floor for adequate BHS commissioning is well-documented. Changi T3 ran more than a million test bags through 90 days of ORAT. Changi T4 ran more than 100 trials with 2,500 airport staff and 1,500 volunteers. Newark Terminal A ran 30 trials over six months. Munich's T1 satellite ran 32 trial days over roughly two months, with external trial passengers from January 2016. The IATA ORAT standard places the integrated operational trial at no less than four months before opening. The Chief Engineer's brief argues — defensibly, in this analyst's view — that six months from iSAT completion to live operations is the floor for a major hub BHS, and that nine months is more defensible for a system of IAD's complexity. None of the airports that produced disaster case studies came close to these standards.

The Aviation Historian brief frames the deeper point: the failure pattern is not a series of bad managers. It is the predictable product of a financing and accountability structure that puts schedule pressure on every project of this scale. Bond financing carries opening-date covenants. Airline move-in commitments precede system validation. Vendor performance specifications measure component throughput, not integrated system behavior. Commissioning, sitting at the end of the schedule, is treated as the last line item and is therefore the first to be cut when the schedule slips. The owner who delays an opening date in defiance of all of this incurs a certain, immediate, visible cost. The owner who does not delay incurs a probabilistic cost that, if it materializes, will be reported as the operator's mistake rather than the financier's. The institutional arithmetic favors compression every time.

### e. Degraded-mode operations is not a feature; it is the most under-designed part of the BHS

When a sortation loop fails — and at scale, it will — the question is whether the airport recovers in hours or in days. The difference is not equipment. It is whether the airport has pre-staffed manual fallback routing, a defined escalation path that does not require a control room decision in real time, a makeup-area buffer sized to absorb the redirected bags without re-saturating the secondary path, and a communication protocol with carriers that prevents simultaneous check-in surges from re-saturating the secondary route. BEUMER's published operational analysis estimates that a ten-minute sortation outage generates approximately 6,000 unresolved bags requiring manual intervention. An airport with pre-positioned manual intervention staff and a defined fallback procedure can clear that backlog within a single bank window. An airport without can turn it into a multi-day cascade.

None of these elements require capital expenditure to install. They require governance documents, drills, and a ramp duty manager with the authority — pre-delegated, in writing — to pull labor from normal positions into emergency sort positions without checking with three carriers and an airport operations center. They also require physical features that are essentially free if designed in from the start, and prohibitively expensive if retrofitted: redundant sortation paths, makeup-area buffer capacity, EBS injection bandwidth that allows the secondary loop to take real load without choking. The Chief Engineer's brief notes correctly that most BHS designs optimize for nominal throughput and are never formally tested at degraded capacity. That is a schematic-design choice that becomes irreversible the moment the tunnel cross-sections are poured.

The Virtual Christian brief's framing of this — that the read-rate metric is a way of avoiding the harder question of what happens to the bags the system loses track of — is the cleanest articulation of the point this report would make on its own. A 99.5% read rate with no degraded-mode protocol is operationally inferior to a 95% read rate with a practiced one. The first sounds better in a board presentation. The second wins on the morning that matters.

## IV. The Counter-Case, Honestly Presented

The strongest argument against the governance thesis is not that the governance failures didn't happen. It is that the governance framing, applied to airports whose problem is something else, becomes a distraction. The Contrarian brief makes this case directly, and the case has weight that this report will not pretend away.

The first counter-argument is physical. Every BHS has a hard ceiling set by its iron. Linear feet of conveyor per minute, cars per hour a sortation loop can process, makeup positions, claim carousels, square footage of EBS. No governance improvement, no testing discipline, no software layer expands that ceiling. At airports where peak-hour bag volumes are pressing against a 30-year-old system running at 90% utilization, the governance lessons from T5 and DEN are simply not the relevant ones. The relevant question is whether the pipe has enough capacity. Seattle-Tacoma is the contemporary reference case: a system of 10 miles of conveyor, 3,000 motors, and 28 CTX machines, physically saturated at approximately 60 million annual passengers, being replaced in three phases over twelve years at approximately $1 billion. That program is not a governance remediation. It is a capacity response to a binding physical constraint, executed under live operations. Melbourne Airport's $500 million international BHS replacement — doubling capacity from 1,800 to 4,000+ bags per hour to support a planned doubling of international traffic from 12–13 million to 24–25 million annually — is the same logic. The system Melbourne is replacing is not failing because someone didn't test it. It is being replaced because it has run out of room.

The second counter-argument is the asset-life one. Industry standard design life for BHS conveyors is approximately 15 years without major refurbishment. The U.S. is currently in a renewal cycle for systems installed in the late 1980s and 1990s — twice their design life. Predictive maintenance software can give a three-week warning that a bearing is failing. It cannot replace the bearing. It cannot increase the conveyor's rated speed. It cannot add sortation capacity the system was never designed to have. At the point of asset exhaustion, the choice is not "software versus infrastructure." It is replace or close. A 30-year-old conveyor that has been governed perfectly is still a 30-year-old conveyor.

The third counter-argument concerns EBS specifically. Undersized Early Bag Storage is sometimes framed as an operational problem solved by smarter scheduling. The Contrarian brief, and BEUMER's own published analysis, points out that this is wrong: throughput rate at the EBS is a physical specification driven by induction and retrieval bandwidth, square footage, and equipment capacity. You can run the cleverest release algorithm in the industry on an undersized EBS and still lose bags when the wave breaks. Schiphol's EBS handles 4,000 bags at Changi T4 and 2,400 at Changi T2 because those airports sized them for the throughput their schedules require. The fix is physical. It belongs in the capital program, not the operations procedure.

The fourth counter-argument is the global comparison the technology layer cannot explain on its own. IATA's 2024 mishandling data shows Asia-Pacific at 3.1 bags per 1,000 passengers, North and South America at 5.5, Europe at 12.3 — a four-to-one spread between Asia and Europe. The technologists will attribute Asia's lead to RFID penetration (88% full adoption in China and North Asia against roughly 40% in Europe). But Asia also has the newest BHS infrastructure on the planet. Changi, Hamad, Incheon, KLIA, and a wave of Chinese greenfield airports were built to current specification within the last fifteen years. The technology advantage and the infrastructure advantage arrived together. Attributing the operational performance gap solely to software adoption is a correlation-causation confusion that flatters software vendors and undersells the role of having modern iron in the ground.

The fifth counter-argument, made by the Slacker brief and reinforced by the Operations Analyst brief, is about the failure taxonomy itself. The four canonical cases are not four instances of the same failure. Denver was primarily a scope-creep failure layered under a testing-compression problem. T5 was primarily a testing-compression failure with a software component nested inside an organizational-boundary failure. Hong Kong was a cascading IT failure across multiple integrated systems in which BHS was a downstream casualty rather than the originating fault. BER was an envelope failure of fire-life-safety and general construction in which the BHS appears as a victim. Lumping these into "governance failure" is convenient for a thesis. It also smooths over the fact that the specific interventions required to prevent each one are not the same. Scope-creep failures require contracting discipline. Testing-compression failures require schedule architecture. Cascading IT failures require system-of-systems engineering. Envelope failures require honest oversight boards. The thesis owes the reader a more precise taxonomy than "governance."

The sixth counter-argument is structural and worth taking on its own terms. The carriers and vendors who participate in BHS commissioning have an incentive structure that runs against the owner's governance discipline. Vendors are paid against commissioning milestones; declaring the system "not ready" delays payment. Carriers have contracted move-in dates and have already negotiated transit agreements based on the published opening. Owners who delay openings face bond market consequences for the publicized slip. None of the parties in the room at the readiness review has an unalloyed financial incentive to call the system not ready. If the institutional arithmetic always favors "go," then governance discipline in the abstract is not enough. It has to be built into the contracts.

The seventh and final counter-argument is the regulatory and political reality the Regulatory and Political brief makes explicit. The owner does not control the full schedule. TSA's PGDS revision cycle, the iSAT clock that starts at 30% design approval, the 60-day TSA review window on facility modifications, the Buy American supply chain on AIP-funded components — these all introduce schedule constraints the airport cannot relax. The DCA perimeter rule has been modified ten times in 24 years; the next slot expansion will arrive without consultation on BHS sizing at DCA. The political environment around MWAA's $9 billion-plus capital program is not background noise. It is the source of the schedule pressure that produces compression. Governance recommendations that ignore the political stack are recommendations that will fail in execution.

These objections deserve to be taken seriously, and the next section does so.

## V. Why the Counter-Case Is Insufficient

Each of those counter-arguments is partly right. None of them dissolves the governance thesis, and several of them, properly understood, reinforce it.

Start with the capacity-exhaustion argument because it is the strongest and the most cleanly bounded. Seattle-Tacoma's BHS Optimization Program and Melbourne's international BHS replacement are real infrastructure responses to real physical constraints. Their existence does not contradict the governance thesis; it bounds it. The thesis is not that physical infrastructure does not matter. It is that, conditional on building physical infrastructure, the governance discipline around testing, integration, and the opening date is what determines whether the new infrastructure performs at its design specification or repeats the cycle of disaster. Seattle is not failing because the system was poorly governed; it is replacing the system because the system reached its physical ceiling. The right question for Seattle was never "should we replace it" — it was always "how do we replace it on a live airport without producing a Denver-shaped disaster on the morning of cutover." The answer involves twelve years of phased construction, dual-track operation during cutover, and an iSAT clock that started years before the program ended. Governance is exactly the variable that determines whether Seattle's $1 billion program produces a good outcome or another case study. The capacity argument and the governance argument are not substitutes. They are sequential.

The same logic applies to the aging-asset argument. Yes, the U.S. is replacing systems at twice their design life. Yes, those systems must be replaced because they are at end-of-life. Both statements are true. Neither tells you what to build, how to size it, how to test it, or how to phase the cutover. The Chief Engineer's brief documents in detail what bad phasing looks like at a live hub: night-work windows that compress productive hours, dual-track operation across the cutover, TSA recertification that cannot be crashed, utility relocations under active aprons that require FAA-coordinated airside access windows. Each of these is a governance question that determines whether the asset replacement succeeds or replicates the institutional pattern. The thesis is not that aging assets do not need to be replaced. It is that replacing them well is harder than the capital request suggests, and the discipline that makes replacement work is the discipline this report is naming.

The EBS objection is the one this analyst would concede most directly, and the concession sharpens rather than weakens the thesis. EBS is genuinely a physical specification. The throughput rate and footprint are determined at design, and they are not amenable to operational improvement after the fact. But the reason airports get EBS wrong at design — sizing for static capacity rather than for flow — is exactly the governance problem this report is describing. EBS sizing decisions are routinely made by engineers optimizing for the average operating day, not by operators planning for the abnormal one. The COO's brief and the Virtual Christian brief both make the point: the EBS is not a warehouse; it is a pressure regulator. Sizing it for capacity rather than flow is the conceptual error, and the conceptual error happens because the operators who would catch it are not in the room at schematic design. That is a governance condition, not an engineering one. The fix is to put the operating chain in the design room early enough that EBS gets sized for the morning it has to absorb four waves and a degraded loop simultaneously.

The Asia-versus-Europe argument is real, and it does not say what the Contrarian brief implies. Asia's lead in mishandling rate is partly infrastructure vintage; that is right. It is also partly governance and operational culture — the same hubs that built modern infrastructure also adopted ORAT-style commissioning rigorously, integrated airline operations into design from the schematic phase, and built operational discipline into the institutional fabric. Changi did not just open with new iron; it opened with 100 trials over six months and a million test bags through the system. The technology advantage and the governance advantage arrived together at Asia's hubs because the same institutional posture produced both. Europe's lagging performance reflects older infrastructure, but it also reflects fragmented airline-operator-handler accountability at hubs that have not invested in integrated commissioning. Calling this "infrastructure vintage" rather than "infrastructure and governance vintage together" understates the strategic lesson. The countries that get BHS right invest in both at once. The U.S. industry has invested in neither at the scale Asia has and will, over the next decade, learn this lesson again at considerable cost unless it does both.

The taxonomy objection — Denver was scope creep, T5 was testing compression, HKG was cascading IT — is well taken and this report should treat the failure modes with more precision than a single label allows. Scope creep, testing compression, cascading integration failure, and envelope failure are different failure modes with different upstream interventions. They share a final common pathway: an opening date the institution cannot move, accountability that dies at organizational boundaries, and warning signs logged and ignored. The thesis is not that all four failures were the same. It is that all four sat inside an institutional structure that produced the same outcome through different mechanisms. The Operations Analyst brief's framing of this is correct: governance is the failure mode at the boundary level. Scope creep is what governance fails to control during design. Testing compression is what governance fails to protect during commissioning. Cascading IT failure is what governance fails to architect at a system-of-systems level. Envelope failure is what governance fails to oversee at the program level. The single label is a synthesis, not a denial of distinctions. A more precise reading of the four cases produces more precise governance interventions, which this report identifies in Section VI.

The vendor and carrier incentive objection is the most important counter-argument and the one this report concedes without hesitation. The Airline Commercial Strategist brief makes the structural case: at T5, BA had the most to gain from opening on schedule; at Denver, United had the most to gain from opening on schedule; at Concourse E in 2026, United will have the most to gain from opening on schedule. The carrier's business case for the new facility depends on occupying it, occupying it depends on opening it, and pushing the opening date back to complete testing imposes a certain immediate cost while the risk of opening without adequate testing remains probabilistic. The owner who relies on the carrier's voluntary support for a testing delay is relying on the wrong party. The discipline has to be contractual. There must be a stop/go gate that does not depend on the carrier's consent and that the carrier has agreed in advance to honor. The Operations Analyst brief is right to insist on this: any MWAA BHS investment program should include a contractually defined testing completion standard that is not subject to modification based on airline schedule pressure. Without that, the governance discipline is a hope, not a system.

The regulatory and political objection is correct on the facts and is also why the governance thesis matters more, not less. The Regulatory and Political brief catalogues what cannot be moved within a project timeline — the TSA PGDS, the 100% screening mandate, the Buy American provision, the iSAT clock — and ends with the observation that the opening date is, in fact, the one variable airport leadership entirely controls if it chooses to exercise that control. Every airport that failed had the authority to delay. None of them used it. The political environment makes the use of that authority harder. It does not make it impossible. The MWAA board structure, with seventeen members appointed from three states and the federal government, is exactly the political condition in which the discipline has to be architected before the pressure arrives, because once it arrives, the political costs of holding will be visible and the governance benefits of holding will not. The board structure does not condemn MWAA to the Hong Kong outcome. It makes the structural separation between the political authority that approves the date and the operational authority that signs the readiness gate a design problem to be solved before construction begins, not after.

The counter-case, in short, is not that the governance framing is wrong. It is that the governance framing is incomplete and that the politically and physically constrained environment in which it must operate makes it harder to apply than the framing alone suggests. Both of those observations are correct. They reinforce the conclusion: the governance discipline must be built into the contracts, the schematic design, the board procedure, and the construction sequence — not added at commissioning. The airports that fail are not the ones whose program managers were incompetent. They are the ones whose institutional architecture did not protect the testing window when the institutional pressure to compress it arrived.

## VI. Implications for MWAA

The conditions that produce a Denver-shaped or a T5-shaped outcome are not hypothetical at IAD. They are the operating environment of the next thirty months. This section is specific.

The schedule pressure is real and is structural. Concourse E is targeted for autumn 2026. MWAA plans to issue roughly $5.5 billion in new bonds through 2028. Debt per O&D enplanement is projected to rise from $223 in 2024 to approximately $400 by 2028. Debt service coverage will decline from 3.16x in 2024 toward 1.3x by 2028 against Moody's downgrade trigger at 1.1x. Moody's changed the U.S. airport sector outlook to negative in May 2025. United Airlines, holding roughly 62% of IAD enplanements, has announced 280 daily departures by April 2025 and a $500 million-plus Concourse E project sized for fourteen United gates and the elimination of mobile-lounge ground boarding. None of these conditions is exceptional. They are the conditions every prior failed BHS opened into. The financial pressure that drove Denver's mayor to hold the February 1995 opening date is the same financial pressure that will be applied to MWAA's board in 2026.

The testing window is the variable to protect. The Munich-style minimum — two years of build, six months of 24/7 testing — was the original benchmark for a substantially smaller system. The Changi standards — a million test bags, 100+ trials, 4,000 participants — are the modern benchmarks. The IATA minimum is four months of integrated operational trial before opening. The Chief Engineer's brief argues that nine months from iSAT completion to live operation is the defensible floor for a system of IAD's complexity. For MWAA, the relevant question is not whether ORAT will be planned — it will be — but whether it will be protected when the construction schedule slips. Construction schedules slip. The recommendation is straightforward: the testing completion standard for Concourse E's BHS should be defined contractually at the highest level of MWAA's governance, signed by United as the dominant carrier, and structured so that the opening date adjusts to the testing readiness rather than the other way around. The 2024–2039 use agreement is the natural place for this clause. It does not exist there yet.

The architecture decision is irreversible and is being made now. The dedicated baggage tunnels, the new makeup rooms, the expanded BHS space — these are the physical features that determine which sortation architecture is even available to IAD for the next thirty years. The Technology Scout brief is clear that ICS deserves serious consideration at IAD given the long below-grade distances between the Main Terminal and the concourses, the same engineering condition that drove Denver to DCV in 1992. ICS handles the vertical lifts tilt-tray cannot. ICS energy consumption runs roughly a third of conventional conveyor for equivalent throughput. The 25% reduction in EDS throughput from the larger tote footprint can be designed around by sizing EDS capacity up at programming, but only if the EDS specification is set against the tote dimension rather than the bare bag dimension. If the tunnel geometry is designed first and the BHS architecture is fit to whatever the tunnel will accommodate, IAD will own — for thirty years — a suboptimal system locked into a $150 million below-grade structure. The architecture decision belongs in schematic design. It cannot be moved later.

EBS sizing belongs in the same conversation. The COO's brief and the Virtual Christian brief converge on the same point: at a hub with United's wave structure and international transfer flows, the EBS must be sized for flow rather than for static capacity, with induct and retrieval bandwidth specified against the worst-case interaction of early international check-in, late-connecting domestic, and a degraded sortation loop. Frankfurt and Amsterdam are the available references; the dynamic bag store at Melbourne's new system, sized at 1,400 bags specifically to support anytime check-in at the 24–25 million international passenger target, is the most recent benchmark. At IAD, this is a programming-phase decision that will be made in the next eighteen months. It is the decision most likely to be deferred and most expensive to retrofit.

Degraded-mode operations is a governance product, not a procurement product. The recovery profile at a hub airport is determined not by what is bought but by what is rehearsed. The recommendation here is that MWAA pre-stage, in writing, a degraded-mode protocol that includes manual fallback routing, redirected staffing authority at the ramp duty manager level, makeup-area buffer capacity, and an airline communication protocol that prevents simultaneous check-in surges from saturating the secondary path. These features are nearly free. They require institutional negotiation with United, with the ground handlers United subcontracts to, and with TSA. The negotiation should be completed before commissioning, not after the first sortation outage produces 6,000 bags an analyst can quote in a quarterly review.

The governance gate is the structural protection. MWAA's 17-member board, appointed across three states and the federal executive, is precisely the structure in which the discipline to delay an opening date must be architected in advance. The Airport CEO brief identifies the right framing: the CEO who walks into a Moody's call in late 2026 explaining that Concourse E is opening late because the system was not ready will be making a far easier defense than the CEO who walks in explaining that Concourse E opened on time and produced a T5-class commissioning failure. The first conversation is uncomfortable; the second is a credit event. The structural separation between the political authority that sets the date and the operational authority that approves the readiness must be written into board procedure, into the CEO's authority, and into the use agreement before the pressure arrives. After it arrives, the procedural argument will be lost.

The federal funding window matters and is closing. The IIJA Airport Terminal Program's FY2026 round is the final ATP announcement. AIP does not fund BHS. The PFC cap has been frozen at $4.50 since 2000 and was not raised in the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024. The MWAA program embedded in the use agreement carries $235.8 million in grant funding for Concourse E. BHS scope that is not already inside an approved program or grant application will be financed at whatever spread the bond market quotes against a negative-outlook sector and a declining DSCR. That is a financing condition that argues, on its own, for naming the BHS scope inside the current program — not deferring it to a 2030 capital cycle that will face worse credit conditions and a closed federal window.

The cybersecurity dimension belongs in this section even though no brief made it the centerpiece. Berlin Brandenburg's 2025 cyberattack on Collins Aerospace, the airport's outsourced BHS software provider, produced days of manual check-in and baggage delays. The technology risk in 2026 has migrated from hardware to software and vendor dependency. The architecture choice for the IAD BHS software stack — including how it is isolated from third-party systems that maintain internet-facing exposure — is a design constraint, not an operational afterthought. Each of the next decade's BHS programs will face some version of this question. IAD's specification should treat it as foundational, not as a vendor add-on.

These observations are not a recommendation to spend more or to spend less. The Infrastructure Economist brief models the capital trade clearly: a $241.5 million Austin-Bergstrom-scale BHS at IAD, properly designed and commissioned, would deliver roughly $0.40–0.55 per passenger in annual debt service — a defensible figure. A $400–500 million BHS at IAD with deferred integrated testing and optimistic throughput assumptions delivers the Denver outcome at the upper bound. The interesting variable in that comparison is not the spread between the two capital numbers. It is the testing schedule and the governance structure that decide whether each dollar buys reliable infrastructure or buys the next case study.

The capital program is the opportunity. The governance discipline around it is the variable.

## VII.

There is a moment at every airport, on the first day of operations at a new facility, when the first bag rides the first belt for the first time. At Heathrow Terminal 5 on March 27, 2008, that bag never reached its aircraft, because a line of code from a rehearsal had not been removed, and because no one at the company that built the system or the carrier that occupied it or the airport that owned it was empowered to delay the morning long enough to find out. The filter cost almost nothing to remove. The morning cost roughly £100 million in direct damage and a decade of institutional memory loss whose true cost is the next airport that opens with a filter still in place. The question that ought to keep the people running the Dulles capital program up at night is not what the next failure will look like. It is who, on that morning, will have the authority to delay it — and whether the authority will be there before the pressure to use it arrives, or after.
