# Airline Commercial Strategist Brief: BHS Performance and Airline Economics at IAD

**Run:** BHS Best Practices  
**Stage:** 1 — Research  
**Date:** June 3, 2026  
**Lens:** Airline commercial dynamics — how BHS performance, failures, and investment affect carrier economics, airport-airline relationships, and network decisions

---

## Key Findings

1. **Baggage fee revenue is now a $7.27B annual industry profit line that BHS failure directly erodes.** In 2024, US carriers collected $7.27 billion in checked baggage fees — American ($1.50B), United ($1.34B), Delta ($1.06B) leading. [Source: BTS Form 41] Every bag that is mishandled, delayed, or destroyed represents a direct clawback against that revenue line: delayed bags cost approximately $90 each in direct handling costs, damaged bags $180, permanently lost bags $950. With 33.4 million bags mishandled globally in 2024, the industry absorbed an estimated $5 billion in mishandling costs. The net of baggage fees minus mishandling costs is the actual margin — and BHS reliability is the variable that sets it.

2. **United's CPE sensitivity at IAD is historically demonstrated, not theoretical.** When IAD's cost per enplanement peaked at $26.47 in 2013–2014, United CEO Jeff Smisek publicly called the debt burden "a terrible competitive burden and inappropriate burden on this hub" — language that was understood as a thinly-veiled dehibbing threat. United cut seats at Dulles 6.6% from 2010 to 2013. Virginia responded with $50 million in cost relief. MWAA restructured its rate methodology. IAD's CPE has since fallen to approximately $9.56 (FY2025). Any capital program — including BHS — that drives CPE back toward the $15–20 range enters well-charted danger territory. The new 15-year use agreement (effective January 1, 2025) commits $6.99 billion in Dulles capital spending, which will put upward pressure on CPE throughout the construction cycle.

3. **The T5 failure in 2008 established the precedent that airline bears BHS failure costs, not the airport operator.** British Airways lost an estimated £16 million in direct costs from the first 10 days of T5 operations — 500 cancelled flights, 28,000 mishandled bags, executive resignations, and BA publicly demanding £10 million from BAA as reimbursement (a demand that was contested). The critical commercial finding: BA bore the initial cost of every cancelled flight, every passenger re-accommodation, every courier shipment of delayed bags. BAA built the terminal and the BHS; BA absorbed the revenue damage. At IAD, an analogous failure during a major capital construction program would hit United — not MWAA — on its income statement first.

4. **The Denver BHS case established that a carrier will absorb operational degradation for years before forcing a shutdown — and that $1 million/month in maintenance is the explicit threshold where a carrier will publicly pull the plug.** United operated DEN's automated BHS for a decade despite persistent failures, ultimately because the system served only United's outbound bags at Concourse B. When maintenance hit $1 million per month, United's spokesman Jeff Green stated plainly: "Using this system simply doesn't allow us to provide the level of quality customer service that we want to provide." The total BHS-related cost overrun at DEN exceeded $560 million, the airport opened 16 months late, and the system was eventually replaced by manual tug-and-trailer operations. The lesson is not that automation failed — it is that scope expansion mid-build, untested load assumptions, and exclusion of the primary carrier from design decisions created a system no airline could operate at acceptable cost or quality.

5. **IATA Resolution 753 has shifted the commercial dynamic: bag data is now a product airlines sell to passengers, and airports that cannot support RFID-level tracking are subtracting from that product.** Delta's $50 million RFID deployment at 344 airports (2016) reduced mishandling rates by 10–13% and achieved 99.9% read accuracy versus roughly 95% for barcode systems. Delta used RFID tracking as a customer experience differentiator — bags visible in the Fly Delta app in real time. American Airlines' COO explicitly declined RFID investment as recently as 2019, citing infrastructure incompatibility at its hubs. The divergence is commercial, not technical: carriers that invest in RFID do so because they have a revenue case (fee protection, loyalty differentiation, reduced liability exposure). An airport whose BHS cannot support RFID read points undercuts that revenue case.

6. **United dominates IAD at roughly 62% of total enplanements (mainline plus regional), which creates a structural MII veto over any capital program — but also means United's commercial success at IAD is MWAA's commercial success at IAD.** The 2025–2039 use agreement requires sign-off from airlines representing a majority of the airports' flights before it takes effect. United's market share makes it the effective majority-in-interest holder at IAD. The same dominance that gives MWAA leverage in negotiating United commitments in exchange for capital investment (Concourse E, BHS upgrades) also means that any capital program which impairs United's operations during construction is a commercial risk MWAA bears through CPE recalculation. The relationship is structurally co-dependent — not adversarial.

7. **BHS construction disruption risk is the under-modeled threat in MWAA's capital program.** IAD processes connecting transatlantic traffic — the highest-liability bag category (Montreal Convention, approximately $2,175 per passenger in SDR terms). A bag that misses a transatlantic connection at IAD triggers: (1) Montreal Convention liability, (2) international re-accommodation costs, (3) customer satisfaction damage for United's premium passengers. MWAA's new construction program includes baggage tunnels, new makeup rooms, and expanded BHS space embedded in the $6.99 billion Dulles capital plan. The question is not whether the finished system will perform — it is whether the construction sequence allows United to maintain connection bag reliability during the build. No public documentation reviewed for this brief addresses the construction-phase operational continuity plan for BHS.

8. **The Southwest meltdown (December 2022) is the $1.1 billion case study showing what happens when baggage and passenger systems fail together.** The cause was crew-scheduling technology, not airport BHS — but the outcome is instructive: 17,000 cancelled flights, 2 million stranded passengers, $1.1 billion in total financial impact (refunds, reimbursements, lost revenue), and a $140 million federal settlement. The scale of financial damage from a single operational failure — even one not caused by airport infrastructure — establishes the order of magnitude that carriers model when evaluating operational risk at a given hub.

## Evidence

### 1. The Airline Cost of BHS Failure

The direct cost structure per mishandled bag is documented in industry analysis: delayed bags cost approximately $90 each, damaged bags $180, pilfered bags $260, and permanently lost bags approximately $950. For a mid-size airline handling 20 million passengers annually at the global average mishandling rate, direct costs alone reach approximately $15.5 million, rising past $18 million when manual tracing, call center load, station overtime, and delivery failures are included. [Source: https://zafire.aero/baggage-mishandling-costs/]

Globally, SITA's 2024 Baggage IT Insights report documented 33.4 million mishandled bags costing the industry approximately $5 billion, at a rate of 6.3 bags per 1,000 passengers — a 67% improvement since 2007. [Source: https://www.sita.aero/pressroom/news-releases/more-air-passengers-than-ever-with-one-of-the-lowest-rates-of-mishandled-baggage-thanks-to-tech-investments/]

The DOT baggage liability framework: domestic flights are covered up to $4,700 per passenger. International flights under the Montreal Convention are capped at 1,519 Special Drawing Rights per passenger (approximately $2,175 at current exchange rates), subject to five-year ICAO inflationary adjustment. For a hub like IAD with heavy transatlantic connecting traffic, the practical exposure per mishandled international connection bag is the Montreal Convention limit plus re-accommodation costs, plus the downstream loyalty damage of a premium passenger's experience destroyed at check-in.

### 2. Baggage Fee Revenue as Commercial Stake

The BTS Form 41 Schedule P-6 data for 2024 is unambiguous: U.S. airlines collected $7.27 billion in baggage fees, up from $7.07 billion in 2023. American Airlines led at $1.50 billion, United second at $1.34 billion, Delta third at $1.06 billion. [Source: https://www.bts.gov/topics/airlines-and-airports/baggage-fees-airline-2024]

Delta's RFID investment, announced May 2016 and deployed across 344 airports, cost approximately $50 million. The stated commercial rationale was explicit: reduce mishandling rates, protect baggage fee revenue as a net margin line, and differentiate the customer experience through real-time bag tracking in the Fly Delta app. Delta reported a 10–13% reduction in mishandled bag rates by spring 2018, achieving 99.9% read accuracy versus approximately 95% for barcode systems. Delta reported the lowest mishandled bag rate among U.S. carriers in May 2018 at 1.54 per 1,000 passengers. [Source: https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/delta-leverages-rfid-introduces-innovative-baggage-tracking-process-300259988.html] [Source: https://www.internationalairportreview.com/article/75586/baggage-performance-rfid/]

American Airlines' position was the explicit opposite. COO David Seymour said it did not make sense to upgrade baggage tracking technology until airline partners had done so first. CEO Robert Isom stated that "RFID is a limited technology and that some of their hubs have such backwards technology that upgrading how they track bags in this fashion is too big of a challenge to undertake." [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/american-airlines-not-ready-to-invest-in-better-technology-to-track-your-luggage/] This is a carrier telling investors and airports, in plain language, that its physical infrastructure at its own hubs is too degraded to support modern tracking. That is a network planning disclosure wrapped in a technology objection.

### 3. Carrier Control vs. Airport-Owned BHS

BHS ownership at US airports is predominantly held by airport authorities, not airlines. Carriers occupy space, use the system, and pay for it through lease and use agreement cost recovery mechanisms. The exceptions are cases where a dominant hub carrier has negotiated physical control over specific baggage makeup areas or inline screening configurations — but the conveyance infrastructure itself is generally airport-owned.

At ATL, Delta operates in a facility it has effectively co-designed over decades, including a $110 million investment in advanced infrastructure connecting baggage systems in Concourses B, C, and T. [Source: https://www.kclu.org/national-and-global/2026-05-26/inside-atl-how-delta-juggles-100-000-bags-a-day-at-the-worlds-busiest-airport] Delta processes approximately 100,000 bags per day at ATL — a scale that makes ATL's BHS operationally a Delta facility that sits on airport property. The commercial implication: Delta's investment in ATL BHS infrastructure is a sunk cost that raises the exit barrier, not an airport capital decision that raises Delta's CPE.

At IAD, MWAA owns and operates the BHS. MWAA has contracted John Bean Technologies Corporation (JBT AeroTech) for BHS operations and maintenance at Dulles. The capital costs flow through the airport's rate base and are recovered from airlines through the residual-hybrid rate structure in the use agreement. United does not own IAD's BHS; United pays for it through CPE.

### 4. BHS Capital Costs in Use Agreements and CPE

MWAA's use agreements use a hybrid residual methodology: airlines collectively cover costs allocable to Airline Supported Areas, with nonaeronautical revenue applied to defray the total. Any increase in debt service from capital programs — including BHS — flows into the cost base and is allocated to signatory airlines. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

The historical CPE trajectory at IAD is the best evidence of the mechanism:
- FY2013–2014: CPE peaked at $26.47–$26.55. United CEO Smisek publicly characterized Dulles as bearing "a terrible competitive burden." United cut seats 6.6% from 2010 to 2013. [Source: https://www.flightglobal.com/airport-debt-hinders-united-expansion-at-washington-dulles-smisek/111798.article]
- FY2016: MWAA signed a seven-year lease extension with United, supported by $50 million in Virginia state funding ($25 million annually in 2017–2018) to reduce operating costs.
- FY2025: CPE has declined to approximately $9.56. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airports/iad]
- Under the 2025–2039 use agreement with $6.99 billion in Dulles capital spending, projections in some analyses have CPE rising to $12.77 by FY2026 and potentially much higher by 2035 absent federal grant support.

One projection, cited in media coverage of MWAA's Dulles expansion planning, suggested CPE could reach $90.64 by 2035 without significant federal subsidies to offset the capital program. That figure is scenario-dependent and not MWAA's official projection, but it establishes the scale of the CPE risk embedded in the current capital plan.

The structural tension: MWAA needs United to sign the use agreement and commit to the airport to justify $6.99 billion in capital. United needs the capital program (particularly Concourse E and BHS upgrades) to maintain operational competitiveness at its transatlantic gateway. But United retains the credible threat of seat reduction — or refusal to sign long-dated use agreements — if CPE rises past the threshold where IAD becomes less competitive than EWR or ORD for its transatlantic banking.

### 5. IATA Resolution 753: Compliance and Commercial Implications

IATA Resolution 753, approved in 2013 and effective June 1, 2018, requires all IATA member airlines to track baggage at four key journey points: at acquisition from the passenger, at delivery to the aircraft, during custody transfers between carriers, and at final delivery to the passenger. The resolution was explicitly designed to reduce mishandling, enable automated proration among interline partners, and support SLA measurement. [Source: https://www.iata.org/contentassets/5c4aa8b8b3b1432697d2bf3301450684/iata-resolution-753-implementation-plan_final.pdf]

The airport-side implication is physical: RFID read points must exist at the relevant handoff locations. A BHS that cannot support Resolution 753-compliant scan points at aircraft loading — the most critical data point for misconnection detection — is commercially obsolete for any carrier using RFID. Delta and Alaska have RFID-compliant systems. American does not, per its own executives' statements. United has deployed RFID tracking through its app and customer-facing systems, though the extent of its IAD-specific BHS integration with RFID infrastructure is not documented in public materials reviewed for this brief.

The commercial dynamic created by Resolution 753: airports that invest in BHS infrastructure capable of supporting RFID read points position themselves favorably with carriers making technology-driven baggage investment decisions. Airports that do not become structural obstacles to those carriers' operational reliability targets.

### 6. IAD-Specific Airline Commercial Dynamics

United's share of IAD operations: United mainline represents 49.9% of Dulles enplanements (35.7% domestic, 14.2% international). With United Express regional operations, the total reaches approximately 62% of all passengers and 59.6% of landed weight. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

United has operated a hub at IAD since 1986. IAD functions as United's primary transatlantic gateway for the mid-Atlantic region and, increasingly, as a growth platform for new long-haul routes (Dakar, Cape Town, Athens, Lagos, Berlin, Nice, Venice launched or announced 2024–2025). United CEO Scott Kirby described Dulles as having "emerged as a global gateway" in the December 2024 announcement of the $500 million-plus Concourse E project. [Source: https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/more-than-half-a-billion-dollar-expansion--modernization-coming-to-uniteds-washington-dulles-hub-302320375.html]

United's United Next strategy has driven a 25% increase in IAD flights compared to 2023, with plans to expand to 280 daily departures by April 2025. This growth trajectory makes IAD an active investment target for United — not a network risk — under current CPE conditions. The Concourse E project, opening late 2026, will add 14 dedicated United gates and eliminate the ground-boarding mobile lounge operation for many flights.

The connection bag vulnerability: IAD's transatlantic banking structure means large volumes of domestic passengers connect to international departures within tight windows. Any BHS degradation — whether caused by construction interference, system failure, or capacity constraints — creates direct Montreal Convention liability exposure on the most expensive bag category in United's network. United does not disclose IAD-specific baggage performance data in public filings, but the pattern of transatlantic hub exposure is structurally identical to the T5 scenario.

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: Denver International Airport BHS (1994–2005)

**Background:** Denver International Airport opened February 28, 1995 — 16 months after its originally scheduled opening date of October 1993. The primary cause of delay was the automated baggage handling system, designed and built by BAE Automated Systems. The BHS was originally scoped for a single concourse serving United Airlines. During design and construction, scope expanded to cover all three concourses and 20 different airlines — a fundamental change made without rebaselining the budget or schedule.

**Costs:** Total BHS-related cost overruns exceeded $560 million. The city of Denver incurred approximately $1.1 million per day in costs during the 16-month delay from maintaining the completed airport before it could open. What began as a $193 million contract spiraled past $400 million. [Source: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/damien-eddy_failure-friday-case-file-the-560m-baggage-activity-7377160220223082496-UD06]

**Airline-side impact:** The system that ultimately went operational served only United's outbound flights at Concourse B. Inbound bags were handled manually from opening day. All other carriers used manual systems throughout. United bore the operational consequences of a system that never achieved its designed scope — but also bore the sunk cost through airport rates and the competitive disadvantage of operating in a facility that opened over a year late.

**The 2005 shutdown:** In August 2005, United announced it was decommissioning the automated BHS. United spokesman Jeff Green stated: "Using this system simply doesn't allow us to provide the level of quality customer service that we want to provide." The airline expected to save $1 million per month in maintenance expenses by switching to manual tug-and-trailer operations — the same method used for inbound bags since 1995. [Source: https://www.computerworld.com/article/1720999/united-axes-troubled-baggage-system-at-denver-airport.html]

**The commercial mechanism:** United tolerated a decade of substandard automated performance because the system was already sunk into the airport's cost base and served one direction of one carrier's operations. The tipping point was not operational catastrophe — it was a monthly maintenance line item that exceeded the cost of manual alternatives. The lesson: carriers will accept degraded performance indefinitely if the alternative is absorbing a write-down. The DEN system was never shut down because it was unacceptable; it was shut down when the ongoing cost of keeping it marginally functional exceeded the cost of giving up on it.

**What the airport did or didn't do:** Denver awarded the BHS contract without adequate testing requirements, allowed scope expansion mid-project without restructuring the contract, and excluded airlines from the initial design process. When the airlines (primarily United) were brought in late, their requirements changes cascaded through a system already under construction. The GAO issued multiple reports through 1995–1996 documenting the failure of project oversight. None of the governance deficiencies were technical; all were process and management failures.

### Case Study 2: Heathrow Terminal 5 / British Airways BHS (2008)

**Background:** Terminal 5 at Heathrow opened March 27, 2008, after 20 years of planning at a cost of £4.3 billion. It was designed exclusively for British Airways operations. The BHS was one of the most sophisticated automated systems in the world at the time of commissioning.

**The failure:** In the first 10 days of operation, over 28,000 bags were mishandled. Approximately 500 flights were cancelled. The failure persisted for five days of severe disruption before approaching stabilization.

**Root causes:** Not primarily technical. Contributing factors documented in post-incident analysis included: insufficient staff training (only 80% of staff had completed familiarization training by opening day), failure to test the system under realistic load conditions, programming errors in the baggage system that were not identified before live operations, and poor staff morale that prevented adequate problem response when failures began cascading. These are governance and testing failures, not technology failures.

**Financial cost:** British Airways' estimated direct financial losses from the T5 opening were approximately £16 million, with BA itself characterizing the compensation and manual handling costs at approximately £10 million. BA subsequently demanded £10 million in compensation from BAA (the airport operator), arguing that the airport's system and facilities caused the failure. BAA disputed that characterization. [Source: https://www.nomadlawyer.org/heathrow-baggage-failure-travel-chaos-british-airways-demands-compensation]

**Who bore the costs:** British Airways bore the initial costs of every cancelled flight's revenue loss, every passenger's re-accommodation, every courier delivery of delayed bags. BAA bore reputational and political costs plus whatever settlement eventually resulted from BA's compensation demand. The structure mirrors what would occur at IAD: MWAA would own the failed system; United would absorb the operational revenue damage.

**CEO Willie Walsh's public statement:** Walsh stated the T5 opening was "not our finest hour." Beyond that phrase, BA's communications focused on accepting responsibility for the passenger experience impact while disputing the underlying operational accountability with BAA. This public/private split — accepting customer responsibility while demanding reimbursement from the airport — is the carrier playbook in a BHS failure involving airport-owned infrastructure.

**The T5 paradox:** BA had been the most vocal advocate for a dedicated terminal at Heathrow. T5 was designed around BA's operations. BA participated in design review throughout the project. And yet the testing was compressed, the staff training was incomplete, and the real-world load test was the first day of live operations. The lesson is structural: the carrier that stands to gain most from a new facility has the least incentive to delay its opening, and therefore has commercial pressure to allow compressed testing even when the operational risk is obvious. At IAD, United has exactly this incentive structure around Concourse E and any adjacent BHS work.

## Analytical Assessment: The Carrier Response Test

The thesis of this brief's Council run holds that BHS failures trace to governance and testing failures, not technology failures. The airline commercial perspective confirms this and adds a structural reason why the pattern repeats: the dominant hub carrier has commercial incentives that run directly against adequate testing time. At T5, British Airways wanted T5 open. At DEN, United wanted DEN open. The carrier's business case for the new facility depends on occupying it, and occupying it depends on it opening, and pushing back the opening date to complete testing imposes a certain, immediate cost while the risk of opening without adequate testing remains probabilistic.

At IAD, the relevant test for any BHS capital decision is: does United have an incentive to sign off on adequate testing time, or does it have an incentive to push for operational entry at the first opportunity? Under the current 2025–2039 use agreement, with $6.99 billion committed and United already announcing 280 daily departures by April 2025, United's commercial posture at IAD is expansionary. That expansion depends on Concourse E, and Concourse E connects to BHS infrastructure. The pressure to compress testing will be real.

MWAA's governance task is to resist that pressure regardless of commercial relationship quality. The T5 and DEN case studies both involved sophisticated parties who understood the risks and deferred to operational urgency anyway. The brief recommendation: any MWAA BHS investment program should include a contractually defined testing completion standard that is not subject to modification based on airline schedule pressure, and a phased entry plan that allows operational validation before full-load live operations begin.
