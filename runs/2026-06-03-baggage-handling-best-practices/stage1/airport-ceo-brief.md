# Airport CEO Brief: BHS Governance, Finance, and the IAD Capital Window
**Stage 1 Research Brief — Airport CEO Lens**
*Prepared for the Transform Airports AI Council | Run: Baggage Handling Best Practices*

## Key Findings

1. **The rating agencies are watching MWAA's debt trajectory.** MWAA's DSCR stood at 3.16x at year-end 2024 and will decline to approximately 1.3x by 2028 as the authority draws down $5.5 billion in new debt. Moody's changed its U.S. airport sector outlook to **negative** in May 2025, citing supply-chain-driven capital cost escalation and airline capacity uncertainty. Moody's has indicated that a decline below 1.1x could trigger a downgrade from the current Aa3. Any BHS capital overrun or rework cost that is not already embedded in the approved program is not a maintenance problem — it is a credit event. [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-coming-to-market-with-829-million-municipal-bond-deal; https://www.airportxnews.com/moodys-changes-airport-sector-outlook-to-negative/]

2. **The new 15-year use agreement is the financial architecture BHS decisions must fit inside.** The agreement that took effect January 1, 2025 — which required majority-in-interest airline approval — allocates $6.99 billion in capital at IAD, maintains a 140% (1.4x) airline-funded debt service coverage target, and runs through 2039. BHS scope that was scoped and priced inside the approved capital program is cost-recoverable. BHS scope that migrates outside it — through change orders, rework, or delayed commissioning that shifts cost assumptions — requires either a supplemental agreement with airline approval or absorbs into the authority's own reserves. That is the MII clause in practice. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-completes-negotiations-new-use-and-lease-agreement; https://www.wusa9.com/article/news/local/virginia/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-approves-9-billion-for-projects-at-dulles-reagan/]

3. **The failure mode is governance, not technology — and the historical record is unambiguous.** Denver 1995, Hong Kong 1998, Heathrow T5 2008, Berlin Brandenburg 2020: each case involved an externally imposed or politically honored opening deadline that the governing body lacked the authority or discipline to override when the system was not ready. DEN's automated BHS cost $560 million in overruns, delayed opening by 16 months, and was decommissioned entirely in 2005. HKG opened on a national political timeline, generated an estimated £102 million in economic damage in its first days, and lost 10,000 bags. T5's five-day crisis destroyed 28,000 bags and canceled hundreds of flights — a BAA and British Airways coordination failure, not a technology failure. BER's supervisory board was stocked with politicians who had no mechanism to override sunk-cost escalation. The CEO who cannot stop a premature opening will eventually explain to a board, rating agencies, and the press why the system they approved failed at the worst possible moment.

4. **United's leverage at IAD defines the political economy of any BHS disruption.** United Airlines controls the dominant share of IAD departures. A BHS commissioning failure during major construction — particularly one that degrades connection performance at United's hub — is not a press cycle that lasts a week. It affects United's operating cost base at the airport, surfaces in its public earnings calls, and converts a capital program narrative into a carrier relationship crisis. The new use agreement was signed by United. Any major capital disruption that United can credibly tie to an authority governance failure puts the CEO on the wrong side of the next renegotiation.

5. **The federal funding window is closing.** The IIJA Airport Terminal Program (ATP) runs at $1 billion per year from FY2022 through FY2026; the FY2026 round is the **final** ATP announcement. The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 authorizes AIP at $4.0 billion/year through FY2028 — but AIP covers airside projects, not BHS. PFC revenues can fund baggage makeup and claim areas when not under long-term exclusive lease; MWAA's Concourse E project already carries $235.8 million in grant funding. BHS capital that is not already inside an approved grant application or the existing capital program will not find a federal offset in this funding environment. TSA's CBIS commissioning timeline — governed by its own procurement and certification cycles — is the external dependency that most resembles the failure mode in the historical cases. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/iija/airport-terminals; https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip]

6. **The board's political composition creates the DEN/HKG risk in structural form.** MWAA's 17-member board is drawn from Virginia (7 seats), the District of Columbia (4), Maryland (3), and Presidential appointment (3), with six-year terms. The chair and vice chair are elected annually. This composition means that a major capital opening — a new concourse, a rebuilt international arrivals flow, a modernized baggage tunnel — carries political significance to at least three sets of appointing authorities. The historical pattern is clear: politically significant openings get opened. The CEO's job is to construct the decision-making architecture — milestones, authority, stop/go criteria — before the political pressure arrives, not after it.

7. **The 5% rule is the place to start the capital conversation.** Industry practice benchmarks BHS at roughly 5% of total airport capital investment. Against MWAA's $6.99 billion IAD program, that suggests a BHS capital exposure in the range of $300–350 million — a number large enough to move DSCR projections if it slips, and large enough to require rating-agency disclosure in bond documents if scope changes materially. The number is not a ceiling; the architecture question (dedicated baggage tunnels, expanded makeup rooms, early bag storage sizing) determines whether the real figure is 4% or 8%. The point is that the CEO should frame this as a balance-sheet decision, not a facilities decision.

## Evidence

### Bond Rating and Credit Profile

MWAA's three-agency ratings — Moody's Aa3, S&P AA-minus, Fitch AA-minus, all stable — reflect the authority's **strong liquidity position** (1,006 days cash on hand as of December 31, 2024) and a DSCR of 3.16x in 2024, up from 2.77x in 2023. [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-coming-to-market-with-829-million-municipal-bond-deal]

The authority's planned debt issuance of approximately $5.5 billion through 2028 will compress the DSCR to roughly 1.3x — a decline of nearly 60 percent from current levels. Moody's has identified 1.1x as a potential downgrade trigger. At that margin, unplanned capital additions carry real credit risk, particularly under a sector outlook that Moody's changed to **negative** in May 2025, citing construction cost escalation, airline capacity uncertainty, and trade-driven supply chain disruption. [Source: https://www.airportxnews.com/moodys-changes-airport-sector-outlook-to-negative/]

Moody's 2024 rating methodology for U.S. airport revenue bonds emphasizes operational revenue sustainability, covenant strength (with residual frameworks generally supporting higher ratings than compensatory frameworks), and additional bonds tests typically in the 1.5x–2.0x range. MWAA's new use agreement explicitly targets 140% (1.4x) airline-funded debt service coverage. [Source: https://ma.moodys.com/rs/961-KCJ-308/images/PBM_1401434.pdf; https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-bond-ratings]

### Airline Use-and-Lease Structure

MWAA's new 15-year Airport Use Agreement and Premises Lease took effect January 1, 2025, replacing the agreement that expired at the end of 2024. The agreement required signatures from airlines representing a majority of both airports' flights — the Majority-in-Interest clause — before taking effect. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-completes-negotiations-new-use-and-lease-agreement]

The capital program embedded in the agreement totals $9 billion across both airports: $6.99 billion at IAD (new 14-gate concourse, runway planning, future development) and $2.39 billion at DCA. The agreement provides cost recovery structures for approved capital. Capital that migrates outside the approved program — through BHS change orders, rework, or delay-driven cost absorption — is not automatically recoverable through the rate base. That is the financial risk of a BHS commissioning failure: it can convert an already-priced airline cost into an unrecoverable authority liability. [Source: https://www.wusa9.com/article/news/local/virginia/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-approves-9-billion-for-projects-at-dulles-reagan/]

The prior agreement gave United Airlines — which operates the dominant share of IAD enplanements — a 15-year presence through the extended and renewed cycle, with a prior extension through 2024 and now the new long-term agreement through 2039. Virginia contributed $50 million in state funding under the prior agreement cycle to reduce airline costs, signaling active political and financial partnership with the airport. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/airports-authority-and-united-airlines-execute-long-term-lease-dulles-international]

### Federal Stack

**AIP:** $4.0 billion/year authorized through FY2028 under the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024, up from the prior $3.35 billion/year. Airside only; no BHS eligibility. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip]

**IIJA/BIL Airport Terminal Program:** $1 billion/year competitive grants for FY2022–FY2026; the FY2026 announcement is the final ATP round. MWAA's Concourse E project carries $235.8 million in grant funding. The window to capture additional ATP grants for BHS-related terminal work closes with FY2026 applications. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/iija/airport-terminals]

**PFC:** Capped at $4.50/enplanement since 2001. PFC revenues can fund baggage makeup and claim areas not under long-term exclusive lease, and can be applied to debt service. PFC is a durable funding source; the cap has not moved despite ACI-NA advocacy for years. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/pfc]

**TSA/CBIS:** The TSA's Planning Guidelines and Design Standards for Checked Baggage Inspection Systems govern commissioning milestones and Integrated Site Acceptance Tests. TSA's equipment certification and procurement timeline is not controlled by the airport; it is an external dependency that has historically created schedule compression of exactly the type that precedes BHS commissioning failures. [Source: https://iabsc.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/Planning-Guidelines-and-Design-Standards-for-Checked-Baggage-Inspection-Systems-V7.0.pdf]

### Board Governance

MWAA is governed by a 17-member board: 7 appointees from the Governor of Virginia, 4 from the Mayor of DC, 3 from the Governor of Maryland, and 3 Presidential appointees. Six-year terms, one reappointment permitted. Chair and vice chair elected annually. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Washington_Airports_Authority]

The governance model is a compact authority — legally distinct from a city department, less constrained than a municipal department, but still deeply embedded in the political economy of three jurisdictions and the federal government. Capital decisions that benefit one airport visibly over the other (IAD vs. DCA) routinely become board politics. A $6.99 billion IAD capital program alongside $2.39 billion at DCA is a balance the current board has ratified; any material IAD scope increase without corresponding DCA narrative will test that balance.

### Historical Failure Taxonomy

**Denver (1995):** 16-month opening delay attributed significantly to the automated BHS. The system was treated as a standard infrastructure build rather than an R&D project; airlines were excluded from planning; risk contingencies were inadequate; and governance did not separate political commitment from technical readiness. Cost overrun: approximately $560 million. System decommissioned 2005. Total useful life: 10 years. [Source: https://peimpact.com/the-denver-international-airport-automated-baggage-handling-system/; https://www.cs.vassar.edu/~cs335/Crisis/Denver.pdf]

**Hong Kong Chek Lap Kok (1998):** Opened July 6, 1998, to meet the political deadline of the first anniversary of Hong Kong's reversion to Chinese rule — after two prior postponements. Within hours: BHS failures, computer failures, aircraft parking failures, 10,000 bags misrouted. Estimated economic damage: £102 million in the first days. The external political deadline was the proximate cause of the decision not to delay. [Source: https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/politics/1998/07/09/set-to-fly-hong-kongs-airport-flops-instead/b2a4d876-0d88-44e1-a092-7f859f206f6a/]

**Heathrow T5 (2008):** March 27, 2008 opening. Five-day crisis. 28,000 bags lost. Hundreds of flights canceled. Root causes: inadequate staff training, BAA and British Airways coordination failure, software errors, and management pressure to open on deadline despite known risks. The Transport Select Committee found poor communication and preparation on both parties. [Source: https://hbr.org/2008/04/how-can-british-airways-recove; https://www.businesstraveller.com/news/2008/11/03/t5-the-lessons-learned/]

**Berlin Brandenburg (2020):** Nine years late. Final cost exceeding €7 billion — three times the initial estimate. Supervisory board composed largely of politicians with no mechanism to evaluate or override technical readiness. Governance failures included frequent CEO changes, corruption, and information withholding to the oversight body. [Source: https://www.airportwatch.org.uk/2020/10/nine-years-late-and-x3-over-budget-due-to-problems-berlins-brandenburg-airport-finally-opens-during-a-pandemic/]

## Financial-and-Political Case: For and Against

### The Case For

The IAD capital program creates a time-limited opportunity to rebuild BHS architecture from first principles — dedicated tunnels, properly sized makeup rooms, adequate early bag storage — that cannot be accomplished piecemeal on an operating airport. The cost of that architecture, embedded in an already-approved $6.99 billion capital program with airline cost recovery built in at 1.4x DSCR, is the cheapest it will ever be, relative to the alternative of incremental retrofit.

The 15-year use agreement closes the window on renegotiation until 2039. BHS improvements delivered within the current program scope are recoverable. BHS improvements attempted outside the program, after the agreement is locked, face airline pushback and potential MII blockage. If MWAA is going to rethink its baggage architecture — and the IAD major capital program is the moment to do so — the argument belongs in the current program, not the next one.

The technology risk is genuinely low. The academic and industry literature confirms that modern sortation systems (DCV, tilt-tray, cross-belt) are mature. The failure modes since 2000 have not been system failures — they have been integration failures, testing compression failures, and governance failures. A well-structured commissioning program, with an authority-owned stop/go gate backed by board-level authority, addresses the actual risk profile.

### The Case Against

MWAA's DSCR trajectory is the most important constraint. The authority will spend the next four years managing its credit profile through a massive debt ramp. Rating agencies have already changed sector outlook to negative. The CEO who opens a conversation with Moody's in 2026 about BHS scope expansion outside the approved capital program, while DSCR is declining toward 1.3x, is asking the agencies to hold their rating through an adverse scenario. That is a difficult conversation.

United Airlines is not a passive cost-recovery recipient. As the dominant hub carrier at IAD, United has both economic leverage (it drives the majority of the traffic assumptions in MWAA's bond documents) and political leverage (Virginia state government views United's presence at IAD as an economic imperative). Any BHS construction program that creates sustained disruption to United's hub operations — regardless of the long-term improvement — is a relationship risk. The CEO must calibrate the construction sequencing and commissioning schedule against United's operational tolerance, not just against the engineering timeline.

The federal funding window closes. FY2026 is the final IIJA ATP round. AIP does not fund BHS. PFC has not been raised in 24 years. The financing structure for BHS capital that is not already inside the approved program and not eligible for federal grants is revenue bonds — which add to the $5.5 billion debt ramp and are priced at whatever spread the market demands in a negative-outlook sector environment. That is not an argument against the investment; it is an argument for doing the financial modeling before the architecture decision, not after.

## Specific Data Points and Precedents

**1. MWAA DSCR trajectory and the 1.1x floor.**
Current DSCR: 3.16x (2024). Projected: ~1.3x by 2028 across $5.5 billion in new debt. Moody's identified 1.1x as a potential downgrade trigger. The margin between 1.3x and 1.1x is not large. Capital additions outside the approved program, or cost overruns within it, compress that margin. Any BHS scope decision that adds to the debt program needs a DSCR sensitivity model before the board approves it.
[Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-coming-to-market-with-829-million-municipal-bond-deal]

**2. Denver's $560 million BHS overrun and 10-year system life.**
The DEN automated BHS cost $560 million in overruns, delayed opening 16 months, and was decommissioned in 2005 — meaning the system had a useful life of 10 years against a capital investment that was designed to anchor a 30-year amortization. The governance failure was not recognizing the system as R&D rather than infrastructure, and not separating political commitment from technical readiness. Both failure modes are present in any politically significant airport opening.
[Source: https://peimpact.com/the-denver-international-airport-automated-baggage-handling-system/]

**3. The 5% BHS capital benchmark, applied to IAD's $6.99 billion program.**
Industry practice benchmarks BHS at roughly 5% of total airport capital investment. Applied to the IAD program, that implies a BHS capital exposure in the $300–350 million range. This is the number the CEO should have in the rating-agency briefing as a reference point — and the number against which scope additions should be measured before they become change orders.
[Source: https://www.beumergroup.com/knowledge/airport/what-is-the-most-cost-effective-bhs-and-how-do-we-calculate-it/]

**4. HKG's politically driven opening and the £102 million damage estimate.**
Hong Kong's Chek Lap Kok was delayed twice before a political deadline — the first anniversary of Chinese sovereignty — overrode technical readiness. The resulting first-day failures generated an estimated £102 million in economic damage. The governance lesson is not that airports should never open on deadline; it is that the decision authority to hold an opening needs to be structurally separated from the political authority that set the deadline. For MWAA, with a board drawn from three political jurisdictions, that structural separation requires explicit board governance design, not implicit CEO discretion.
[Source: https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/politics/1998/07/09/set-to-fly-hong-kongs-airport-flops-instead/b2a4d876-0d88-44e1-a092-7f859f206f6a/]

**5. The FY2026 IIJA ATP final round as a hard financing deadline.**
The IIJA Airport Terminal Program's FY2026 competitive grant round is the final ATP announcement. MWAA's Concourse E already carries $235.8 million in grant funding. BHS capital that is terminal-eligible under ATP criteria — and that is not already in an approved application — has one remaining grant window. The CEO who misses that window because the BHS architecture decision was deferred into 2027 will be financing those costs entirely through revenue bonds, at a spread determined by a rating agency looking at a negative sector outlook and a DSCR approaching 1.3x.
[Source: https://www.faa.gov/iija/airport-terminals; https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-coming-to-market-with-829-million-municipal-bond-deal]
