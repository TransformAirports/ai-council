# Regulatory and Political Forces Shaping BHS Capital Decisions at US Airports
## With Specific Attention to MWAA (IAD and DCA)

**Stage 1 Research Brief — Regulatory and Political Analyst**
**Date:** June 3, 2026
**Thesis under review:** BHS failures trace to the same small set of causes — integrated testing compressed or deferred, optimistic throughput assumptions, and an owner unwilling to delay opening for a system that wasn't ready. The state of the art in BHS is mature; governance, testing discipline, and operational integration are the failure modes. MWAA's BHS investments at IAD and DCA should be evaluated against this reality, not against vendor performance specs.

---

## Key Findings

1. **The TSA EDS mandate is non-negotiable and perpetually re-triggering.** The Implementing Recommendations of the 9/11 Commission Act of 2007 (P.L. 110-53) codified at 49 U.S.C. § 44901 requires 100% checked baggage screening at all US airports. This is not a choice airports made — it is a statutory command. What it means for BHS capital is durable: every time TSA recapitalizes EDS equipment, airports face another round of BHS modification requirements. TSA's own capital planning documents acknowledge that recapitalization at adequate funding levels ($500 million/year) will take 15 years; at current funding ($250 million/year), the schedule stretches to 35 years. Any BHS project at IAD should assume a mandatory mid-life TSA-driven modification. This is a near-certainty, not a risk.

2. **The cost-share model makes airports co-investors in federal security infrastructure they do not control.** Under the Vision 100 — Century of Aviation Reauthorization Act (P.L. 108-176, 2003), TSA reimburses 90% of BHS modification costs at large and medium hub airports (95% at smaller airports). The airport bears the remaining 10% plus owns and operates the modified infrastructure. TSA controls the design standards (PGDS v8.0 as of March 2023), approves the facility modification applications under a 60-day statutory review window (added by the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2018), and can change those standards with the next PGDS revision. The airport owns infrastructure built to someone else's spec, on someone else's schedule.

3. **The PFC cap is a political choice masquerading as a legal constraint.** The $4.50 cap on Passenger Facility Charges has been unchanged since 2000 and represents roughly $8.10 in 2024 purchasing power. The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (P.L. 118-63) did not raise the cap despite years of industry advocacy. For large hub airports like IAD, this is the binding constraint on terminal and landside capital formation: AIP entitlement is capped at $22 million annually and the PFC cap limits the bond capacity airports can deploy for non-airfield improvements including BHS. Congress could raise the cap; it has chosen not to for 24 years. This is politics, not law.

4. **MWAA's governance structure is uniquely exposed to congressional politics.** The Metropolitan Washington Airports Act of 1986 (Title VI of P.L. 99-500) created an authority whose board includes presidential appointees, and whose original congressional review mechanism was struck down by the Supreme Court in *Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority v. Citizens for the Abatement of Aircraft Noise* (501 U.S. 252, 1991). The shadow of that structure persists: DCA's perimeter rule and slot controls are codified in federal law (codified at 49 U.S.C. Chapter 491), not just regulation, meaning they cannot be changed by the FAA through rulemaking — they require an act of Congress. The 2024 FAA Reauthorization added 10 new slot exemptions at DCA, directly affecting the airport's operational profile. MWAA's capital decisions occur within an oversight environment where congressional delegations from Virginia, Maryland, and D.C. are actively engaged stakeholders, not passive observers.

5. **The ribbon-cutting imperative is the highest-probability governance failure mode, and the DEN case quantifies it.** Denver International Airport's 1995 opening was delayed 16 months and cost approximately $360 million in direct delay costs (including $230 million in operating deficits, $51 million for a manual backup system, and $35 million in automated system modifications) because the City's mayor controlled the opening decision and the financial pressure of bond rating downgrades ultimately forced the hand. The GAO documented that the airport could not open "until an operating baggage system has been successfully tested" — but the political and fiscal pressure overrode technical readiness for a year and a half. Heathrow T5 in 2008 opened with a system that had never been tested under realistic load conditions, losing 28,000 bags in the first week. Both cases share a common cause: an owner that made a date commitment before the system was ready.

6. **TSA's BHS design approval process embeds federal approval as a project dependency.** Under 49 CFR § 1542.107, any change to the layout or physical structure of areas used to support the screening process requires TSA notification and approval. The TSA PGDS v8.0 design standards (released February 2023, comments closed October 2023 for v9.0 consideration) govern how inline EDS systems must be configured. TSA is statutorily required to respond to a facility modification application within 60 days, but that clock does not start until TSA deems the application complete. Any BHS capital project at IAD that involves the inline screening area is a federal approval process, not purely an airport capital decision.

7. **MWAA's $9.4 billion capital program creates both opportunity and pressure.** The 2024 airline use and lease agreement (effective January 1, 2025, valid through 2039) contemplates $9.4 billion in capital at IAD and DCA. To fund this, MWAA plans to issue $5.5 billion in new debt through 2028, pushing adjusted debt per origin-and-destination enplanement to approximately $400 — significantly above the $223 recorded in 2024. Moody's (Aa3), S&P (AA-), and Fitch (AA-) all maintain stable outlooks but cite the capital program as the primary credit stress. In this environment, schedule pressure on major projects is a financial reality. That pressure makes the ribbon-cutting risk at Concourse E (targeted opening: autumn 2026) concrete, not hypothetical.

## Evidence Section

### 1. TSA Inline EDS Mandate — Regulation

The statutory basis is 49 U.S.C. § 44901, which requires the TSA Administrator to screen all checked baggage on passenger aircraft. The 9/11 Commission Act of 2007 (P.L. 110-53) reinforced and extended these requirements. This is regulation: changing it requires legislation. [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/49/44901]

The DHS Inspector General's September 2024 report (OIG-24-50) found that TSA has made progress implementing 9/11 Commission Act requirements but continues to face challenges completing the mandate. [Source: https://www.oig.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/assets/2024-09/OIG-24-50-Sept24.pdf]

TSA's Planning Guidelines and Design Standards (PGDS) v8.0 was released February 23, 2023, superseding v7.0. The PGDS is the operational document that airports must comply with when designing inline EDS systems. Any BHS project touching checked baggage screening infrastructure must comply with the current PGDS at the time of application. Comments on v8.0 closed October 31, 2023, and a v9.0 is in development — meaning the design standards airports must meet will shift again. [Source: https://www.hstoday.us/federal-pages/dhs/tsa-releases-planning-guidelines-and-design-standards-pgds-version-8-0-for-checked-baggage-inspection-systems/]

TSA's facility modification application process requires airports to obtain TSA approval before implementing changes to inline EDS areas. By statute (FAA Reauthorization Act of 2018), TSA must respond within 60 days of a complete application — but the completeness determination adds additional schedule risk. [Source: https://www.tsa.gov/travel/frequently-asked-questions/what-application-process]

### 2. TSA-Airport Cost-Share Structure — Regulation and Procurement

The Vision 100 — Century of Aviation Reauthorization Act (P.L. 108-176, December 12, 2003) established the 90% federal / 10% airport cost-share for BHS modification projects at medium and large hub airports (95%/5% at smaller airports). GAO has documented this structure extensively across multiple reports. GAO-12-266 noted that reducing the TSA share from 90% to 75% would lower federal costs by approximately $300 million between FY2012 and FY2030 — a figure that implies the total federal investment at 90% was on the order of $1.2 billion over that period. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-12-266]

Smiths Detection holds an IDIQ contract for CTX EDS equipment supply to TSA airports, with 486 systems operating at 41 airports as of recent reporting. The recapitalization of CTX-9000 and CTX-9400 machines to CTX-9800 DSi units is ongoing. Per TSA's FY2026-2030 Capital Investment Plan (published November 2025): "Recapitalization will take approximately 15 years at $500 million per year versus 35 years at $250 million per year." 159 EDS units were at end-of-life in FY2025 and in the process of being recapitalized. [Source: https://www.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/2026-02/25_1215_mgmt-tsa-capital-investment-Plan-fy_2026-2030-fy-2025.pdf]

Denver International Airport received a $26.6 million FAA Airport Terminal Program grant (from Bipartisan Infrastructure Law funds) specifically for BHS modernization — replacing infrastructure in place for 25+ years. The project is also installing new EDS units in Module 1 as the first baggage EDS upgrades at DEN in more than two decades. This illustrates the intersection of FAA capital grants and TSA equipment recapitalization creating simultaneous BHS disruption. [Source: https://www.passengerterminaltoday.com/news/baggage/faa-invests-us26-6m-in-denver-airports-baggage-handling-system-project.html]

### 3. AIP and BIL Funding for BHS — Procurement

Airport Improvement Program eligibility for BHS/security-related construction is defined under 49 U.S.C. § 47119 (terminal development) and post-9/11 amendments. The key constraint: AIP funds cannot be used for baggage conveyor systems or terminal reconfigurations solely to accommodate bulk EDS. TSA must provide written agreement that any EDS space funded through AIP/ATP meets minimum space requirements. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/overview]

The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (P.L. 117-58, November 2021) created three aviation programs: the Airport Terminal Program ($1 billion annually for FY2022-2026), the Airport Infrastructure Grants program ($15 billion over five years), and an ATC tower program. Terminal Program grants are competitive and explicitly eligible for BHS modernization and security checkpoint improvements. MWAA and its peer large-hub airports compete for these funds. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/iija/airport-terminals]

AIP entitlement funding for large hub airports is capped at $22 million annually under 49 U.S.C. § 47114. For context, MWAA's capital program contemplates $9.4 billion over 15 years — AIP entitlement covers a fraction of one percent of annual capital deployment. Large hubs rely on revenue bonds backed by PFC collections and airline rates and charges, not AIP, for major capital. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-capital-funding-and-the-infrastructure-gap]

Buy American provisions (49 U.S.C. § 50101) apply to all AIP-funded projects, requiring all steel and manufactured goods to be produced in the United States. Waivers are available but require public posting for 10 days following the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 requirement. This affects BHS equipment procurement for any project using AIP or BIL funds. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/buy_american]

### 4. PFC Cap — Politics

The $4.50 PFC cap has been unchanged since 2000. In 2024 purchasing power, it is equivalent to approximately $8.10, representing an erosion of approximately 45% in real terms. The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (P.L. 118-63, signed May 16, 2024) increased AIP authorization to $4.0 billion annually through FY2028 but did not raise the PFC cap. Airlines opposed any PFC increase, and the cap held. [Source: https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/IF12482.html]

For MWAA, the PFC cap creates a specific funding architecture problem: revenue bonds backed by PFC collections are the primary vehicle for non-airfield terminal capital. A capped PFC constrains bond capacity at precisely the moment MWAA is executing its largest capital program in decades. [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-coming-to-market-with-829-million-municipal-bond-deal]

Under 49 U.S.C. § 47114(f), large hub airports imposing a $4.50 PFC face a 60% reduction in their AIP apportionment (reduced from 75% by the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024). This means MWAA's choice to collect a $4.50 PFC already costs it a significant portion of its AIP entitlement — trading a federal formula grant for PFC bond capacity.

### 5. MWAA Governance — Politics and Structure

MWAA was created by the Metropolitan Washington Airports Act of 1986 (Title VI of P.L. 99-500), which transferred Dulles and Reagan National from the federal government to the bi-state authority under a 50-year lease. The Board has 17 members: 7 appointed by Virginia's governor, 4 by D.C.'s mayor, 3 by Maryland's governor, and 3 by the President of the United States. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Washington_Airports_Authority]

The original congressional Board of Review — which gave members of Congress direct veto power over MWAA's budget and bond issuance — was struck down by the Supreme Court in 1991 (*MWAA v. Citizens for the Abatement of Aircraft Noise*, 501 U.S. 252) as violating separation of powers. However, the statutory framework governing DCA's perimeter rule and slot controls remains enacted law (49 U.S.C. Chapter 491), enforceable by DOT and only changeable by Congress. [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/501/252]

The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (P.L. 118-63, § 502) added 10 new daily slot exemptions at DCA, enabling service to San Diego, San Antonio, San Francisco, Las Vegas, and Seattle. The FAA increased DCA's effective hourly capacity from 67 to 77 slots as of early 2025. Each slot addition at DCA incrementally increases checked baggage volume without proportional change to existing BHS infrastructure — a structural mismatch that accumulates with each congressional exemption grant. [Source: https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/IN12504.html]

MWAA's $9.4 billion capital program through 2039 includes Dulles Concourse E (435,000 sq ft, United Airlines, AeroTrain-connected, target opening: autumn 2026), Concourse A extension, new security screening and baggage facilities, and a future Tier 3 concourse. The authority plans to issue approximately $5.5 billion in new debt through 2028, with debt per O&D enplanement rising from $223 (2024) to approximately $400 (2028 peak). All three rating agencies maintain stable outlooks but cite capital program execution risk. [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-coming-to-market-with-829-million-municipal-bond-deal]

### 6. TSA's Authority Over BHS Design — Regulation

Under 49 CFR Part 1542 (Airport Security), airport operators must maintain a TSA-approved Airport Security Program (ASP). Any change to the layout or physical structure of an area used to support baggage screening requires notification to TSA within six hours of discovery of the changed condition, followed by a written amendment request within 30 days for changes expected to last 60 days or more (49 CFR § 1542.107). [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/49/1542.107]

This regulatory authority means that a BHS capital project at IAD is not simply a construction project — it is a security program amendment. The TSA Federal Security Director at IAD is a co-approval authority on the BHS design for all inline EDS areas. The TSA's PGDS v8.0 governs the geometry, throughput specifications, and equipment placement. An airport cannot deviate from PGDS without TSA concurrence. This is regulation.

### 7. NEPA and Environmental Review — Regulation

FAA Order 5050.4B establishes NEPA implementing procedures for airport actions. BHS modifications within existing terminal footprints generally qualify for categorical exclusions (CatEx) and do not require Environmental Assessments. However, new terminal construction (such as Concourse E at IAD) requires environmental review proportional to its scope. For a new concourse with new baggage facilities, an Environmental Assessment is the standard review level. FAA categorical exclusions are available for projects that do not individually or cumulatively have significant environmental effects. Projects at federally leased airports like IAD and DCA may involve additional review steps given DOT's retained federal interest. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/environmental/nepa]

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: Denver International Airport (1995) — Ribbon-Cutting as Governance Failure

Denver's automated baggage handling system, contracted to BAE Automated Systems as a $193 million project, became the canonical case of what happens when political and financial pressure overrides technical readiness. The City of Denver's Department of Aviation reported directly to the Mayor. When the system's 72 unresolved deficiencies became public in early 1994, the Mayor announced a February 28, 1995 opening date as a political commitment — not a technical one.

The financial pressure was real: $13-19 million monthly operating deficits were accumulating, and the bond rating agencies were threatening junk status. But the political decision to hold to the date rather than wait for a tested system cost more than the delay would have. GAO (RCED-95-35BR) documented total delay costs at approximately $360 million, including $230 million in operating deficits, $51 million for a manual backup system installation, $35 million in automated system modifications, and additional bond fees. The automated system was abandoned entirely in 2005.

The governance lesson: the opening decision sat with the Mayor, not with the operator. The operator (City aviation staff) knew the system was not ready. The financial pressure created the same effect as political pressure — an opening date commitment that outran technical readiness. MWAA's governance structure, with a 17-member board and a capital program being financed in the bond market, is not immune to the same dynamic. [Source: https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GAOREPORTS-RCED-95-35BR/html/GAOREPORTS-RCED-95-35BR.htm]

### Case Study 2: Heathrow Terminal 5 (2008) — Testing Compressed by Construction Delay

British Airports Authority and British Airways opened Terminal 5 on March 27, 2008, after a £4.3 billion construction program. The opening was described by BAA as "tried, tested and ready to go." Within the first day, 28,000 bags were lost, 500 flights were cancelled, and the chaos continued for five days.

Post-incident analysis identified the root cause: testing was compressed because construction delays pushed back the available testing window, and no stress test under realistic baggage load conditions was ever completed before opening. Staff received four days of training — in a different building, because the terminal was not complete. BAA and British Airways held no joint operational planning meetings until after the failure began.

This is the Heathrow version of the same failure pattern: construction delays compressed testing, a fixed opening date was treated as non-negotiable, and the operational and governance accountability was split between two organizations (BAA as operator, British Airways as tenant) with different incentives. Top British Airways executives resigned. The system worked — but it was overwhelmed because it was never tested at volume. [Source: https://calleam.com/WTPF/?p=473]

### Case Study 3: Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER, 2020) — Governance Failure as Structural Condition

Berlin Brandenburg Airport finally opened October 31, 2020, nine years after its originally planned 2011 opening, at a cost of approximately €6.5-7 billion against an initial estimate of €2.8 billion. The governance structure — a company jointly owned by the German federal government, the State of Berlin, and the State of Brandenburg, supervised by a board of politicians with limited technical expertise — produced the conditions for systematic failure.

Management falsified internal conclusions, minutes, and timetables. Reporting to the supervisory board did not correspond to the truth. Members of parliament serving as overseers did not receive adequate information about plans and cost adjustments; instead, they received selective disclosure designed to secure additional funding approval. The airport's fire-safety system alone required over 100,000 corrections.

BER is not primarily a BHS story — it is a full-system governance collapse. But it demonstrates that political accountability structures governing capital projects routinely produce information suppression, optimistic scheduling, and systematic understatement of problems to those with authority to act. The pattern is not aberrational; it is structural. [Source: https://policycommons.net/artifacts/3860870/governance-failures-in-the-management-of-the-berlin-brandon-international-airport/4666825/]

### Case Study 4: DCA Project Journey (2021) — BHS Capital Embedded in Political Capital Program

Reagan National Airport's $1 billion Project Journey capital program included $269.8 million for two new 50,000-square-foot security checkpoint buildings, opened November 2021. The project expanded TSA screening from 20 to 23 lanes (with capacity for 28), and the baggage handling system was provided by Jervis B. Webb Co. as part of the terminal construction.

The DCA perimeter rule expansion embedded in the 2024 FAA Reauthorization Act (adding 10 new daily slots, growing hourly capacity from 67 to 77) was decided by Congress without reference to BHS or screening capacity at the airport. New routes to San Diego, San Antonio, San Francisco, Las Vegas, and Seattle add checked baggage volume at an airport whose BHS was sized to the slot-constrained operational profile that preceded the exemption grant. Congressional capacity decisions and airport infrastructure capacity decisions operate on completely different timelines and through completely different decision processes. They meet in the baggage hall.

**5. MWAA's debt trajectory and what it means for schedule pressure:**

## What Can and Cannot Be Moved, and on What Timeline

**Cannot be moved on any project timeline:**
- The 100% checked baggage screening mandate (49 U.S.C. § 44901) — statutory, requires legislation to change
- TSA's authority to approve or reject BHS designs through the ASP/PGDS process (49 CFR Part 1542) — regulatory, requires rulemaking to change
- The DCA perimeter rule at 1,250 miles (49 U.S.C. Chapter 491) — statutory, requires legislation; Congress just moved it slightly in 2024 after two decades of stasis
- DCA's slot controls — regulatory but effectively statutory given Congressional protection; the last major expansion took 20+ years of advocacy
- Buy American requirements on AIP-funded projects (49 U.S.C. § 50101) — statutory

**Cannot be moved on a 5-10 year timeline (structural politics):**
- The $4.50 PFC cap — Congress has refused to raise it for 24 years; airlines oppose it; the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 passed without a cap increase despite sustained advocacy
- TSA's PGDS revision cycle — PGDS v9.0 is in development now; airports must comply with the version current at the time of application; this creates permanent design standard uncertainty

**Can be managed within a project timeline, but requires active governance:**
- TSA's 60-day review window for facility modification applications — real but manageable with early engagement and complete applications
- NEPA categorical exclusion applicability — BHS modifications within existing terminal footprint typically qualify; new concourse construction requires EA at minimum
- AIP and BIL grant timing — competitive; requires advance application coordination with FAA regional offices

**Entirely controllable by airport leadership if they choose to exercise that control:**
- The opening date decision — this is the single highest-probability governance failure mode; it sits with the airport CEO and board, not with the FAA, TSA, or Congress; it can be committed to a date before systems are ready, or it can be held until readiness is demonstrated; the political and financial pressure to open on schedule is real, but the authority to say "not yet" exists and has never been exercised by the airports that failed

The analysis is clear: the political and regulatory environment at IAD and DCA is more constraining than the economics suggest, and the governance risk is not distributed across agencies — it concentrates in the opening decision, which MWAA controls entirely.
