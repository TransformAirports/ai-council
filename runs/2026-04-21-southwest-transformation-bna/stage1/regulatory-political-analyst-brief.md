# Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief
## Southwest Transformation at BNA: The Regulatory and Political Architecture
### Stage 1 Research — Council Run: southwest-transformation-bna

---

## Key Findings

1. **MNAA's governance is contested at the highest levels of Tennessee politics and will not be stable in the near term.** The Tennessee General Assembly in 2023 attempted to seize board appointment authority from Nashville's mayor, a move struck down as unconstitutional by two successive courts. The Tennessee Supreme Court has accepted the case. Until the court resolves it — and possibly beyond — every MNAA capital commitment of consequence carries a shadow governance risk that no bond prospectus can fully price.

2. **The airline use agreement signed in July 2023 eliminated the majority-in-interest clause that gave signatory airlines, effectively Southwest, veto power over capital projects.** This is the single most consequential regulatory-contractual shift in the BNA-Southwest relationship in a generation. The airport can now build without airline consent on terminal projects. But the agreement runs through June 30, 2031 — Southwest negotiated fixed terminal rental rates for the full term precisely when its own revenue model was changing fastest.

3. **Southwest accounts for roughly 54% of BNA enplanements and 60% of seat capacity. Rating agencies have flagged this as higher-than-average concentration.** The $1.28 billion bond offering priced in early 2026 disclosed the risk explicitly; KBRA still upgraded the senior lien to AA, largely on the strength of BNA's 80% origin-and-destination passenger base, which reduces but does not eliminate the exposure. Southwest's operational model shift changes the revenue characteristics of those O&D passengers — not the count, but the economics.

4. **The PFC cap at $4.50 per enplanement, unchanged since 2001, is a political artifact with no near-term path to change.** The 2024 FAA Reauthorization Act (Public Law 118-63) preserved it. Airports have been fighting this cap for two decades. Airlines have consistently treated any increase as a tax on passengers and lobbied accordingly. BNA's New Horizon program is a $3 billion capital program financed 58% by bonds — because the airport has no other mechanism. The PFC cap is the reason the debt service coverage ratio drops from 5.95x in FY2025 to 1.96x in FY2029.

5. **DOT consumer protection rulemakings that would have disciplined carrier behavior — including Southwest's new bag fees — have been systematically unwound.** The Biden-era junk fees disclosure rule was vacated by the Fifth Circuit in January 2025 on procedural grounds; the airline passenger rights proposed rulemaking published December 2024 was withdrawn by the Trump administration in November 2025. Southwest's transformation proceeds in a regulatory environment that has moved decisively in the carrier's favor.

6. **The Tennessee state-federal jurisdictional tension over MNAA board composition created real operational uncertainty during the 2023 capital-planning cycle.** While the old board was reinstated pending litigation, the episode consumed legal bandwidth, distracted executive leadership, and introduced delay into the New Horizon planning sequence. Bond buyers are aware of it; it appears in the risk disclosures.

7. **Southwest's $53 million lounge permit filing at BNA — "The Oasis," 30,000 square feet in the central mezzanine — is the most vivid physical evidence of carrier transformation, and it forces a capital planning question MNAA has not yet publicly answered.** Lounge space is airline-controlled real estate carved from common-use airport terminal square footage. Its presence changes the flow dynamics, the concession economics, and the gate adjacency assumptions that BNA's New Horizon program was designed around.

8. **The FAA's grant assurance framework constrains what MNAA can and cannot do with Southwest's gates if Southwest reduces utilization.** The grant assurances prohibit exclusive use rights in perpetuity; the airline use agreement contains minimum gate activity requirements that allow the airport to recapture underutilized facilities. Whether those recapture provisions survive a negotiated extension — and Southwest will negotiate — is the operative legal question for the 2030 scenario.

## Evidence Section

### I. MNAA Governance: The Board Battle and Its Consequences

The Metropolitan Nashville Airport Authority is a component unit of Metropolitan Nashville and Davidson County government. Its seven-member Board of Commissioners is appointed by the mayor and confirmed by the Metropolitan Council. The board sets policy, approves capital programs, authorizes bond issuances, and — critically — negotiates the airline use agreements that define the financial relationship with carriers.

In 2023, the Tennessee General Assembly passed legislation restructuring the board to give the governor, House speaker, and Senate speaker two appointments each, leaving the Nashville mayor with only two of eight seats. The stated rationale was improving regional governance. The actual impetus was retaliation: Republican lawmakers passed several pieces of legislation targeting Metro Nashville boards after the Metro Council declined to host the 2024 Republican National Convention. [Source: https://tennesseelookout.com/2023/10/31/court-strikes-down-state-law-creating-new-nashville-airport-board/]

A three-judge panel struck the law down in October 2023, ruling it violated Tennessee's Home Rule Amendment, which prevents the legislature from singling out a city without local approval. [Source: https://www.newschannel5.com/news/tennessee-court-of-appeals-rules-nashville-can-pick-its-airport-authority] The Tennessee Court of Appeals affirmed in April 2025. [Source: https://nashvillebanner.com/2025/04/28/tennessee-airport-authority-ruling/] The Tennessee Supreme Court then accepted the state's appeal and has consolidated it with a parallel case about Metro Council size. [Source: https://nashvillebanner.com/2026/02/12/nashville-tennessee-metro-council-size-mnaa/]

This is regulation in the most direct sense — who controls the board determines who approves the capital program, who sets the borrowing strategy, and who decides whether to renegotiate with Southwest. The current board, restored by court order, is the mayor's board. If the Supreme Court reverses, it becomes the state's board. That discontinuity is not hypothetical; it has already happened once.

For a $3 billion capital program dependent on bond market confidence, governance instability is a real cost. KBRA and Fitch upgraded BNA's outlook in 2026 despite noting the litigation. They did so because the O&D traffic base, the non-airline revenue diversification (64% of operating revenue in FY2025), and the unrestricted cash position ($920.9 million) provide a buffer. But the buffer is being consumed: BNA's total debt is $1.8 billion today and will grow materially through 2029 as New Horizon phases deliver. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airports/bna]

### II. The Airline Use Agreement: What Changed and Why It Matters

The previous BNA airline use agreement gave signatory airlines — dominated by Southwest — majority-in-interest veto rights over the full operating budget and capital improvements. Under that structure, Southwest could effectively hold the airport's capital plan hostage to its own network priorities. MNAA spent years trying to exit that structure.

In July 2023, after securing unanimous airline consent to terminate the old deal, MNAA executed a new seven-year agreement running through June 30, 2031. The new agreement made three pivotal changes:

First, it eliminated MII approval rights for terminal capital projects. The airport authority can now pursue terminal improvements — including New Horizon's Concourse A reconstruction and the Central Core enhancement — without airline consent. [Source: https://airportimprovement.com/article/nashville-int-l-establishes-new-7-year-airline-agreement-fosters-facility-expansion-meet-growing-demand/]

Second, it preserved residual-style MII rights for airfield capital, where airlines remain on the hook for cost recovery and retain approval authority. This is the standard split in hybrid compensatory agreements: terminal is compensatory (airport bears risk), airfield is residual (airlines bear risk). At BNA, airfield costs are recovered through landing fees calculated on a residual basis, with parking revenues offsetting the airfield requirement. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airports/bna]

Third, it included minimum gate activity requirements allowing MNAA to recapture underutilized gates. This provision was not present in the old agreement. It is the contractual mechanism by which MNAA could recover Concourse D gates if Southwest were to reduce operations. Whether the threshold is calibrated to Southwest's current operational tempo — or to the historical tempo the airline maintained before the transformation — is a question the public record does not answer.

The terminal rental rates are fixed for the full seven-year term, at $90 to $111 per square foot. Southwest negotiated those rates in mid-2023, precisely as the airline was working through its Elliott-driven transformation planning internally. Fixed rates are good for airlines in inflationary environments. They are less good for airports that need to service growing debt on capital programs that were sized against a different carrier behavior model.

A rate methodology note for the Strategist: DWU Consulting classifies BNA as Hybrid Compensatory. The airfield is residual; the terminal is commercial compensatory with fixed escalation. This is material because it means MNAA cannot use the terminal rate-setting mechanism to pass through rising debt service costs to Southwest through 2031. The airport's financial model depends on growing non-airline revenue — parking, concessions, ground transportation — to cover the gap. That model was built on passenger growth projections. Southwest's transformation does not directly threaten passenger volume at BNA; it does change the composition and spending behavior of those passengers in ways that may affect concession yields.

### III. Federal Regulatory Architecture: FAA, AIP, and the PFC Trap

The Airport Improvement Program provides formula-based entitlement grants to airports based on passenger volume, supplemented by discretionary grants for projects that compete nationally. BNA, as a large hub, has annual AIP entitlements. The New Horizon program funding structure — bonds (58% of remaining costs), federal and state aviation grants, PFC revenue, and other airport funds — relies on AIP grants for a meaningful share of non-debt capital.

The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, 2021) provided supplemental aviation funding above the AIP baseline, including $5 billion for airport terminal development grants. BNA has been an active recipient. The BNA Vision program, completed in February 2024 at a total scope of roughly $1.5 billion over its phases, drew on AIP entitlements and discretionary grants alongside PFC and bond proceeds.

The PFC cap is the load-bearing political artifact. At $4.50 per enplanement, where it has sat since 2000-era legislation, the cap generates approximately $32 million annually for BNA at current passenger volumes. Inflation has eroded the real purchasing power of that revenue by roughly 50% since 2001. The 2024 FAA Reauthorization Act (Public Law 118-63, signed May 16, 2024) did not raise the cap. It made one incremental adjustment: airports charging the maximum $4.50 now return only 60% of their primary AIP entitlement, rather than 75%. That is a marginal improvement, not a structural fix. [Source: https://aviationweek.com/air-transport/airports-networks/aci-na-meeting-dot-push-pfc-increase]

Why does this matter for the Southwest story? Because the PFC cap is the reason BNA is bonding at the level it is. If PFC were $8.00 — a commonly proposed level, and a level the Airport Council International has advocated repeatedly — BNA would generate roughly $64 million per year in self-funded capital. Instead, the airport borrows. The debt service coverage ratio declining from 5.95x to 1.96x through FY2029 is not a sign of financial distress; it is a sign of a capital program that could not be funded any other way under current federal law. [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/nashville-airport-to-offer-1-28-billion-of-bonds]

Airlines — particularly Southwest, historically — have opposed PFC increases at every legislative opportunity. Southwest's model as a low-cost carrier was structurally dependent on minimizing airport costs, including PFC passthrough to passengers. As Southwest pivots toward revenue optimization, its institutional opposition to PFC increases becomes slightly less doctrinally coherent — but it remains organizationally embedded.

FAA grant assurances are the other federal constraint worth naming. Grant Assurance 22 prohibits airports from granting exclusive rights to any aeronautical service provider. This means MNAA cannot give Southwest an exclusive on Concourse D in perpetuity, even if it wanted to. The minimum gate activity provisions in the 2023 airline use agreement are the airport's practical tool for managing underutilization — but the assurance framework establishes the floor below which MNAA cannot go in favoring any single carrier. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/grant_assurances/assurances-airport-sponsors-2025]

### IV. TSA and CBP: Capital Dependencies That Are Not Negotiable

The Transportation Security Administration controls checkpoint design standards, screening equipment procurement, and staffing levels. TSA's FY2025 budget allocates $70.4 million for Checkpoint Property Screening Systems and $11 million for Credential Authentication Technology units. [Source: https://www.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/2024-12/2024_1003_tsa_capital_investment_plan.pdf] CT scanners — which allow 3D bag imaging and eliminate the liquids-out requirement for screened passengers — are being deployed at major airports. BNA's checkpoint reconfiguration under BNA Vision included new central screening infrastructure. TSA equipment deployments are federally funded and federally timed; airports have limited ability to accelerate them.

This matters for Southwest specifically. The carrier's historical competitive advantage included fast turns enabled in part by the efficiency of its homogeneous passenger base — no complex connections, no international passengers, relatively predictable bag counts. As Southwest adds checked bag fees, premium products, and an eventual international code-share, its passenger complexity at BNA will grow. The checkpoint designed for Southwest's 2019 passenger profile may not be optimally calibrated for its 2030 profile.

CBP's Federal Inspection Services infrastructure at BNA received a modern international arrivals facility as part of BNA Vision, completed in October 2023. The facility has six international gates and can process 800 passengers per hour using biometric technology. [Source: https://www.futuretravelexperience.com/2023/10/nashville-international-airport-opens-new-international-arrivals-facility-and-bna-marketplace/] CBP staffing at Nashville remains a constraint on international growth. Southwest's partnership with Icelandair, announced for 2025, represents the carrier's first tentative step into international connectivity. That route operates into CBP-constrained space. Southwest's transformation toward a hybrid model increases the competitive pressure on BNA to grow international capacity — but CBP staffing is federally controlled and cannot be meaningfully accelerated by MNAA.

### V. Congressional Dynamics: What Is and Is Not Movable

The 2024 FAA Reauthorization (Public Law 118-63) passed 88-4 in the Senate and 387-26 in the House — enormous bipartisan margins that obscure significant intra-industry horse-trading. The final bill:

- Preserved the $4.50 PFC cap (airlines won)
- Added 10 new DCA slot exemptions for beyond-perimeter routes (a partial loss for United, a partial win for Delta and regional access advocates) [Source: https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IN12504]
- Included modest terminal development grants above baseline AIP
- Extended the AATF expenditure authority through FY2028

The DCA slot fight is the canonical example of what a policy analyst means by "politically immovable." Every credible economist who has studied the issue agrees that slot controls at DCA create inefficiencies and restrict competition. Congress has granted 40 beyond-perimeter exemptions since 2000, in small tranches, after years of lobbying. The 2024 act added 10 more. The cap itself stays. The pattern will continue indefinitely because the political economy — United's IAD operation, the Virginia delegation, the regional airline coalition — is more durable than any analytical consensus.

The PFC cap follows exactly the same logic. Airlines have a concentrated, organized interest in keeping it low. Airports have a diffuse interest in raising it. Passengers do not show up at markup hearings. The dynamic will not change on the timescale of BNA's New Horizon program.

The Southwest meltdown of December 2022 — 16,900 canceled flights, more than 2 million stranded passengers, a $140 million DOT penalty — produced vigorous congressional hearings and a suite of proposed consumer protection rulemakings. The Biden administration finalized a refund rule and proposed airline passenger rights standards. The Fifth Circuit vacated the junk-fees disclosure rule in January 2025 on procedural grounds. [Source: https://skift.com/2025/01/29/appeals-court-blocks-airline-junk-fee-rule-2/] The Trump administration withdrew the airline passenger rights rulemaking in November 2025. [Source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/11/17/2025-20042/airline-passenger-rights-withdrawal] Southwest's $140 million penalty stands, but the broader consumer protection architecture that might have constrained carrier behavior around ancillary fees did not survive. Southwest's bag fees — implemented May 28, 2025, for tickets purchased on that date forward — are structurally unconstrained by federal rule. [Source: https://www.cnbc.com/2025/05/26/southwest-airlines-checked-bag-fees.html]

### VI. Southwest's Transformation: What Investor Day 2024 Actually Said

Southwest's September 26, 2024 Investor Day — branded "Southwest. Even Better." — was the clearest public statement of the carrier's new commercial architecture. The press release and presentation record document:

- Assigned seating, beginning sales H2 2025, first flights in H1 2026 [Source: https://www.southwestairlinesinvestorrelations.com/news-events/press-releases/detail/30/southwest-airlines-unveils-its-southwest-even-better-transformational-plan-at-investor-day]
- Premium extra-legroom seating ("up to five additional inches of pitch for approximately a third of its seats")
- 24-hour redeye operations beginning February 2025
- International partnerships (Icelandair, 2025)
- Financial target: approximately $4 billion in cumulative incremental EBIT contribution by 2027, ROIC of 15% or greater

Notably, the September 2024 Investor Day presentation preserved the "bags fly free" policy for two checked bags, determining that elimination "would drive down demand and far outweigh any revenue gains." Southwest reversed this position eight months later, announcing bag fees in early 2025 effective May 28, 2025 — a $35/$45 structure at booking with $50+ at the gate. [Source: https://www.cnbc.com/2025/05/26/southwest-airlines-checked-bag-fees.html] The reversal, according to reporting citing the carrier's internal projections, was driven by bag fee revenue projections of $1 billion to $1.5 billion annually against an expected market-share loss of $1.8 billion — a bet that the revenue exceeded the defection.

Elliott Investment Management, which disclosed its initial approximately $2 billion stake in June 2024, drove the board restructuring. Five Elliott-nominated directors joined the Southwest board effective November 1, 2024. Executive Chairman Gary Kelly, the longest-serving custodian of Southwest's institutional identity, departed the same day. [Source: https://www.cnbc.com/2024/10/24/southwest-elliott-near-settlement-which-would-end-proxy-fight-source-says.html]

The lounge program — announced internally, with permit filings appearing publicly in February 2026 for the Nashville location — is the downstream consequence of the board composition change. Southwest is planning between 7 and 20 lounges anchored by a premium Chase credit card carrying a projected $500-$600 annual fee. [Source: https://loyaltylobby.com/2025/12/17/southwest-airlines-working-opening-7-to-20-lounges-with-chase/] Nashville is first or among the first sites, with a 30,000 square-foot permit filing and $53 million fit-out cost in BNA's central mezzanine. [Source: https://www.nashvillepost.com/business/tourism/southwest-american-airlines-plan-new-lounges-at-bna/article_11d691f5-675d-4a2d-a507-6c092c198fc4.html]

### VII. The Political Economy of Nashville and BNA

Nashville's growth has been exceptional by any measure: passenger counts doubled over the decade to 21.9 million at the time Southwest announced its crew base (August 2023), and BNA served 24.8 million passengers in 2025. [Source: https://flynashville.com/news/nashville-international-airport-to-become-new-crew-base-for-southwest-airlines] The airport is completing a $3 billion capital program largely on its own revenue — no local tax dollars — which gives MNAA a governance posture of relative independence from the city's annual budget cycle.

That independence is politically significant. MNAA issues its own revenue bonds, sets its own rates within the airline use agreement, and controls its own capital program. The board composition fight matters precisely because this independent financial structure gives the board real power. A state-appointed board majority would have different instincts about carrier negotiations, about Concourse A design standards, about whether to push Southwest on lounge space terms.

The city's congressional delegation — Senators Bill Hagerty and Marsha Blackburn, Representative Mark Green covering the district — are Republican and aligned with the national airline industry's preferences on PFC and consumer protection. Hagerty serves on the Senate Commerce Committee, which holds jurisdiction over aviation. None of the delegation has been a visible advocate for PFC cap increases. The Tennessee state aviation program provides matching for AIP grants but has no independent capital authority over MNAA.

Noise politics at BNA are less acute than at comparable East Coast airports. The Part 150 noise study most recently updated in 2020 showed a manageable contour, and BNA's runway orientation relative to Nashville's urban core reduces the community exposure that has paralyzed expansion discussions at airports like Boston Logan and New York's Laguardia. MNAA has a Noise Study Advisory Committee with community representation, but there is no organized coalition of the scale that has influenced, say, San Diego or Reagan National operations. This is a relative political advantage for BNA capital planning.

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: The Tennessee Board Takeover (2023–Present)

In March 2023, the Tennessee General Assembly passed a law restructuring MNAA's board, shifting appointment power from the Nashville mayor to a combination of state legislative leaders and the governor. The trigger was explicitly political — retaliation for Nashville's rejection of the 2024 Republican National Convention. The airport, in the middle of finalizing the New Horizon capital program and preparing the July 2023 airline use agreement, spent months operating under legal uncertainty about which board was legitimate.

Metro Nashville sued immediately. A three-judge panel ruled in Nashville's favor in October 2023, reinstating the mayor's board. The state appealed. The appeals court affirmed in April 2025. The state petitioned the Supreme Court, which accepted the case and consolidated it with the Metro Council size dispute. The Tennessee Supreme Court's decision — expected in 2026 — could retroactively unsettle the governance structure under which New Horizon was authorized and the 2023 airline use agreement was executed.

The lesson: political actors in Tennessee are willing to use airport governance as a tool in unrelated political disputes. Any capital commitment MNAA makes carries tail risk proportional to the political temperature between Nashville and the state legislature.

### Case Study 2: The Majority-in-Interest Elimination

For roughly 30 years, MNAA's airline use agreement contained MII provisions giving signatory airlines — in practice, any coalition including Southwest — veto rights over capital projects and the operating budget. The agreement that governed BNA's development from the 1980s through 2022 was, as the Airport Improvement trade publication described it, a structure requiring "28 years of financial control by the airlines."

Eliminating that structure required unanimous airline consent. MNAA secured it in mid-2023 and executed the new seven-year agreement July 1, 2023. The episode illustrates both the durability and the eventual solvability of this type of political-contractual constraint. It took nearly three decades, the leverage of unprecedented passenger growth, and a carrier base that was finally willing to trade MII control for fixed-rate certainty to get it done.

The consequence for the Southwest story: MNAA can now build Concourse A's 16 gates, redesign the Central Core, and expand ground-side infrastructure without Southwest's approval. But it cannot unilaterally change the rental rates or the gate configurations for Concourse D until 2031. Southwest negotiated those terms, knows the rate structure it will pay for the next five years, and has locked in favorable economics precisely as it is investing $53 million in a lounge that will extract value from the concession environment Southwest's gate presence creates.

### Case Study 3: DOT Enforcement and Its Limits — The 2022 Meltdown

Southwest's December 2022 operational collapse provides a concrete data point on what federal enforcement actually accomplishes. The DOT assessed a $140 million civil penalty — 30 times larger than any previous consumer protection penalty in aviation history — and required $90 million in customer vouchers and $600 million in refunds already provided. [Source: https://www.transportation.gov/briefing-room/dot-penalizes-southwest-airlines-140-million-2022-holiday-meltdown]

The meltdown's root cause was the carrier's outdated crew scheduling IT — specifically the SkySolver system, which could not process the cascading crew reassignments a major weather event required. Southwest's COO told the Senate Commerce Committee: "We messed up." [Source: https://www.cnn.com/2023/02/09/business/southwest-meltdown-hearing/index.html]

Two years later, the Trump administration waived $11 million of the remaining penalty balance. [Source: https://www.cnbc.com/2025/12/06/trump-administration-waives-11-million-southwest-fine.html] The Biden-era rulemaking that might have required airlines to maintain minimum IT resilience standards was never completed. The broader consumer protection architecture proposed in response to the meltdown was withdrawn.

The regulatory lesson: even record enforcement actions do not produce structural regulatory constraints on airline operational choices. The penalty was large; the behavioral change is occurring, but driven by Elliott's board activism, not by DOT enforcement. Federal regulators can impose costs; they cannot impose operational models.

## What Can and Cannot Move, and on What Timeline

**Cannot move:**
- The PFC cap at $4.50. Not on any five-year horizon. The 2024 reauthorization cycle just closed. Airlines remain organized on this issue. The next opening is FAA reauthorization in 2028.
- The Tennessee Supreme Court timeline on board governance. The court will decide when it decides.
- The airline use agreement terms through June 30, 2031. Fixed terminal rates are a contract.
- CBP staffing levels at BNA's international arrivals facility. These are federal appropriations, not airport decisions.

**Can move, but slowly and at political cost:**
- MNAA's capital program prioritization within New Horizon. The Concourse A reconstruction (16 gates, completion July 2028) is committed but not fully built. Scope or design adjustments are theoretically possible before construction lock-in.
- Gate recapture from Southwest if minimum activity thresholds are not met. The contractual mechanism exists. Exercising it against the dominant carrier at your airport is a political decision more than a legal one.
- The lounge space negotiation. Southwest is in permit filing stage, not construction. The terms of the lounge lease — rent, term, adjacency, concession revenue sharing — are still negotiable.

**Can move if political conditions shift:**
- AIP discretionary grant allocations, which are influenced by congressional delegation effectiveness and FAA Southern Region relationships. Tennessee's congressional delegation is in the majority; that matters.
- The regulatory posture on airline consumer protection, which shifted substantially between the Biden and Trump administrations and will shift again.

The strategist who assumes political and regulatory conditions are stable through BNA's New Horizon completion date of 2029 is planning for an environment that does not exist.
