# Chief Engineer Brief: BNA Construction Reality and the Southwest Transformation
## Engineering and Constructability Evidence Brief
**Prepared for:** Transform Airports AI Council — Southwest Transformation at BNA Run
**Stage:** 1 of 4 — Primary Research
**Date:** April 21, 2026

## Key Findings

1. **BNA Vision is complete; New Horizon is mid-execution with $855M of committed concourse work still to deliver.** The $4.5B combined program was calibrated to passenger growth, not to any specific carrier behavioral model. The Concourse A reconstruction ($855M, 16 gates, 374,000 sq ft) opens July 2028. The baggage handling system capable of 40M annual passengers completes October 2028. Both were sized around a Southwest that carried 143 passengers on a 737-700 with no checked bag fees. Southwest now flies 175-seat MAX 8s and will generate an estimated $1B annually from bag fees — a 22% increase in passenger volume per gate position, plus a structural shift in baggage throughput demand, against infrastructure that was designed before either change was locked in.

2. **A Southwest lounge at BNA is not a furniture project. It is a 30,000 sq ft structural renovation inside a live terminal, with $53M in fit-out costs and demolition prerequisites.** Permit filings from February 2026 show "The Oasis" requires demolition of the Acme Feed & Seed restaurant space in BNA's central mezzanine before construction can begin. That sequence — tenant exit, demolition, structural assessment, utility rerouting, new MEP, finish build-out — runs 18 to 30 months in a live-operations environment where construction access is governed by the airport's project management plan, limited to off-peak windows, and subject to noise and dust containment protocols that add cost and compress crew productivity. The Delta Sky Club at Atlanta Hartsfield-Jackson offers the baseline: a 20,000 sq ft lounge budgeted at $33M in 2021 reached $92M by 2022 once concourse-related scope was included. At $1,766 per square foot on a modest reading of the BNA fit-out cost ($53M / 30,000 sq ft), Southwest is in the efficient zone — but that number almost certainly excludes the structural and MEP work required to turn a food-and-beverage mezzanine into a climate-controlled, plumbed, high-occupancy lounge with commercial kitchen capacity.

3. **The gate hold room configuration Southwest has operated at Concourse D since 2025 was designed for open seating and high-frequency turns. The airline's move to assigned seating, grouped boarding zones, and premium preboarding areas requires physical reconfiguration of gate podium areas, queuing infrastructure, and signage systems — none of which is trivial inside an 11-gate operating concourse.** FAA AC 150/5360-13A establishes that gate hold room sizing should be based on peak gate occupancy, and the planning standard of 10-13 square feet per waiting passenger (ACRP Report 25, Level of Service B/C) means a 175-seat MAX 8 requires roughly 1,750 to 2,275 square feet of hold room area at peak occupancy — more than Southwest's historical model assumed when those gates were sized for 143-seat 737-700 loads. Southwest has publicly stated it is replacing numbered metal stanchions with dual-lane digital boarding queues at all stations, a process the airline said "could take about two months to complete" system-wide. At BNA specifically, the Concourse D extension opened July 2025 with hold rooms calibrated to prior-generation throughput assumptions. Reconfiguring those rooms — installing new display systems, restructuring the gate podium, rerouting queuing lanes — requires coordination with the airport authority, construction permits, and work window restrictions. It is not a weekend rearrangement.

4. **The Central Ramp Expansion (target: September 30, 2027) is a prerequisite for Concourse A opening, not a standalone project.** Seven remain-overnight and deicing positions are being added. That apron expansion requires FAA-compliant pavement design per AC 150/5370-10H (Standard Specifications for Construction of Airports), pavement sections designed for Boeing 737 and narrowbody equivalent loading, and separation from active taxiway operations during construction. For Southwest, longer ground times — a structural consequence of assigned seating, bag-fee implementation, and a fleet shifting toward 175-seat MAX 8s — means more gate conflicts during peak banks, higher demand for remain-overnight positions, and greater pressure on deicing pad availability in winter operations. The apron was sequenced to support existing Southwest turn practices. If those practices are changing, the apron geometry may be adequate in count but misaligned in the configuration of power, data, and preconditioned air (PCA) hookups that a carrier spending longer at the gate will increasingly need.

5. **The BNA baggage handling system capable of 40M annual passengers (3 miles of conveyor, target completion October 2028) was designed before Southwest began charging for checked bags.** Southwest's own financial disclosures project more than $1B in annual checked bag revenue once the policy fully cycles through its booking curve. Even a conservative behavioral assumption — say, 25-30% of passengers who previously carried on now check bags — materially increases the bag-per-departure count flowing through BNA's outbound screening and make-up systems. The BHS design assumed a certain proportion of checked bags per enplanement based on historical Southwest load data. That assumption is now wrong. The question is whether the margin exists within the system's designed throughput to absorb a structural increase in checked bag volume without queue backup or conveyor saturation, particularly during peak banks where Southwest generates 190 departures on a peak day at BNA. There is no publicly available analysis confirming the BHS sizing accounts for the post-bag-fee baggage mix. MNAA should be asking its systems integrator for a load case that applies the new bag rate to peak-hour departure counts before the October 2028 commissioning date.

6. **Concurrent major programs at a single operating airport multiply interface risk in ways that sequential programs do not.** BNA is running at least four substantial construction programs simultaneously inside an operating terminal envelope through 2028: the Central Core Enhancement ($40M, 18 months, June 2026–December 2027), the Central Ramp Expansion (2027), the Concourse A Reconstruction ($855M, ongoing), and the CONRAC program ($900M, Messer Sundt, completion 2029). Add Southwest's Oasis lounge fit-out in the central mezzanine (permit filings February 2026), and American Airlines' lounge build-out in new Concourse A (construction scheduled 2027). Every one of these programs shares infrastructure — utility tunnels, HVAC distribution systems, the central utility plant completed under BNA Vision, the vertical circulation core being overhauled by the Central Core Enhancement. The Central Core Enhancement alone, escalating from 6 to 16 escalators in a live terminal, will require gate closures, passenger detour routing, and phased operational impacts through December 2027 — exactly the window when Southwest's Oasis lounge is trying to pull construction crews through the same mezzanine level.

7. **The Concourse A demolition and reconstruction creates a governance and operational risk that BNA's program structure must explicitly address.** Hensel Phelps holds the CM at Risk contract on Concourse A. The delivery method is appropriate for this scope — but the DEN Great Hall failure offers a direct cautionary data point: DIA terminated a $1.8B DBFOM contract after one year when owner decision-making authority was unclear, the design change process broke down, and $288M in projected overruns accumulated before the contract was severed. The DEN after-action report concluded the airport lacked "a standard process to select the best project delivery method for its construction projects and ensure consistent management of complex projects." MNAA's program is better structured — CM at Risk versus a P3 DBFOM — but the interface between the Concourse A reconstruction, the simultaneous BHS installation, and the Central Core Enhancement still creates a multi-prime coordination problem. BNA's program management office needs a single point of authority for utility routing conflicts, operational impact decisions, and change-order adjudication that does not run through three separate project teams.

## Evidence Section

### BNA New Horizon Program Structure

BNA Vision (2017–February 2024) delivered the expanded terminal lobby, central checkpoint, additional gates, international arrivals facility, and three parking garages, at a program total that contributed to the $4.5B combined investment across both programs. [Source: https://bnavisionnashville.com/] [Source: https://flynashville.com/bna-new-horizon]

New Horizon, the successor program, carries a $3B headline and targets 40M annual passengers by 2029. Its committed projects include:

- **Concourse D Extension** (completed July 8, 2025): 5 gates added (D1–D11 now exclusive Southwest), 160,000+ sq ft, $229.6M contract value per Hensel Phelps, Progressive Design-Build delivery. [Source: https://www.henselphelps.com/project/bna-concourse-d-extension/]
- **Central Core Enhancement** ($40M, 18 months, June 2026–December 2027): escalators increased from 6 to 16, additional elevator, vertical circulation upgrades. [Source: https://hoodline.com/2026/03/bna-braces-for-18-month-shake-up-as-40-million-overhaul-takes-off/]
- **Central Ramp Expansion** (target September 30, 2027): 7 remain-overnight and deicing positions, prerequisite for Concourse A.
- **Concourse A Reconstruction** (target July 11, 2028): full demolition of 6-gate concourse, reconstruction to 16 gates, 374,000 sq ft, $855M. CM at Risk via Hensel Phelps, designed by Fentress Architects, LEED Silver. Includes dual ADG III taxi lanes with apron expansion. [Source: https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/hensel-phelps-awarded-concourse-a-reconstruction-project-at-nashville-international-airport-302301774.html]
- **Baggage Handling System** (target October 2028): 3 miles of conveyor, capacity for 40M annual passengers.
- **CONRAC** ($900M, Messer Sundt JV, target 2029): 4,700-vehicle rental car complex, 3,000-space garage.

[Source: https://flynashville.com/bna-new-horizon] [Source: https://www.sundt.com/2026/messer-sundt-begins-work-on-900-conrac-program/]

### Southwest's Physical Footprint at BNA

Concourse D is exclusive Southwest territory: 11 gates (D1–D11), with the July 2025 extension adding 5 gates (previously 6). Southwest also operates from gates in Concourse C — C4, C7, C9, C16, C18, C19, C20, C21, C22, and C25, some of which require a shuttle bus connection from the main terminal. Southwest accounts for 54% of BNA traffic and 60% of seat capacity; 190 peak-day departures represent the carrier's highest frequency at any airport. [Source: https://upgradedpoints.com/travel/airports/nashville-international-bna-airport/] [Source: https://aeronauticsmagazine.com/news/from-no-frills-to-first-class-waiting-rooms-southwest-airlines-eyes-five-airport-lounges-in-premium-overhaul]

### The Southwest Oasis Lounge at BNA

Permit filings from February 2026 detail a 30,000-square-foot lounge in BNA's central mezzanine at a fit-out cost of $53 million. The space requires demolition of the existing Acme Feed & Seed restaurant. Southwest's lease for the Honolulu lounge (12,241 sq ft, $20M minimum improvement, ~$1.91M annual rent) provides a comparable data point for the financial structure of these deals. [Source: https://aeronauticsmagazine.com/news/from-no-frills-to-first-class-waiting-rooms-southwest-airlines-eyes-five-airport-lounges-in-premium-overhaul] [Source: https://hoodline.com/2026/04/southwest-drops-53m-on-swanky-new-bna-lounge-for-music-city-flyers/]

### Industry Lounge Construction Benchmarks

At $1,400–$1,800 per square foot for a modestly appointed US lounge, Southwest's stated $53M for 30,000 sq ft ($1,766/sq ft) is at the upper edge of modest but below premium-grade. The Delta Sky Club expansion at Seattle-Tacoma (21,000 sq ft, part of a $126M airport-sponsored project) illustrates how lounge square footage understates total project cost when concourse structural work is involved. The Atlanta Delta Sky Club (20,000 sq ft) went from $33M estimated (2021) to $92M actual (2022) once concourse scope was integrated — a 179% cost increase within 12 months. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/why-airport-lounges-are-big-business-an-inside-look-at-costs-and-profits-from-100m-construction-to-credit-card-deals/]

### Construction in a Live Terminal: The Engineering Reality

Swinerton, which has delivered over $200M in lounge projects across 50 airports, characterizes lounge construction inside operating concourses as "performing surgery mid-flight." The firm's project documentation identifies ceiling congestion, evolving design standards, and coordination with airport operations as the three persistent schedule drivers. Air conditioning supply, plumbing for food-and-beverage service, fire suppression zoning, and egress path analysis all require work in ceiling plenums that are shared with active terminal mechanical systems. [Source: https://swinerton.com/elevating-travel-the-art-and-challenge-of-building-luxury-airport-lounges/]

### Baggage System: The Checked-Bag Demand Shift

Southwest implemented a first-checked-bag fee ($35, then raised to $45 effective April 2026; second bag $55). The carrier projects more than $1B in annual EBIT from checked bag revenue once the policy cycles through its booking curve. [Source: https://aerospaceglobalnews.com/news/southwest-airlines-checked-bag-fee-billion-dollar/] The behavioral consequence — a structural increase in checked bag volume per departure — has direct implications for the outbound BHS make-up system and the TSA Checked Baggage Inspection System (CBIS). The GAO has documented that only 36% of larger US airports have complete optimal inline CBIS systems because they require significant airport infrastructure modification; installing or upgrading these systems is not incidental to a BHS expansion. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-12-266] Seattle's analogous BHS replacement program ran to just over $1B across three phases (Phase 1: Q1 2017–Q2 2020; Phase 2: Q3 2020–Q3 2024; Phase 3: Q1 2025–Q4 2029), underscoring that these systems are not built in a single commissioning event. [Source: https://www.portseattle.org/projects/baggage-handling-system-optimization]

### Fleet Upgauging and Gate Throughput

Southwest has shifted its fleet composition from approximately 65% 737-700s (143 seats) to roughly 65% 737-800/MAX 8 aircraft (175 seats) — a 22.4% increase in passengers per gate position. [Source: https://airinsight.com/the-rising-turn-time-impact-at-southwest-airlines/] The carrier's current average turn time is approximately 49 minutes system-wide, with a stated goal of reducing that figure by 5 minutes through technology (RIDS — Ramp Information Display Systems) and process. [Source: https://crankyflier.com/2024/10/03/southwest-is-reducing-turn-times-dont-panic-yet/] The turn-time pressure runs in the opposite direction from the carrier's other initiatives: assigned seating, premium preboarding, checked baggage processing, and a lounge-using premium segment all structurally lengthen gate occupancy relative to the open-seating, carry-on-everything model the BNA gates were originally designed around.

### Gate Infrastructure: FAA Design Standards

FAA AC 150/5360-13A (Airport Terminal Planning) provides the governing framework for gate hold room sizing, concourse width, and passenger boarding bridge configuration. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/documentLibrary/media/Advisory_Circular/AC-150-5360-13A-Airport-Terminal-Planning.pdf] ACRP Report 25 (Airport Passenger Terminal Planning and Design) establishes Level of Service B/C hold room planning factors at 10–13 square feet per waiting passenger. For a 175-seat aircraft at full load, that implies 1,750–2,275 sq ft of hold room space at peak occupancy. The Concourse D extension gates opened in July 2025 — before Southwest's assigned seating rollout was complete and before the new boarding queue infrastructure was deployed. Whether those hold rooms were sized to the old or new throughput model is a question the program record should answer.

### Dallas Love Field: The Comparable Program

Southwest controls 90% of gates at Dallas Love Field. The city's $1B terminal renovation program — construction beginning 2027, running approximately six years to 2033 — pushes terminal walls outward by 50 feet along the full concourse length to create room for the airport's first passenger lounges, wider hold rooms, and premium adjacencies. Southwest is expected to fund most of the project. [Source: https://viewfromthewing.com/1-billion-transformation-means-southwest-airlines-dallas-love-field-fortress-will-get-lounges-for-the-first-time/] [Source: https://airlinegeeks.com/2025/10/02/dallas-love-field-expansion-includes-plans-for-lounges/] This is the structural analog to the BNA question: when a carrier's operational model changes after a terminal was designed around that model, the gap is filled by a capital program, and it runs in years and hundreds of millions of dollars, not months and tenant improvement allowances.

### Construction Cost Escalation

Airport construction costs increased 38.3% between 2014 and 2025, against CPI growth of 31.7% — a persistent 6.6 percentage point gap driven by labor scarcity in heavy construction trades, consolidated supplier chains for specialized materials, and concurrent federal infrastructure demand. The IIJA's supplemental aviation funding expires September 30, 2026; after that, the baseline Airport Improvement Program reverts to approximately $4B annually, against which large hub airports receive discretionary grants of $20–100M. BNA's New Horizon program is mid-execution precisely at the transition from abundant federal supplemental funding to constrained baseline-only support. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-construction-cost-iija-funding]

### Megaproject Failure: The Denver Cautionary Case

DIA terminated its $1.8B Great Hall Partnership in August 2019, one year into construction, after projected overruns reached $288M and schedule delays exceeded two years. The DEN after-action report is direct: "The Great Hall project may have been better served as a design-bid-build or construction manager/general contractor approach." The root causes were unclear decision-making authority, airport leadership inexperienced with long-term contract management, and insufficient oversight of contractor procurement. [Source: https://www.flydenver.com/app/uploads/2024/06/greathall_AfterActionReport-2.pdf] BNA's CM at Risk structure for Concourse A is a more defensible delivery model — but the interface risk between Hensel Phelps (Concourse A), the BHS systems integrator, and the Central Core Enhancement contractor is a governance problem of the same species, if not the same magnitude.

## Engineer's Assessment: What the Strategist Needs to Confront

The central tension in the BNA–Southwest thesis is not financial. It is physical. Terminal buildings are not software; they do not update when the carrier's business model changes. The Concourse D extension that opened in July 2025 — 11 gates, $229.6M, designed and built for Southwest's open-seating, high-frequency, low-bag-volume operation — is now the fixed infrastructure against which Southwest is deploying assigned seating, premium boarding zones, checked bag processing, and a 30,000 sq ft lounge in the adjacent mezzanine.

None of those changes are trivial to accommodate inside a concourse that opened nine months ago. The boarding queue infrastructure is being replaced system-wide. The lounge requires structural demolition. The baggage system being built to serve those gates was designed before the carrier decided to charge for bags. The apron positions added by the Central Ramp Expansion were sized around remain-overnight counts from a carrier that turned planes faster than any competitor and kept gate occupancy deliberately short.

What has changed is not Southwest's presence at BNA. Southwest will continue to dominate Concourse D. What has changed is the time a Southwest aircraft spends at the gate, the amount of baggage flowing through the make-up system, the space a Southwest passenger expects to occupy before boarding, and the physical amenities that Southwest is now contractually committed to delivering inside that terminal. The BNA capital program was built around a carrier model that no longer fully exists. The question is not whether MNAA needs to respond — it does — but whether the remaining discretionary tranches of New Horizon are being re-evaluated against the new model before they are committed, or after.

The answer to that question is not available in any public document. That is the most important finding in this brief.
