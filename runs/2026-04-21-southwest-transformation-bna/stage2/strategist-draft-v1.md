# The Airport Southwest Was, The Airport Southwest Is Becoming
## Nashville's Capital Program and a Carrier in Mid-Transformation

**Stage 2 — Strategist Draft v1**
**Run:** Southwest Transformation at BNA
**Date:** April 21, 2026

---

## I. Gate D6, 6:14 a.m.

On a Tuesday in April 2026, Gate D6 on Concourse D at Nashville International Airport was running a turn. The flight, WN 1442 to Baltimore, had blocked in at 5:47. Scheduled departure was 6:20. The 737 MAX 8 carried 175 seats, all sold, and the first passengers were forming up in what used to be the A-group corral and is now the Group 1 lane — Business Select, then Preferred, then A-List Preferred, then Extra Legroom, then A-List, then Rapid Rewards cardholders, then Groups 7 and 8, the last of those being the passengers who bought Basic fares that no longer change, no longer refund, and whose seat will be whatever the computer assigned them at check-in the night before. Eight groups, where for fifty-four years there were three.

A ramp lead checked a tablet. A gate agent in the new uniform was working through a scan sequence that the carrier's training material estimates at two seconds per boarding pass; the old sequence, under open seating, ran closer to one. The aircraft's forward bin was already full. It was full at Group 2. Above the cabin, two rows of passengers who had not yet boarded were going to try to stow carry-ons above rows they did not sit in, walk forward against the boarding stream, and produce the bin-stow congestion that the Transport Workers Union had filed a formal grievance about two months earlier. [Technology Scout brief, Key Findings]

The flight left at 6:31. Eleven minutes late. Ground time from block-in to pushback, 44 minutes.

Two decades earlier, a turn like this would have taken twenty. The Southwest Airlines that broke even in 1972 by inventing the ten-minute turn — Bill Franklin's operational miracle, three planes flying a four-plane schedule through Texas — did so with a single fleet type, open seating, two free bags, one cabin, and a boarding process so mechanical that the gate agent barely spoke. [Historian brief, Evidence Section; Historian brief, Key Findings 1] By 2024, the carrier's system average turn had risen to 49 minutes. [Operations brief, Key Findings 1; Historian brief, Quote 2] The goal now is 44 minutes — a five-minute reclamation that Southwest's own planners say would be worth the equivalent of sixteen additional aircraft's worth of flying. [Operations brief, Key Findings 8; Contrarian brief, Evidence] The goal sits one level above the legacy carriers and three levels above Spirit.

Three floors up from Gate D6, at the central mezzanine, the Acme Feed & Seed restaurant was still open that morning. It will not be open by 2027. Its lease is ending. The 30,000 square feet it occupies, above the lobby, will be demolished to accommodate a 30,000-square-foot Southwest lounge called The Oasis, fit-out cost $53 million, permit filed February 2026, the largest single lounge in Southwest's corporate pipeline. [Engineer brief, Key Findings 2; Economist brief, Key Findings 5; Airline brief, Key Findings 3] The lounge is carrier-funded. It sits inside airport-controlled real estate. It is being built for a Chase co-branded credit card projected at $500–$600 per year, competing directly with the Delta Reserve card's $650 and United Club's Infinite tier. [Airline brief, Evidence; Regulatory brief, Evidence] It is, in concrete and drywall, the physical evidence that the airline whose name still hangs above Concourse D is not the airline that designed Concourse D.

This essay argues three things. First, Southwest is completing a decade-compressed transformation from the highest-productivity point-to-point carrier in U.S. aviation history to a hybrid revenue-optimization carrier, and the transformation is structurally irreversible, not cosmetic. Second, Nashville International Airport's capital program — $4.5 billion committed since 2017, with roughly half of New Horizon still ahead of the concrete truck — was designed against the Southwest that existed when ground broke, not against the Southwest that will be flying in 2030. Third, the gap between those two airlines is narrow enough to be invisible from a glance at the gate chart, and wide enough that MNAA's next three capital decisions should be pressure-tested against the new model before they are committed, not after.

The argument is not that Southwest is leaving. It is not. The argument is that Southwest is staying, and that what "staying" means has changed.

## II. Executive Summary

1. **Southwest's transformation has moved past the point of reversal.** Between September 2024 and January 2026, the carrier eliminated open seating, introduced checked-bag fees (now $45/$55), launched basic economy, joined major OTAs for the first time in nineteen years, began redeye flying, and committed to premium extra-legroom retrofits on one-third of its fleet. Each of these changes carries sunk capital, contractual commitment, or FAA-certified modification. None can be walked back without material cost. [Airline brief, Key Findings 1]

2. **Southwest's presence at BNA is deepening even as its operating character changes.** The carrier controls 54% of total BNA traffic and 60% of seat capacity, opened its 12th crew base at BNA in May 2024, operates up to 166 daily departures rising to a planned 215 by fall 2026, and has designated Nashville as one of four coordinated connection banks alongside Midway, Denver, and St. Louis. [Airline brief, Key Findings 3; Airline brief, Key Findings 4; Contrarian brief, Evidence] This is the opposite of a departure signal. It is also the opposite of the high-frequency, quick-turn, point-to-point relationship that Concourse D was designed around.

3. **The operational math of the transformation runs against gate productivity in the short term.** Southwest's own 2006 internal testing showed assigned seating adds 1–4 minutes of boarding time. [Operations brief, Key Findings 3] A 3-minute average increase across 166 daily Concourse D departures consumes roughly eight gate-hours per day. [Operations brief, Key Findings 1] The fleet is simultaneously upgauging from 143-seat 737-700s to 175-seat MAX 8s, a 22% increase in passengers per gate cycle on infrastructure sized before the shift. [Engineer brief, Key Findings 1; Engineer brief, Evidence]

4. **BNA's cost per enplanement is projected to rise from $8.49 in FY2024 to $17.48 by FY2032 — a 106% increase driven almost entirely by debt service on New Horizon.** [Economist brief, Key Findings 4; Economist brief, Quotable 2] Southwest's January 2025 letter to MNAA asking the Authority to "keep cost increases to a reasonable level" is the revealed-preference data point that matters. [Airline brief, Evidence] Carriers send letters like that 18 months before they begin diverting growth.

5. **Senior debt service coverage is projected to fall from 19.11x in FY2024 to 1.80x in FY2030, against a covenant floor of 1.25x.** [Economist brief, Key Findings 3] A 55-basis-point margin above covenant at the trough is not crisis. It is also not cushion. It is a capital program operating on the assumption that the carrier that pays 36% of airport operating revenue will continue to do so at the same intensity and the same growth rate through the full debt amortization curve.

6. **The airline use agreement executed in July 2023 eliminated Southwest's majority-in-interest veto over terminal capital — a genuine generational win for MNAA — but locked terminal rental rates through June 30, 2031.** [Regulatory brief, Key Findings 2] Southwest negotiated those rates during the same quarter it was finalizing its Investor Day transformation internally. The airport gained independence over what to build. The airline gained certainty over what it would pay. Both sides got what they wanted. One side knew more about what was coming.

7. **The historical analogies are imperfect and the strongest warning is the US Airways / America West case, not Pittsburgh or Cincinnati.** Pittsburgh and CVG are dehubbing stories; Southwest is not dehubbing Nashville. The US Airways–America West transition from 2005 through 2013 was a gradual LCC-to-legacy drift that neither the carrier's management nor its airport partners fully recognized until it was complete. [Historian brief, Key Findings 2; Historian brief, Case 3] Phoenix adjusted, but not without friction. The BNA-Southwest friction is now visible in a single permit filing.

8. **The uncommitted tranches of New Horizon — specifically the Concourse A reconstruction (16 gates, $855M, July 2028), the baggage handling system expansion (40M passengers, October 2028), and the central ramp expansion (7 RON and deicing positions, September 2027) — were scoped before the carrier's transformation.** [Engineer brief, Key Findings 4; Engineer brief, Real-World Cost Examples] Whether their design assumptions still hold against a Southwest that turns aircraft in 49 minutes, charges for bags, stocks a lounge, and schedules connecting banks is a question the public record does not answer. That is the single most important finding of this analysis.

## III. The Argument

### a. The 54-year policy that ended with commemorative bag tags

On January 26, 2026, Southwest flight WN 1971 departed Honolulu for Los Angeles. The flight number was not random. [Historian brief, Quote 1] The gate agents applauded. A flight attendant told CNBC after landing that she was "so happy she wanted to cry" because having customers roam the aisle looking for seats had been stressful for crew. WN 1971 was the last Southwest flight to operate with open seating.

Fifty-four years of a policy every competitor had tried to replicate and failed, ended with applause.

What those bag tags commemorated was more than a boarding system. The 10-minute turn, invented in 1972 by Bill Franklin and a ground crew that actually achieved 22 minutes on a schedule designed for four aircraft flown by three, was not a trick. [Historian brief, Evidence] It was a cost structure. Every piece of Southwest's commercial model — the single aircraft type, the two free bags, the point-to-point network without banks, the open seating — was a mechanical component of a machine designed to generate more flying hours per aircraft per day than any competitor could match. The model built thirty-five consecutive profitable years through deregulation, September 11, and the Great Recession. [Historian brief, Key Findings 1]

The machine began to fail under cost pressure that ground time alone could not absorb. Full-year 2024 CASM-X grew 7.8% against a full-year operating margin of 1.2% — $321 million in operating income on $27.5 billion in revenue. [Airline brief, Key Findings 2; Airline brief, Evidence] Delta posted 10% that year. United cleared 8%. In June 2024, Elliott Management disclosed a roughly $2 billion stake and demanded board change. By October, Elliott had five directors on the Southwest board. The long-serving Executive Chairman Gary Kelly — the carrier's institutional memory — left the same day. [Regulatory brief, Evidence] Twelve weeks later, the September 2024 Investor Day announced the transformation.

The transformation's defensive punctuation was not an accident. "Southwest. Even Better." is a sentence you write when the answer to "what changed?" is "everything structural, but we need you to feel nothing has." [Airline brief, Evidence] Assigned seating beginning H2 2025, operating H1 2026. Premium extra-legroom seating across a third of the fleet. Redeye flying. International codeshare, beginning with Icelandair at BWI. A $500 million cost reduction program. A target of $4 billion in cumulative incremental EBIT by 2027 and 15%-plus after-tax ROIC. [Airline brief, Evidence]

In February 2025, Southwest conducted its first mass layoff in 53 years — 1,750 corporate positions, 15% of the corporate workforce. [Airline brief, Evidence] That number carries a cultural weight that CASM calculations cannot express. Nine months later, COO Andrew Watterson, explaining bag fees on the same day the carrier announced them, said five words that would have been unthinkable at any Investor Day before 2024: "We need more revenue to cover our costs." [Airline brief, Quote 2]

### b. What Concourse D was built for

BNA Vision — the predecessor program to New Horizon — launched in 2017 with a $1.2 billion budget and delivered in February 2024 at $1.5 billion, a 25% escalation that the Authority has not publicly characterized as overrun. [Economist brief, Key Findings 6; Economist brief, Evidence] The program delivered a new terminal lobby, an international arrivals facility, an expanded central checkpoint, three parking garages, an on-site hotel, and — the centerpiece of the Southwest story — a purpose-built six-gate concourse for the carrier, Concourse D, opened to a Southwest flight to Denver from Gate D1.

Concourse D was designed around Southwest's 2017 operating model. Eleven gates now, after the July 2025 extension added five more at $229.6 million under a Hensel Phelps progressive design-build contract. [Engineer brief, Real-World Cost Examples; Economist brief, Evidence] Every planning assumption embedded in that concrete — the hold room sizing, the gate podium configuration, the absence of lounge space, the minimal premium-queue infrastructure, the baggage-system sizing downstream — reflected the Southwest that was arriving in 2017. That carrier turned aircraft in 25 minutes, carried 143 passengers on a 737-700 with no checked bag fees, boarded in three groups with no assigned seats, and needed no lounge because Southwest had no lounges.

By the time Concourse D's extension opened in July 2025, that Southwest was already gone. The carrier had announced Investor Day in September 2024. It had announced bag fees in March 2025, effective May 28. It had started redeye operations in February 2025. It had sold its first assigned-seat ticket on July 29, 2025 — three weeks after the extension's ribbon cutting. The gates opened to a carrier whose fleet composition was already roughly 65% MAX 8s at 175 seats, a 22% increase in passenger density per turn. [Engineer brief, Key Findings 1; Engineer brief, Quote 3] ACRP Report 25's Level of Service B/C hold room planning factor of 10–13 square feet per waiting passenger means a fully boarded MAX 8 requires 1,750 to 2,275 square feet of hold room at peak occupancy — more than a 737-700 required under the prior fleet mix. [Engineer brief, Evidence] Whether the new extension's hold rooms were sized to the old or the new throughput model is a question no public document answers.

### c. The Oasis, and what it costs to carve a lounge out of a mezzanine

The permit filing for The Oasis arrived in February 2026. Thirty thousand square feet, central mezzanine, fit-out cost $53 million, demolition of the space currently occupied above and around the Acme Feed & Seed restaurant. [Engineer brief, Evidence; Airline brief, Quote 4] At $1,766 per square foot, Southwest's stated fit-out cost sits at the upper edge of modest airport lounges and below the premium-grade line — except that a lounge inside a live terminal mezzanine is almost never fit-out only.

The precedent to hold up against The Oasis is the Delta Sky Club at Atlanta: 20,000 square feet, budgeted at $33 million in 2021, actual $92 million by 2022. [Engineer brief, Evidence] A 179% cost increase within a year. The increase did not come from furniture. It came from the concourse-related scope — the structural, mechanical, and utility work required when you turn a food-and-beverage mezzanine into a climate-controlled, plumbed, high-occupancy lounge with a commercial kitchen, fire suppression zoning that matches its new occupancy classification, and an egress path calculation that redoes the entire floor's crowd dynamics.

Swinerton, which has delivered over $200 million in lounge projects at fifty airports, describes this work as "performing surgery mid-flight." [Engineer brief, Evidence] Ceiling plenums shared with active mechanical systems. Air conditioning supply that has to be rerouted above passengers walking below. Plumbing that has to be tapped into a building that did not plan for it. Every one of those conflicts has to be negotiated against BNA's simultaneous Central Core Enhancement — a $40 million, 18-month overhaul running June 2026 through December 2027 that expands escalator count from 6 to 16 across the same vertical circulation core the lounge will depend on. [Engineer brief, Evidence; Economist brief, Evidence]

The Oasis is one of five or more lounges in Southwest's pipeline: Honolulu (12,241 square feet, $20 million minimum improvement, $1.91M annual rent), Nashville, Denver, Austin, and Dallas Love Field, with reporting suggesting the internal target is 7 to 20 stations. [Airline brief, Evidence; Engineer brief, Evidence] Nashville is the largest. It is, in every sense that matters to a carrier's strategic planning, the proof of concept. If The Oasis works — if it drives Chase co-brand card acquisition at the rate Southwest's internal models require, if it generates the lounge-access revenue that funds the $500–$600 annual fee card's economics — then Nashville demonstrates the template for the rest of the network. If it fails, Nashville absorbs a $53 million write-off and Southwest reconsiders the pipeline.

The airport benefits either way from the capital investment. But only the success scenario produces the strategic depth that MNAA's capital program's revenue assumptions are calibrated against. A failed lounge in the central mezzanine is a vacancy at the geographic heart of the terminal, above the lobby, through which every connecting passenger passes.

### d. The banking hub Southwest will not call a hub

In August 2025, the Cranky Flier identified Nashville as one of four "coordinating banks" in Southwest's restructured schedule, alongside Chicago Midway, Denver, and St. Louis. Each runs four connecting banks daily, two morning and two evening. [Airline brief, Key Findings 4; Airline brief, Evidence] Nashville converted its evening arrivals bank to redeye service, extending the carrier's connecting reach into the Pacific Northwest. Seattle–Nashville redeye is a Pacific Time connection that Southwest's network could not offer when the carrier had no redeyes at all.

Southwest does not use the word "hub." The silence matters. A carrier that uses the word opens itself to majority-in-interest arguments, to slot-like regulatory scrutiny, to different labor contract provisions, and to the political baggage that hub status carries. A carrier that builds hub economics without declaring hub status preserves optionality. Southwest has been doing this at Nashville for years. The crew base — the 12th in the network, opened May 2024, housing 650 to 1,300 pilots and flight attendants — is hub-scale infrastructure. [Airline brief, Evidence; Contrarian brief, Evidence] Crew bases are not temporary installations. They require training facilities, scheduling infrastructure, domicile hotel arrangements, and union agreements that take years to unwind.

But a banking hub, even one that is not called one, needs what a point-to-point operation does not. Precise gate scheduling during bank peaks. Lounge space for connecting passengers with layovers in the two- to four-hour range, which is where the revenue premium lives. Premium amenities that justify a connecting fare the passenger could otherwise get nonstop from Atlanta or Charlotte. Concourse geometry that minimizes connection walk times. [Airline brief, Key Findings 8] And, increasingly, remain-overnight parking positions for the redeye aircraft that arrive in the morning and depart the same evening.

Concourse D was not designed for any of that. Its gate geometry is linear — a single corridor optimized for quick in-and-out turns, not for connecting passengers moving between gates with checked bags and overhead rollerboards. There is no lounge in its original design. There is no premium-lane queuing infrastructure at the gate podium. The remain-overnight positions being added under the Central Ramp Expansion (seven positions, targeted September 2027) were scoped before Southwest's redeye network's overnight aircraft counts were set. [Engineer brief, Key Findings 4]

The Contrarian brief's strongest point — that a banking structure will make Southwest a more intensive user of BNA facilities, not a lighter one — is correct. [Contrarian brief, Key Findings 4] The question is whether the facility was designed for intensive use of this particular kind.

### e. What bag fees do to a conveyor belt designed for free bags

Southwest's first-checked-bag fee went into effect May 28, 2025, at $35. It rose to $45 later the same year, and the second-bag fee to $55. [Airline brief, Evidence; Engineer brief, Key Findings 5] The carrier's own pre-announcement modeling projected $1 to $1.5 billion in annual revenue upside against a potential $1.8 billion market share loss — a range that made the decision genuinely uncertain at the analytical level. [Airline brief, Evidence] Southwest moved forward anyway. By Q2 2025 earnings, the carrier was projecting $1 billion in annualized bag-fee revenue at full run-rate.

BNA's New Horizon baggage handling system expansion — three miles of conveyor, capacity for 40 million annual passengers, target completion October 2028 — was scoped when Southwest carried two bags free. [Engineer brief, Key Findings 5; Technology brief, Key Findings] The design assumed a certain proportion of checked bags per enplanement, derived from historical Southwest load data. That assumption is now wrong in both directions.

The bag-fee regime is pushing some passengers toward carry-on, which creates overhead-bin pressure at the gate and slows boarding — the phenomenon the TWU grievance documented, and the phenomenon that Southwest is retrofitting seven of every ten aircraft with 50%-larger bins to absorb by end of 2026. [Technology brief, Evidence] Simultaneously, the premium segment Southwest is building — Extra Legroom seats, Preferred seats, and eventually a true first-class cabin — includes priority checked-bag handling as a product feature. Priority bags require tag-recognition capability, dedicated sortation lanes, and first-bag delivery guarantees that put loading pressure at particular points in the system.

The GAO has documented that only 36% of larger U.S. airports have complete optimal inline Checked Baggage Inspection Systems, in part because these systems require significant infrastructure modification. [Engineer brief, Evidence] Seattle's analogous BHS optimization program ran to just over $1 billion across three phases from 2017 to 2029, replacing ten miles of conveyor. [Engineer brief, Real-World Cost Examples] BNA's three-mile system is simpler, but it is not being built in a single event either. The commissioning is scheduled for October 2028, against a bag mix that the carrier changed eighteen months before design finalization.

No publicly available analysis confirms that BNA's BHS sizing was re-run against the post-bag-fee mix. [Engineer brief, Key Findings 5, UNVERIFIED tag] That is the single most specific stress test MNAA should ask its systems integrator to perform before the system is commissioned.

### f. The cost per enplanement trajectory that Southwest is already modeling

In January 2025, Southwest's finance team sent MNAA a letter. It asked the Authority to review operations and maintenance expenses and keep cost increases "reasonable," citing debt service obligations on new capital facilities. [Airline brief, Key Findings 5; Airline brief, Evidence] That letter is the revealed-preference data point that matters most in this analysis. An airline cutting 15% of its corporate workforce and writing to airports about O&M restraint is an airline whose finance department is already modeling airport cost against the carrier's own cost-reduction program.

The numbers behind the letter: BNA's cost per enplanement was $8.49 in FY2024, $9.41 in FY2025, and is projected to reach $10.91 in FY2026 — a 29% increase in two years. [Economist brief, Quotable 1; Airline brief, Quote 5] The Authority's own rate-of-return and cost study projects the trajectory continuing to $17.48 by FY2032 — a 106% increase over eight years, driven almost entirely by debt service on New Horizon. [Economist brief, Key Findings 4; Economist brief, Quotable 2]

A CPE of $17.48 is not JFK ($36) and it is not LAX ($30). It is, however, a doubling of what Southwest was paying at BNA in the year it added the crew base. The airline use agreement that locks terminal rental rates through June 30, 2031 means the pass-through mechanism through the contract term is bounded. [Regulatory brief, Evidence] After 2031, the airport has to renegotiate, and the leverage at that table will depend on how much growth Southwest has been willing to absorb in the CPE curve, and how much it has been willing to divert.

The senior bond debt service coverage trajectory — 19.11x in FY2024, 5.95x in FY2025, 1.96x in FY2029, 1.80x in FY2030 — reflects a capital program operating at 55 basis points above its covenant floor at the trough. [Economist brief, Key Findings 3; Economist brief, Quotable 1] Rating agencies accepted it. KBRA upgraded the senior lien to AA in December 2025. Fitch revised to positive outlook the same month. The upgrades were predicated on 80% origin-and-destination traffic, 64% of operating revenue coming from non-airline sources, and $920.9 million in unrestricted cash. [Regulatory brief, Evidence] These are real strengths. They are not the same as saying the capital program is robust to a Southwest that slows its growth rate at Nashville by even 15%.

### g. The airline use agreement that both sides got what they wanted from

In July 2023, MNAA and its signatory carriers executed a new seven-year airline use agreement running through June 30, 2031. The agreement did three things that matter for this story.

First, it eliminated the majority-in-interest veto over terminal capital projects. [Regulatory brief, Key Findings 2; Regulatory brief, Evidence] For thirty years, signatory airlines — which in practical terms meant Southwest — had held veto rights over capital improvements. The old structure was described by Airport Improvement as "28 years of financial control by the airlines." MNAA spent years trying to exit it. The authority can now build without airline consent on terminal projects. That is a genuine generational win, and the fact that the New Horizon program is proceeding at scale is the direct consequence of it.

Second, the agreement fixed terminal rental rates at $90 to $111 per square foot for the full seven-year term. [Regulatory brief, Evidence] Fixed rates in an inflationary environment are a carrier's friend. They are less friendly to an airport whose debt service on the same square footage is rising.

Third, the agreement included minimum gate activity requirements that allow MNAA to recapture underutilized gates. [Regulatory brief, Key Findings 8] This is the legal mechanism by which the airport could reclaim Concourse D space if Southwest reduced operations. The contractual tool exists. Exercising it against the carrier that employs 1,300 people in Nashville, pays 36% of airport operating revenue, and is simultaneously investing $53 million in a lounge is a political decision, not a legal one.

The timing of the negotiation matters. Southwest signed the agreement in July 2023 — the same quarter Bob Jordan was publicly insisting, as recently as July 2024, that "bags fly free" would never change. [Regulatory brief, Evidence] Elliott disclosed in June 2024. Investor Day was September 2024. Bag fees were announced in March 2025 and effective May 28, 2025. The airline that negotiated fixed rental rates in mid-2023 was not yet the airline that has emerged. Both sides signed the agreement they wanted. One side knew more about what was coming.

### h. Governance that is not quite settled

There is one more element of the architecture that a capital planner cannot assume away. In March 2023, the Tennessee General Assembly passed a law restructuring MNAA's board to give the governor, House speaker, and Senate speaker two appointments each, leaving Nashville's mayor with two of eight seats. [Regulatory brief, Key Findings 1; Regulatory brief, Case 1] The trigger was political — retaliation for Metro Nashville's decision not to host the 2024 Republican National Convention. A three-judge panel struck the law down in October 2023 under the state's Home Rule Amendment. The Court of Appeals affirmed in April 2025. The Tennessee Supreme Court accepted the state's appeal and consolidated it with a parallel case on Metro Council size. A ruling is expected in 2026.

For a $3 billion capital program dependent on bond market confidence, the shadow governance risk is a real cost. Rating agencies have noted it in their disclosures. The buffer exists. It is being consumed. A state-appointed board majority, should the Supreme Court reverse, would have different instincts about carrier negotiations, about Concourse A design, about whether to push Southwest on lounge space terms. [Regulatory brief, Evidence] The board that executed the July 2023 use agreement may not be the board that negotiates its successor.

## IV. The Counter-Case, Honestly Presented

A capital planner reading this essay who is inclined to resist its thesis has reasons that deserve full statement before rebuttal. The Contrarian brief makes the strongest version of that resistance. It deserves to be rendered in its own terms.

**Southwest is adding, not subtracting.** The carrier will operate 190 peak-day departures from BNA in spring 2026 — the highest in its history at any single airport — rising to 215 by fall 2026. [Contrarian brief, Evidence] It added the crew base in May 2024. It filed lounge permits in February 2026. It designated Nashville as a connecting node. None of this is the behavior of a carrier preparing to divert growth. A strategist reading the January 2025 cost-restraint letter as an early warning is reading it selectively against other signals that point in the opposite direction. Airlines do not commit $53 million to lounge fit-outs at airports they are preparing to retrench from. If anything, the operational changes Southwest is making — banking, redeyes, connecting schedules — suggest the airline will use BNA more intensively, not less. [Contrarian brief, Key Findings 4]

**BNA built to demand, not to carrier specification.** This is the deepest version of the counter-case and the one that requires the most serious engagement. BNA Vision was scaled against FAA passenger forecasts. [Contrarian brief, Key Findings 2] Nashville's metropolitan population grew at nearly triple the national rate for a decade. Enplanements grew 34.9% from 2019 to 2024, reaching approximately 12.5 million in FY2025 against a total passenger count of 24.8 million. [Historian brief, Evidence; Economist brief, Evidence] The airport doubled its traffic in ten years. None of that growth was a function of open seating. The New Horizon program's target of 40 million passengers by the mid-2030s is a demand projection, not a carrier-behavior projection. The right response to uncertainty about a carrier's operating model is to build to demand, which is what BNA has done — and which is exactly the textbook lesson from every airport that has survived a carrier identity shift intact.

**The historical analogies the thesis reaches for are the wrong ones.** Pittsburgh lost a billion-dollar terminal designed around US Airways' connecting hub when US Airways walked away in bankruptcy. [Historian brief, Case 1; Contrarian brief, Evidence] Cincinnati lost Delta in 2013 and watched enplanements fall from 22.7 million to under 6 million, a 74% decline. [Historian brief, Case 2] Memphis lost Delta, was left with 83 gates for a 23-gate operation, and had to demolish two concourses to right-size. [Contrarian brief, Evidence] Every one of these cases is a dehubbing story — a connecting function withdrawn. Southwest is not withdrawing any function from BNA. The analogy is rhetorically powerful and analytically wrong, and using it against BNA's capital program produces a recommendation shaped by the wrong precedent.

**The operational changes are being actively offset.** Southwest's turn-time target — 44 minutes, a 5-minute improvement — is not happening by accident. The carrier is deploying RIDS (Ramp Information Display Systems), tablet-based crews, paperless turns, and predictive baggage-load reporting, all aimed at faster ground operations, not slower ones. [Contrarian brief, Key Findings 3] The equivalent of sixteen additional aircraft's worth of flying is being generated through process, not through concrete. If Southwest recovers the five minutes, the net operational change at Concourse D may be smaller than a naive reading of assigned-seat boarding impacts would suggest.

**Airport capital plans should not be built around carrier product strategy in any case.** The Contrarian brief's meta-argument is the hardest to dismiss. Airport capital programs take five to fifteen years to conceive, permit, finance, and build. Carrier product strategies change on the timescale of investor day presentations. Southwest executives were publicly insisting in July 2024 that bags-fly-free would not change. [Contrarian brief, The Meta-Argument] Nine months later, bag fees were announced. A capital program that recalibrated around Southwest's September 2024 Investor Day would have been wrong by March 2025. The correct response to carrier volatility is to build demand-robust infrastructure, not carrier-specific infrastructure. Build for the passengers of a growing Nashville. The carriers who serve them will adjust to the facility. They always have.

**Southwest's fundamental cost advantage is single-fleet simplicity, and it is not changing.** The 737 discipline remains. The pilot training pipeline. The maintenance inventory. The scheduling architecture. None of these are on the table. Southwest's 2025 RASM of 15.59 cents, with Q1 2026 guidance of at least 9.5% growth year-over-year, is the validation signal. [Contrarian brief, Evidence] The stock rose 23% in early 2026 as the transformation hit its initial revenue targets. A carrier whose revenue architecture is working does not require a capital program to be rebuilt around it. The strategist who argues otherwise is forecasting airline management decisions, which is not what airports are built to serve.

**The Tennessee governance fight is real but bounded.** It has already been resolved once in Nashville's favor by a three-judge panel and again by the Court of Appeals. [Regulatory brief, Case 1] The Supreme Court may reverse, but even if it does, the fundamental capital decisions have already been made. The board that approved New Horizon's committed projects is the board whose decisions stand. Building future decisions around a hypothetical state-board majority is planning for a world that may never arrive.

Taken together, these arguments are coherent. They are grounded in evidence. They point to a defensible conclusion: BNA has built to demand, Southwest is embedding rather than exiting, the operational changes are being actively offset, and the historical analogies the thesis relies upon are the wrong ones. If the counter-case is correct, MNAA's capital program requires no structural rethink — only ordinary project-level diligence, which is what the program is already doing.

## V. Why the Counter-Case Is Insufficient

The counter-case is strong on three of its claims. It is right that Southwest is adding, not subtracting. It is right that BNA's demand projections are sound. It is right that airport capital plans should not be rebuilt every time a carrier's investor day produces a new press release. These concessions are not rhetorical. They are central to any responsible reading of the situation.

What the counter-case does not address — cannot address — is the specific mismatch between a carrier's operational character and the infrastructure built around its prior character, at an airport where that carrier's concentration is higher than any comparable U.S. large hub can match. This is where the thesis holds.

**First, the demand-versus-carrier-specification distinction collapses when the carrier is the demand.** The Contrarian brief's strongest argument — BNA built to demand, not to Southwest specification — is correct in its premise and incomplete in its conclusion. At 54% of enplanements and 60% of seat capacity, Southwest is not one carrier among several serving BNA's demand. Southwest is the mechanism through which more than half of BNA's demand reaches the airport. [Airline brief, Evidence; Economist brief, Key Findings 2] When the carrier's operating character changes, the demand it delivers changes too — not in count, but in the economics of its handling. A passenger who checks a bag, uses a lounge, and expects an assigned seat is not the same infrastructure customer as a passenger who carries everything on, boards in order of arrival, and leaves in 25 minutes. The demand forecast stays. The airport's physical response to that demand does not.

Pittsburgh's failure was not that its demand forecast was wrong — by 2010, Pittsburgh's O&D demand had stabilized. [Historian brief, Case 1] Pittsburgh's failure was that its terminal had been optimized for connecting flows that had disappeared, and retrofitting an X-shaped airside designed for gate-to-gate transfer to serve a 97% O&D market was catastrophically expensive. The failure mode at BNA is the inverse and subtler: a terminal designed for high-frequency point-to-point flows will now handle a banking connecting overlay, longer dwell times, premium queue differentiation, lounge-bound passengers, and redeye RON positions. The geometry is not catastrophically wrong. It is inconveniently under-specified.

**Second, "Southwest is adding" is not the same as "Southwest is adding the same thing." ** The 190 peak-day departures rising to 215 are real. They are also the growth vector the Contrarian brief gives the most weight to, and they obscure the compositional shift. More departures on longer turns with more bags, more premium passengers, and redeye RON aircraft is not the same gate loading as more departures on 25-minute turns with no bags. [Operations brief, Key Findings 8] A 3-minute average increase in gate dwell across 166 daily departures consumes approximately eight gate-hours per day. [Operations brief, Key Findings 1] If Southwest reaches 215 departures without reducing the new dwell times, the gate loading at Concourse D will exceed what the extension was sized to support at peak. Eleven gates cycling 215 departures requires 19.5 departures per gate per day, which at 45-minute turns consumes almost all of the 16-hour operational day in gate occupancy alone.

The math is not impossible. The math is narrow. And the narrower it gets, the more each one of Southwest's initiatives — lounge space, premium queuing, priority bag handling — has to fit inside the existing concrete rather than require new concrete that the program has not funded.

**Third, the turn-time recovery argument depends on Southwest winning both halves of a bet it may only half win.** The sixteen-phantom-aircraft figure is real. [Operations brief, Key Findings 1; Contrarian brief, Evidence] It is also a best case. Southwest's own 2006 internal testing — 200 flights out of San Diego, repeated in San Antonio — demonstrated that assigned seating increases boarding time by 1 to 4 minutes. [Operations brief, Key Findings 3; Operations brief, Evidence] The carrier's 49-minute current average is the floor against which the 5-minute improvement must be measured, and the assigned-seat rollout is one of several changes pushing in the opposite direction simultaneously. The TWU grievance about overhead bin crowding is not a one-airport anomaly. [Technology brief, Evidence] The bag-fee-induced carry-on surge is not a transition artifact that will resolve. The eight-group boarding sequence is a structural change, not a procedural one.

If Southwest recovers the full 5 minutes, gate productivity at Concourse D roughly holds. If it recovers only two minutes, it does not. The infrastructure the airport is building has to work under both scenarios. The 2028 BHS, the 2027 central ramp, and the 2028 Concourse A are being committed against a range of carrier outcomes that the planning record does not yet make explicit.

**Fourth, the historical analogies that fit are not the ones the Contrarian brief or the thesis relies upon.** Both the Pittsburgh case and the CVG case are genuinely imperfect — the dehubbing mechanism does not apply. [Historian brief, Case 1; Historian brief, Case 2] The US Airways / America West case is closer and harder. [Historian brief, Case 3] America West's LCC DNA survived three years of nominal identity change before gradually eroding under the weight of network-carrier cost structure, Star Alliance membership, and integrated labor contracts. By 2009, the carrier was operating more like a traditional network airline than like America West. The transformation was never formally announced. It was visible in retrospect, not in the moment. Phoenix Sky Harbor adjusted. It did so without acute crisis because no single airport had a 54% dependency on America West.

The closest precedent in kind, not in scale, is what Midway absorbed as Southwest grew from small regional carrier to dominant tenant in the 1980s and 1990s. [Historian brief, Note on Limits of Analogy] Midway retrofitted to a direction it could plan for. BNA's challenge is retrofitting to a direction still being determined by Southwest's own management.

**Fifth, the counter-case treats the capital program's tranches as if they were equally committed, and they are not.** The Concourse D extension is open. The BNA Vision program is complete. These are sunk costs; no amount of rethinking unbuilds them. What remains discretionary is the Concourse A reconstruction ($855 million, 16 gates, July 2028) in its detailed design scope; the baggage handling system ($3 miles of conveyor, October 2028) in its specification for priority bag handling; the central ramp expansion (seven positions, September 2027) in its hookup configuration for power, data, and preconditioned air; and the Central Core Enhancement ($40 million, December 2027) in its passenger flow assumptions. [Engineer brief, Real-World Cost Examples; Economist brief, Evidence] Each of these has a design-lock window that has not closed. Each can be stress-tested against the post-transformation Southwest before it is committed. The question MNAA faces is not whether to rebuild the program. It is whether the remaining tranches still make sense, project by project, against the carrier that will actually be flying through them.

**Sixth, the January 2025 cost-restraint letter is not a threat, but it is a signal that deserves more weight than the Contrarian brief gives it.** Carriers that are not under financial pressure do not send cost-restraint letters. [Airline brief, Key Findings 5] Carriers whose finance departments are modeling airport CPE against their own cost-reduction programs do. Southwest is modeling Nashville against its own program. The CPE curve is rising into a range where the airline's finance team will begin finding reasons to divert marginal growth elsewhere, or to push harder in the 2031 use agreement renegotiation than it otherwise would. The use agreement runs to June 30, 2031. [Regulatory brief, Evidence] The peak debt service coverage stress is FY2030. [Economist brief, Key Findings 3] These two dates are not a coincidence.

**Seventh, the counter-case's meta-argument — airport capital should not be built around carrier product strategy — is correct in principle and insufficient in application.** The strategist who accepts this argument without reservation concludes that no capital planning adjustment is warranted. The strategist who takes it seriously concludes that capital planning must be demand-robust AND carrier-resilient — meaning the facility works under the widest plausible range of carrier behaviors, including ones the carrier itself has not yet fully articulated. Demand-robust alone is not sufficient when one carrier produces more than half the demand and is mid-transformation on the operational side of the ledger. The infrastructure must be demand-robust, and it must be robust to the specific carrier behaviors the dominant tenant is most likely to exhibit across the capital program's amortization curve.

The final concession is this: the counter-case is right that the physical capital already committed — BNA Vision, the Concourse D extension — is sound. The demand forecast that justified it has been validated. The concrete is poured and the gates are open. The counter-case is wrong that no stress test of the remaining tranches is warranted. The Concourse A design can still be adjusted. The BHS specification can still be stress-tested against the post-bag-fee mix. The central ramp's configuration can still be tuned for the RON and premium-turnaround requirements Southwest's new operating model will exhibit. Each of these is a decision MNAA can still make deliberately rather than by default.

The thesis does not require the counter-case to be wrong. The thesis requires only that the counter-case is an insufficient basis for committing the remaining tranches without stress-testing them. And it is.

## VI. Implications for MNAA

The question MNAA's board is actually being asked to decide over the next eighteen months is not whether Southwest will stay at BNA. It almost certainly will. The question is what to commit next, and against what assumptions.

Three decisions carry load-bearing weight.

**Concourse A reconstruction design lock.** Hensel Phelps holds the CM-at-Risk contract on Concourse A: 16 gates, 374,000 square feet, $855 million, LEED Silver, target July 2028. [Engineer brief, Real-World Cost Examples; Economist brief, Evidence] The design has not been fully locked. The gate hold room sizing, the gate podium configuration, the premium queuing lane geometry, the lounge adjacencies, the baggage claim configuration — each of these can still be adjusted within the contract. American Airlines is already committed to lounge space in new Concourse A; its scheduled 2027 build-out is already in planning. [Engineer brief, Key Findings 6] The question is whether the Southwest-usable portions of Concourse A — the gates that may receive Southwest overflow as its Concourse D operation approaches saturation under the new dwell times — are sized to the old 737-700 / open-seating model or to the MAX 8 / assigned-seating model. The ACRP 25 hold room factor of 10–13 square feet per waiting passenger produces a different answer under each assumption. [Engineer brief, Evidence] MNAA's program management office should request, in writing, the carrier-mix throughput case against which each Concourse A gate is being sized.

**Baggage handling system specification.** The BHS commissioning is October 2028. The detailed design basis is not October 2028; it is earlier, and it is closing. [Engineer brief, Key Findings 5; Technology brief, Evidence] The load case that should be explicit in the program record is: what is the peak-hour bag count through the outbound sortation system under a bag mix reflecting (a) $45/$55 checked-bag fees, (b) a 25–30% passenger shift from carry-on to check, (c) Southwest's projected premium-cabin rollout with priority-tag requirements, and (d) the 215 peak-day departure schedule? This is a specific request. It is not research. It is a load case the systems integrator should have in its model before commissioning. If the load case has not been re-run since the May 2025 bag-fee rollout, it needs to be, and the margin in the system's designed throughput needs to be published in the Authority's capital record.

**Central ramp expansion's hookup configuration.** Seven remain-overnight and deicing positions are being added under the Central Ramp Expansion, target September 30, 2027. [Engineer brief, Key Findings 4; Economist brief, Evidence] The apron is FAA-compliant by AC 150/5370-10H; that is not in question. What is in question is the hookup configuration at each position — power, data, preconditioned air, ground-power units. [Engineer brief, Key Findings 4] A carrier whose redeye fleet now sits overnight at BNA, whose premium passengers expect cabin-conditioned aircraft before boarding, and whose gate-hold and turn-preparation processes run longer than they used to, has a different hookup density per position than a carrier that turned aircraft quickly and kept overnight counts to a minimum. The difference between a minimum-specification position and a premium-turnaround-ready position is, at industry standard costs, five to ten percent of the per-position build. That delta is negligible against the carrier's emerging requirements. Locking the minimum specification into a concrete pour that will last forty years is not.

A fourth consideration is not a capital decision but a negotiating position.

**The 2031 use agreement renegotiation.** The current airline use agreement runs through June 30, 2031. [Regulatory brief, Key Findings 2; Regulatory brief, Evidence] Southwest's crew base commitment, its $53 million lounge investment, its 215 peak-day departure schedule, and its connecting-bank designation at BNA are contractually reinforced lock-ins. The airport's negotiating position in 2030 will depend on how Concourse A's construction has landed, how Southwest's CPE trajectory compares to peer airports the carrier has added capacity to, and whether the lounge — by then two years into operation — is meeting Southwest's own traffic and revenue targets. The airport should start positioning for that renegotiation now. Not in 2030. The lounge negotiations already underway are the leading edge of it. Lease term, concession revenue share, adjacency requirements, and minimum activity thresholds for the lounge space itself should be calibrated to what MNAA will want in 2031, not to what the airport will accept in 2026 to move the project forward.

One further note on the Tennessee governance fight. A state Supreme Court ruling in 2026 that reverses the lower courts and transfers board appointment authority would not unwind the commitments already made, but it would reshape the carrier negotiation dynamic in ways that should be factored into the 2031 preparation. [Regulatory brief, Case 1] Any significant extension of Southwest's lounge lease, or any significant adjustment to the Concourse A design that affects Southwest-adjacent gates, should be executed under the current governance structure rather than deferred into the uncertainty window.

The question MNAA's planners should ask at their next capital planning meeting is not "will Southwest still be here in 2030?" That question has an easy answer. The question to ask is: "What does each remaining New Horizon tranche look like if Southwest's 2030 operation runs 49-minute turns on 175-seat aircraft, banks four connections daily at Nashville, sends half its connecting passengers through a 30,000-square-foot lounge above the lobby, and charges $55 for a checked bag?" Every element of that sentence is a fact already established by Southwest's own filings and operating record. None of them requires a forecast. The question is whether the airport being built for 2028, 2029, and beyond was designed with that sentence as an assumption, or whether it was designed before the sentence existed.

## VII.

A few hundred yards north of Gate D6, across the apron, the concrete for the Concourse A reconstruction will be poured starting this summer. Sixteen gates, three-quarters of a million square feet, $855 million. The design review that governs its gate hold rooms, its premium queuing lanes, its lounge adjacencies, and its baggage infrastructure is closing on decisions that will live in that concrete for forty years. A Southwest MAX 8 will arrive at one of those gates in 2029. It will carry 175 passengers. They will board in eight groups. Some of them will have come from a lounge upstairs. Many of them will have checked a bag. The aircraft will sit at the gate for forty-five or fifty minutes and then push back for Phoenix.

The question is not whether that gate will work. The question is whether it was designed knowing what that gate would be asked to do.
