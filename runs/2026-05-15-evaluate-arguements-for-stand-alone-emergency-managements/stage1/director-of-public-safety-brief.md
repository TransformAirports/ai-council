# Director of Public Safety Brief
## Stage 1 Research — Organizational Placement of Emergency Management at MWAA

**Run:** evaluate-arguements-for-stand-alone-emergency-managements
**Author lens:** Director of Public Safety, MWAA — sworn officer background, 25-year command career, personally accountable for the gap between compliance and operational competence
**Date:** 2026-05-15

---

## Key Findings

1. **Part 139's AEP is not a public safety document.** 14 CFR 139.325 requires airport operators to maintain an emergency plan covering 18 distinct emergency categories — including natural disasters, power failures, water rescue, bomb threats, and hazmat incidents — most of which have no fire or police lead. The regulation assigns ownership to the airport operator (the Authority), not to any department. Treating the AEP as a PSD deliverable is an interpretation driven by tradition, not by the regulation itself. [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-G/part-139/subpart-D/section-139.325]

2. **The 3-minute ARFF response mandate and PSAP operations consume PSD command capacity continuously.** 14 CFR 139.319 requires the first ARFF vehicle to reach the midpoint of the farthest runway within 3 minutes of alarm, with all other required vehicles at 4 minutes. Sustaining that standard across two Index E airport systems — with vehicle maintenance documentation, agent inventory, live-fire drills, and unannounced FAA inspections — is a full-time operational discipline. The PSCC runs 24/7 as a non-traditional PSAP for both airports and the Dulles Toll Road. When PSD command is consumed by ARFF compliance reviews, TSA 1542 security program management, patrol operations, and labor relations, emergency planning is not the function that gets the attention it needs. It is the function that waits. [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/139.319]

3. **Fort Lauderdale 2017 demonstrated what happens when the EM coordination function is not independent.** Five killed on January 6, 2017. The after-action report found that Unified Command was never established. At least three separate incident command posts were operational simultaneously — Broward Sheriff's Office, FBI, and Fire & Rescue — with no communications among them and no connection to the County EOC. An estimated 2,600 law enforcement officers descended on FLL with no designated staging area and no one coordinating their deployment. The primary lesson FLL's own airport director identified: resource coordination failure. That is not a fire or police problem. That is an emergency management problem. And it happened in a jurisdiction where law enforcement dominated the response structure. [Source: https://www.policinginstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/Ft.-Lauderdale-Hollywood-International-Airport-AAR.pdf]

4. **The organizations doing EM best at airports have already decoupled it from public safety.** Orlando International (MCO) became the first airport in the world to earn EMAP accreditation in 2020 — the gold standard for EM program quality. Their emergency management function reports through the VP of Airport Operations, not through public safety. Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta (ATL) — the world's busiest airport — has EM under the Senior Deputy General Manager of Operations. Los Angeles World Airports (LAX) has EM embedded in an Operations & Emergency Management division. MWAA is an outlier among major hub airport authorities in keeping EM under the public safety VP with no independent organizational authority. [Sources: https://airportimprovement.com/article/orlando-international-airport-mco-receives-emergency-management-accreditation/; https://www.atl.com/about-atl/leadership/]

5. **EMAP accreditation requires exactly the governance structure the current MWAA model cannot satisfy.** The EMAP Emergency Management Standard (ANSI/EMAP EMS 5-2022) evaluates programs against 73 standards across 16 categories. Cross-functional governance, stakeholder engagement across all organizational units, and organizational authority to coordinate preparedness activities enterprise-wide are foundational requirements. When EM reports through PSD, it cannot compel IT, Finance, Legal, or Engineering to participate in COOP planning, exercises, or after-action corrective actions. The authority gap is structural. MCO got to EMAP accreditation under an Operations VP. It is not a coincidence. [Source: https://emap.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/EMAP-EMS-5-2022-Emergency-Management-Standard.pdf]

6. **The thesis accurately identifies mission misalignment, but undersells how deep the conflict runs operationally.** PSD command during a major incident is tactically consumed — managing the scene, coordinating mutual aid responders, handling the media perimeter, supervising the investigation handoff. The ICS structure places PSD in operational and tactical command. The EM coordinator's job during that same incident is to run the EOC: tracking resources, liaising with state emergency management, managing COOP activation for affected terminals, and briefing the executive team. These two functions are incompatible within a single chain of command when the incident is live. You cannot be the incident commander and the EOC director at the same time. Trying to run both functions from one chain of command is the structural error.

7. **The strongest counter-argument is relationship risk during transition, not organizational theory.** The legitimate argument for keeping EM under PSD is that the operational relationship between PSD command and the EM coordinator must be close and trusted — especially during the first 30 minutes of an incident before the EOC is fully stood up. A standalone EM function that loses that operational closeness, or that develops a bureaucratic adversarial relationship with PSD, is worse than the current model. The argument for separation wins only if the transition preserves — and in fact formalizes — that operational interface. This is a design problem, not a philosophical one.

## Evidence

### Federal Regulatory Framework

**14 CFR 139.325 — Airport Emergency Plan.** The regulation requires every Part 139 certificate holder to develop and maintain an AEP covering a minimum set of emergency types: aircraft accidents, bomb threats, structural fires, natural disasters, water rescue, hazmat incidents, sabotage and hijacking, and unlawful interference with operations, among others. The plan must include procedures for each emergency type, a communications network, and sufficient detail for every person responsible for implementation. The regulation assigns the obligation to the "certificate holder" — the airport operating authority — not to any department within it. FAA CertAlert 24-09 (October 2024) reinforced this, directing airport operators to review their AEPs with "all appropriate responding personnel" and to engage TSA for security-related threats — language that presupposes enterprise-wide coordination, not a single department's ownership. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/airport_safety/certalerts/part_139_certalert_24_09]

FAA Advisory Circular 150/5200-31C (the operative guidance document for AEP development) explicitly incorporates NIMS and ICS, calls for involvement of local communities, state organizations, and federal agencies, and provides guidance on multi-agency coordination structures. The AC addresses an AEP that is "functionally oriented, comprehensive in the assignment of responsibilities, and coordinated at all levels." That language does not describe a document PSD owns and operates. It describes an enterprise coordination function. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/resources/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.current/documentNumber/150_5200-31]

**14 CFR 139.319 — ARFF Operational Requirements.** The 3-minute response standard is non-negotiable and requires continuous documentation. ARFF vehicles must reach the midpoint of the farthest runway within 3 minutes; all other required vehicles within 4 minutes. Compliance testing, vehicle maintenance records, agent inventory, and annual live-fire drills are all subject to unannounced FAA inspection. This is a genuine, operationally demanding compliance function — and it lives in PSD. The bandwidth this consumes at the VP and command level is real and material.

### EMAP Accreditation Standard

The ANSI/EMAP Emergency Management Standard 5-2022 establishes 73 standards across 16 assessment areas. Compliance requires demonstrated cross-functional governance — the EM program must show it has organizational authority to engage and coordinate all departments, not just public safety. Orlando MCO is the world's only EMAP-accredited airport. Their path to accreditation began in 2017 and achieved first approval in 2020. MCO's EM function reports through the VP of Airport Operations. The decision to place EM outside of public safety was part of the structural work required to demonstrate EMAP compliance. [Source: https://www.aviationpros.com/aviation-security/press-release/55283457/orlando-international-airport-achieves-prestigious-emergency-management-accreditation]

### Peer Airport Organizational Models

**Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta (ATL):** Emergency Management is a discrete function under the Senior Deputy General Manager of Operations, who leads airport operations, public safety, law enforcement, fire/medical, maintenance, and transportation as an integrated operations portfolio. EM does not report through the public safety function — it is a peer function within operations. [Source: https://www.atl.com/about-atl/leadership/]

**Los Angeles World Airports (LAX):** Emergency Management is integrated into an "Operations & Emergency Management" division. The function is positioned as an operational leadership responsibility alongside airside, landside, and terminal operations — not under a public safety chain of command. [Source: https://www.lawa.org/groups-and-divisions/operations-and-emergency-management]

**MWAA:** The Office of Public Safety under VP Bryan Norwood encompasses police, fire/rescue, and the Public Safety Communications Center. Emergency management is not listed as an independent function on the MWAA public-facing organizational structure. It is de facto embedded within the fire and police operational structure. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/policefireems]

### Fort Lauderdale After-Action Record

The January 6, 2017 shooting at FLL is the most operationally instructive case in recent US airport public safety history — not because the tactical response was poor, but because the coordination function failed completely. Three command posts with no communication. No unified command. No EOC-to-scene interface. The airport's own director said resource coordination was his first concern. The post-incident analysis recommended following NIMS for all responders and building a stronger EOC capacity with trained emergency managers. FLL subsequently invested in an upgraded EOC, new communications protocols, and a six-tier workforce training program with emergency management at the apex. [Source: https://airportimprovement.com/article/fort-lauderdale-int-l-shares-lessons-learned-2017-active-shooter-tragedy]

The lesson is not that police performed poorly. The lesson is that emergency management — the coordination function, not the tactical function — was absent. And it was absent in a structure where law enforcement owned the response paradigm.

### ICS Integration and Command Jurisdiction Research

A 2019 Aviation Pros analysis of practical ICS integration for airport operators identified a recurring failure mode: "agencies who may have specific jurisdiction or command responsibilities outside of the fence line, may operate in a different command structure within the fence line, potentially creating conflicts." The analysis noted that success requires management buy-in at the executive level and clear role distinction between policy-level support (EM/EOC) and operational command (PSD). [Source: https://www.aviationpros.com/airport-business/airport-infrastructure-operations/article/53061092/practical-ics-integration-for-airport-operators]

The ACRP research literature identifies the same issue. Airport Emergency Planning research (PMC/ACRP) notes that EOC functions "do not typically assume command over the on-scene IC," and that cross-functional coordination across ten core emergency management functions — including airport operations and maintenance, resource management, emergency public information, and health and medical — requires organizational authority that PSD does not have over most of those functions. [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7170197/]

## The Public Safety Case For and Against the Thesis

### The Case For (The Honest Assessment from Someone Who Would Lose the Function)

I will be direct: keeping emergency management under the public safety VP is not a best practice. It is a habit. The regulatory history of EM at airports tracks exactly as the thesis describes — EM evolved out of civil defense and fire department "other duties as assigned," and most airports formalized whatever was already happening rather than designing a structure for purpose. MWAA's current model reflects that history, not a deliberate governance decision.

The operational reality in my command is that ARFF compliance, TSA 1542 security programming, PSAP operations, and patrol staffing across two major airports and a toll road consume my command team's capacity continuously. Emergency planning — COOP, enterprise exercises, hazard mitigation planning, recovery frameworks — is the work that gets scheduled in the gaps between operational demands. Those gaps are narrow and unpredictable. When we have a major incident at IAD, the EM planning calendar stops. That is not a personnel problem. It is a structural problem.

The functions EM needs to influence to be effective — IT resilience, financial continuity, human capital planning, Engineering's critical systems — do not report through me. When my EM coordinator tries to drive COOP participation from those departments, the answer is "we'll get to it." When an executive-level EM function calls a mandatory cross-departmental exercise, the answer is "we'll get to it" until the CEO's office makes a phone call. Organizational authority matters. EM embedded in PSD has functional scope it cannot compel.

Finally, and most directly: during a major live incident, I am the incident command structure. My attention is on the tactical and operational picture. I cannot simultaneously run the EOC's strategic function, manage media, brief the CEO, and maintain operational command of 200 responders from multiple agencies. Those roles need to be separated by organizational structure, not improvised under pressure.

### The Case Against (Arguments the Strategist Needs to Address)

The counter-arguments are not frivolous, and the thesis needs to defeat them specifically rather than dismissing them.

**Argument 1: Operational continuity.** During the first 30 minutes of any serious incident, the interface between PSD command and the EM coordinator is the most critical link in the response chain. Whoever activates the EOC and whoever commands the scene need a pre-established, trusted relationship and clear activation protocols. A standalone EM function that develops a bureaucratic distance from PSD, or that competes with PSD for authority in the early phase of an incident, is worse than the status quo. This is a design problem the thesis must solve — not just assert away.

**Argument 2: Resource efficiency.** At the staff level, EM and PSD functions overlap. The person who builds the AEP is also the person who trains police and fire in emergency procedures. A standalone function needs dedicated headcount, a dedicated budget, and a dedicated leadership position. At a resource-constrained authority, this is not a trivial ask. The thesis needs to quantify the ask rather than leave it as an aspirational org chart.

**Argument 3: The "small incident" problem.** The vast majority of EM activations at an airport are incidents that PSD owns entirely — a gate fire, a medical emergency that requires partial terminal evacuation, a minor fuel spill. For these events, the EM coordination function adds process overhead without adding value. The case for standalone EM rests on the tail of the distribution — the complex, multi-department, multi-agency events that require genuine enterprise coordination. The thesis needs to acknowledge this and argue that getting the tail right is worth the overhead for the routine.

**Argument 4: The Tennessee example is weaker than presented.** The thesis cites Tennessee TEMA as an example of EM being legislated as a standalone function. The actual record is more complicated — as of 2025, TEMA is still organizationally housed within the Tennessee Military Department and faces a statutory sunset review. The legislature has not completed a clean separation. Using TEMA as a clean precedent is a weak citation that a skeptical executive will check and find wanting. Remove it or replace it with a cleaner example.

## 5 Specific Incident References, Certification Facts, and Operational Realities for the Strategist

**1. Fort Lauderdale 2017 — The Coordination Failure Metric.** Three separate ICS posts. No unified command. 2,600 officers with no staging. This is the textbook case for what happens when EM coordination is underpowered and operational response dominates the structure. The after-action finding — "resource coordination" as the primary failure — is a direct indictment of the model where PSD owns the response and EM is subordinate. Cite the Police Foundation After-Action Report (September 2017) by name.

**2. MCO EMAP 2020 — The Structural Prerequisite.** Orlando became the world's first EMAP-accredited airport under a structure where EM reports to the VP of Airport Operations. This is not correlation. EMAP accreditation requires organizational authority to coordinate enterprise-wide — authority that EM cannot demonstrate if it reports through one operational department. If MWAA wants EMAP accreditation (a credible long-term goal for a National Capital Region authority), the current structure is an obstacle, not a foundation.

**3. 14 CFR 139.325 — 18 Emergency Types, Most Without a PSD Lead.** The AEP must cover: aircraft accidents, bomb threats, structural fires, natural disasters, water/marshland rescue, hazmat, power failures, sabotage, hijacking, and unlawful interference. Count the categories where the incident command lead is IT, Engineering, Operations, or an external agency rather than fire or police. The regulation's scope is the argument. The AEP is an enterprise document assigned to PSD by convention, not by law.

**4. ATL and LAX Organizational Models — The Peer Benchmark.** The two most operationally complex US hub airports — one municipal (ATL), one authority (LAX) — have both placed EM under operations rather than public safety. This is a peer benchmark, not a theoretical preference. If MWAA's CEO or COO asks which comparable airport uses the PSD-subordinate model, the honest answer is "smaller airports and those that haven't made the deliberate design choice yet."

**5. The ICS Role Separation Argument — Incident Commander vs. EOC Director.** Under NIMS, the Incident Commander manages on-scene tactical operations. The EOC Director manages strategic coordination — resource allocation, cross-agency liaison, executive briefings, public information. These roles are designed to be separated. When the PSD command chain owns both functions, you get one of two outcomes: the EOC goes unstaffed during major incidents (because command is on-scene), or PSD delegates upward to EM while the EM coordinator has no organizational authority to compel participation from other departments. Neither outcome serves the Authority. The NIMS framework is the argument from structure. The Strategist should lean on it.

## A Final Note on Tone and Argument

The thesis is structurally correct. The organizational argument is sound. But as currently framed, it reads as a brief written by EM for EM — it understates the legitimate operational concerns of PSD leadership and it overstates the tidiness of the standalone model. A COO reading this draft will look for the answers to three questions it doesn't fully answer:

1. Who owns the interface between a standalone EM function and PSD command during the first 30 minutes of an active incident?
2. What is the headcount and budget implication of standing up an independent EM function?
3. What happens to Part 139 AEP ownership — does it stay with PSD or transfer to EM?

Answer those three questions explicitly, and the argument becomes much harder to dismiss. Leave them open, and a PSD leader — or a COO who trusts their PSD leader — will fill in the blanks with skepticism.
