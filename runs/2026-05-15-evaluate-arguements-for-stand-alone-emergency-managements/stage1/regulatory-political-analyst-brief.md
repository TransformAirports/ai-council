# Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief
## Organizational Placement of Emergency Management at MWAA
### Stage 1 Evidence Brief | May 2026

---

## Key Findings

1. **Federal regulation has already answered this question — in MWAA's favor.** FAA 14 CFR §139.325 requires the Airport Emergency Plan to be coordinated with "law enforcement agencies, rescue and firefighting agencies, medical personnel and organizations, the principal tenants at the airport, and *all other persons who have responsibilities under the plan*." The regulation assigns ownership to the Airport Manager — not to the VP of Public Safety. The accountable executive under the FAA's 2023 SMS rule (14 CFR Subpart E) must have "control of the certificate holder's human *and* financial resources" with "ultimate responsibility to the FAA" for safety performance. That is an enterprise-level job description. Public Safety is a participant, not the owner.

2. **The political constraint isn't law — it's tradition misread as law.** No federal regulation requires EM to report through public safety. The historical arrangement reflects the post-WWII civil defense structure that attached emergency functions to whichever department had communications radios. That tradition calcified into organizational habit, and at MWAA it was reinforced when the airports transferred from federal control in 1987 with the FAA's firefighting branch as its institutional nucleus. The case for the status quo is essentially inertial, not regulatory.

3. **The FAA's 2023 SMS rule created a new regulatory lever — and most airport leaders haven't fully deployed it.** The SMS rule (effective April 24, 2023; Phase 1 compliance covering large, medium, and small hub airports) requires an organization-wide safety management system anchored in an accountable executive with enterprise-level authority. Where EM sits relative to the SMS accountable executive matters: if EM is nested two levels below, enterprise risk information degrades before it reaches the person the FAA holds responsible. Smart MWAA leadership will recognize that elevating EM aligns with, rather than conflicts with, FAA's stated direction of travel.

4. **EMAP accreditation is the competitive differentiator — and it demands cross-functional governance that a PSD-subordinate EM cannot consistently deliver.** Orlando International Airport (MCO) is the only airport in the world to have achieved Emergency Management Accreditation Program (EMAP) accreditation, under the ANSI/EMAP 5-2022 standard. EMAP evaluates programs against 73 standards spanning program management, prevention, communications, training, incident management, and finance — nearly all of which require authority and access that EM cannot reliably exercise when it is perceived as a PSD program by the rest of the airport.

5. **Major peer airports have already separated EM from exclusive public safety control.** Los Angeles World Airports (LAWA) positioned Emergency Management within a distinct "Operations and Emergency Management" division, separate from the Airport Police, and charged it with running the Airport Response Coordination Center as an "interdivisional operational communication and coordination hub." Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta's emergency management (the C4 Division) reports up through the Senior Deputy General Manager of Operations — *alongside* public safety, law enforcement, fire, maintenance, and transportation — not through any one of them. Denver International embedded emergency management under COO/SVP of Airport Operations portfolio for the same reason. The peer cohort has already moved.

6. **The organizational resistance will come from within PSD, not from regulators — and it will be framed as a safety argument.** Any proposal to separate EM from PSD will generate institutional friction, likely expressed as concern that bifurcating command-and-control will degrade first-responder coordination. This argument is empirically weak (NIMS/ICS handles cross-organizational coordination by design; LAWA, ATL, and DEN operate effectively with separated structures), but politically it carries weight with a board that will want to avoid any appearance of a public safety downgrade. The strategist should anticipate this and pre-wire the counter-argument.

7. **The politics of bistate governance add a layer the private-airport playbook misses.** MWAA operates under a congressionally-approved interstate compact, with a board that includes presidential appointees and representatives from Virginia, Maryland, and the District of Columbia. Organizational restructuring at MWAA is not purely a management prerogative — it has board-visibility, compact-compliance, and political-optics dimensions that a single-jurisdiction airport authority does not face. Any reorganization brief that ignores this is incomplete.

## Evidence

### FAA Part 139: Enterprise Accountability Is Already the Law

The Airport Emergency Plan regulation at 14 CFR §139.325 establishes three facts relevant to this argument.

First, the plan must address nine hazard categories including natural disasters, hazardous materials incidents, power failures, and public health emergencies — categories that have no inherent connection to police or fire response and that require, respectively, IT coordination, facilities management, and medical/public health engagement [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/139.325]. These are not PSD threats. They are enterprise threats that happen to require PSD *participation*.

Second, the regulation mandates annual review of the plan "with all of the parties with whom the plan is coordinated." That coordination circle includes principal tenants, utilities, hospitals, military installations, and regional government agencies. EM, to execute this function credibly, must be perceived as a neutral enterprise facilitator — not as a PSD sub-unit calling a meeting.

Third, FAA CertAlert 24-09 (October 2024) reinforces this by requiring airports to integrate TSA, FBI, and "all other agencies identified in their approved Airport Emergency Plan" into planning and exercises [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/airport_safety/certalerts/part_139_certalert_24_09]. The FAA is not imagining EM as a PSD staff function. The FAA is imagining EM as an enterprise coordination capacity.

### The SMS Accountable Executive: A New Regulatory Anchor

The FAA's final Airport Safety Management System rule (effective April 24, 2023) introduced the concept of the "accountable executive" into Part 139 airport regulation. Per the rule, this individual must have "control of the certificate holder's human and financial resources" and "ultimate responsibility to the FAA" for safety performance [Source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/02/23/2023-03597/airport-safety-management-system]. The Airport Manager (or equivalent title) holds this role.

SMS requires four components operating organization-wide: safety policy, safety risk management, safety assurance, and safety promotion. The FAA describes SMS as "a formal, top-down, organization-wide approach to managing safety risk" — language that explicitly exceeds any single department's remit [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/airport_safety/safety_management_systems/external/faqs].

For MWAA, the SMS compliance timeline is live: large hub airports must submit implementation plans and full SMS deployment within approximately 36 months of plan approval. An EM function subordinated to PSD cannot serve as the enterprise-level risk integration mechanism that SMS requires. Conversely, an EM function at the executive level can serve as the enterprise safety integration partner for the SMS accountable executive — which is an affirmative regulatory argument for elevation, not merely a conceptual one.

### EMAP: The Accreditation that Structurally Requires Enterprise EM

The Emergency Management Accreditation Program, operating under the ANSI/EMAP 5-2022 standard, evaluates programs against 73 standards [Source: https://emap.org/the-emergency-management-standard/]. Orlando International Airport achieved full EMAP accreditation in 2020 and remains the only airport in the world to hold it [Source: https://www.internationalairportreview.com/news/110570/orlando-international-earns-full-emap-accreditation/].

EMAP defines "emergency management" to encompass "all organizations with emergency/disaster functions in a jurisdiction" — encompassing prevention, mitigation, preparedness, response, and recovery across the *whole* organization. The 73 standards span administration, finance, legal, communications, training, and incident management — all of which require access to resources, authorities, and executive engagement that a PSD-subordinate EM cannot consistently secure.

That MCO began this process in 2017, accelerated it through the pandemic period, and emerged as the global benchmark should register as a competitive signal. No MWAA peer airport with comparable traffic volumes — DCA handles ~24 million annual passengers, IAD ~27 million — holds EMAP accreditation. Achieving it requires organizational positioning that makes it possible to meet the standard.

### FEMA's Continuity Framework: Enterprise Coordination Is the Mandate

The FEMA Continuity Guidance Circular (updated August 2024) defines continuity planning as "whole community guidance to support the development of continuity capabilities across the nation" with the explicit purpose of ensuring organizations can perform essential functions across all threat types [Source: https://www.fema.gov/emergency-managers/national-preparedness/continuity/circular]. A COOP program that lives under PSD is, by structure, a PSD COOP plan — not an Authority-wide COOP plan.

ACRP Research Report 268 (Integrating Crisis Management and Business Continuity at Airports, 2024) makes the organizational argument directly: "the chief operating officer (COO) or senior leaders in the operations team are often logical positions to sponsor or champion the continuity program." The report further specifies that a continuity champion must have "authority to allocate resources, educate stakeholders, and develop planning efforts" — a description that fits an executive-level function, not a department-level one [Source: https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/27915/chapter/6].

### ICAO Annex 14: International Standard Sets Enterprise Expectation

ICAO Annex 14 requires that the aerodrome emergency operations centre be responsible for "the overall coordination and general direction of the response to an emergency" [Source: https://www.icao.int/sites/default/files/safety/CAPSCA/PublishingImages/Pages/ICAO-SARPs-(Annexes-and-PANS)/an14_1.pdf]. This is not PSD's coordination — it is the aerodrome's coordination. The EOC is not a fire department asset; it is an airport authority asset. Where the EM function sits determines whether the EOC functions as an enterprise tool or as an extension of a single department.

### The Peer Airport Case Studies

**Los Angeles World Airports (LAWA)**: Emergency Management is a standalone division within "Operations and Emergency Management," explicitly separate from the Airport Police. The Airport Response Coordination Center (ARCC) is charged with "synchronizing all aspects of the ARCC's business operations including planning, organization, equipment, training, exercises, and continuous improvement" — an enterprise mission [Source: https://www.lawa.org/groups-and-divisions/emergency-management]. Emergency Management works *with* the Public Safety Group to identify threats; it does not report through it.

**Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International (ATL)**: The emergency management function (C4 Division) sits under the Senior Deputy General Manager of Operations alongside — not below — public safety, law enforcement, fire, medical, maintenance, and transportation. The common supervisor is the operations general manager, not the public safety director. The design deliberately prevents any single operational division from claiming ownership of emergency management [Source: https://atlcollab.com/atlsync4/atlsync4/about-atl/leadership/].

**Denver International Airport (DEN)**: Emergency preparedness and management historically sat in the COO's portfolio alongside airside and terminal operations and safety. Denver's SVP of Airport Operations had direct oversight of "Airside and Terminal Operations, Safety, Security, Emergency Preparedness and Management, Contract Administration and the Communications Center" — a structure that treats EM as a peer function to operations, not as a sub-function of public safety [Source: https://www.aviationpros.com/airport-business/airport-infrastructure-operations/press-release/55124821/dave-laporte-named-denver-international-airport-chief-operating-officer-executive-vice-president].

## The Political Dynamics: What Can Be Moved and When

### What Is Regulation (and Cannot Be Argued Away)

The FAA's enterprise-accountability framework under 14 CFR §139.325 and the SMS rule under Subpart E is binding regulation. DCA and IAD cannot choose to have a narrowly scoped EM function and remain compliant with the enterprise coordination requirements baked into the AEP and SMS frameworks. This is not arguable. It is rulemaking.

The SMS compliance clock is already running. Class I airports face a phased implementation timeline; MWAA cannot defer indefinitely. The accountable executive concept will become an audit finding if MWAA's safety management system is enterprise-wide in name but siloed in practice.

### What Is Politics (and Can Be Changed — But Won't Move Fast)

The decision to keep EM under PSD is an organizational choice, not a regulatory mandate. No federal statute, no FAA regulation, and no DHS directive requires EM to report through a public safety chain of command. This is, as the thesis correctly identifies, tradition disguised as policy.

But tradition at a bistate authority with presidential board appointments and active congressional oversight (MWAA's enabling legislation sits in federal statute, 49 U.S.C. §49103) has a long half-life. Organizational restructuring that touches the public safety brand — at any airport, but especially one that handles the President's air travel at DCA — requires board alignment, careful political management, and usually a triggering event or a change in leadership that creates a reorganization window.

The Tennessee model cited in the thesis is instructive but imperfect as an analogy. TEMA's separation from the Military Department was a legislative act that took years of advocacy and a specific political moment to execute [Source: https://www.tn.gov/tema/the-agency.html]. MWAA's reorganization is an executive action that requires board approval — which is lower in friction than a legislature, but higher in visibility than an internal memo.

### The Counter-Argument and Its Weakness

The predictable institutional resistance will be framed around command-and-control: "separating EM from PSD will create confusion at the incident site." This is a weak argument for three reasons. First, NIMS and ICS are explicitly designed to manage multi-organizational response — the FAA's own certification framework embeds ICS. Second, LAWA, ATL, and DEN operate with separated EM structures and have not experienced the coordination failures this argument predicts. Third, the confusion argument actually inverts the problem: the current structure creates confusion *for all non-PSD departments* about whether EM is an authority function or a police/fire program.

### Realistic Timeline

A baseline reorganization (EM reporting directly to COO or CEO) is achievable within a single budget cycle if executive leadership is aligned, because it does not require new legislation, new positions, or new federal approvals. The political work is internal: securing buy-in from the VP of Public Safety that their remit is not being diminished, securing board briefing before the change is announced, and ensuring the airport managers at DCA and IAD understand the new coordination model.

The enhanced model — a VP of Preparedness consolidating EM, risk management, SMS safety functions, and engineering safety personnel — requires a position reclassification, a potential compensation adjustment, and likely a competitive search. That is an 18-to-24 month project if budget is available and executive will is present.

What cannot move on project timelines: board politics, bistate compact sensitivities, and the institutional memory within PSD that will read any reorganization as a reduction in stature. These require sustained political management, not a single memo.

## Direct Quotes and Data Points for the Strategist

**1. The FAA's own words on enterprise accountability:** Under the 2023 SMS rule, the accountable executive must have "control of the certificate holder's human and financial resources for operations conducted under an Airport Operating Certificate" and "ultimate responsibility to the FAA, on behalf of the certificate holder, for the safety performance of operations." [Source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/02/23/2023-03597/airport-safety-management-system] — This is not a description of any single department head. This is a description of an enterprise executive.

**2. ACRP Research Report 268 on the logical executive home for continuity:** "The chief operating officer (COO) or senior leaders in the operations team are often logical positions to sponsor or champion the continuity program." [Source: https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/27915/chapter/6] — A national academies publication, produced under FAA-sponsored research, makes the organizational argument directly.

**3. EMAP's scope definition:** EMAP defines "emergency management" to encompass "all organizations with emergency/disaster functions in a jurisdiction" — prevention, mitigation, preparedness, response, and recovery across the whole organization. [Source: https://emap.org/what-is-emap/] — If MWAA pursues EMAP accreditation (and it should), this scope definition makes a PSD-subordinate EM structurally unable to comply.

**4. The NACo data point on organizational placement trends:** In a national survey of county emergency management programs, 46 percent of chief emergency management officials report directly to the county board (elected officials) and 44 percent report directly to the county administrator, executive, or manager — meaning 90 percent report at or near the top of the organization. [Source: https://www.naco.org/resources/emergency-management-county-government-national-survey] — This is the baseline expectation in the broader EM profession.

**5. FAA Part 139 §139.325 on what the AEP must coordinate:** The plan must be coordinated with "law enforcement agencies, rescue and firefighting agencies, medical personnel and organizations, the principal tenants at the airport, and all other persons who have responsibilities under the plan." [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/139.325] — The regulation envisions an enterprise coordinator, not a department manager.

## The Analyst's Bottom Line

The regulatory framework does not merely permit a standalone EM function at MWAA — it effectively demands enterprise-level EM governance through the AEP coordination requirement, the SMS accountable executive standard, FEMA's continuity framework, and ICAO's aerodrome-level EOC mandate. The thesis is on solid regulatory ground.

The political constraint is internal and institutional, not external and regulatory. The VP of Public Safety's span of control, the organizational identity of MWAA's public safety professionals, and the board's sensitivity to any appearance of weakening security at DCA are the real friction points. None of these are immovable. All of them require deliberate political management rather than regulatory argument alone.

The peer benchmark makes the competitive case: LAWA, ATL, and DEN have already made this structural decision. The argument at MWAA is not whether to join an emerging trend — it is whether to catch up with a settled one.

Any strategist who presents this as a regulatory inevitability will be right but will also be ignored. Any strategist who presents this as an organizational modernization aligned with federal standards, peer airport practice, and the COO's operational accountability will have a path to a yes.
