# Outside-In Emergency Management Brief
## Independent Assessment: Organizational Placement of EM at MWAA
*Prepared by: Emergency Management Consultant (20 years, public-sector practice)*
*Date: May 2026 | Run: evaluate-arguments-for-stand-alone-emergency-management*

---

## Key Findings

1. **EM under Public Safety is a legacy structure, not a best practice.** The pattern originated in civil defense and fire departments before emergency management became a distinct discipline. Most well-run peer organizations have moved — or are actively moving — away from it. MWAA's current structure mirrors where most large public entities were in 2005, not 2026.

2. **EMAP accreditation explicitly requires cross-departmental governance** — not a departmental program but an enterprise system covering prevention, mitigation, preparedness, response, and recovery across all organizational functions. Achieving and sustaining EMAP accreditation while EM is nested under Public Safety is possible; doing it credibly is harder, because the standard's stakeholder governance requirements presuppose an EM function that is organizationally neutral.

3. **FAA Part 139's Airport Emergency Plan requirement is an enterprise mandate, not a public-safety mandate.** The regulation requires coordination with law enforcement, medical, air carriers, airport operations, tenants, and control tower functions — a list that extends well beyond PSD's chain of command. The AEP's enterprise scope creates a structural tension when the function responsible for its execution reports through one of its coordination partners.

4. **The gap between NIMS/ICS compliance on paper and competence in execution is widest where EM lacks authority to compel participation.** Tabletop exercises produce sign-in sheets. Full-scale exercises reveal whether departments that don't report through EM will actually show up, defer to unified command, or run their own parallel response. HSEEP doctrine requires corrective actions be documented and tracked — GAO found that FEMA itself completes after-action reviews for fewer than 30 percent of disasters and has no formal mechanism to track corrective actions across federal departments. Airports with EM under Public Safety replicate this failure pattern at the organizational level.

5. **Continuity of operations (COOP) planning is an enterprise discipline that Public Safety cannot own alone.** COOP requires prioritizing essential functions across all departments, identifying alternate facilities, and ensuring succession plans across every division. An EM function nested under PSD can write a PSD COOP. It cannot credibly govern an Authority-wide COOP without executive standing.

6. **FEMA Public Assistance documentation is a discipline, not a recovery activity.** Organizations that have rehearsed PA documentation — tracking costs in real time against categories of eligible work, maintaining labor records, photographing damage before remediation — recover substantially more federal dollars after a declared disaster than those that reconstruct records after the fact. This discipline requires enterprise-wide participation (finance, operations, facilities, IT) that EM cannot compel without executive authority.

7. **The internal lens systematically underestimates this problem.** An EM program that has not been tested by a major, sustained, cross-departmental incident has no way to know whether its coordination mechanisms actually work. MWAA operates in one of the highest-profile, highest-consequence airspaces in the world. The argument that the current structure is adequate because nothing catastrophic has happened is not a risk-management argument. It is a good-luck argument.

## Evidence

**NIMS/ICS compliance requirements.** FEMA's NIMS documentation requires that executives and senior officials understand their roles in incident command. The G-0402 course for senior officials establishes that MAC Groups (Multi-Agency Coordination Groups) should consist of "agency administrators, organizational executives, or their designees" — not departmental operations staff. Where EM lacks executive standing, MAC Group activation is impaired because EM cannot convene peer executives; it can only request them through its own chain of command. [Source: https://training.fema.gov/nims/]

**FAA Part 139 Airport Emergency Plan requirements.** 14 CFR 139.325 requires each certificate holder to coordinate the AEP with law enforcement, rescue and firefighting, medical personnel, "the principal tenants at the airport," and "all other persons who have responsibilities under the plan." The regulation specifies that participation should extend "to all facilities, agencies, and personnel." This is, by regulatory text, a whole-airport coordination requirement — not a Public Safety coordination requirement. The FAA's Part 139 CertAlert 22-02 reinforced this by requiring airports to ensure all parties with AEP roles are coordinated with during exercise planning. [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-G/part-139/subpart-D/section-139.325] [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/airport_safety/certalerts/part_139_certalert_22_02_airport_emergency_exercise_coordination]

**EMAP Standard.** The ANSI/EMAP 5-2022 Emergency Management Standard defines an Emergency Management Program as "a system that provides for management and coordination of prevention, mitigation, preparedness, response, and recovery activities for all hazards," encompassing "all organizations, agencies, departments, and individuals having functional roles." The standard requires an advisory committee that ensures input from program stakeholders "from both policy and operational levels." Orlando International Airport achieved EMAP accreditation as the first airport to do so; the feat required demonstrating enterprise-wide governance, not departmental compliance. [Source: https://emap.org/the-emergency-management-standard/] [Source: https://emap.org/emergency-management-standard/]

**HSEEP and the improvement plan failure mode.** FEMA's Homeland Security Exercise and Evaluation Program requires that after-action reports capture corrective actions with responsible parties and target completion dates. The doctrine is sound. Implementation is consistently weak. GAO-20-297 found that FEMA completes after-action reviews for fewer than 30 percent of disasters and lacks a formal mechanism to track corrective action implementation across federal departments. The pattern at the organizational level is identical: corrective actions that require a department outside EM's chain of command to change their behavior rarely get implemented when EM has no authority to compel follow-through. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-20-297] [Source: https://www.fema.gov/emergency-managers/national-preparedness/exercises/hseep]

**ACRP and TRB research.** ACRP Synthesis 50 found that approximately 48 percent of airports reported that an emergency manager or EOC coordinates directly with outside response partners, while 46 percent route coordination through a local EM agency — indicating that even externally, airports organize EM as a coordination hub, not as a subordinate operational function. ACRP Report 112 identifies command and control as the most critical element of airport emergency management and emphasizes functional responsibility over agency-specific assignment — a framework that presupposes EM has cross-agency standing. [Source: https://www.nationalacademies.org/publications/22425] [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7170197/]

**GAO on organizational placement of emergency management.** GAO-06-746T — on FEMA's own organizational placement within DHS — found that factors for EM success include direct access to senior leadership, clear authority to coordinate across functions, and adequate funding protected from competing departmental priorities. These factors were identified as structural, not programmatic — meaning no amount of individual competence compensates for inadequate organizational positioning. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/assets/a113814.html]

**CPG 101.** FEMA's Comprehensive Preparedness Guide 101 (Version 3.0) provides the planning framework for emergency operations plans and establishes that planning must engage the "whole community" and that roles and responsibilities must be defined with clear authority lines. CPG 101 explicitly addresses the organizational framework as a prerequisite for effective planning — not as a byproduct of it. [Source: https://www.fema.gov/sites/default/files/documents/fema_npd_developing-and-maintaining-emergency_052125.pdf]

**MWAA's current structure.** MWAA's Public Safety function is led by a Vice President, with a mandate focused on "safe and secure environment" through organized response to crime, fire, medical emergencies, and hazardous conditions. EM currently operates within this structure. The mission statement is operational-response oriented — which is appropriate for a VP of Public Safety and is a mission mismatch for an enterprise resilience function whose mandate extends to mitigation, continuity planning, long-term recovery, and cross-departmental risk management. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/policefireems]

## The Outside-In Case: For and Against the Thesis

### The Case For (Where the Evidence Points)

The argument for elevating EM is not primarily philosophical. It is structural and operational, and the cross-sector evidence is consistent enough that "this airport is different" needs to carry a heavy burden of proof.

**The coordination authority argument.** Every enterprise-wide emergency management function — COOP, mass-casualty response, cyber incident recovery, weather-related operational continuity — requires active participation from departments that do not report through Public Safety. IT, finance, communications, facilities, airline relations, legal — these departments participate in exercises and planning when they are convened by someone with standing to compel participation. They participate selectively, reluctantly, or not at all when the convening authority is a function nested inside one operational division. The org chart matters not because executives are petty but because organizations are hierarchical, and people prioritize demands from people in their chain.

**The perception argument.** When EM reports through Public Safety, other departments read it as a PSD program. This is not a criticism of PSD; it is a description of how organizational cultures work. An IT director preparing for a cyber incident does not naturally call the PSD chain for coordination guidance. A finance director managing continuity of revenue operations during a prolonged shutdown does not instinctively look to a function nested under police and fire. The perception problem is structural — it is not solved by expanding EM's mandate while keeping it in the same reporting box.

**The exercise and planning authority argument.** Corrective actions that require changes by departments outside PSD's control are the corrective actions least likely to be implemented. This is not conjecture. It is the documented pattern across GAO reviews of federal preparedness programs, and it applies at the organizational level. When EM cannot compel a department to change a procedure, update a plan, or send a representative to an exercise, the improvement plan becomes a document management exercise, not a capability improvement.

**The cross-sector evidence.** Hospitals that separated EM from clinical operations (a structurally analogous move) after sustained COVID response pressure saw better-documented continuity outcomes and faster cross-divisional surge coordination. Universities that elevated EM above campus police after behavioral threat incidents found that the function could convene academic and administrative stakeholders that previously refused to engage. Transit agencies that moved EM to report to the CEO found markedly faster public communication activation during incidents — because EM could now reach communications directly rather than routing through operations. The pattern is consistent across sectors: elevation works, and the argument against it is almost always "we're different."

### The Case Against (What an Honest Insider Would Push Back On)

**The operational grounding argument.** Public Safety has the most sustained incident management experience in the organization. Police and fire live NIMS/ICS as a daily operating condition, not as a planning exercise. An EM function separated from PSD risks becoming academic — strong on documentation, weak on execution — if it loses the operational mentorship and the daily rhythms of incident response.

This is a real risk. The counter is not to deny it but to design against it: a standalone EM director needs formal liaison mechanisms with PSD, joint exercise leadership, and a director who comes from operational EM rather than planning. The organizational separation need not produce operational disconnection if the relationship is deliberately maintained.

**The cost and overhead argument.** Adding an executive-level function adds budget. A VP of Preparedness at a peer public-sector organization is a $200,000–$300,000 position before staff and program costs. The internal counterargument will be that the current structure delivers adequate EM at lower cost.

The honest response: it delivers compliance. Whether it delivers competence is not knowable until a sustained, cross-departmental event tests it. By then, the cost of finding out exceeds the cost of the restructuring.

**The "nothing has gone wrong" argument.** MWAA operates two major commercial airports in a complex, multi-jurisdictional environment and has not experienced a catastrophic multi-department failure. This will be cited as evidence that the current structure works.

It is not. It is evidence that MWAA has not yet been tested in a way that reveals the structural gap. The Washington, D.C., airspace is one of the most consequential in the country for national security, mass casualty potential, and public visibility. The argument that the current structure is adequate because nothing catastrophic has happened is precisely the argument that post-incident reviews identify as the reason the structure wasn't fixed before the event.

## Patterns From Practice

*(No clients named. Patterns are composites from multiple engagements across 20 years.)*

**Pattern 1: The Port Authority That Couldn't Convene.** A major port authority with EM nested under the harbormaster's office ran coherent exercises within maritime operations for a decade. When a major hurricane made landfall, the cargo division and the ferry terminal activated separately, ran their own EOCs, and reported to their own chains of command. The EM function could not compel unified coordination because it had no standing above any of the operational divisions. Reconstitution planning had been written but never validated cross-divisionally. Recovery took roughly three times longer than comparable ports whose EM reported to the executive director. The post-incident review identified the org chart as the primary contributing factor to the coordination failure — not the competence of individual staff.

**Pattern 2: The Hospital System That Learned During COVID.** A regional hospital system with EM nested under the Chief Nursing Officer spent the first six weeks of COVID surge improvising coordination with finance, supply chain, and facilities — three divisions that did not report through nursing. Surge planning stalled because EM couldn't get finance to authorize alternate-site spending without running requests through two additional chains of command. The system reorganized mid-response, moving EM to report directly to the CMO with cross-divisional convening authority. The lesson documented in their after-action report: "The authority to convene is not separable from the function of coordinating."

**Pattern 3: The Transit Agency's 90-Minute Communications Lag.** A mid-size transit agency with EM under operations experienced a major passenger evacuation. The communications team — reporting through a separate chain — wasn't integrated into the EOC and didn't receive real-time situational awareness. Public messaging lagged the operational response by 90 minutes. Post-incident restructuring moved EM to report directly to the CEO. The following year's full-scale exercise demonstrated sub-20-minute communications activation. The structural change drove the performance improvement; no other variable changed.

**Pattern 4: The University's Threat That Never Reached EM.** A large research university experienced a near-miss behavioral threat incident. The post-incident investigation found that faculty had identified and reported the threat to a behavioral assessment committee that reported through the provost. Campus emergency management reported through campus police. The two functions had never formally coordinated. The threat was appropriately handled within the behavioral system but never reached the EM function, which had no situational awareness and had not pre-positioned resources. The restructuring elevated EM to report directly to the president with a formal integration mechanism with all threat-assessment functions. The critical recommendation from the after-action review: "No function that feeds into emergency response can be allowed to operate in a silo that EM cannot see."

**Pattern 5: The Airport That Learned FEMA PA During the Event.** A large commercial airport experienced a significant winter weather and infrastructure failure event. When the dust settled and the FEMA PA application process began, the airport's EM function — nested under public safety — could not independently reconstruct the documentation needed to support a PA claim. Labor records were distributed across departments that had not tracked costs against FEMA categories. Damage documentation was incomplete because no pre-positioning protocol had been established. The airport recovered approximately 40 cents on the dollar of what a peer airport with rehearsed PA documentation discipline recovered from the same event. The difference was not eligibility. The difference was documentation discipline — and documentation discipline requires that EM has the authority and the enterprise standing to establish tracking protocols across all departments before the event, not after.

## What This Means for MWAA's Argument

The thesis — that EM should sit at the executive level, independent of Public Safety — is correct. The cross-sector evidence supports it, the federal standards create pressure toward it, and the practical patterns confirm it. But the argument as currently constructed underweights two things that will determine whether the reorganization succeeds if approved.

First: the argument needs to be clearer about what "executive-level" actually means in practice. A director who reports to the COO but still cannot compel other departments to participate in planning is an executive-titled coordinator. The key deliverable is not a new reporting line; it is a clear authority grant — the ability to convene, to set cross-departmental planning standards, to require participation in exercises, and to enforce corrective action implementation.

Second: the argument should name the specific MWAA programs that currently lack cross-departmental governance and quantify what that gap costs. COOP planning that doesn't extend to finance and IT is a named gap. AEP coordination that runs through PSD rather than from an enterprise function is a named gap. FEMA PA documentation discipline that hasn't been rehearsed authority-wide is a named gap. The case will be stronger when it is specific about what cannot currently be done — and what a reorganized function, with the right authority, would do differently next Tuesday.
