# Airport CEO Brief: Organizational Placement of Emergency Management at MWAA
**Stage 1 Research Brief | Airport CEO Lens**
*Prepared for the Transform Airports AI Council | May 2026*

---

## Key Findings
*(For the board chair: five minutes, maximum retention)*

1. **The financial case for elevation is not a rating argument — it is an incident-cost argument.** MWAA carries Aa3/AA- ratings with 3.16x debt service coverage and 1,006 days cash on hand as of 2024. No rating analyst will add a notch because MWAA restructures its emergency management function. But that same credit profile rests on revenue continuity at two of the most politically exposed airports in the country. DCA serves Congress; IAD serves the federal government's international travel. A mismanaged enterprise-scale incident — not a bad incident, a mismanaged one — can surface a governance deficiency that stresses the agencies' qualitative management factor. Pre-incident reorganization is cheap. Post-incident reorganization under a Congressional subpoena is not.

2. **No federal regulation mandates where EM sits in the org chart, but the regulatory stack increasingly assumes it reports upward, not sideways.** The FAA's 2023 Safety Management Systems rule designates the Airport Manager as the "accountable executive" for safety across the entire organization. TSA's 2023 cybersecurity directive creates cross-departmental compliance obligations that no single department can own alone. FEMA's emergency preparedness grant programs reward coordinated, enterprise-level planning. The compliance architecture has been building the case for executive-level EM for a decade; MWAA's organizational chart has not caught up.

3. **Three peer hub airports have already solved this problem, in different ways, with documented results.** LAX created a Deputy Executive Director for Operations and Emergency Management at the C-2 level — EM as a peer to airside, landside, and terminal operations, not subordinate to public safety. Atlanta's Hartsfield-Jackson placed Emergency Management under a Senior Deputy General Manager whose portfolio also includes Public Safety and Airport Operations — effectively making EM coordination the second-ranking operational position. Denver's Senior VP of Airport Operations owned Emergency Preparedness and Management as a named subset of the SVP portfolio. DFW and MSP left EM inside Public Safety — the configuration closest to MWAA's current structure and the one with the weakest cross-departmental reach.

4. **The Atlanta 2017 power outage is the single most instructive precedent.** A private utility consortium controlled the electrical infrastructure. When both primary and backup power failed simultaneously, no organizational authority had the cross-departmental reach to manage the cascading consequences — not the airport's public safety director, not operations, not communications. Delta reported approximately $50 million in losses from a single 11-hour outage and 1,200 cancelled flights. GAO subsequently audited 321 power outages at 24 commercial service airports from 2015 to 2022 and found that neither the FAA nor airports had consistent cross-departmental coordination mechanisms for multi-system failures. The structural lesson is simple: when an incident crosses from Public Safety's domain into Facilities, IT, Operations, and Communications simultaneously, an EM function subordinated to Public Safety has no organizational authority over any of the other domains.

5. **The political risk of inaction is asymmetric.** A CEO who elevates EM and then manages a major incident well gets credit for the structure she built. A CEO who leaves EM subordinated to Public Safety and then watches an enterprise-scale incident cascade across departments — with no single cross-departmental coordinator — answers for that choice before the board, the rating agencies, the FAA, and the Congressional delegations that both Virginia and DC's governments will mobilize within 48 hours of any significant event at DCA or IAD. The board's 17 members represent four distinct political appointing authorities. After an incident, every one of them will ask why the organizational structure did not prevent the coordination failure.

6. **The primary internal risk is a coordination seam, not a budget problem.** The cost of creating a VP of Preparedness or an enterprise EM function is trivial relative to MWAA's capital program. The real risk is creating an organizational gap between the incident commanders in Public Safety (who will always run the on-the-ground response under NIMS) and the enterprise coordinator (who now sits in a separate office). The most successful peer models — LAX and ATL — addressed this by keeping Public Safety and EM inside the same senior executive portfolio, not by separating them entirely. That nuance matters for how this restructuring is designed and how it is explained to the VP for Public Safety.

7. **The CEO's strategic window is defined by the board's governance clock, not by the incident calendar.** MWAA's 17-member board serves staggered six-year terms with a chair elected annually. A governance-sensitive organizational change — and this one is — needs a working majority (nine of seventeen members) who understand the rationale before the proposal arrives. Virginia's seven appointees are the largest single bloc and the most relevant to cultivate first. The current chair election cycle is the clock that matters, not the next weather event.

## Evidence Section

### Bond-Rating and Financial Exposure

MWAA's current credit profile is Fitch AA- (stable), Moody's Aa3 (stable), and S&P AA- (stable), with a 2024 debt service coverage ratio of 3.16x (up from 2.77x in 2023) and 1,006 days of liquidity. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/financial-statistics/investor-relations/credit-ratings]

Moody's updated its US Airport Revenue Bond methodology in February 2023. The framework scores market position, financial metrics, and covenants/legal provisions. Operational risk management is embedded in the "management quality" subfactor under market position — a qualitative assessment where the agencies retain discretion to adjust anchor ratings. [Source: Moody's US Airport Revenue Bond Rating Methodology, February 2023 — accessible at https://ratings.moodys.com/api/rmc-documents/398689]

Fitch published updated US Airports Criteria in January 2025. Its five-factor model includes Infrastructure Development and Renewal as the factor most relevant to resilience — assessing whether the airport maintains its asset base and can absorb capital shocks. DSCR thresholds: AAA requires above 2.0x coverage; AA requires 1.5–2.0x. MWAA at 3.16x holds substantial cushion. A single major incident that compresses revenue for 60 to 90 days is unlikely to trigger a rating action by itself — unless it also surfaces a governance deficiency that puts management quality under review. [Source: Fitch US Airports Rating Criteria, January 2025; DWU Consulting synthesis at https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-bond-ratings]

In May 2025, Moody's shifted the US airport sector outlook from stable to negative, citing decelerating economic growth and airline capacity adjustments. This tighter sector backdrop means any management-quality concern that might previously have been absorbed without consequence now carries more weight. [Source: Widely reported; primary document not independently retrieved — treat as widely understood but unverified at primary source level]

The financial case for EM elevation is not that it improves the credit rating. It is that the most plausible pathway to a rating event — an unexpected, multi-departmental operational failure at DCA or IAD — is also the scenario where a Public Safety-subordinated EM function has the least authority to prevent escalation. MWAA's financial cushion buys time; it does not substitute for organizational capacity.

### Board Governance and Accountability

MWAA's board has 17 members appointed by four political principals: Virginia Governor (7 members, subject to General Assembly confirmation); DC Mayor (4 members, subject to DC Council confirmation); Maryland Governor (3 members); President of the United States (3 members, subject to Senate confirmation). Terms are six years with one permissible reappointment. The chair is elected annually by the board. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/about-authority/board-directors; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Washington_Airports_Authority]

The FAA's 2023 Airport Safety Management Systems rule (effective April 24, 2023) designates the Airport Manager as the "accountable executive" for safety across the organization, responsible for resource allocation and continuous safety improvement. This applies to large and medium hub airports including both IAD and DCA. The rule establishes a documented CEO-level accountability pathway for safety governance — the organizational hook for arguing that enterprise EM should report upward. [Source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/02/23/2023-03597/airport-safety-management-system]

EMAP (Emergency Management Accreditation Program, ANSI/EMAP 5-2022) evaluates programs across 73 standards including planning, resource management, training, exercises, and cross-jurisdictional coordination. The standard does not prescribe a reporting line but does require documented authority, adequate resources, and effective cross-departmental coordination. No large commercial US airport currently holds EMAP accreditation — EMAP predominantly accredits state and county EM offices, with Amtrak as a notable infrastructure-sector precedent. Pursuing EMAP accreditation would itself be a board-level strategic signal and a differentiator in the rating-agency management narrative, independent of the specific org-chart placement question. [Source: https://emap.org/emergency-management-standard/; https://emap.org/category/accredited/]

The corporate governance principle articulated in Caremark (applied by analogy to public authority boards) holds that fiduciaries who fail to implement adequate risk-oversight systems face governance exposure when a foreseeable risk materializes. MWAA board members are not Delaware corporate directors, but the principle applies: if a post-incident review reveals that EM lacked cross-departmental authority, the board's own governance record is implicated alongside the CEO's. [Source: Harvard Law School Forum on Corporate Governance, https://corpgov.law.harvard.edu/2023/09/30/risk-management-and-the-board-of-directors-9/ — applied by analogy, not a direct legal citation]

### Federal Compliance Stack

FAA Part 139 (14 CFR 139.325) requires certificated airports to maintain an Airport Emergency Plan, review it annually with all coordinating parties, and conduct a full-scale exercise every 36 months. It does not specify where the emergency coordinator sits in the organizational hierarchy. [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-G/part-139/subpart-D/section-139.325]

FAA Advisory Circular 150/5200-31C provides guidance on AEP development and references NFPA 1600. It describes an Emergency Response Organization Responsibility Matrix but does not mandate a specific org-chart placement for the EM coordinator. The NIMS framework referenced throughout AC 150/5200-31C operates through Incident Command structures that sit in Public Safety regardless of where EM is organizationally housed — this is the source of the coordination seam concern for any restructuring model. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/resources/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.current/documentNumber/150_5200-31]

TSA's March 2023 cybersecurity requirements for airport and aircraft operators mandate: reporting of significant cyber incidents to CISA, a designated cybersecurity point of contact, a cybersecurity incident response plan, and a completed vulnerability assessment. Cyber incidents inherently cross IT, Operations, Security, and Public Safety simultaneously — precisely the multi-departmental emergency that a Public Safety-subordinated EM function is structurally least equipped to coordinate. [Source: https://www.tsa.gov/news/press/releases/2023/03/07/tsa-issues-new-cybersecurity-requirements-for-airport-and-aircraft]

FEMA EMPG funded $319.55 million nationally in FY 2024. Eligibility runs through state administrative agencies; MWAA as an interstate compact authority cannot apply directly and must work through Virginia or DC channels. An enterprise EM function with dedicated staff for federal grant coordination is materially better positioned to capture EMPG and BRIC dollars than a function buried in a Public Safety department with no dedicated grant management capacity. [Source: https://www.fema.gov/grants/preparedness/emergency-management-performance; https://www.fema.gov/grants/mitigation/learn/building-resilient-infrastructure-communities]

### Peer Hub Organizational Models

**LAX:** The Deputy Executive Director for Operations and Emergency Management is a named C-2 level role at LAWA, with Emergency Management as one of five direct-report divisions alongside Airside Operations, Landside Operations, Terminal Operations/Guest Services, and Operations Administrative Support. EM is structurally a peer to airside and terminal operations, not subordinate to public safety. [Source: https://www.lawa.org/en/groups-and-divisions/operations-and-emergency-management]

**ATL:** Augustus Hudson holds the title Senior Deputy General Manager of Operations, with Emergency Management among his areas of authority alongside Airport Operations, Public Safety and Security, Law Enforcement, Fire and Medical, Maintenance, and Transportation. This is a documented case where EM's organizational home is the COO-equivalent's portfolio — not the Public Safety director's. [Source: https://www.atl.com/about-atl/leadership/augustus-gus-hudson/]

**DEN:** The Senior VP of Airport Operations historically owned Emergency Preparedness and Management as a named function within the SVP portfolio alongside Airside and Terminal Operations, Safety, Security, and the Communications Center. EM was positioned as a cross-operational function, not a public safety sub-function. [Source: https://www.aviationpros.com/airport-business/airport-infrastructure-operations/press-release/55124821/dave-laporte-named-denver-international-airport-chief-operating-officer-executive-vice-president]

**DFW and MSP:** Emergency Management is embedded in the Department of Public Safety at both airports. The EM Administrator at DFW is a departmental-level position within DPS, not a standalone executive function — the configuration most similar to MWAA's current structure. [Source: https://www.dfwairport.com/dps/; https://mspairport.com/public-safety/airport-police/police-department-structure]

### The Atlanta 2017 Power Outage

The December 17, 2017 outage at Hartsfield-Jackson caused approximately 1,200 flight cancellations and cost Delta Airlines an estimated $50 million in a single 11-hour event. A private utility consortium controlled the electrical infrastructure, and when both primary and backup power failed, no organizational authority held the cross-departmental reach to manage cascading consequences across Facilities, Operations, and Communications simultaneously. The airport's EM function was not visible in the public response. [Source: https://www.cnbc.com/2017/12/17/atlanta-airport-the-worlds-busiest-reports-power-outage.html]

GAO subsequently reviewed 321 power outages at 24 commercial service airports from 2015 to 2022 in GAO-23-105203 (released August 2023) and found that both FAA and airports share resilience responsibility, but that clear cross-departmental coordination mechanisms were consistently absent. The report specifically identified the absence of a unified authority structure as a contributing factor in the most damaging outages. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105203]

## The Financial and Political Case: For and Against

### The Case for Elevation

From the CEO's chair, the question is not whether MWAA can afford to elevate EM. With 3.16x coverage and 1,006 days of liquidity, the institution can. The question is whether MWAA can afford not to, given where the risk concentration lies.

DCA and IAD are not like other hub airports. Both are operated under a compact authority that answers to four distinct political principals and sits in the direct sightline of Congress. Every major incident at DCA gets a call from a Senate staff director within two hours. Every disruption at IAD that affects international federal travel gets noticed at State and Defense. The reputational and political exposure from a mismanaged enterprise-scale incident — a cyber attack that crosses IT and Operations, a pandemic recurrence that requires coordinated business continuity across all departments, a major weather event with communication failures like Atlanta's — is asymmetrically larger at MWAA than at most peer airports precisely because of this political geography.

The LAX and ATL models give the CEO documented peer-hub precedents. Both survived their respective governance gauntlets — LAWA through city council oversight; ATL through mayoral appointment processes substantially similar in political complexity to MWAA's board structure. Both are currently operating this way without reported coordination failures between the enterprise EM function and Public Safety incident commanders. The CEO can cite these models rather than asking the board to approve an untested structure.

The FAA's 2023 SMS rule is the best available regulatory anchor. It places accountable executive responsibility for safety governance at the CEO level — which is legally and regulatorily inconsistent with an organizational structure where the person responsible for cross-departmental safety coordination reports two levels below the CEO, inside a single department. The SMS rule does not mandate EM elevation, but it creates the policy vocabulary for arguing that it should happen.

The grant argument is secondary but worth making. An enterprise EM function with dedicated staff for federal grant coordination is better positioned to access FEMA EMPG and BRIC dollars through Virginia and DC channels than one that currently operates without that dedicated capacity. These grants fund training, exercises, planning, and equipment — costs that would otherwise fall entirely on MWAA's operating budget.

### The Case Against

The honest counterargument is that the industry modal configuration — EM inside Public Safety — exists because most airport emergencies are operationally triggered events where fire, police, and EMS are the primary responders. NIMS works through Incident Command structures that sit in Public Safety regardless of where EM is organizationally housed. Pulling EM out does not change who runs the response at the incident level; it changes who sits at the executive coordination table. The risk is that creating a new executive-level EM function adds a coordination layer that the police and fire chiefs work around rather than through, because they were trained to work with each other and will continue to do so regardless of the org chart.

The internal political cost is real. The VP for Public Safety currently owns EM. Reorganizing it out from under that portfolio is a personnel move, not just a governance move, and it will be read as one. If the VP for Public Safety interprets this as a no-confidence signal, the CEO risks losing a senior leader or creating a lasting operational friction at the exact moment she needs Public Safety fully engaged. Virginia's seven board appointees, who have historically been attentive to public safety issues at MWAA, will hear about it and will ask questions. The CEO needs to frame this as an expansion of enterprise capability, not a criticism of Public Safety's leadership — and that framing is harder to sustain if the reorganization is perceived as removing authority rather than adding it.

The budget argument is minor but visible. Even a modest VP of Preparedness structure requires headcount and operating budget that will appear in the next board budget presentation. The finance and audit committee will notice it, even if the number is small.

## Specific Data Points and Precedents for the Strategist

**1. MWAA's 2024 financial metrics.** Debt service coverage 3.16x (up from 2.77x in 2023); 1,006 days cash on hand. These numbers place MWAA solidly in the Aa/AA band with cushion. Any argument that EM elevation is required to protect the credit rating is not credible and should not be made — the rating is not under stress. The correct argument is that these strong metrics give the CEO the balance sheet room to invest in enterprise resilience infrastructure without financial pressure, and that doing it proactively costs far less than doing it under duress after an incident. *Use this to preempt the budget objection, not to manufacture urgency that doesn't exist.* [Source: MWAA Investor Relations; Fitch US Airports Criteria 2025]

**2. Atlanta 2017: $50 million in Delta losses, 1,200 cancellations, 11 hours.** A single power outage with an organizational split between the airport and a private utility consortium produced the most expensive non-weather disruption at a US hub airport in the decade before COVID. No enforcement action, no regulatory mandate, no reorganization followed — but the structural gap that allowed it to happen (no single authority with cross-departmental reach) was documented by GAO five years later. The argument is not that the same thing will happen at MWAA; it is that the same structural gap currently exists, and the cost of closing it is trivial relative to the cost of the incident it prevents. [Source: CNBC; GAO-23-105203; AJC]

**3. LAX organizational structure.** The Deputy Executive Director for Operations and Emergency Management is a C-2 level position at LAWA, with EM as one of five peer divisions. This model has survived public-sector union consultation, city council oversight, and FAA certification review — the governance equivalent of MWAA's board approval process. LAWA is the closest operational and governance analog to what MWAA is considering. *If any board member asks whether this has been done before and whether it works, this is the answer.* [Source: LAWA.org organizational structure]

**4. FAA SMS accountable executive rule (effective April 24, 2023).** The rule designates the Airport Manager as accountable for safety resource allocation across the entire organization. This is not specific to EM, but it establishes the legal and regulatory principle that enterprise safety governance at a certificated airport runs to the CEO — not to any department head. An enterprise EM function reporting to the COO or CEO is consistent with this framework. One that reports to the VP of Public Safety is arguably in tension with its intent, even if technically compliant with its letter. *Use this as the regulatory anchor for the governance argument, not as a compliance argument.* [Source: Federal Register 2023-03597]

**5. The MWAA board governance clock.** Seventeen members, four appointing authorities, six-year staggered terms, annual chair election. The Virginia bloc at seven members is the largest single appointing authority and the most consequential political constituency for any governance-sensitive reorganization. The strategic window is the period immediately following a chair election in which the CEO has cultivated at least a working majority — nine of seventeen members — who understand the rationale before the proposal reaches the agenda. A CEO who brings this reorganization proposal to a board meeting without that preparation is not managing the political risk; she is creating it. *Map the board composition and appointment schedule before setting a timeline for this reorganization.* [Source: MWAA Board of Directors page; Virginia.gov authority listing]
