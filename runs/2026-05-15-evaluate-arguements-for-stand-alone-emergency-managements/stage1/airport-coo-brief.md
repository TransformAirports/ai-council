# Airport COO Brief: Organizational Placement of Emergency Management at MWAA
**Stage 1 Research Brief — Airport COO Lens**
**Run:** evaluate-arguments-for-stand-alone-emergency-management
**Date:** 2026-05-15

---

## Key Findings

1. **The biggest operational disruptions at a hub airport are not Public Safety incidents.** Major weather events, power grid failures, IT outages, fuel supply disruptions, and airline irregular operations require enterprise coordination across Facilities, IT, Communications, Finance, and the airline community — none of which sit in PSD's chain of command. Placing EM under PSD optimizes it for the 10% of disruptions where police or fire lead; it under-equips it for the 90%.

2. **Organizational placement shapes engagement. At hierarchical authorities like MWAA, reporting lines carry disproportionate weight.** When EM reports through PSD, every other division reads it as a PSD program. Facilities treats plan compliance as optional. IT doesn't send a representative to exercises. The airline relations team doesn't build IROPS coordination into their EM protocol. This is not a culture problem — it is a structure problem that culture cannot fix from inside PSD.

3. **The only airport system in the world with EMAP accreditation — Orlando International (GOAA) — structured its Emergency Management under the Vice President of Airport Operations, not under Public Safety.** That structure is not incidental. EMAP's 64 standards require cross-functional governance, enterprise-wide training, and coordination across all departments. You cannot demonstrate compliance with those standards when EM's authority is bounded by one division.

4. **The 2023 FAA Airport SMS rule creates an enterprise-level Accountable Executive with authority across operations, maintenance, and public safety.** Running SMS under the COO while EM sits under PSD produces two parallel enterprise risk functions with blind spots at their seam — hazard identification, risk prioritization, corrective action authority. These functions belong at the same organizational level.

5. **IROPS execution exposes the structural weakness faster than any other test.** In the first 90 minutes of a major weather event, the COO's desk is working gate reassignments, baggage belt prioritization, de-ice pad coordination, FIDS systems, and ground handler positioning. The Emergency Manager — if seated under PSD — has no direct authority over any of those levers. The COO becomes the de facto emergency coordinator by default, which is not a resilient model.

6. **COOP planning cannot function under PSD.** Continuity of Operations requires EM to define mission-essential functions, establish succession, and coordinate resource substitution across every department. A COOP developed under PSD will be treated by Facilities, IT, Finance, and HR as a PSD document — and it will be. ACRP Research Report 27915 (2024) identifies the COO or senior operations leadership as the logical executive sponsor for continuity precisely because these programs must span the enterprise.

7. **The argument has a real weakness that needs to be addressed before it reaches the CEO.** The Tennessee TEMA example cited in the internal memo is not accurate as stated. Current Tennessee statute places TEMA within the Tennessee Military Department; it is not a standalone agency. Using an inaccurate comparator undercuts a credible argument. There are better examples — including MCO's EMAP structure and several peer hub models — that carry more weight.

8. **The "VP of Preparedness" enhanced option is the stronger long-term model but requires careful boundary-setting.** SMS has a direct FAA regulatory line that must remain clean, and Engineering Safety carries professional credentials distinct from EM. Consolidation is achievable; it requires explicit authority maps, not just org chart changes.

## Evidence

### FAA Regulatory Framework

**14 CFR § 139.325** requires the Airport Emergency Plan to coordinate with law enforcement, ARFF, medical personnel, principal tenants, and "all other persons who have responsibilities under the plan." The regulatory text explicitly envisions an enterprise-wide function, not a PSD-bounded one. The airport operator — not the PSD director — bears the certification obligation. [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-G/part-139/subpart-D/section-139.325]

**FAA AC 150/5200-31C (Airport Emergency Plan)** requires the AEP to be "functionally oriented, comprehensive in the assignment of responsibilities, and coordinated at all levels." The AC places overall plan authority with the airport operator and requires coordination across the breadth of airport operations — not just emergency response agencies. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/resources/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.current/documentNumber/150_5200-31]

**FAA Airport SMS Final Rule (effective April 24, 2023)** requires a single Accountable Executive with "control of the certificate holder's human and financial resources" across operations, maintenance, and public safety for safety risk management purposes. Safety responsibility flows from this executive through all departments. This creates an enterprise risk function that is structurally parallel to EM — both require authority that crosses departmental lines. Splitting SMS under the COO and EM under PSD fragments enterprise risk governance. [Source: https://ballotpedia.org/Airport_Safety_Management_System_rule_(2023)]

### EMAP Accreditation

**EMAP's Emergency Management Standard (2022, Edition 5)** governs accreditation across 64 standards in 16 areas, including program management, administration and finance, planning, resource management, training, exercises, and incident management. The standard explicitly requires that "at a minimum, public, private, and non-governmental agencies, departments, organizations, and individuals that have functional roles in the Emergency Management are stakeholders." A program bounded by PSD cannot demonstrate that breadth of stakeholder engagement. [Source: https://emap.org/emergency-management-standard/]

**Orlando International Airport (MCO/GOAA)** became the first — and as of 2026, still the only — airport system in the world to achieve EMAP accreditation, earned in 2020 and maintained since. The program's EM function sits under the Vice President of Airport Operations, not under Public Safety. That is a direct refutation of the conventional "EM under fire/police" model. [Source: https://airportimprovement.com/article/orlando-international-airport-mco-receives-emergency-management-accreditation/]

### Peer Hub Structures

**Los Angeles World Airports (LAWA)** positions Emergency Management within an "Operations & Emergency Management" division — structurally separate from the Airport Police department. The Emergency Management Division is responsible for synchronizing all aspects of LAWA's Airport Response Coordination Center (ARCC) operations including planning, organization, equipment, training, exercises, and continuous improvement. [Source: https://www.lawa.org/groups-and-divisions/emergency-management]

**Metropolitan Airports Commission (MSP)** titles its Emergency Manager under "Integrated Operations," not Public Safety. The function's mandate covers natural disasters, aviation incidents, communicable diseases, and other emergencies — an enterprise scope that mirrors the argument in the thesis. [Source: https://metroairports.org/divisions-depts/emergency-management]

### ACRP Research

**ACRP Research Report (NCHRP Project 10-28, National Academies, 2024): "Integrating Crisis Management and Business Continuity at Airports"** explicitly identifies the COO or senior operations leadership as "logical positions to sponsor or champion the continuity program." It frames the EM/Risk Manager as the one who "will develop the capability, serve as an adviser throughout a crisis or disruption, and manage the coordinated response across the organization to ensure a rapid resumption of essential functions." That framing only works when EM has cross-organizational standing — which requires organizational placement above any single division. [Source: https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/27915/chapter/6]

The same guide identifies a critical pathology: airports that treat "continuity plans as a recovery function instead of as a response priority," creating gaps in crisis response — a pathology more likely when EM sits inside PSD, where cultural gravity pulls toward response over mitigation and continuity. [Source: https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/27915/chapter/4]

## The Operational Case For and Against

### The Case For — From the Operator's Chair

The events that break a hub airport are not aircraft accidents. Aircraft accidents we plan for. The events that break us have no single departmental owner: an ice storm that takes out the de-ice pad while the FIDS system locks up; a power failure that cascades to baggage because backup power doesn't reach carousel 7; a contractor breach that locks down Terminal B while gate holds pile up in Terminal A. In every one of those scenarios, the person with authority to compel Facilities, IT, Airlines, and Communications is the COO — not because that's the plan, but because no one else has the cross-departmental reach.

That is the structural problem the thesis is trying to solve. The Emergency Manager should fill that role before the COO has to. But when EM reports through PSD, it arrives at the table with the authority of one division — and every other division at the table knows it.

The operational case runs through exercises, too. Tabletops where Facilities sends a planner instead of a director because it's "a police exercise." IT declining to participate in full-scale drills because EM is seen as a PSD shop with no standing to compel IT's presence. When EM has COO backing, directors show up. That is not a culture fix — it is a structure fix.

COOP is the clearest case. A continuity plan requires EM to walk into HR, Finance, IT, Facilities, and every operational department and define their mission-essential functions. That conversation goes differently when EM has executive-level standing versus when it is a subset of one of the departments it is trying to govern.

The SMS parallel is worth the COO's attention. The 2023 Airport SMS rule requires an Accountable Executive at COO/CEO level to own enterprise safety risk. Running SMS under the COO and EM under PSD creates two parallel enterprise risk programs with different principals and different reporting lines. When the next incident review asks who was responsible for identifying the hazard, both programs will point at each other.

### The Case Against — Intellectually Honest

The counter-argument is not trivial, and the thesis doesn't engage it honestly enough.

First: In almost every declared incident, the Incident Commander is a PSD person — ARFF chief, police commander, or a unified command that PSD chairs. EM's role in an activated incident is coordination and resource management, not command. Separating EM from PSD can create a seam where EM thinks it is in charge and PSD knows it isn't. That ambiguity costs time in the first 20 minutes — which are the minutes that matter.

Second: PSD has the bodies. Police, fire, ARFF, dispatch — those are EM's primary partners in execution. An EM function that lives outside PSD has to work harder to maintain those relationships and risks being treated as an outside party during fast-moving events. The institutional trust that comes from shared command is not trivial.

Third: Budget and standing. Under PSD, EM benefits from a sworn department's political authority and budget history. A standalone function competes for resources against established operational departments. In lean budget years, small standalone functions are first to be restructured.

Fourth: The "VP of Preparedness" model consolidates too much too fast. SMS has a direct FAA regulatory relationship with the Accountable Executive. Engineering Safety personnel carry professional credentials and liability that differ from EM. Folding these under a single VP requires explicit authority maps — not just org chart changes — or the VP's authority in each domain will be contested.

These are not reasons to keep EM under PSD. They are reasons the thesis needs sharper language on authority deconfliction, particularly the ICS/Unified Command interface.

## Specific Examples That Frame the Thesis

**1. GOAA/MCO and EMAP — The Only Data Point That Matters**
Orlando International is the only airport in the world with EMAP accreditation. Its Emergency Management sits under the Vice President of Airport Operations — not under Public Safety. This is not a coincidence. EMAP requires the EM program to demonstrate cross-functional governance, enterprise-wide stakeholder engagement, and authority that spans all departments. The structure is the prerequisite for the accreditation. If MWAA wants to pursue EMAP or position itself as a national leader in resilience, the organizational structure follows from that goal, not the reverse.

**2. IROPS — The 90-Minute Test**
In a major weather event, the first 90 minutes require coordination of de-ice pad sequencing (Facilities and airlines), gate reassignments (Airport Operations and airlines), passenger notification (Communications), and stranded bag disposition (Facilities and ground handlers). The FAA controls airspace, the airlines control their metal, and the airport's Emergency Manager — under a PSD structure — has no direct authority over any of those operational levers. The operations director becomes the de facto coordinator. That is a structural failure dressed up as heroics. [Source: https://nap.edu/catalog/13647/guidebook-for-airport-irregular-operations-irops-contingency-planning]

**3. COOP — The Power Failure Scenario**
A utility failure at Dulles that takes the main terminal to emergency power for six hours requires EM to coordinate: backup power coverage for TSA screening (federal, not MWAA), gate assignments relying on power (airline relations), HVAC load management on backup generators (Facilities), FIDS on backup systems (IT), and passenger service staging (terminal operations). Not one of those functions is under PSD. EM under PSD can call those department heads. It cannot compel their resources. EM under the COO can.

**4. FAA Airport SMS — The Structural Seam**
MWAA's SMS Accountable Executive sits at the COO/senior leadership level per the 2023 FAA rule. SMS covers safety risk management across all operations. EM covers enterprise hazard identification and mitigation. Both should identify the same hazard set, coordinate on the same corrective actions, and feed the same executive dashboard. When SMS is under the COO and EM is under PSD, each builds its hazard register independently. In practice: the runway FOD risk in the SMS program and the airfield emergency response protocol in the AEP may never share a corrective action loop. That gap has both regulatory and operational consequences.

**5. The Tennessee Comparator — A Weakness to Fix**
The internal memo references Tennessee's legislation allowing TEMA to become its own standalone agency separate from the Military Department. Current Tennessee statute (T.C.A. § 58-2-101 et seq.) places TEMA within the Tennessee Military Department, with the TEMA Director reporting to the Adjutant General in non-emergency periods. If recent legislation has changed this, that legislation should be cited specifically with a bill number and effective date before it is used in an argument to MWAA leadership. Airport executives will check. Using an unsupported comparator in a brief to the CEO invites the entire argument to be dismissed on the strength of one bad footnote. Drop this example or cite it precisely.

## COO's Bottom Line Assessment

The thesis is operationally correct. Emergency Management at a hub airport is an enterprise function, not a departmental one. The disruptions that matter most — weather, infrastructure failure, IT, public health, airline operational collapse — require cross-departmental coordination that PSD's command structure cannot provide. The organizational solution is supported by GOAA/MCO, LAWA, MSP, ACRP research, and the FAA's own SMS rule structure.

Four things are needed before this reaches the CEO:

1. Remove or precisely cite the Tennessee comparator — airport executives will check, and a bad footnote can sink a credible argument.
2. Add ICS/Unified Command language: structural elevation does not change incident command primacy in declared events. EM coordinates; it does not command. Answer that objection before the PSD director raises it.
3. The VP of Preparedness option needs an authority map — what does the VP compel vs. coordinate vs. advise, and how does the FAA SMS regulatory line run through the structure?
4. Lead with MCO/EMAP. It is the most concrete proof of concept in the industry, from a peer hub, on the record. Skeptical executives respond to examples, not frameworks.

The structural argument is strong. The execution has cracks. Both are worth fixing.
