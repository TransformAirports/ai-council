# Contrarian Brief: The Case Against a Standalone Emergency Management Function at MWAA

**Stage 1 Research Brief — Contrarian Lens**
**Run:** Evaluate Arguments for Standalone Emergency Management
**Date:** 2026-05-15

---

> *This brief argues the opposing position as forcefully as the evidence allows. Its purpose is to stress-test the thesis before it reaches the COO and CEO. If the pro-standalone argument cannot survive these objections, it does not deserve to be published.*

---

## 1. The Strongest Version of the Pro-Subordination Argument

**The doctrine says the EOC serves the IC — not the other way around.** NIMS is unambiguous: the Emergency Operations Center provides operational support and resource acquisition for the Incident Commander. The IC holds tactical authority on-scene. At every commercial airport, that IC is a PSD officer — ARFF commander for aircraft emergencies, police for security incidents. An EM function elevated above PSD on the org chart does not change who runs the incident. What it does is introduce a structural ambiguity: during a declared emergency, whose authority governs? The senior EM executive at the COO level has no line authority over the IC. The result is not empowerment; it is a second voice with no operational leverage. [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7170197/]

**Authority without resources is political theater.** The thesis promises "organizational authority to engage change across all divisions." But what backs that authority? A standalone EM office at MWAA is realistically a staff of three to seven people — no sworn officers, no ARFF apparatus, no dispatch center. Under PSD, EM draws on the largest operational budget and staffing pool in the Authority. Elevated to executive level, it competes against PSD, Airports, and Engineering for budget share — with no union, no constituent advocacy, and no regulatory mandate that places EM spending ahead of ARFF certification or police staffing minimums. The Argonne National Laboratory's 2025 national study found that funding and staffing remain the top challenge for EM agencies, and that "challenges establishing agency priorities when directors lack the authority to do so" compound the problem. [Source: https://www.anl.gov/dis/npac/EMStudy]

**Standalone status is correlated with resource starvation, not empowerment.** ProPublica's reporting on the Argonne findings documents the pattern directly: standalone EM directors accumulate mission scope without corresponding resources. In Saluda County, South Carolina, the EM director's six-person team absorbed IT and a spay-and-neuter program. In Yancey County, North Carolina, the EM director requested additional staff for years — and commissioners only hired them after Hurricane Helene devastated the county in 2024. The structural dynamic is predictable: legislatures and executive teams grant EM independence as a cost-neutral gesture, then underfund it because the constituency pressure comes from police and fire, not emergency planners. Elevation at MWAA without a resource commitment locked at the board level repeats this error. [Source: https://www.propublica.org/article/emergency-managers-united-states-resource-needs]

**The "disengagement" problem is a leadership failure, not an org chart problem.** The memo argues that when EM reports through PSD, other divisions read it as a PSD program and disengage. This is a real phenomenon. But the mechanism is not the reporting line — it is the absence of a commanding EM leader with the credibility and relationships to pull every department into the exercise cycle and planning process. Elevating a weak EM function to the COO level does not make it credible; it makes it visible and scrutinized. A well-led EM program embedded in PSD, with an EM director who has standing relationships across Airport Operations, IT, Communications, and Facilities, will produce better cross-departmental engagement than a poorly-supported VP of Preparedness who wins the org chart battle and loses the room. The GAO's post-Katrina analysis of FEMA is instructive: "a number of factors may be ultimately more important to FEMA's success than its organizational placement" — including leadership quality, mission clarity, and resource adequacy. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/assets/a113814.html]

**The VP of Preparedness model is a structural experiment with no commercial aviation benchmark.** The memo presents the consolidated VP model — EM, risk management, SMS, and engineering safety under one executive — as forward-leaning modernization. But no major U.S. commercial airport authority operates this model in documented form. A search of organizational structures at LAX, ORD, DFW, ATL, DEN, and DCA/IAD finds EM functions housed variously under operations and public safety — not under a consolidated resilience executive. Proposing an untested model to the CEO and COO requires the proponent to demonstrate what success looks like before the structure is built, not after. The absence of peer benchmarks is not evidence of MWAA's leadership — it is a risk flag. [Source: https://www.lawa.org/groups-and-divisions/emergency-management]

**FAA Part 139 regulatory accountability is anchored in ARFF, not in strategic planning.** The regulatory framework for airport emergency response under 14 CFR 139.325 requires the airport emergency plan to establish command and control, define the relationship between the EOC and the IC, and ensure that first responder agencies — ARFF, police, medical — operate within a coordinated structure. ARFF is a 139 certification requirement. The institutional knowledge required to execute and exercise the AEP lives in PSD, not in a strategic planning office. Separating EM from PSD does not redistribute this knowledge — it creates a gap between the function responsible for writing and exercising the plan and the operational departments that have to execute it under live conditions. [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-G/part-139/subpart-D/section-139.325]

**Creating a new executive-level function adds organizational overhead without guaranteed return.** A VP of Preparedness requires a VP-level salary, administrative staff, a defined budget line, and a place in the executive decision-making structure. At an authority of 1,750 employees operating two major airports with a billion-dollar capital program, a new C-minus-1 position competes for executive attention with airports, engineering, finance, and legal. The cost-benefit argument has to clear a high bar: not "would this be better than the current state," but "is this the highest-value use of the organizational investment?" The memo does not attempt this calculation.

**The "perceptions" argument cuts in both directions.** The memo states that other departments perceive EM as a PSD program when it reports through PSD. But elevation to executive status carries its own perception risk: departments that have managed their own continuity plans, safety programs, and incident protocols may perceive a standalone EM executive as a compliance function — an inspector who arrives at exercises with a clipboard, not a partner. Executive placement does not guarantee cross-departmental trust. It may accelerate defensiveness in departments that already believe they handle their own emergencies adequately.

## 2. Evidence Section

### On Organizational Placement and Effectiveness

The most directly relevant empirical study is the Argonne National Laboratory's national survey of emergency management organizational structures, released in 2025 in partnership with IAEM, NEMA, and FEMA. The study found that local agencies operating as standalone entities reported a higher likelihood of successfully meeting community needs compared to those under other departments. [Source: https://globalcmrpt.wordpress.com/2024/08/18/emergency-management-organizational-structures-staffing-and-capacity-study/]

This finding, which the memo's proponents will cite, deserves careful reading. The comparison is between standalone agencies and agencies embedded within operational departments — police, fire, public works. The mechanism driving the positive outcome for standalone agencies is most plausibly dedicated staff, clear mission, and unambiguous budget lines — not the metaphysics of where the director sits on the org chart. MWAA's question is whether those enabling conditions follow automatically from elevation, or whether they must be deliberately constructed and funded regardless of reporting line.

The same study flags the resource dynamic that cuts against elevation: more than half of local EM agencies have one or fewer full-time permanent employees, with staffing constraints, funding gaps, and role ambiguity as the top challenges. Agencies that won organizational independence without commensurate resource commitments did not become more effective — they became more isolated. [Source: https://www.anl.gov/dis/npac/EMStudy]

### On NIMS, ICS, and the EOC-IC Relationship

FEMA's NIMS guidance is explicit about the relationship between the EOC and the IC. The IC handles tactical activities on-scene. The EOC handles operational and strategic coordination, resource acquisition, and information sharing. The EOC supports the IC — it does not command the IC. [Source: https://www.fema.gov/emergency-managers/nims/components]

At airports, this creates a specific organizational reality: the FAA's command and control guidance for Part 139 facilities establishes a "blended structure, with the IC on-scene, and an EOC commander in the EOC designated as Deputy IC." [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7170197/] The IC in this structure is almost always a PSD officer. Whether EM reports to PSD or to the COO does not change who has operational authority when the alarm sounds.

### On the FEMA Precedent

GAO testimony (GAO-06-746T) delivered after Hurricane Katrina addressed directly whether FEMA's organizational placement within DHS caused its failures. The GAO's conclusion was carefully stated: a number of factors may be ultimately more important to FEMA's success than its organizational placement — including the clarity of FEMA's mission, its related responsibilities and authorities, and the experience of FEMA leadership. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/assets/a113814.html]

The lesson is not that organizational placement is irrelevant. It is that organizational placement is neither sufficient nor necessary for EM effectiveness. The post-Katrina debate produced FEMA remaining within DHS — with a direct reporting line to the DHS Secretary and enhanced authorities. The fix was resources, leadership, and legal authorities — not a standalone box on the org chart.

### On Airport-Specific EM Structures

LAWA (Los Angeles World Airports) maintains an Emergency Management division at the department level, integrated within its overall organizational structure rather than at a VP of Preparedness equivalent. [Source: https://www.lawa.org/groups-and-divisions/emergency-management] Chicago O'Hare's Aviation Emergency Management Coordinator classification is coded under the public safety family in the city's position classification system. [Source: https://www.chicago.gov/content/dam/city/depts/dhr/supp_info/JobSpecifications/PublicSafetyServices/8600_EmergencyCommunicationsSeries/8630_AVIATION_EMERGENCY_MANAGEMENT_COORDINATOR.pdf] These are not arguments that peer structures are correct — they are evidence that the case for departing from industry practice carries the burden of proof.

## 3. Specific Scenarios Where the Thesis Is Wrong

**Scenario 1: The high-severity incident.** A Category IV ARFF response at Dulles — aircraft down, mass casualty event, mutual aid from six jurisdictions activated. The IC is the ARFF commander. The EOC is activated. The new VP of Preparedness is in the EOC, coordinating across departments. But the operational decisions — apparatus deployment, medical staging, airfield access — run through the IC. The VP of Preparedness's cross-departmental coordination authority, so compelling in the org chart presentation, is irrelevant in the first four hours of the incident. The people in the room who matter are the ARFF commander, the police captain, and the airport duty manager. Organizational elevation did not change this arithmetic.

**Scenario 2: The budget cycle.** The VP of Preparedness model is approved. EM, risk management, SMS, and engineering safety are consolidated under a new executive. In year two of a capital program that has absorbed a $400M cost overrun, the COO is directed to reduce administrative overhead by 12%. The VP of Preparedness, with no sworn personnel, no aircraft, and no certified ARFF function, is the softest target in the organizational structure. The consolidation that was presented as strengthening preparedness has created a single point of failure: one executive whose departure or budget reduction strips all four functions simultaneously.

**Scenario 3: The SMS integration.** Safety Management Systems require an independent reporting channel to the accountable executive — the Accountable Manager in FAA SMS parlance — to be credible. Consolidating SMS under a VP of Preparedness who also owns EM and risk management creates a structural conflict: the function responsible for identifying systemic safety risks reports through the same executive who manages the response to those risks. Independence is not enhanced — it is compromised.

**Scenario 4: The EMAP accreditation path.** The memo invokes EMAP accreditation as evidence that cross-functional governance requires executive-level EM placement. The EMAP standard does not specify organizational placement. It requires that the EM program demonstrate cross-functional coordination, documented planning, and exercised capabilities — all achievable with strong leadership regardless of where EM reports. Seeking EMAP accreditation while under PSD, with documented cross-departmental participation in planning and exercises, would be a more compelling proof-of-concept than a structural reorganization followed by a future accreditation attempt. [Source: https://emap.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/EMAP-EMS-5-2022-Emergency-Management-Standard.pdf]

## 4. Concessions the Strategist Must Address

These are not strawmen. They are the sharpest points in the counterargument.

**Concession 1:** *"The Argonne study found standalone EM agencies report higher effectiveness — but the study's positive finding applies to jurisdictions where standalone status came with dedicated staff and clear mission. MWAA's proposal to elevate EM without a binding resource commitment replicates the structural form without the enabling conditions."* If the memo cannot specify exactly what headcount, budget, and authorities attach to the elevated function, the Argonne finding is being misapplied.

**Concession 2:** *"NIMS doctrine places the EOC in support of the IC — and at MWAA, the IC will always be a PSD officer for the incidents that matter most. No organizational chart changes this."* The strategist must explain how executive-level EM changes the operational outcome, not just the strategic planning calendar.

**Concession 3 (direct quote, GAO-06-746T):** *"While a number of factors may be ultimately more important to FEMA's success than its organizational placement, these include the clarity of FEMA's mission and its related responsibilities and authorities, and the experience of and training provided to FEMA leadership."* If the FEMA precedent — the most studied organizational EM placement in history — cannot establish that placement is the primary driver of effectiveness, the MWAA memo must explain why a smaller, more resource-constrained airport authority would produce a different result.

**Concession 4:** *"A VP of Preparedness consolidating EM, risk management, SMS, and engineering safety under one executive creates a single point of organizational failure. MWAA must explain how this model maintains SMS independence — specifically the direct reporting channel to the Accountable Manager required for credible safety oversight."* This is not a theoretical concern. FAA SMS framework depends on the safety function being positioned to surface risks that implicate the same executives who manage day-to-day operations.

**Concession 5:** *"No peer commercial airport authority in the United States operates a documented VP of Preparedness model of the type proposed. The memo's recommendation asks the MWAA CEO and COO to approve a structural experiment. The burden of proof falls on the proponent to demonstrate how MWAA will manage the risk of being the first — not the first institution to elevate EM, but the first to do it this way, at this scale, at an authority of this regulatory complexity."*

---

## Conclusion: What This Argument Needs to Survive

The pro-standalone EM argument is sound on first principles. Emergency management has become a distinct discipline. Organizational placement does shape perceived authority. Cross-departmental coordination is genuinely impeded when EM is perceived as a PSD subsidiary.

But the argument as currently framed rests on three unstated assumptions that the counterargument will expose:

1. That elevation automatically produces the enabling conditions — staff, budget, authority — rather than requiring them to be explicitly negotiated and protected.
2. That organizational placement drives EM effectiveness more than leadership quality, relationship capital, and resource adequacy.
3. That the VP of Preparedness consolidation is self-evidently coherent rather than a structural experiment that combines functions with different independence requirements and different stakeholder audiences.

The memo does not have to abandon the thesis. It has to answer these three questions before it reaches the COO's desk. If it cannot, a more defensible position is the baseline recommendation — EM reporting to the COO directly, with a defined budget and headcount guarantee — rather than the enhanced VP model that opens four new lines of attack simultaneously.
