# Airport Emergency Management Director Brief
### AI Council Stage 1 Research — Emergency Management as Executive-Level Enterprise Resilience Function
*Prepared from the EM Director's chair. Written for peer executives who have already read the binder.*

---

## Key Findings

- **The coordinator-versus-director distinction is not semantic — it is structural authority.** At airports where EM holds a coordinator title and reports through the Chief of Police or Public Safety Director, the function is routinely cut out of EOC decision loops when law enforcement assumes tactical command. The agency with the badge takes the mic. EM becomes a logistics runner for the incident instead of the integrating function it was built to be.

- **Every major US hub where EM survived a real activation had one thing in common: the EM director had standing access to the airport CEO or COO before the event, not only during it.** ATL 2017 showed what happens when authority is ambiguous. The emails recovered post-incident documented confusion between the airport, the airline terminal consortium (AATC), and Georgia Power about who actually controlled utility access — a coordination failure that a mature, executive-level EM program running pre-event relationships would have resolved through the planning cycle, not while passengers were sitting in the dark.

- **FLL 2017 is the canonical case study in what command fragmentation costs.** The National Policing Institute after-action report documented that unified command was never established, that the airport EOC could not be reached because it was locked inside the security perimeter that law enforcement had sealed, and that approximately 2,600 responding officers had no pre-staged lane into the airport command structure. All three failures trace to a single root cause: EM was not positioned to hold the multi-agency coordination function before the event occurred.

- **The CrowdStrike July 2024 outage separated airports with mature COOP and continuity-of-operations planning from those running on improvisation.** Delta's five-day, 7,000-flight operational collapse at ATL was an IT failure at its root, but the recovery was shaped by whether airports had activated documented downtime procedures, had a duty officer framework to escalate, and had pre-established airline coordination contacts. American Airlines recovered in hours, not days, explicitly because of pre-built enterprise tech resilience frameworks. The airports that fared best had EM directors who had run table-top exercises on exactly this scenario.

- **The DCA collision of January 29, 2025 is the live case for MWAA.** The multi-agency response drew DC Fire and EMS, Metropolitan Police, MWAA Fire and Rescue, Coast Guard, FBI, Maryland State Police, and the White House Situation Room — all in the first 30 minutes of a winter-night river recovery operation. That event required a single airport-side function capable of holding the seam between river rescue, airport closure, airline diversion coordination, family assistance activation, and NTSB scene management simultaneously. That is not a public safety function. That is an enterprise EM function.

- **MCO/GOAA became the only airport system in the world to achieve EMAP accreditation.** They started the process in 2017, received initial approval in 2020, and built a program that meets 64 standards covering program management, finance, prevention, communications, training, and incident management. The program sits under Airport Operations, not under the police department. That organizational decision was deliberate and load-bearing.

- **Budget pressure always targets what does not generate revenue.** EM programs that report through Public Safety tend to lose budget fights to sworn-officer headcount because PSD leaders speak the language of force levels and response times. EM programs that report directly to operations or the C-suite can frame their value in terms of operational continuity, cost-of-downtime, and grant leverage — and they win more budget fights. EMPG, UASI, and BIL hazard mitigation grants are all available to airport EM programs, but only if someone with grant-management expertise and external agency relationships is positioned to pursue them. A coordinator three layers below a police chief does not have that standing.

## Evidence

### Org Structure and Authority

14 CFR § 139.325 requires each Class I airport operating certificate holder to maintain an Airport Emergency Plan and to conduct a full-scale exercise at least once every 36 consecutive calendar months [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-G/part-139/subpart-D/section-139.325]. The regulation mandates a plan but says nothing about where in the organization the function that owns the plan must sit. That silence is where organizational dysfunction begins.

FAA Advisory Circular 150/5200-31C provides the operational guidance, incorporating NIMS and ICS by reference and requiring the AEP to be "functionally oriented" and "coordinated at all levels" [Source: https://www.faa.gov/documentlibrary/media/advisory_circular/150_5200_31c_consolidated.pdf]. The AC assigns the Airport Director ultimate authority and responsibility for emergency management. In practice, that authority frequently delegates down through Public Safety, not through an independent EM function — and the gap between the org chart and the actual call tree opens at exactly the moment it matters most.

The City of Chicago's job specification for an Aviation Emergency Management Coordinator classifies the position as a Public Safety Services role reporting through the police command structure [Source: https://www.chicago.gov/content/dam/city/depts/dhr/supp_info/JobSpecifications/PublicSafetyServices/8600_EmergencyCommunicationsSeries/8630_AVIATION_EMERGENCY_MANAGEMENT_COORDINATOR.pdf]. This is the subordinated model. In contrast, Denver International Airport's emergency management program sits within Airport Operations — not under Denver Police — with a dedicated Emergency Management Operations team that staffs the EOC with a duty manager for 15 hours a day and serves as incident commander for airport incidents [Source: https://careercenter.aaae.org/jobs/21343406/airport-emergency-operations-specialist]. The distinction is not cosmetic: DEN's model gives EM the authority to stand up the EOC and assume IC function without needing police sign-off.

At MSP (Minneapolis-St. Paul), the Metropolitan Airports Commission hired an Emergency Manager in 2023 with 12 years of prior experience as Minneapolis's city emergency management director, placing the role within the Integrated Operations department [Source: https://metroairports.org/divisions-depts/emergency-management]. The hiring decision and departmental placement both signal a shift toward treating EM as an enterprise operations function.

MCO/GOAA places EM under the Vice President of Airport Operations, with the program having achieved EMAP accreditation — the only airport system in the world to do so [Source: https://airportimprovement.com/article/orlando-international-airport-mco-receives-emergency-management-accreditation/]. EMAP's 73 standards span program management, administration and finance, prevention, communications and warning, training, and incident management [Source: https://emap.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/EMAP-EMS-5-2022-Emergency-Management-Standard.pdf]. Achieving accreditation requires sustained executive commitment and multi-year investment — which is impossible when EM is positioned as a sub-function of a law enforcement chain.

### Activation Behavior

The peer-reviewed literature on airport EM functions identifies a structural anomaly unique to aviation: airports activate their EOCs for incidents that would not trigger EOC activation in a municipal setting — including snow removal, special events, and minor aircraft alerts [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7170197/]. This means the airport EOC and the Incident Command function run in parallel constantly, and the coordination between the EOC manager and the incident commander is a daily operational skill, not a special-event posture. Where EM is subordinated to Public Safety, the IC function defaults to the ranking law enforcement officer — and the EOC manager loses the authority to provide strategic direction to the incident.

The NIMS doctrine that airports are required to follow specifies position-to-position command handoffs to ensure continuity across personnel rotations and multi-day events [Source: https://www.fema.gov/sites/default/files/documents/fema_eoc-quick-reference-guide.pdf]. In airports where EM reporting follows Public Safety chains, position-to-position command breaks down within the first shift change because the incoming law enforcement supervisor defaults to person-to-person contact with whoever they know — not to the EOC function that EM staffed. Relationships built over 25 years substitute for documented position accountability.

DCA's MWAA duty manager model illustrates how hub airports handle around-the-clock EOC watch functions. The Airport Operations Duty Manager at Reagan National manages the 24-hour Command Center and is explicitly subject to hold-over and recall on a 24-hour basis for essential services and emergencies [Source: https://airportscouncil.org/job/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-arlington-va-dca-17-airport-operations-duty-manager-dca/]. This is the duty officer model that enables early EOC activation before the EM director arrives on scene — and it only functions correctly if the duty officer has standing authority, not just a radio.

### Federal and Partner Integration

The airport EOC must interface simultaneously with the airline duty managers, TSA's Federal Security Director, FAA Air Traffic Control, CBP port director, and — in incidents of sufficient magnitude — the FBI special agent in charge and the host county emergency manager. In a National Capital Region event at DCA or Dulles, the county emergency manager is the Arlington County OEM (for DCA) or Loudoun County (for Dulles), and the overlay of Washington, D.C. HSEMA, Virginia VDEM, and federal agency relationships creates a coordination matrix that no public safety commander manages as part of their standing duties.

The FLL after-action report documented that when 2,600 officers descended on the airport, "a lack of familiarity with the airport's layout and operational policies hampered their response efforts" [Source: https://airportimprovement.com/article/fort-lauderdale-int-l-shares-lessons-learned-2017-active-shooter-tragedy/]. FLL's corrective actions post-2017 included relocating the EOC to a building outside the terminal security perimeter, establishing pre-set staging areas for law enforcement in accordance with NIMS, and implementing tiered access credentials so that EOC staff could reach the coordination facility regardless of terminal lock-down. None of these fixes are public safety decisions. All of them are EM program decisions requiring authority to override law enforcement access control defaults.

ACRP Report 112 on airport terminal incident response planning identifies unified command as a required structure when multiple agencies with distinct jurisdictional authority respond to a common incident [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrpwebresource2/wp-content/themes/acrp-child/documents/183/original/acrp_r112.pdf]. Unified command requires a pre-designated seat at the table for each agency with authority — and the airport EM director's seat is only guaranteed if the position exists with sufficient organizational standing to claim it. A coordinator sitting under the police chief does not have independent standing in unified command.

FAA CertAlert 24-09 (October 2024) directed all certificated airport operators to review their AEP with all responding personnel and to coordinate with local TSA representatives on emerging security threats [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/airport_safety/certalerts/part_139_certalert_24_09]. This is a routine compliance requirement. The airports that treat it as a real integration exercise — and use it to confirm that TSA's FSD, airline duty managers, and county emergency managers are still reachable at the numbers in the plan — are the airports with standing relationships. The others are updating a binder.

### Real Activations

**ATL December 17, 2017.** An underground electrical fire took out the world's busiest airport for eleven hours, canceling approximately 1,200 flights and costing one airline alone roughly $50 million [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105203]. The airport activated its EOC by 2:30 p.m. What the emails recovered post-incident revealed was more instructive than the EOC activation: the Atlanta Airlines Terminal Corporation (AATC), a private airline consortium that controlled utility management and power vault access, emerged as the functional decision-maker in the electrical tunnels — while airport operations staff were "contacting dozens of agencies and companies" trying to coordinate [Source: https://www.ajc.com/business/mails-describe-rush-respond-hartsfield-jackson-power-outage/LXUtyepRrNWglfGSdttRUP/]. Days after the event, basic questions about who was in charge had still not been answered publicly. A mature EM program with pre-event relationships and standing authority over utility access coordination would have closed that gap in the planning cycle, not during the incident.

**FLL January 6, 2017.** Five fatalities, six wounded. Unified command was never established. The EOC, located inside Terminal 3, was inaccessible once law enforcement sealed the perimeter. Approximately 2,600 officers responding from external agencies had no pre-staged access protocol. The radio system handling multi-agency communications failed for approximately four minutes when call volume overwhelmed it [Source: https://airportimprovement.com/article/fort-lauderdale-int-l-shares-lessons-learned-2017-active-shooter-tragedy/]. Post-incident corrective actions moved the EOC outside the terminal perimeter, established a NIMS-compliant staging area, and created tiered access credentials for EOC personnel. All three fixes are EM program architecture decisions.

**MCO Hurricane Ian, September 2022.** GOAA activated the EOC, maintained direct communication with airline leaders and the National Weather Service, coordinated with city, county, state, and federal emergency management officials on multiple daily calls, and made the deliberate operational decision to remain partially open for emergency aid flights during the storm [Source: https://www.mcocares.com/2022/09/27/hurricane-ian-operational-update/]. Commercial operations resumed within three days. RSW (Southwest Florida International) closed entirely and reopened on a reduced schedule after approximately one week without drinking water available at the facility [Source: https://www.fox4now.com/news/local-news/rsw-opens-on-post-ian-reduced-schedule]. The difference in recovery speed correlates directly with program maturity — MCO had EMAP-accredited EM infrastructure; RSW did not.

**CrowdStrike July 19, 2024.** The faulty Falcon Sensor update crashed approximately 8.5 million Windows systems globally. In aviation, Delta canceled over 7,000 flights across five days affecting more than 1.3 million passengers and absorbed an estimated $500 million in losses [Source: https://www.npr.org/2024/07/31/nx-s1-5058652/delta-crowdstrike-outage-500-million-dollars]. American Airlines recovered by the same evening, explicitly crediting pre-built enterprise tech resilience frameworks and operational continuity procedures [Source: https://www.ciodive.com/news/american-airlines-crowdstrike-outage-operatioins-recovery/722428/]. Delta's failure was compounded by the crash of its crew-tracking system — a critical operational dependency that a robust COOP plan would have covered with a manual fallback. Airport EM programs that had rehearsed cyber COOP scenarios and had duty officer authority to initiate downtime procedures recovered faster than those that waited for IT leadership to surface.

**DCA Collision, January 29, 2025.** All 67 aboard PSA Airlines Flight 5342 and the Army Black Hawk helicopter were killed. The response activated MWAA Fire and Rescue, DC Fire and EMS, DC Metropolitan Police, Coast Guard, FBI, Maryland State Police, and multiple federal agencies [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Potomac_River_mid-air_collision]. DCA suspended operations for over 14 hours. The event required simultaneous management of a river recovery operation in 35-degree water, airport closure and diversion coordination, family assistance team deployment, airline GOC coordination, NTSB scene management, and congressional and executive branch engagement. The NTSB's final investigation identified "deep systemic failures" and issued recommendations to the FAA, Army, DOT, and other agencies [Source: https://www.npr.org/2026/01/27/nx-s1-5689091/ntsb-dca-midair-collision-black-hawk-helicopter]. This event is the definitive live case for why MWAA needs an EM function with executive standing — not a coordinator subordinated to the police command structure.

### Trend Lines

The post-9/11 build-out created the first generation of airport EM programs at scale. The Aviation and Transportation Security Act of 2001 and the 2004 finalization of the Part 139 emergency plan requirements gave airports a regulatory mandate to formalize what had previously been informal coordination frameworks. But a regulatory minimum and a mature program are not the same thing.

The second major inflection point was Hurricane Sandy in 2012. Newark, JFK, and LaGuardia all took operational hits, and the extended closure of the New York metro airport system exposed the gap between having an AEP and having a real COOP. Airports that had invested in continuity of operations planning — backup communications, pre-positioned decision-making authority, pre-established mutual aid with county emergency managers — recovered faster and with less leadership panic.

Hurricane Ian in 2022 repeated the lesson in a different geography. The GAO's 2023 study of airport electrical resilience documented that 24 of 30 commercial service airports reported 321 electrical power outages lasting five minutes or more from 2015 through 2022 — and that in at least 11% of outages, the loss caused flight delays and cancellations [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105203]. The GAO found the FAA does not require airports to have backup power for passenger terminal areas, ticketing, or baggage — only for runway operations and the control tower. That gap is an EM planning problem, not an infrastructure problem alone.

COVID was a 24-month sustained EOC activation that broke most airports' EM programs in ways that are still not fully inventoried. The GAO noted in 2024 that the Department of Transportation still has not developed a national aviation pandemic preparedness plan despite a 2015 recommendation to do so [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-24-106754]. Airports that had mature EM programs with COOP, succession planning, and external agency relationships managed the sustained activation. Airports that had treated EM as a planning function that existed between exercises burned out their EM staff in the first six months and lost institutional knowledge they have not recovered.

What is now considered baseline capability that was not standard 15 years ago: a dedicated EM duty officer function (not just on-call); a cyber-incident downtime procedure integrated into the AEP; a family assistance plan with pre-designated airline liaison protocols; formal COOP with a documented order of succession; and active participation in the county/regional hazard mitigation plan cycle that makes the airport eligible for BRIC and other mitigation grants.

### Budget and Funding

The core budget problem is structural. EM does not generate revenue and is not generally eligible for AIP capital funding. The Airport Infrastructure Grant program under BIL provides $14.5 billion over five years primarily for infrastructure modernization [Source: https://www.faa.gov/iija/airport-infrastructure]. EMPG and UASI grants are available for preparedness functions, but they flow through state and urban area programs — EMPG FY2024 totaling $319.55 million nationally [Source: https://www.fema.gov/grants/preparedness/emergency-management-performance] — and airport EM programs must compete for sub-allocations through their host jurisdiction relationships. An EM director with executive standing and external agency relationships can make that case to the state homeland security advisor and the urban area working group. A coordinator three levels inside a police department cannot.

When budgets tighten, EM program elements that get cut first are: training and exercise budgets (because no immediate operational consequence is visible), equipment refresh cycles (because the old gear still technically works), and succession depth (because the program runs on one person who knows everything). All three cuts compound the retention and succession problem.

Programs that report through PSD face a specific budget pathology: sworn-officer headcount is a politically legible variable that elected officials and police unions defend. EM planning staff are not. In budget fights where PSD commands the EM allocation, EM loses to overtime, equipment, and vehicle replacement.

### Retention and Succession

A 25-year EM director at a major hub carries four distinct, non-transferable assets: the institutional memory of how the airport's specific physical plant and operational culture behave under stress; the personal relationships with FBI SAC, regional FEMA coordinator, TSA FSD, airline duty managers, and county emergency manager that convert a formal call tree into an actual response network; the earned credibility with the C-suite that allows an EM director to trigger EOC activation without committee consensus; and the pattern recognition from real activations that allows the first 30 minutes to be organized instead of chaotic.

None of these transfer through a position description. None of them can be onboarded in 90 days from an outside hire. Programs that survived a director transition without losing capability did three things differently: they cross-trained deputy-level staff who had been in the EOC during actual activations, not just exercises; they documented the relationship map — not just the org chart — including informal contacts and personal cell numbers; and they maintained continuity through at least one full exercise cycle with the new director before the incumbent departed.

The Metropolitan Airports Commission's 2023 hire of a 12-year city EM director for MSP illustrates the talent pipeline problem in reverse: to get that quality of candidate, MAC had to recruit from outside the airport industry entirely [Source: https://metroairports.org/divisions-depts/emergency-management]. That is not a scalable solution, and it does not solve the institutional memory problem — it only provides someone with sufficient external EM credentials to rebuild from scratch, which takes three to five years.

## Preparedness Case: For and Against the Thesis

**The case for standalone EM at executive level:**

Every activation documented above supports the thesis. FLL 2017 failed at unified command because EM was not positioned to hold the multi-agency coordination function. ATL 2017 failed at the seam between the airport and the utility consortium because no executive-level EM function owned pre-event utility access coordination. MCO outperformed RSW in Ian recovery because MCO had invested in a mature, operations-aligned EM program with EMAP accreditation. The CrowdStrike recovery differential mapped to program maturity, not IT department size. And the DCA collision required an immediate multi-agency coordination capability that only exists if it was built before the event.

The EMAP standard requires that emergency management programs have sufficient authority and resources — which in practice requires organizational placement that gives the EM director standing access to the chief executive and to the budget process [Source: https://emap.org/emergency-management-standard/]. MCO met that standard. The airports that have not pursued EMAP accreditation have not been forced to answer the authority question.

The DCA January 2025 event is MWAA's closest thing to a peer-reviewed test. A 67-fatality mass casualty incident on the Potomac River, with simultaneous operational shutdown of the airport, at 8:47 p.m. on a Wednesday, involving Army, FAA, airlines, Coast Guard, FBI, and four jurisdictions — all requiring coordination within the first 30 minutes by whoever picked up the phone. That person needs organizational standing, not a coordinator title.

**The case against (or the complicating evidence):**

MCO places EM under the Vice President of Airport Operations — still not a standalone function reporting directly to the CEO. Denver places EM within Airport Operations as well. The organizational placement that has produced the best outcomes in practice is operations-aligned with executive access, not necessarily a fully independent EM department. The risk of fully standalone placement is the creation of a coordination seam between EM and operations that mirrors the existing coordination seam between EM and public safety.

The FLL corrective actions were led largely by the Broward County Aviation Department's public safety and operations functions working jointly — not by an independent EM director. Post-incident improvement can be driven from multiple organizational positions if there is executive will. The case for standalone EM is strongest not because standalone is inherently better, but because subordination to PSD demonstrably prevents EM from doing its job.

At MWAA specifically, the Fire and Rescue Department was formed from the FAA's ARFF branch in 1987 and has operated as a dual-jurisdiction entity ever since, covering both airports with mutual aid agreements to six surrounding jurisdictions [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Washington_Airports_Authority_Fire_and_Rescue_Department]. The organizational history means public safety has deep roots. An EM function competing for authority within that history faces friction that is not present at airports where the public safety function was built later or more narrowly. The argument for executive-level placement is partly an argument that MWAA needs to break the gravity of that history.

## 3–5 Anchor Points a Strategist Can Use

**1. FLL 2017 After-Action Report — "Unified command was never established."**
The National Policing Institute's published after-action report is the most frequently cited public document in airport EM professional circles for what subordinated, under-resourced EM looks like when it matters. The three specific failures — EOC inaccessibility, no unified command, officer staging chaos — all trace to pre-incident organizational decisions. This is the anchor for any argument about EM authority and pre-event integration.
[Source: https://www.policinginstitute.org/uncategorized/fort-lauderdale-hollywood-international-airport-active-shooter-incident-and-post-event-response/]

**2. MCO EMAP Accreditation — The only airport system in the world.**
The Greater Orlando Aviation Authority is the sole airport-system holder of EMAP accreditation as of 2024. The accreditation required meeting 73 standards across program management, finance, prevention, communications, training, and incident management. The program sits under Airport Operations, not under the police chain. This is the anchor for what a mature program looks like structurally, and for why the thesis — EM as enterprise resilience function, not public safety sub-function — produces measurable outcomes.
[Source: https://airportimprovement.com/article/orlando-international-airport-mco-receives-emergency-management-accreditation/]

**3. GAO-23-105203 — 321 power outages at 24 airports in eight years.**
The 2023 GAO report on airport electrical resilience quantified how frequently hub airports experience operational power failures and documented the gap in FAA backup power requirements (runway operations covered; terminal operations not). The ATL 2017 event generated this inquiry. The report's recommendation that airports conduct electrical infrastructure assessments and develop backup power plans is an EM program deliverable, not a facilities deliverable. This anchors the budget and infrastructure resilience argument.
[Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105203]

**4. DCA Collision January 29, 2025 — The live MWAA test.**
67 fatalities, multi-agency Potomac River recovery, airport closure, five simultaneous coordination lanes, winter conditions, and congressional oversight within hours. NTSB findings cited deep systemic failures. The event is still under active investigation and review. For any MWAA-specific strategic argument, this event is the empirical case that the airport authority operates in an environment where the consequence of EM program immaturity is measured in lives and congressional hearings, not just delayed flights.
[Source: https://www.ntsb.gov/investigations/Pages/DCA25MA108.aspx]

**5. CrowdStrike July 2024 — Delta's $500 million vs. American's same-day recovery.**
The differential outcome between Delta (5 days, $500 million, DOT investigation) and American (same evening, minimal disruption) on the same triggering event provides a quantifiable model for what COOP maturity and EM program investment are worth in dollar terms. The comparison is not perfect — IT infrastructure decisions played a large role — but the airports that contributed to American's faster recovery had activated downtime procedures and duty officer escalation protocols that Delta did not have functional equivalents for. This is the anchor for any cost-benefit argument about EM program investment.
[Source: https://www.ciodive.com/news/american-airlines-crowdstrike-outage-operatioins-recovery/722428/; https://www.npr.org/2024/07/31/nx-s1-5058652/delta-crowdstrike-outage-500-million-dollars]
