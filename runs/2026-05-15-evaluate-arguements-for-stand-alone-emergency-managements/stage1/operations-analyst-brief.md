## Key Findings

1. **The first national study on EM organizational structures (Argonne National Laboratory/IAEM/NEMA/FEMA, July 2025) found that local emergency management agencies operating as standalone entities reported a higher likelihood of successfully meeting community needs compared to those embedded in other departments.** Of state EM directors surveyed, only 27% report directly to their governor; 59% are at a second tier of separation; 14% are three or more levels removed. The study explicitly flagged authority ambiguity as a persistent challenge when directors lack standing to set agency priorities across departments. [Source: https://www.anl.gov/dis/npac/EMStudy]

2. **FAA Part 139.325 requires annual coordination of the Airport Emergency Plan with law enforcement, ARFF, EMS, air carriers, media, airport tenants, and hospitals — plus a full-scale exercise every 36 months involving all parties.** This regulatory mandate creates an enterprise-wide coordination obligation that cannot be executed credibly from within the Public Safety Department when roughly half the required parties (tenants, airlines, media, hospitals) are not PSD subordinates. EM programs inside PSD hold informal authority to request participation from peer or superior departments; standalone EM programs hold structural authority. [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/139.325]

3. **The FAA Airport Safety Management System final rule, effective April 24, 2023, requires large and medium hub airports to implement a "formal, top-down, organization-wide approach" to safety risk management.** MWAA now maintains two enterprise risk functions — SMS and EM — each requiring cross-departmental authority. Neither can fulfill its mandate if it reports to a single operational department. The structural consequence is either duplication without integration, or informal authority conflicts that slow both programs. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/newsroom/sms_final_rule.pdf]

4. **Orlando International Airport (MCO), the only airport in the world with full EMAP accreditation, placed Emergency Management under the Vice President of Airport Operations — not under Public Safety.** Minneapolis-Saint Paul (MSP) Metropolitan Airports Commission similarly positions its Emergency Manager within Integrated Operations, not in the public safety hierarchy. The pattern holds: airports that have moved toward enterprise-grade EM programs have decoupled them from first-responder chains of command. [Sources: https://airportimprovement.com/article/orlando-international-airport-mco-receives-emergency-management-accreditation/ and https://metroairports.org/divisions-depts/emergency-management]

5. **The National Association of Counties (NACo) documented a consistent coordination failure pattern: when EM is housed in the sheriff's office, fire does not fully support it; when housed in fire, law enforcement does not.** At MWAA, where police and fire operate as distinct divisions within Public Safety, the same zero-sum dynamic applies. Other departments outside PSD — operations, engineering, information technology, communications, finance — receive an implicit signal that EM is a PSD program. They engage accordingly: at the margins and only when compelled, not as institutional partners. [Source: https://www.naco.org/sites/default/files/documents/Emergency%20Management%20in%20County%20Government_03.25.19.pdf]

6. **A 2021 Government Technology analysis of EM reporting structures found that placement under law enforcement consistently shifts program emphasis toward terrorism and security; placement under the National Guard toward disaster response rather than recovery.** The article concludes: "The key element is the leadership and support" provided by the parent organization or executive — which means that parent-organization mission bias is not incidental, it is structural. At MWAA, housing EM within a department whose core mandate is incident control and regulatory enforcement produces predictable emphasis on response over mitigation, COOP, and recovery. [Source: https://www.govtech.com/em/disaster/should-emergency-management-report.html]

7. **Presidential Policy Directive 8 (PPD-8) defines national preparedness across five mission areas: prevention, protection, mitigation, response, and recovery.** An EM function subordinated to a Public Safety Department naturally contracts to response and portions of mitigation, because those are the missions PSD leadership understands, budgets for, and exercises. The other three mission areas — prevention, protection from systemic risk, and long-term recovery — require enterprise governance that PSD leadership is neither positioned nor incentivized to provide. [Source: https://www.dhs.gov/presidential-policy-directive-8-national-preparedness]

8. **The National Academies ACRP guide on airport crisis management identifies the COO as the correct champion for continuity programs**, writing that the COO should "support the Emergency or Risk Manager who will develop the capability, serve as an adviser throughout a crisis or disruption, and manage the coordinated response across the organization to ensure a rapid resumption of essential functions." The model is EM reporting to or alongside the COO — not EM under a department reporting to the COO. [Source: https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/27915/chapter/6]

## Evidence

### The Operational Coordination Problem

The core operational argument is not philosophical — it is structural. An airport authority of MWAA's complexity (two major certificated airports, shared infrastructure, multi-department administration, extensive external agency relationships) requires EM to coordinate across entities that exist in at least four different chains of command: Public Safety, Airport Operations, Engineering/Facilities, and external agencies (TSA, local jurisdictions, federal partners). A function housed within one of those chains cannot effectively direct the others.

This is how throughput bottlenecks work in any complex system: the constraint is not always at the most active node. In airport operations analysis, runway capacity is the visible metric, but the actual throughput constraint is often gate utilization, taxi sequencing, or ground-stop management — the coordination layer. At MWAA, EM's constraint is not plan quality or personnel competence. It is the organizational authority to compel cross-departmental action. No amount of capability improvement inside PSD removes that constraint.

The FAA's own CertAlert 22-02 on Airport Emergency Exercise Coordination underscores this: FAA expects all parties with roles in the AEP — including air carriers, airport tenants, and media — to be involved in exercise planning, execution, and evaluation. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/airport_safety/certalerts/part_139_certalert_22_02_airport_emergency_exercise_coordination] Securing that participation from airlines and airport tenants requires institutional standing. A program inside PSD negotiates for that participation; a program at enterprise level expects it.

### The SMS-EM Structural Conflict

The 2023 FAA Airport SMS rule creates a second enterprise-wide safety and risk function that must operate in parallel with EM. Both SMS and EM are explicitly "organization-wide" programs. Both require hazard identification, risk assessment, and corrective action across all departments. Both require leadership commitment from the top of the organization.

The operational problem: if SMS reports into Airport Operations and EM reports into Public Safety, neither has enterprise authority over the other or over departments outside their chain. The result is two programs that are technically compliant, operationally fragmented, and practically unable to integrate. The structured solution — supported by the Tidal Basin Group's analysis of airport emergency planning challenges — is to position both functions at a level where they have peer authority with operational departments. [Source: https://www.tidalbasingroup.com/developing-an-effective-airport-emergency-plan-aep/]

### Mission Scope and Threat Profile

MWAA's most operationally disruptive incidents are not primarily public safety events. The threat profile includes severe weather, utility and power failures, IT/cyber incidents, airline operational disruptions, and public health events. None of these incidents begin with a 911 call or a PSD response; all of them require enterprise coordination: communications, operations, engineering, IT, finance, and external agency management.

The 2025 European airport cyber incidents — ransomware affecting Collins Aerospace systems and cascading into flight operations at Heathrow, Berlin, and Brussels — illustrate the point. Those incidents required immediate engagement from IT, operations, airlines, and communications leadership, with public safety playing a supporting role. An EM program housed within PSD is institutionally aligned to lead response to the second category of threat (security incidents, medical emergencies, ARFF events) but structurally dependent on other departments to lead response to the first category. [Source: https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/09/european-airports-cyber-incident-critical-infrastructure/]

### The EMAP Evidence

EMAP accreditation requires demonstrated cross-functional governance — specifically, that the EM program has visible leadership support and can engage stakeholders across the entire jurisdiction. The accreditation process assesses 64 standards covering program management, authority, training, exercises, and incident management. [Source: https://emap.org/the-emergency-management-standard/]

Orlando MCO began its accreditation process in February 2017, specifically motivated by the goal of "building a new emergency management program" — one positioned to operate across the airport enterprise, not inside a single department. The program took three years to achieve initial accreditation. The structural placement under VP of Airport Operations, not under Public Safety, was integral to meeting the cross-functional governance standards. [Source: https://airportimprovement.com/article/orlando-international-airport-mco-receives-emergency-management-accreditation/]

MWAA does not currently hold EMAP accreditation. If it pursued accreditation with EM housed within PSD, the cross-functional governance standards would be the primary structural obstacle.

### The Authority-to-Direct Problem

The Argonne study's finding on standalone agencies requires careful interpretation. The study does not argue that standalone agencies are inherently better resourced or better led. It argues that standalone agencies are better positioned to set cross-departmental priorities — because they are not subordinate to any operational department's competing priorities.

The operational mechanism: when EM must request resources, personnel, or participation from departments outside its chain, it is competing with that department's own operational priorities. A standalone program with direct executive access can escalate those conflicts to the appropriate decision level. A program inside PSD cannot escalate over its own chain; it must negotiate laterally, with no structural leverage.

At the scale of MWAA — with distinct police, fire, airport operations, engineering, IT, communications, and corporate functions — this is not a theoretical concern. It is the daily operational reality of any cross-departmental program that lacks independent standing.

## Counterexamples

### Structure Is Not Destiny

The GAO's 2006 testimony on FEMA's organizational placement remains the most rigorous counterargument available: "Taking actions to improve these operational weaknesses may be more important to FEMA's success than its organizational placement." The GAO identified four factors that matter more than where EM sits: mission clarity, leadership quality, resource adequacy, and planning effectiveness. [Source: https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GAOREPORTS-GAO-06-746T/html/GAOREPORTS-GAO-06-746T.htm]

The Government Technology analysis reaches the same conclusion: "emergency management can function well in many circumstances. The key element is the leadership and support provided" by the executive or parent organization. A strong VP of Public Safety who actively champions enterprise preparedness, runs meaningful cross-departmental exercises, and ensures EM is resourced adequately can create conditions where EM functions effectively despite structural subordination.

This is an honest counterargument. The brief would be dishonest to omit it. The practical implication for MWAA: if the existing leadership of PSD provided active enterprise-level support for EM, the structural argument weakens significantly. The question is whether that support is reliably embedded in the structure or dependent on the individual in the chair.

### Proven Integration Models at Some Airports

Dallas Fort Worth International Airport maintains an Emergency Management Administrator within its Department of Public Safety — and has developed a specialized cross-functional function including an Airline Emergency Working Group that coordinates with airline management and external stakeholders. [Source: https://www.linkedin.com/in/kristina-garrison-c-m-4b6599255/] DFW has not pursued EMAP accreditation, and the cross-functional reach of its EM program from within DPS is difficult to assess externally. But DFW is a data point: large airport, EM under public safety, functioning program.

### Small or Less Complex Organizations

For smaller jurisdictions and simpler organizations, integration under public safety genuinely works. The NACo research that shows coordination problems between fire and police when EM is under one of them is drawing on data from county governments — contexts where those are the primary hazard management entities. MWAA's context is different: the enterprise spans not just fire and police, but airlines, tenants, federal agencies, IT infrastructure, construction programs, and corporate administration. The argument for standalone EM strengthens with organizational complexity.

## Analytical Assessment for the Strategist

The operational evidence supports the standalone argument on three grounds: authority, mission scope, and regulatory compliance architecture. Each ground is independently sufficient; together they are compelling.

**Authority:** EM under PSD cannot independently compel cross-departmental participation in plan development, exercises, or COOP execution. The Argonne study and NACo data both show that this authority gap produces real-world program degradation.

**Mission scope:** The five PPD-8 mission areas require an enterprise function. PSD's mandate covers response and life-safety enforcement. The other three mission areas — prevention, protection, recovery — require sustained investment from IT, engineering, operations, communications, and external affairs. An EM program inside PSD competes for budget and attention with operational public safety priorities; it almost always loses.

**Regulatory compliance architecture:** FAA Part 139.325's enterprise-wide coordination mandate, combined with the new SMS rule's "organization-wide" risk management requirement, creates two regulatory obligations that each demand cross-departmental authority. Placing both programs inside a single operational department satisfies the form of compliance while undermining the substance.

The honest counterargument — that leadership quality matters more than structure — is real but fragile. Structure is repeatable and institutional. Leadership is individual and changeable. MWAA's argument for standalone EM is most durable when made as a structural argument: the coordination obligations that federal law and EMAP accreditation impose on the EM program cannot be reliably discharged from within a single operational department at an authority of this complexity.
