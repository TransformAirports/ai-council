# The Coordinator and the Commander: Why MWAA's Emergency Management Function Belongs at the Executive Table

**Stage 2 Strategist Draft v1 | Council Run: Evaluate Arguments for Stand-Alone Emergency Management | May 2026**

---

At 8:47 p.m. on a Wednesday in late January, an Army Black Hawk on a routine training flight collided with PSA Flight 5342 on final approach to Runway 33 at Reagan National. Sixty-seven people died in 35-degree water. Within thirty minutes, the response inside the wire and on the river drew MWAA Fire and Rescue, DC Fire and EMS, the Metropolitan Police Department, the Coast Guard, the FBI, Maryland State Police, the NTSB, and the White House Situation Room. DCA suspended commercial operations for fourteen hours. Family members began arriving at the airport before the diversion plan had finished printing.

Somebody in that room was holding the seam between a river recovery operation, a closed airfield, an airline diversion plan, a family assistance activation, an NTSB scene, and a Congressional delegation that would call before midnight. That somebody was not the Incident Commander, who was running the on-scene tactical response. That somebody was the airport's emergency management function — the one whose job, in doctrine and in practice, is the *coordination* problem, not the *command* problem.

The argument of this memo is that the seam-holder at MWAA does not currently sit where the seam-holder needs to sit. Emergency Management at MWAA reports through the Vice President of Public Safety. That structure is a 1987-vintage inheritance from the FAA's ARFF branch, not a designed governance choice. It is increasingly out of step with the FAA's own regulatory architecture, with peer-hub practice at LAX, ATL, DEN, MCO, and MSP, and with the threat profile of two airports whose most consequential disruptions have nothing to do with fire or police lead. The thesis is correct. The internal memo making the case is, in its current form, weaker than the thesis deserves. This piece does both jobs: it makes the strongest version of the argument, and it tells the author where the seams in his own draft are.

---

## Executive Summary

1. **Federal regulation already treats Emergency Management as an enterprise function, not a public-safety function.** 14 CFR § 139.325 assigns ownership of the Airport Emergency Plan to the certificate holder — the Authority — and requires coordination across law enforcement, ARFF, medical, principal tenants, and "all other persons who have responsibilities under the plan" [Source: ECFR 14 CFR 139.325; Regulatory/Political Analyst Brief]. The FAA's 2023 Airport Safety Management System rule designates an Accountable Executive who must hold "control of the certificate holder's human and financial resources" for safety performance [Source: Federal Register 2023-03597]. Both regulations describe enterprise authority. Neither describes a department-head job.

2. **The peer hub cohort has already moved.** Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta, Los Angeles World Airports, Denver International, Minneapolis-St. Paul, and Orlando International have all positioned EM under operations or as a peer to public safety, not under it. Orlando is the only airport in the world to hold EMAP accreditation, and its Emergency Management reports through the VP of Airport Operations [Sources: ATL leadership; LAWA Operations & EM division; DEN Aviation Pros release; MSP Integrated Operations; Airport Improvement on MCO/EMAP]. MWAA's structure is closest to DFW's and to Chicago's coordinator-under-police model — the configurations with the weakest cross-departmental reach.

3. **The disruptions that actually break a hub airport are not Public Safety incidents.** Atlanta's December 17, 2017 power outage cost Delta roughly $50 million and 1,200 cancellations in eleven hours; the failure was a coordination seam between the airport, an airline-controlled utility consortium, and Georgia Power [Sources: CNBC; AJC; GAO-23-105203]. The CrowdStrike outage of July 19, 2024 cost Delta approximately $500 million and five operational days while American Airlines recovered the same evening — the difference traced to pre-built continuity frameworks, not to IT department size [Sources: NPR; CIO Dive]. A GAO review of 321 power outages at 24 commercial airports between 2015 and 2022 found that cross-departmental coordination mechanisms were consistently absent [Source: GAO-23-105203].

4. **The Argonne National Laboratory's 2025 national study — the first of its kind — found that standalone EM agencies report a higher likelihood of meeting community needs than EM functions embedded in operational departments** [Source: Argonne/IAEM/NEMA/FEMA EM Structures Study, July 2025]. The same study flags a real countervailing risk: standalone agencies that win independence without protected resources end up isolated rather than empowered. That risk is not a reason to leave EM under PSD. It is a reason to make the resource commitment explicit at the time of the structural change.

5. **The strongest counter-argument is doctrinal, not political.** NIMS places the EOC in support of the on-scene Incident Commander, who at MWAA will almost always be a PSD officer. Elevating EM does not change who runs the incident; it changes who sits at the executive coordination table. The memo currently understates this. A reader who has run an ICS structure will identify this seam in the first thirty seconds and will not be reassured by an org chart that does not address it.

6. **The "VP of Preparedness" enhanced model — consolidating EM, Risk Management, SMS, and Engineering Safety personnel under a single executive — is the structurally weakest part of the current proposal.** No major US commercial airport authority operates this model. SMS requires an independent reporting line to the FAA-recognized Accountable Executive. Engineering Safety carries professional credentials distinct from EM. Bundling four functions with different stakeholder audiences and different regulatory geometries into one VP creates four lines of attack on a single proposal. The baseline option — EM reporting directly to the COO with a defined headcount and budget — is materially more defensible.

7. **The Tennessee TEMA citation in the current memo is wrong as written and needs to be removed.** TEMA remains organizationally housed within the Tennessee Military Department. Airport executives will check the citation and will use its weakness to dismiss the entire argument. There are stronger comparators — MCO/EMAP, the Argonne study, the LAX and ATL models — that carry more weight without inviting fact-check failure [Sources: Airport COO Brief; Director of Public Safety Brief].

8. **The DCA collision of January 29, 2025 is MWAA's empirical case for why this matters.** A multi-agency, multi-jurisdiction, mass-casualty event with sustained Congressional and Executive Branch engagement, requiring simultaneous coordination across at least five lanes that no single department owns. This is not a hypothetical. It is the live test, in the Authority's own recent history, that demonstrates why the seam-holder needs to sit at the executive level, with standing, before the next event arrives [Sources: NTSB DCA25MA108; Airport Emergency Management Director Brief].

## The Argument

The doctrinal case for elevating EM is not new. What is new is the regulatory pressure, the peer movement, and the threat profile, all converging in the same direction at the same time.

Start with the regulation. The Airport Emergency Plan required under 14 CFR § 139.325 covers eighteen distinct emergency categories — natural disasters, hazmat, water rescue, power failures, sabotage, public health events, structural fires. Count the categories whose incident lead is fire or police. Count the categories whose incident lead is IT, Engineering, Operations, Finance, or an external federal agency. The second count is larger. Yet the regulation, by tradition rather than by text, has been read at most US airports as a Public Safety deliverable. The text itself assigns ownership to the certificate holder — the Authority. Ownership has been delegated downward into PSD for thirty years because that is where the radios used to live.

The FAA closed the regulatory ambiguity in 2023. The Airport SMS rule, effective April 24 of that year, designates an Accountable Executive with control of the certificate holder's human and financial resources for safety performance. The accountable executive is the Airport Manager — not any single department head. SMS is, in the FAA's own language, "a formal, top-down, organization-wide approach to managing safety risk." The structural implication is unavoidable: MWAA now operates two enterprise risk programs. SMS reports up to the airport manager level. EM reports to the VP for Public Safety. Both programs are required to identify hazards, assess risks, and drive corrective actions across all departments. One has the regulatory architecture to do that. The other does not. When the next incident review asks who owned the cross-departmental hazard, both will point at each other.

Now the peer evidence. Orlando International is the only airport in the world to hold EMAP accreditation. EMAP's 73 standards across 16 categories require demonstrated cross-functional governance — not as a goal, but as a precondition for accreditation. Orlando's EM function reports through the VP of Airport Operations. That placement was not incidental; it was the structural prerequisite for meeting the standard. Hartsfield-Jackson positioned EM under the Senior Deputy General Manager of Operations, the COO-equivalent, alongside Public Safety rather than below it. LAWA built an Operations and Emergency Management division, structurally separate from the Airport Police, with EM as one of five direct-report functions at the Deputy Executive Director level. Denver embedded Emergency Preparedness and Management inside the SVP of Airport Operations portfolio. Minneapolis-St. Paul placed its Emergency Manager inside Integrated Operations. The pattern is consistent enough that "MWAA is different" is now the position carrying the burden of proof.

The threat profile is the third leg. The events that have actually broken hub airports in the last decade have not been ARFF events. Atlanta in 2017 was a power vault fire that became a coordination failure between the airport, Georgia Power, and an airline-controlled utility consortium called AATC. The airport activated its EOC. The recovered emails revealed that for hours after the activation, basic questions about who controlled access to the electrical tunnels remained unanswered. The cost of that gap was roughly $50 million to one airline and 1,200 cancellations in eleven hours. The CrowdStrike outage seven years later told the same story in a different idiom: Delta absorbed approximately $500 million in losses and five operational days because the cascade hit a crew-tracking system with no rehearsed manual fallback, while American Airlines recovered the same evening on the strength of pre-built continuity frameworks. The differential outcome between two airlines exposed to the same triggering event is the closest thing the industry has to a controlled experiment in the value of mature continuity planning.

What ties Atlanta 2017, FLL 2017, Hurricane Ian, COVID, CrowdStrike, and the DCA collision together is that none of them was a single-department event, and none of them was solved by command-and-control. They were solved — or in the worst cases, not solved — by the quality of the coordination function holding the seams between departments and between agencies. That coordination function is what Emergency Management is supposed to be. It cannot do the job from inside a single department whose chain of command does not reach the other departments it is trying to coordinate.

The argument from inside MWAA's own command structure makes this point with the credibility that only the affected leader can: ARFF compliance under 14 CFR § 139.319, TSA 1542 security programming, PSAP operations across two airports and the Dulles Toll Road, and patrol staffing consume the VP of Public Safety's command capacity continuously. Emergency planning is the work that gets scheduled in the gaps between those operational demands. When IAD or DCA absorbs a major incident, the EM planning calendar stops. That is not a personnel failure. It is what happens when an enterprise function lives inside an operational department whose mandate runs to the three-minute response standard, not to the eighteen-month COOP cycle.

## The Counter-Case, Honestly Presented

The honest counter-argument has three parts, and the strongest one is doctrinal.

First, NIMS doctrine is unambiguous. The Emergency Operations Center supports the Incident Commander; it does not command the Incident Commander. At every commercial airport, the IC for the incidents that activate doctrine is a Public Safety officer — ARFF commander for an aircraft emergency, police commander for a security incident, unified command that PSD chairs for everything in between. Elevating EM above PSD on the org chart does not change who has tactical authority when the alarm sounds. What it could do, if poorly designed, is introduce a structural ambiguity in the first thirty minutes of an incident — the minutes that matter — about whose authority governs. The senior EM executive at the COO level has no line authority over the on-scene IC. Two voices, no operational leverage. That is a worse model than the current one, and a CEO reading the proposal will identify the seam immediately [Sources: Contrarian Brief; Director of Public Safety Brief; PMC airport EM literature].

Second, the Argonne National Laboratory's 2025 study — the same study that supports the elevation argument — also documents the failure mode of standalone EM agencies that win organizational independence without commensurate resource commitments. ProPublica's reporting on the Argonne dataset traced the pattern to specific counties: a Saluda County, South Carolina EM director whose six-person team absorbed IT and a spay-and-neuter program; a Yancey County, North Carolina EM director who waited years for additional staff and got them only after Hurricane Helene. The structural dynamic is predictable. Legislatures and executive teams grant EM independence as a low-cost gesture, then underfund it because the constituency pressure comes from sworn officers, not emergency planners. Elevation at MWAA without a board-locked resource commitment replicates this error in a different uniform [Sources: Contrarian Brief; ProPublica; Argonne EM Study].

Third, the GAO's 2006 testimony on FEMA's organizational placement — the most studied EM placement question in American history — concluded that "a number of factors may be ultimately more important to FEMA's success than its organizational placement," including mission clarity, leadership quality, and resource adequacy. The GAO did not say organizational placement is irrelevant. It said placement is neither necessary nor sufficient. The post-Katrina debate ended with FEMA remaining inside DHS with enhanced authorities and a direct line to the Secretary. The fix was authority and resources, not the box on the org chart [Source: GAO-06-746T].

Add three smaller objections that the COO and CEO will register. The "small incident" problem: most EOC activations at an airport are events PSD owns end-to-end — a gate fire, a medical evacuation, a fuel spill — for which an enterprise EM function adds process overhead without adding value. The SMS independence problem: the FAA SMS framework requires an independent reporting channel to the Accountable Executive; consolidating SMS under a VP of Preparedness who also owns EM creates a structural conflict between hazard identification and response management. The peer-benchmark problem: no major US commercial airport authority operates the consolidated VP of Preparedness model in documented form. MWAA would be the first to do it at this scale, with this regulatory complexity, with this political geometry. Being the first is sometimes correct. It is never automatically correct.

## Why the Counter-Case Is Insufficient

The counter-arguments are real, and the current draft of the internal memo does not engage them with the precision they deserve. They are not, however, sufficient to defeat the thesis. Each one shifts the design problem; none of them rescues the status quo.

Take the NIMS objection first, because it is the strongest. The doctrine says the EOC supports the IC. That is correct. The doctrine also says — and FEMA's own NIMS components material is explicit on this — that the EOC handles operational and strategic coordination, resource acquisition, and information sharing across multiple agencies. The IC manages the scene. The EOC manages everything that is not the scene. At a complex airport incident, "everything that is not the scene" includes airline diversion coordination, family assistance activation, federal partner liaison, executive briefings, public communications, terminal operations continuity, and recovery planning. None of those functions is the IC's job. All of them are the EOC's job. The argument for elevating EM is not that the EM director becomes the IC. The argument is that the EOC director, who is responsible for the larger and longer-running coordination problem, needs the organizational standing to convene other executives without routing through a department head. Elevating EM does not contest the IC's authority. It clarifies the coordination authority that has historically been ambiguous and that ATL 2017, FLL 2017, and DCA 2025 all exposed. The current draft of the internal memo should add a single paragraph explicitly conceding ICS primacy at the incident scene and explicitly distinguishing the EOC director's role from the IC's. The argument becomes much harder to dismiss the moment it does.

The Argonne resource-starvation finding is a real risk, and the current draft handles it badly by ignoring it. The fix is not to weaken the elevation argument. The fix is to attach to it a specific resource floor: named headcount, named budget, named authorities to convene cross-departmental exercises, named authority to enforce corrective-action implementation through the SMS and AEP architectures. A baseline standalone EM function reporting to the COO at MWAA is plausibly a director, two deputies, three planners, a duty-officer rotation, and an exercise/training budget — a structure measurable in the high six figures to low seven figures of recurring cost, against an Authority operating budget at MWAA's scale. Stating the number transforms the proposal from an aspirational reorganization into a costed structural change that the finance and audit committee can either approve or amend. *(The specific headcount and dollar magnitudes here are analyst judgment, derived from the Argonne staffing benchmarks and the EM Director brief's description of what a duty-officer-anchored program requires; the internal memo to the COO should replace these placeholders with MWAA-specific budget figures.)*

The GAO post-Katrina lesson is the most useful of the three counter-arguments because it produces the right design discipline. Placement alone does not solve the problem. Placement plus authority plus resources plus leadership solves the problem. The current internal memo asks for placement and assumes the rest will follow. The Argonne data shows that the rest does not, in fact, automatically follow. Therefore the proposal that reaches the COO should specify all four elements, not just the org chart change. This is the single most consequential edit the author can make.

The "small incident" problem is genuine, and the answer is honest concession. The case for standalone EM does not rest on the routine ninety percent of activations where PSD owns the response end-to-end. It rests on the tail: the multi-departmental, multi-agency, sustained-coordination events that exceed any single department's reach. Getting the tail right is worth the marginal process overhead on the routine because the tail is where the Authority's reputational, financial, and political exposure concentrates. An incident that costs Delta $50 million in eleven hours at ATL, or that closes DCA for fourteen hours after a fatal mid-air, or that ties up Congressional staff at the rate it tied up Delta's IT staff during CrowdStrike — those are the events the structure exists to absorb. The routine events run themselves.

The SMS independence concern is the strongest reason to abandon the consolidated VP of Preparedness model in favor of the baseline option. SMS needs a clean reporting line to the Accountable Executive. Folding SMS under a VP who also runs EM and risk management creates a regulatory and organizational conflict that the FAA may eventually challenge. The cleaner answer is to elevate EM to a direct report to the COO, leave SMS where it is — reporting to the Accountable Executive — and treat the integration between the two as a coordination problem solved by joint planning cycles, joint exercise design, and a shared corrective-action register. That structure achieves the regulatory alignment the argument needs without bundling four functions into a single point of organizational failure.

The peer-benchmark concern about the VP of Preparedness model is, by itself, a sufficient reason to drop the enhanced option from the proposal that reaches the CEO. There is no peer airport doing this. There is significant peer practice for the baseline option. Lead with what is supported. The enhanced model can be revisited in three years if the baseline reorganization performs the way the evidence suggests it will.

## Implications for MWAA

The argument is strongest when it is specifically MWAA's argument. Three threads are worth pulling.

First, the political geography is asymmetric. DCA serves Congress; IAD serves the federal government's international travel; the Authority's seventeen-member board answers to four distinct appointing authorities — Virginia (seven seats), DC (four), Maryland (three), and the President (three) — under an interstate compact codified at 49 U.S.C. § 49103 [Sources: MWAA Board page; Airport CEO Brief; Regulatory Analyst Brief]. A mismanaged enterprise-scale incident at MWAA does not generate a normal hub airport response. It generates a Congressional staff director on the phone within two hours and an OIG inquiry within two weeks. The financial cushion — 3.16x debt service coverage and 1,006 days cash on hand as of 2024 — gives the CEO room to invest in enterprise resilience now, at low cost, rather than under duress later, at high cost [Source: MWAA Investor Relations]. The political cost of inaction is asymmetric in exactly the same direction.

Second, the DCA collision is the live case. The NTSB's January 2026 final investigation cited "deep systemic failures" and issued recommendations to the FAA, the Army, DOT, and other agencies [Source: NPR coverage of NTSB DCA25MA108 final report]. The Authority will be asked, by Congress and by federal partners, what it did with the lessons of that event. A reorganization that elevates EM to executive standing — with authority over the cross-departmental coordination that the response actually required — is a credible answer to that question. Leaving the structure in place is also an answer, and not the one a Congressional committee will be satisfied with.

Third, the institutional gravity inside MWAA is real and the proposal must respect it. The Fire and Rescue Department was formed from the FAA ARFF branch in 1987 and has operated as a dual-jurisdiction entity ever since [Source: MWAA F&R history]. Public Safety carries earned operational credibility and political standing that any reorganization must preserve. The framing that works is "expansion of enterprise capability," not "removal of authority from PSD." The VP for Public Safety needs to be an early partner, not a late objector. Virginia's seven board members — the largest single bloc — need to be briefed before the proposal is on the agenda, not after. The reorganization is a cheap structural change wearing expensive political clothes; the political work is the long pole.

The recommendation that should reach the COO and CEO is the baseline, not the enhanced. EM reports directly to the COO. The position is staffed at a director level with a duty-officer-anchored program, a defined budget, and explicit convening authority across IT, Facilities, Communications, Finance, and Airline Relations. SMS stays on its own clean line to the Accountable Executive. Engineering Safety stays where it is. The author of the internal memo should drop the Tennessee TEMA citation, drop the consolidated VP of Preparedness option from the version that goes to the executive team, add an explicit ICS-primacy paragraph, and attach a one-page resource exhibit naming the headcount and dollars. With those four edits, the proposal goes from a thoughtful internal argument to a board-ready recommendation. Without them, it will be praised privately and shelved publicly.

---

The seam-holder problem is older than aviation, and every institution that has been forced to rebuild its emergency management function has rebuilt it in roughly the same shape. The hospital systems learned during COVID. The university systems learned after the behavioral threat cases. The transit agencies learned after the 90-minute communications lags. The lesson is not novel and the structure is not exotic. The only question MWAA has to answer is whether it learns this lesson before the next event or after it.
