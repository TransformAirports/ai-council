## I. The Scene

At 8:47 p.m. on Wednesday, January 29, 2025, an Army Black Hawk on a training flight collided with PSA Flight 5342 on final approach to Runway 33 at Reagan National. Sixty-seven people died in 35-degree water. Within thirty minutes, the response drew MWAA Fire and Rescue, DC Fire and EMS, the Metropolitan Police Department, the Coast Guard, the FBI, Maryland State Police, the NTSB, and the White House Situation Room. DCA suspended commercial operations for fourteen hours. Family members began arriving at the airport before the diversion plan had finished printing.

Somebody in that room was holding the line between a river recovery, a closed airfield, an airline diversion plan, a family assistance activation, an NTSB scene, and a Congressional delegation that would call before midnight. That somebody was not the Incident Commander, who was running the tactical response on-scene. That somebody was the airport's emergency management function — the one whose job, in doctrine and in practice, is the coordination problem, not the command problem.

The argument is straightforward. The coordinator at MWAA does not sit where the coordinator needs to sit. Emergency Management reports through the Vice President of Public Safety — an arrangement inherited from the FAA Airports Authority transfer of 1987 and never deliberately re-examined. It is out of step with the FAA's 2023 SMS rule, with peer-hub practice at MCO, ATL, LAX, and DEN, and with the threat profile of two airports whose most consequential disruptions exceed any single department's command reach. The author's internal memo makes that case. This piece tightens it.

## II. Executive Summary

1. **Federal regulation already treats Emergency Management as an enterprise function.** 14 CFR §139.325 assigns ownership of the Airport Emergency Plan to the certificate holder — the Authority — and requires coordination across law enforcement, ARFF, medical, principal tenants, and "all other persons who have responsibilities under the plan."¹ The FAA's 2023 Safety Management Systems rule designates an Accountable Executive who must hold "control of the certificate holder's human and financial resources" for safety performance.² ICAO Annex 14 makes the aerodrome EOC, not any department, responsible for "the overall coordination and general direction of the response to an emergency."³ PPD-8 names five federal preparedness mission areas — prevention, protection, mitigation, response, recovery — and only response sits inside any single department.⁴ Four regulatory regimes describe enterprise authority. None describe a department-head job.

2. **The peer hub cohort has moved to a specific configuration.** Orlando International, the only EMAP-accredited airport in the world, placed Emergency Management under the VP of Airport Operations. Atlanta Hartsfield-Jackson places it under the Senior Deputy General Manager of Operations, alongside Public Safety. LAX positions Emergency Management as a peer to Airside, Landside, and Terminal Operations within the Operations and Emergency Management division. Denver embeds it inside the SVP of Airport Operations portfolio.⁵ The pattern is not separation from Public Safety; it is peer status under a single senior operations executive. MWAA's current configuration sits closer to DFW's and MSP's, which keep EM inside Public Safety — the structure with the weakest documented cross-departmental reach.⁶

3. **The disruptions that actually break a hub airport are not Public Safety incidents.** Atlanta's December 17, 2017 power outage cost Delta roughly $50 million and 1,200 cancellations in eleven hours; the failure traced to a coordination gap between AATC (which controlled physical access to the electrical infrastructure), Georgia Power, and the airport.⁷ CrowdStrike on July 19, 2024 cost Delta approximately $500 million and five operational days; American Airlines recovered the same evening on pre-built continuity frameworks.⁸ A 2023 GAO review of 321 power outages at 24 commercial airports found cross-departmental coordination mechanisms consistently absent.⁹ NACo's national survey of county emergency management found 90 percent of EM directors report to elected officials or to the chief executive directly; the comparable state figure is closer to 27 percent, because state EM functions are larger and routed through cabinet structures.¹⁰ The airport authority sits at the county-government scale.

4. **The Argonne 2025 national EM study found standalone agencies report higher effectiveness than embedded ones — but only when paired with dedicated staff and clear mission.**¹¹ Without that pairing, standalone status correlates with isolation rather than empowerment. The conditional is not a reason to keep EM under PSD; it is the design constraint the structural change must satisfy at the moment it is made.

5. **The January 29, 2025 DCA collision is MWAA's empirical case.** A multi-agency, multi-jurisdictional, mass-casualty event with sustained Congressional and Executive Branch engagement, requiring simultaneous coordination across at least five lanes that no single department owns. The NTSB's January 2026 final report cited "deep systemic failures" and issued recommendations to the FAA, the Army, and DOT.¹² The Authority will be asked, by Congress and by federal partners, what it did with the lessons of that event. The reorganization is a credible answer. Leaving the structure in place is also an answer.

## III. The Argument

The Airport Emergency Plan required under 14 CFR §139.325 covers eighteen distinct emergency categories. Aircraft accidents and structural fires are on the list. So are power failures, hazmat, water rescue, public health events, sabotage, and natural disasters. Count the categories whose incident lead is ARFF or police. Then count the categories whose lead is IT, Engineering, Operations, Finance, or an external federal agency. The second count is larger. The text of the regulation assigns ownership to the certificate holder. The text has been read at most US airports — including MWAA — as a Public Safety deliverable.¹³ Why is a question of history more than of law.

Civil defense in the 1950s and 1960s lived in the fire department because that was where the radios were. At MWAA, the FAA's firefighting branch became the institutional nucleus of Public Safety in 1987, when the FAA Airports Authority transferred the airports to interstate-compact governance.¹⁴ The radios moved long ago. The org chart did not. That is the inheritance, and it is a tradition rather than a design.

The FAA closed the ambiguity in 2023. The Airport SMS rule, effective April 24, designates an Accountable Executive who must hold "control of the certificate holder's human and financial resources" for safety performance — at the Airport Manager level, not at any department head.² SMS is, in the FAA's own language, "a formal, top-down, organization-wide approach to managing safety risk." MWAA now operates two enterprise risk programs in parallel: SMS, which reports to the Accountable Executive, and EM, which reports to the VP for Public Safety. Both programs are required to identify hazards, assess risks, and drive corrective actions across all departments. When the next incident review asks who owned the cross-departmental hazard, both programs will point at each other.¹⁵ ICAO Annex 14 ratifies the same answer in international law: the EOC is responsible for the "overall coordination and general direction of the response to an emergency."³ The responsibility belongs to the airport as an institution.

Look at what the peer hubs have done. Orlando International began its EMAP accreditation process in February 2017 and received initial approval in 2020 — three years of stakeholder coordination, joint planning cycles, and exercise discipline that is consistently harder to drive from inside a single operational department.¹⁶ ATL went further: Augustus Hudson, the Senior Deputy General Manager of Operations at Hartsfield-Jackson, holds Emergency Management, Airport Operations, Public Safety, Law Enforcement, Fire and Medical, Maintenance, and Transportation in a single integrated portfolio.¹⁷ EM is a peer to PSD, not below it, and the common supervisor is the operations general manager. LAX is structurally identical — Emergency Management as one of five direct-report functions to the Deputy Executive Director for Operations and Emergency Management, alongside Airside, Landside, Terminal, and Operations Administrative Support.¹⁸ Denver embeds Emergency Preparedness and Management inside the SVP of Airport Operations portfolio.¹⁹

The cohort has not moved to "EM reports somewhere far away from PSD." It has moved to "EM and PSD are peers under a single senior operations executive."

The threat case is sharpest in the AATC tunnel at Hartsfield-Jackson on the afternoon of December 17, 2017. An underground electrical fire took out the world's busiest airport in fewer than ninety seconds.²⁰ The backup system, routed through the same physical tunnels, was unavailable. AATC — the airline-controlled consortium that held the keys to those tunnels — was reachable on Sunday afternoon but not immediately, and not at the level of authority required to open doors. The airport activated its EOC. For hours after activation, basic questions about who controlled physical access to the electrical infrastructure remained unanswered. The cost of that gap was roughly $50 million to one airline, 1,200 cancellations, and eleven hours of darkness.⁷ The GAO subsequently audited 321 power outages at 24 commercial service airports from 2015 to 2022 and found cross-departmental coordination mechanisms consistently absent.⁹

Seven years later, CrowdStrike told the same story in a different idiom: Delta absorbed approximately $500 million in losses and five operational days because the cascade hit a crew-tracking system with no rehearsed manual fallback, while American Airlines recovered the same evening.⁸ The differential between two airlines exposed to the same triggering event exposed the value of mature enterprise continuity planning.

The threat profile also includes the operational tempo that does not make the newspaper. A January IROPS event at IAD with ground stops cascading from Newark and Boston puts the COO's desk in the first ninety minutes on gate reassignments, baggage belt prioritization, de-ice pad coordination, FIDS systems, and ground handler positioning. The Emergency Manager — if seated under the VP of Public Safety — has direct authority over none of those levers. The COO becomes the de facto emergency coordinator by default, because there is no one else with the standing to convene IT, Facilities, Airline Relations, and Operations in the same room within the hour.²¹ IROPS is the recurring test that exposes the structural gap before the catastrophic event does.

A standing duty-officer rotation — staffed 24/7, with authority to activate the EOC without committee consensus — is the operational difference between a planning function and an emergency function. MWAA does not currently have one.²²

What ties Atlanta 2017, CrowdStrike, the IROPS scenario, and DCA together is that none of them was a single-department event, and none of them was solved by command-and-control. They were solved — or in the worst cases not solved — by the coordination function holding the spaces between departments and agencies. The Director of Public Safety knows this and says so in his own brief: ARFF compliance under 14 CFR §139.319, TSA 1542 security programming, PSAP operations across two airports and the Dulles Toll Road, and patrol staffing consume his command capacity continuously. Emergency planning is the work scheduled in the gaps. When IAD or DCA absorbs a major incident, the EM planning calendar stops.²³

## IV. The Counter-Case

The honest counter-argument has four parts.

NIMS doctrine is the strongest of them. The Emergency Operations Center supports the Incident Commander; it does not command the Incident Commander. At every commercial airport, the IC for the incidents that activate doctrine is a Public Safety officer — ARFF commander for an aircraft emergency, police commander for a security incident. Elevating EM above PSD on the org chart does not change who has tactical authority when the alarm sounds. What it could do, if poorly designed, is introduce structural ambiguity in the first thirty minutes of an incident about whose authority governs. A senior EM executive with no line authority over the on-scene IC is two voices and no operational leverage. A CEO reading the proposal will identify the gap immediately.²⁴

The Argonne resource-starvation finding is the second. ProPublica's reporting on the Argonne dataset traced the pattern to specific counties. The Saluda County, South Carolina EM director runs a six-person team that has absorbed the IT department and a spay-and-neuter program. The Yancey County, North Carolina EM director waited years for additional staff and received them only after Hurricane Helene devastated the county in 2024. Legislatures and executive teams grant EM independence as a low-cost gesture, then underfund the function because the constituency pressure comes from sworn officers, not emergency planners.²⁵ Elevation at MWAA without a board-locked resource commitment replicates this error in a different uniform.

GAO's 2006 post-Katrina testimony on FEMA's organizational placement — the most studied EM placement question in American history — concluded that "a number of factors may be ultimately more important to FEMA's success than its organizational placement," including mission clarity, leadership quality, resource adequacy, and planning effectiveness. The post-Katrina debate ended with FEMA inside DHS with enhanced authorities and a direct line to the Secretary. The fix was authority and resources, not the box on the org chart.²⁶

Perception cuts the other way too. When EM reports through PSD, other divisions read it as a PSD program and disengage. That is real. But departments that have managed their own continuity plans, safety programs, and incident protocols may read a standalone EM executive as a compliance function — an inspector arriving at exercises with a clipboard, not a partner who shares the consequences of failure. Executive placement does not by itself produce cross-departmental trust.²⁷

Three smaller objections deserve naming. Most EOC activations are events PSD owns end-to-end, for which an enterprise EM function adds process overhead without adding value.²⁸ SMS requires an independent reporting channel to the Accountable Executive; the v1's consolidated VP-of-Preparedness model creates a regulatory conflict between hazard identification and response management.²⁹ And no major US commercial airport authority operates a documented VP-of-Preparedness model. MWAA would be the first to do it at this scale, with this regulatory complexity, with this political geometry. Being first is sometimes correct. It is never automatically correct.

## V. Where the Counter-Case Lands

The counter-arguments do real work. None of them rescues the status quo. Each one shifts the design.

On NIMS: the doctrine says the EOC supports the IC, and FEMA's own NIMS material is explicit that the EOC handles operational and strategic coordination, resource acquisition, and information sharing across multiple agencies. The IC manages the scene. The EOC manages everything that is not the scene — airline diversion coordination, family assistance activation, federal partner liaison, executive briefings, terminal operations continuity, and recovery planning. The EOC director needs the standing to convene other executives without routing through a department head. The design discipline that makes this work is concrete: joint duty-officer rotation between EM and PSD, joint exercise leadership, and a Director of EM who comes from operational EM rather than from planning.³⁰

On Argonne and post-Katrina: placement alone does not solve the problem. Placement plus authority plus resources plus leadership solves the problem. The EM Consultant brief gives an anchored figure — a director-level EM position runs $200,000 to $300,000 before staff and program costs.³¹ Around that position, the program needs planning staff, the duty-officer rotation, and a training and exercise budget protected from operational-budget volatility. Durability comes from grant leverage: FEMA EMPG, BRIC, and DHS UASI are funding streams an enterprise EM function with dedicated grant-coordination capacity is materially better positioned to access than a function buried inside PSD.³² A program that returns federal dollars at one-to-one or better against its operating cost survives the year-two budget review the Contrarian flagged — the 12 percent overhead cut after a $400 million capital overrun, with the VP of Preparedness as the softest target.³³ A grant-leveraged program is not the softest target. It is the highest-return-per-dollar target.

On perception: an EM director who arrives at IT with a clipboard, or at Facilities with a binder, will receive exactly the engagement she has earned. The fix is in how the elevated function is staffed and chartered — an authority grant to convene, to set cross-departmental planning standards, to require exercise participation, and to enforce corrective-action implementation through the SMS and AEP architectures — combined with a director who treats other departments as partners.³⁴

The "small incident" objection deserves honest concession. The case for elevated EM does not rest on the routine activations PSD owns end-to-end. It rests on the tail: the multi-departmental, multi-agency, sustained-coordination events that exceed any single department's reach. The tail is where the Authority's reputational, financial, and political exposure concentrates.²⁸

The SMS-independence concern is decisive. SMS needs a clean reporting line to the Accountable Executive. Folding SMS under a VP who also runs EM and risk management creates a regulatory and organizational conflict the FAA may eventually challenge.²⁹ The cleaner answer is to elevate EM as a peer to PSD under the COO — the LAX/ATL configuration — leave SMS where it is, and treat the integration between the two as a coordination problem solved by joint planning cycles, joint exercise design, and a shared corrective-action register. The VP-of-Preparedness consolidation should come out of the version that reaches the executive team.

## VI. Implications for MWAA

The political geography is asymmetric. DCA serves Congress; IAD serves the federal government's international travel; the Authority's seventeen-member board answers to four distinct appointing authorities — Virginia (seven seats), DC (four), Maryland (three), and the President (three) — under an interstate compact codified at 49 U.S.C. §49103.³⁵ Major incidents at DCA reach Congressional staff within hours.³⁶ The financial cushion — 3.16x debt service coverage and 1,006 days cash on hand as of 2024 — gives the CEO the room to invest in enterprise resilience now, pre-incident, at low cost.³⁷ The argument is not that the rating is at risk; the rating is not at risk. The argument is that the same balance-sheet strength that makes inaction comfortable now will not exist after a mismanaged enterprise-scale incident, when the same reorganization is being conducted under Congressional subpoena and the qualitative management factor is under review.

The timing is governance-clock, not threat-calendar. The seventeen-member board holds an annual chair election, and the Virginia bloc of seven is the single largest appointing authority. The strategic window is the period immediately following a chair election in which the CEO has cultivated at least a working majority — nine of seventeen — who understand the rationale before the proposal reaches the agenda.³⁸ A CEO who brings the reorganization to a meeting without that preparation is not managing the political risk; she is creating it.

The institutional gravity inside MWAA is real. The Fire and Rescue Department formed from the FAA ARFF branch in 1987 and has operated as a dual-jurisdiction entity ever since.¹⁴ Public Safety carries earned operational credibility and political standing that any reorganization must preserve. The framing that works is "expansion of enterprise capability," not "removal of authority from PSD." The VP for Public Safety should be an early partner — and the structure that makes that politically possible is the LAX/ATL hybrid, in which PSD remains a peer to EM under the same senior operations executive rather than being demoted relative to it.

The recommendation that should reach the COO and CEO is the hybrid: EM and Public Safety as peers to the COO, who is the operating-side executive in MWAA's current structure. EM is staffed at a director level with a duty-officer-anchored program, a defined budget anchored at the Consultant-brief benchmark, explicit convening authority across IT, Facilities, Communications, Finance, and Airline Relations, and a federal grant strategy that returns EMPG, BRIC, and UASI dollars against operating cost. SMS retains its independent line to the Accountable Executive. Joint duty-officer rotation between EM and PSD preserves the operational interface NIMS requires. EMAP accreditation becomes a strategic goal anchored to MCO's three-year benchmark, not a precondition.³⁹

Before the memo reaches the COO, the Tennessee TEMA citation should come out — TEMA remains within the Tennessee Military Department, the citation is inaccurate as written, and a skeptical executive will check it. The MCO/EMAP, LAX, ATL, and NACo county benchmarks do the same work without the vulnerability. The consolidated VP-of-Preparedness option should be reserved for a possible Phase 2 conversation three years after the baseline reorganization has performed. And a one-page resource exhibit — position cost, program staff, duty-officer rotation, grant strategy — converts the proposal from an aspirational reorganization into a costed structural change the finance and audit committee can approve or amend.

## VII. Close

On January 6, 2017, in the Terminal 2 baggage claim at Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International, a passenger retrieved a checked firearm and opened fire. Five people died. The airport's EOC was inside the security perimeter and was rendered inaccessible. Three separate command posts stood up. There was no unified command. Approximately 2,600 officers from forty-five agencies converged with no staging plan. After the incident, the EOC was moved outside the perimeter.⁴⁰

That is what an EM coordination failure looks like at an airport. It does not look like a fire the ARFF chief mishandled or a security incident the police commander botched. It looks like five command posts trying to talk to each other on different radios while the EOC sits in a building no one can enter. The question MWAA has to answer is whether it learns this lesson before the next event or after it.
