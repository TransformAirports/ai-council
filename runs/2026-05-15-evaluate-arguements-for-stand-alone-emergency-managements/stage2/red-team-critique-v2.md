## A. New Problems Introduced in v2

### 1. The "Edits the Author Should Make" sidebar (Section VII) was moved, not solved.
**Location:** Section VII, with cross-references in Sections IV (third smaller objection) and V (final paragraph).
**Issue:** The strategist conceded v1 #1 — split the recommendation from the editorial-note-to-the-author — but kept Section VII inside the same memo. The result is genre confusion. The piece is now reading as analysis through Section VI, then turns into editing instructions in Section VII, then closes with narrative scene in Section VIII. A reader who picks this up does not know whether the document is *the* recommendation or notes on someone else's draft. Worse, Section VII recommendation #3 ("Add an explicit ICS-primacy paragraph and a design-discipline paragraph") is something the *strategist's own* Section V already does. The strategist is instructing the author to write something the strategist has already written in this document.
**Recommendation:** Delete Section VII entirely. Move the four edits into a brief endnote or — better — fold the substantive content (Tennessee TEMA needs replacement; resource exhibit is missing) into the body where it belongs. The piece should read as one thing, not two.

### 2. "Increasingly out of step" — direct violation of project tone rules.
**Location:** Section I, paragraph 3 ("It is increasingly out of step with the FAA's own regulatory architecture…").
**Issue:** "Increasingly" is on the project's banned list of vague quantifiers. The CLAUDE.md rules name it explicitly. The strategist has the data to be specific — 2023 SMS rule, 2024 CertAlert 24-09, MCO 2020 accreditation, ATL/LAX/DEN current configurations — and instead reaches for a hedge.
**Recommendation:** Replace with a date or a specific event. "Out of step with the 2023 SMS rule and with peer-hub practice at LAX, ATL, MCO, and DEN" does the work cleanly.

### 3. The "Georgia Power switching gear room" detail is invented specificity.
**Location:** Section III, paragraph on Atlanta 2017 ("A fire in a Georgia Power switching gear room knocked out primary power.").
**Issue:** No brief specifies a "switching gear room." The CEO brief says "When both primary and backup power failed simultaneously"; the EM Director brief says "An underground electrical fire took out the world's busiest airport"; the AJC source the EM Director brief cites describes the AATC tunnels. The strategist has manufactured a precise mechanical detail to make the scene feel anchored. It will not survive a skeptical executive who Googles it.
**Recommendation:** Either cite a primary source for the switching-gear-room specificity or drop it. "An underground electrical fire" is what the briefs support.

### 4. The "nine inches of snow forecast, eleven inches falling" IROPS detail is invented.
**Location:** Section III, IROPS paragraph ("Picture a January IROPS event at IAD: nine inches of snow forecast, eleven inches falling…").
**Issue:** The COO brief gives the 90-minute structural argument without any snowfall numbers. The 9/11 inches are the strategist's invented specificity, dressed up as if they were retrieved from a brief. This is precisely the failure mode the v1 critique attacked — manufactured anchors that read as data.
**Recommendation:** Drop the inches. "A snowstorm at IAD with ground stops cascading from Newark and Boston" carries the operational argument without the fabricated meteorology.

### 5. "The closest thing the industry has to a controlled experiment."
**Location:** Section III, end of CrowdStrike paragraph.
**Issue:** Two airlines responding to the same triggering event is not a controlled experiment. The framing is grandiose and the kind of consultant-flourish the project bans. The Delta-vs-American comparison is interesting; it does not need to be sold as quasi-science.
**Recommendation:** Cut "the closest thing the industry has to a controlled experiment in." The sentence reads better as: "The differential between two airlines exposed to the same triggering event exposed the value of mature enterprise continuity planning."

### 6. "A CEO reading the proposal will identify the seam in the first sixty seconds."
**Location:** Section IV, doctrinal counter-argument paragraph.
**Issue:** Invented specificity. The CEO does not have a stopwatch. The sentence works without the number.
**Recommendation:** Replace with "…will identify the seam immediately" or simply "will identify the seam." The "sixty seconds" is rhetoric pretending to be measurement.

### 7. "EMAP accreditation becomes a five-year strategic goal."
**Location:** Section VI, final paragraph.
**Issue:** Where does five years come from? MCO took three years (2017–2020). No brief cites a five-year benchmark. This is another fabricated anchor.
**Recommendation:** Either cite MCO's three years and propose a three-to-five-year window with reasoning, or drop the number and call it a "strategic goal, not a precondition."

### 8. FLL 2017 is now a name-drop without content — regression from v1.
**Location:** Section III, paragraph 7 ("What ties Atlanta 2017, FLL 2017, CrowdStrike, the IROPS scenario, and the DCA collision together…").
**Issue:** This is the first and only mention of FLL 2017 in the body of v2. The DPS brief and the EM Director brief both treat FLL 2017 as *the* canonical airport-EM coordination failure case: 5 killed, three separate command posts, no unified command, 2,600 officers with no staging, the EOC locked inside the security perimeter. The strategist invokes the case by name and expects the reader to know what it means. The reader who has not read the briefs has no idea. v1 had FLL more present; v2 demoted it to a label.
**Recommendation:** Either give FLL a paragraph of real treatment (the three command posts and the locked-out EOC are the punchline) or drop the name entirely. Naming-without-explaining is the worst option.

### 9. "Nothing to do with fire or police lead" contradicts the opening scene.
**Location:** Section I, paragraph 3 ("…the threat profile of two airports whose most consequential disruptions have nothing to do with fire or police lead.").
**Issue:** The opening scene the strategist just wrote names MWAA Fire and Rescue as one of the first responders to the DCA collision. Fire and Rescue had a real lead role on the river recovery. "Nothing to do with fire or police lead" overstates the argument and the next paragraph contradicts itself. The real argument is that fire/police don't have the *full coordination* role — not that they have nothing to do with the most consequential incidents.
**Recommendation:** Soften to "whose most consequential disruptions exceed any single department's command reach" or "whose most consequential disruptions cross at least five operational lanes simultaneously." The strategist's own opening scene already makes this case; the bullet just needs to say it correctly.

## D. Missed Lenses

### 23. The doctrine-level Airport Emergency Management Director lens on duty-officer architecture is underused.
**Location:** Sections III–V.
**Issue:** The EM Director brief is dense on operational EM doctrine: duty-officer-anchored EOC activation, the position-to-position command continuity requirement, the standing relationships with FBI SAC / TSA FSD / regional FEMA coordinator that convert call trees into actual response networks. The strategist references duty-officer rotation once in Section V and once in Section VI's recommendation, but never *explains* what a duty-officer rotation actually is or what it costs. A COO reading this needs to understand that the duty officer is the human who is on-call to activate the EOC at 2 a.m. — and that without that role, EM is a planning function that arrives the next morning.
**Recommendation:** Add a sentence in Section III or Section VI that names the duty-officer architecture concretely: "A standing duty-officer rotation — staffed 24/7, with authority to activate the EOC without committee consensus — is the operational difference between a planning function and an emergency function. MWAA does not currently have one."

### 24. The Contrarian brief's "budget cycle" and "softest target" scenario is unaddressed at body level.
**Location:** Section IV second counter-argument lightly addresses this; Section V responds with the grant-leverage argument.
**Issue:** v1 critique #21 flagged that the political/budget zero-sum dynamic was glossed. v2 addresses it via the grant-leverage paragraph in Section V ("FEMA EMPG, BRIC, and DHS UASI") — which is real progress. The VP of Preparedness, with no sworn personnel, no aircraft, and no certified ARFF function, is the softest target" — is *more vivid* than the strategist's response. The strategist neutralized the analytic objection but did not engage with the operating-pressure scenario the Contrarian wrote.
**Recommendation:** Add a sentence to the Section V grant-leverage paragraph that names the scenario: "A program that pulls back its own operating cost in federal grants survives the year-two budget review the Contrarian flagged: the 12% overhead cut after a $400M capital overrun, with the VP of Preparedness as the softest target.
