## I. The Scene

At 8:47 p.m. on Wednesday, January 29, 2025, an Army Black Hawk on a training flight collided with PSA Flight 5342 on final approach to Runway 33 at Reagan National. Sixty-seven people died in 35-degree water. Within thirty minutes, the response inside the wire and on the river drew MWAA Fire and Rescue, DC Fire and EMS, the Metropolitan Police Department, the Coast Guard, the FBI, Maryland State Police, the NTSB, and the White House Situation Room. DCA suspended commercial operations for fourteen hours. Family members began arriving at the airport before the diversion plan had finished printing.

Somebody in that room was holding the seam between a river recovery operation, a closed airfield, an airline diversion plan, a family assistance activation, an NTSB scene, and a Congressional delegation that would call before midnight. That somebody was not the Incident Commander, who was running the tactical response on-scene. That somebody was the airport's emergency management function — the one whose job, in doctrine and in practice, is the *coordination* problem, not the *command* problem.

The argument of this memo is straightforward. The coordinator at MWAA does not currently sit where the coordinator needs to sit. Emergency Management reports through the Vice President of Public Safety — an arrangement inherited from the FAA Airports Authority transfer of 1987 and never deliberately re-examined. It is increasingly out of step with the FAA's own regulatory architecture, with peer-hub practice at MCO, ATL, LAX, and DEN, and with the threat profile of two airports whose most consequential disruptions have nothing to do with fire or police lead. The author's internal memo makes that case. This piece tightens it.

## II. Executive Summary

1. **Federal regulation already treats Emergency Management as an enterprise function, not a public-safety function.** 14 CFR §139.325 assigns ownership of the Airport Emergency Plan to the certificate holder — the Authority — and requires coordination across law enforcement, ARFF, medical, principal tenants, and "all other persons who have responsibilities under the plan."¹ The FAA's 2023 Safety Management Systems rule designates an Accountable Executive who must hold "control of the certificate holder's human and financial resources" for safety performance.² ICAO Annex 14 makes the aerodrome EOC, not any single department, responsible for "the overall coordination and general direction of the response to an emergency."³ Three different regulatory regimes — domestic, international, and emerging — describe enterprise authority. None describe a department-head job.

2. **The peer hub cohort has already moved — and toward a specific configuration.** Orlando International, the only EMAP-accredited airport in the world, placed Emergency Management under the VP of Airport Operations. Atlanta Hartsfield-Jackson places it under the Senior Deputy General Manager of Operations, *alongside* Public Safety. LAX positions Emergency Management as a peer to Airside, Landside, and Terminal Operations within a combined Operations and Emergency Management division. Denver embeds Emergency Preparedness and Management inside the SVP of Airport Operations portfolio. The pattern is not separation from Public Safety; it is *peer status with* Public Safety under a single senior operations executive.⁴ MWAA's current configuration is closest to DFW's and MSP's, which keep EM inside the Public Safety chain — the structure with the weakest documented cross-departmental reach.⁵

3. **The disruptions that actually break a hub airport are not Public Safety incidents.** Atlanta's December 17, 2017 power outage cost Delta roughly $50 million and 1,200 cancellations in eleven hours; the failure was a coordination seam between AATC (which controlled access to the electrical tunnels), Georgia Power (which supplied the power), and the airport (which owned the consequences).⁶ CrowdStrike on July 19, 2024 cost Delta approximately $500 million and five operational days while American Airlines recovered the same evening — the difference traced to pre-built continuity frameworks, not to IT department size.⁷ A 2023 GAO review of 321 power outages at 24 commercial airports found cross-departmental coordination mechanisms consistently absent.⁸ NACo's national survey of county emergency management programs found 90 percent of EM directors report to elected officials or to the chief executive directly — the cross-sector baseline expectation that MWAA does not meet.⁹

4. **The Argonne National Laboratory's 2025 national EM study found that standalone agencies report higher effectiveness than embedded ones — but only when paired with dedicated staff and clear mission.**¹⁰ Without that pairing, standalone status correlates with isolation rather than empowerment. That conditional is not a reason to keep EM under PSD. It is the design constraint the proposal must satisfy at the time of the structural change.

5. **The January 29, 2025 collision at DCA is MWAA's empirical case.** A multi-agency, multi-jurisdictional, mass-casualty event with sustained Congressional and Executive Branch engagement, requiring simultaneous coordination across at least five lanes that no single department owns. The NTSB's final report in January 2026 cited "deep systemic failures" and issued recommendations to the FAA, the Army, and DOT.¹¹ The Authority will be asked, by Congress and federal partners, what it did with the lessons of that event. The reorganization is a credible answer. Leaving the structure in place is also an answer.

## III. The Argument

Start with the regulation, but read it carefully. The Airport Emergency Plan required under 14 CFR §139.325 covers eighteen distinct emergency categories. Aircraft accidents and structural fires are on the list. So are power failures, hazmat, water rescue, public health events, sabotage, and natural disasters. Count the categories whose incident lead is ARFF or police. Then count the categories whose incident lead is IT, Engineering, Operations, Finance, or an external federal agency. The second count is larger. The text of the regulation assigns ownership to the certificate holder. The text has been read at most US airports — including MWAA — as a Public Safety deliverable, because the radio communications backbone of civil defense in the 1950s and 1960s lived in the fire department, and the discipline never moved.¹² The civil-defense legacy is a tradition. The regulation is a text. They no longer agree.

The FAA closed the ambiguity in 2023. The Airport SMS rule, effective April 24 of that year, designates an Accountable Executive who must hold "control of the certificate holder's human and financial resources" for safety performance — at the Airport Manager level, not at any department head.² SMS is, in the FAA's own language, "a formal, top-down, organization-wide approach to managing safety risk." The structural implication is that MWAA now operates two enterprise risk programs in parallel: SMS, which reports to the Accountable Executive, and EM, which reports to the VP for Public Safety. Both programs are required to identify hazards, assess risks, and drive corrective actions across all departments. One has the regulatory architecture to do that. The other does not. When the next incident review asks who owned the cross-departmental hazard, both will point at each other.¹³ ICAO Annex 14 ratifies the same answer in international law: the aerodrome's EOC is responsible for the "overall coordination and general direction of the response to an emergency."³ That responsibility belongs to the airport as an institution, not to any one of its departments.

Now consider what the peer hubs have actually done — and what they have not. Orlando International began its EMAP accreditation process in February 2017 and received initial approval in 2020. The placement under the VP of Airport Operations was not the *prerequisite* the v1 of this memo claimed; EMAP does not prescribe organizational placement.¹⁴ But the placement was the operational choice that made the cross-functional governance standard achievable in practice. Three years of stakeholder coordination, joint planning cycles, and exercise discipline could not have been run from a desk inside the police department. ATL went further: Augustus Hudson, the Senior Deputy General Manager of Operations at Hartsfield-Jackson, holds Emergency Management, Airport Operations, Public Safety, Law Enforcement, Fire and Medical, Maintenance, and Transportation in a single integrated portfolio.¹⁵ EM is a peer to PSD, not below it, and the common supervisor is the operations general manager. LAX's structure is structurally identical — Emergency Management as one of five direct-report functions to the Deputy Executive Director for Operations and Emergency Management, alongside Airside, Landside, Terminal, and Operations Administrative Support.¹⁶ Denver embeds Emergency Preparedness and Management inside the SVP of Airport Operations portfolio.¹⁷

The pattern is consistent and the pattern is *specific*. The peer cohort has not moved to "EM reports somewhere far away from PSD." It has moved to "EM and PSD are peers under a single senior operations executive." The v1 of this memo recommended a structure that departed from peer practice. The cleaner reading of the evidence is to match it.

The threat profile is the third leg, and the strongest empirical case is the AATC tunnel at Hartsfield-Jackson on the afternoon of December 17, 2017. A fire in a Georgia Power switching gear room knocked out primary power. The backup system, routed through the same physical tunnels, was unavailable. AATC, an airline-controlled consortium, held the keys to those tunnels. The airport activated its EOC. The recovered correspondence revealed that for hours after activation, basic questions about who controlled physical access to the electrical infrastructure remained unanswered. The cost of that gap was roughly $50 million to one airline, 1,200 cancellations, and the eleven hours.⁶ The GAO subsequently audited 321 power outages at 24 commercial service airports from 2015 to 2022 and found cross-departmental coordination mechanisms consistently absent.⁸ Seven years later, CrowdStrike told the same story in a different idiom: Delta absorbed approximately $500 million in losses and five operational days because the cascade hit a crew-tracking system with no rehearsed manual fallback, while American Airlines recovered the same evening on the strength of pre-built continuity frameworks.⁷ The differential between two airlines exposed to the same triggering event is the closest thing the industry has to a controlled experiment in the value of mature enterprise continuity planning.

The threat profile also includes the operational tempo that does not make the newspaper. Picture a January IROPS event at IAD: nine inches of snow forecast, eleven inches falling, ground stops cascading from Newark and Boston. The COO's desk in the first ninety minutes is gate reassignments, baggage belt prioritization, de-ice pad coordination, FIDS systems, and ground handler positioning. The Emergency Manager — if seated under the VP of Public Safety — has direct authority over none of those levers. The COO becomes the de facto emergency coordinator by default, because there is no one else with the standing to convene IT, Facilities, Airline Relations, and Operations in the same room within the hour.¹⁸ That model works until it doesn't. IROPS is the recurring test that exposes the structural seam before the catastrophic event does.

What ties Atlanta 2017, FLL 2017, CrowdStrike, the IROPS scenario, and the DCA collision together is that none of them was a single-department event, and none of them was solved by command-and-control. They were solved — or in the worst cases, not solved — by the quality of the coordination function holding the spaces between departments and between agencies. That coordination function is what Emergency Management is supposed to be. It cannot do the job from inside a single department whose chain of command does not reach the other departments it is trying to coordinate. The Director of Public Safety knows this, says so directly, and identifies in his own brief that ARFF compliance under 14 CFR §139.319, TSA 1542 security programming, PSAP operations across two airports and the Dulles Toll Road, and patrol staffing consume his command capacity continuously. Emergency planning is the work scheduled in the gaps. When IAD or DCA absorbs a major incident, the EM planning calendar stops.¹⁹

## IV. The Counter-Case

The honest counter-argument has four parts.

The first is doctrinal, and it is the strongest. NIMS is unambiguous: the Emergency Operations Center supports the Incident Commander; it does not command the Incident Commander. At every commercial airport, the IC for the incidents that activate doctrine is a Public Safety officer — ARFF commander for an aircraft emergency, police commander for a security incident. Elevating EM above PSD on the org chart does not change who has tactical authority when the alarm sounds. What it could do, if poorly designed, is introduce structural ambiguity in the first thirty minutes of an incident — the minutes that matter — about whose authority governs. A senior EM executive with no line authority over the on-scene IC is two voices and no operational leverage. That is a worse model than the current one, and a CEO reading the proposal will identify the seam in the first sixty seconds.²⁰

The second is the Argonne resource-starvation finding. ProPublica's reporting on the Argonne dataset traced the pattern to specific counties. The Saluda County, South Carolina EM director runs a six-person team that has absorbed the IT department and a spay-and-neuter program. The Yancey County, North Carolina EM director waited years for additional staff and received them only after Hurricane Helene devastated the county in 2024. The structural dynamic is predictable: legislatures and executive teams grant EM independence as a low-cost gesture, then underfund the function because the constituency pressure comes from sworn officers, not emergency planners.²¹ Elevation at MWAA without a board-locked resource commitment replicates this error in a different uniform.

The third is the GAO's 2006 post-Katrina testimony on FEMA's organizational placement — the most studied EM placement question in American history. The GAO concluded that "a number of factors may be ultimately more important to FEMA's success than its organizational placement," including mission clarity, leadership quality, resource adequacy, and planning effectiveness. The post-Katrina debate ended with FEMA remaining inside DHS with enhanced authorities and a direct line to the Secretary. The fix was authority and resources, not the box on the org chart.²²

The fourth is the perception risk that cuts the other way. The v1 of this memo argued that when EM reports through PSD, other divisions read it as a PSD program and disengage. That is real. But elevation carries its own perception risk: departments that have managed their own continuity plans, safety programs, and incident protocols may read a standalone EM executive as a compliance function — an inspector arriving at exercises with a clipboard, not a partner who shares the consequences of failure. Executive placement does not by itself produce cross-departmental trust. It can produce defensive disengagement from departments that already believe they handle their own emergencies adequately.²³

Add three smaller objections worth naming. The "small incident" problem: the majority of EOC activations at an airport are events PSD owns end-to-end, for which an enterprise EM function adds process overhead without adding value.²⁴ The SMS independence problem: the FAA SMS framework requires an independent reporting channel to the Accountable Executive; the v1's consolidated VP-of-Preparedness model creates a structural conflict between hazard identification and response management.²⁵ The peer-benchmark problem in its narrower form: no major US commercial airport authority operates a documented VP-of-Preparedness model. MWAA would be the first to do it at this scale, with this regulatory complexity, with this political geometry. Being the first is sometimes correct. It is never automatically correct.

## V. Why the Counter-Case Reshapes the Recommendation Rather Than Defeats It

The counter-arguments do real work. They do not, however, defeat the thesis. Each one shifts the design. None of them rescues the status quo.

Start with NIMS. The doctrine says the EOC supports the IC. That is correct. It also says — and FEMA's own NIMS components material is explicit on this — that the EOC handles operational and strategic coordination, resource acquisition, and information sharing across multiple agencies. The IC manages the scene. The EOC manages everything that is not the scene. At a complex airport incident, "everything that is not the scene" includes airline diversion coordination, family assistance activation, federal partner liaison, executive briefings, terminal operations continuity, and recovery planning. None of those functions is the IC's job. All of them are the EOC's job. The argument is not that the EM director becomes the IC. The argument is that the EOC director needs the organizational standing to convene other executives without routing through a department head.²⁰ The design discipline that makes this work is concrete: joint duty-officer rotation between EM and PSD, joint exercise leadership, and a Director of EM who comes from operational EM rather than from planning.²⁶ A standalone director who has never run an ICS structure will fail at the interface. A standalone director who has done both will not.

The Argonne resource-starvation pattern is the most important constraint and the easiest one to fix at the time of the structural change. The EM Consultant brief gives an anchored figure: a director-level EM position at peer public-sector organizations runs $200,000 to $300,000 before staff and program costs.²⁷ Around that position, the program needs a small planning staff, a duty-officer rotation, and a training and exercise budget protected from operational-budget volatility. The financial scale matters less than the durability — and durability comes from grant leverage. The CEO brief flags FEMA EMPG, BRIC, and DHS UASI as funding streams an enterprise EM function with dedicated grant-coordination capacity is materially better positioned to access than a function buried inside PSD.²⁸ A program that returns federal dollars at one-to-one or better against its operating cost survives the third-FY budget review. A program that does not, does not. The proposal that reaches the COO should specify the position, the program staff, and the grant strategy together. None of them on its own is sufficient.

The GAO post-Katrina lesson produces the right design discipline. Placement alone does not solve the problem. Placement plus authority plus resources plus leadership solves the problem. The internal memo's v1 asks for placement and assumes the rest will follow. The Argonne data shows it does not. The proposal that reaches the COO and CEO should specify all four elements at once, not just the org chart change.²²

The perception-cuts-both-ways concern is real and it points directly at the recommended structure. An EM director who arrives at IT with a clipboard, or at Facilities with a binder, will receive exactly the engagement she has earned. The fix is in how the elevated function is staffed and chartered — a clear authority grant to convene, to set cross-departmental planning standards, to require exercise participation, and to enforce corrective-action implementation through the SMS and AEP architectures, combined with a director who treats other departments as partners. The fix is not in the org chart alone.²⁹

The "small incident" objection deserves honest concession. The case for elevated EM does not rest on the routine activations PSD owns end-to-end. It rests on the tail: the multi-departmental, multi-agency, sustained-coordination events that exceed any single department's reach. Getting the tail right is worth the marginal process overhead on the routine, because the tail is where the Authority's reputational, financial, and political exposure concentrates.²⁴

The SMS-independence concern is the decisive reason to drop the v1's consolidated VP-of-Preparedness model. SMS needs a clean reporting line to the Accountable Executive. Folding SMS under a VP who also runs EM and risk management creates a regulatory and organizational conflict the FAA may eventually challenge.²⁵ The cleaner answer is to elevate EM as a peer to PSD under a single senior operations executive — the LAX/ATL configuration — leave SMS where it is, and treat the integration between the two as a coordination problem solved by joint planning cycles, joint exercise design, and a shared corrective-action register.

The peer-benchmark problem in its narrow form is, by itself, sufficient reason to drop the VP-of-Preparedness option from the version that reaches the CEO. There is no peer airport doing the consolidated model. There is significant peer practice for the hybrid model — EM and PSD as peers under a senior operations executive. Lead with what is supported.

## VI. Implications for MWAA

The argument is strongest as MWAA's argument. Three threads are worth pulling.

First, the political geography is asymmetric. DCA serves Congress; IAD serves the federal government's international travel; the Authority's seventeen-member board answers to four distinct appointing authorities — Virginia (seven seats), DC (four), Maryland (three), and the President (three) — under an interstate compact codified at 49 U.S.C. §49103.³⁰ Every major incident at DCA gets a call from a Senate staff director within two hours.³¹ The financial cushion — 3.16x debt service coverage and 1,006 days cash on hand as of 2024 — gives the CEO the room to invest in enterprise resilience now, pre-incident, at low cost.³² The argument is not that the rating is at risk; the rating is not at risk. The argument is that the same balance-sheet strength that makes inaction comfortable now will not exist after a mismanaged enterprise-scale incident, when the cost of the same reorganization has multiplied.

Second, the timing is not driven by the threat calendar. It is driven by the board's governance clock. The CEO brief's most actionable political point is the seventeen-member board with annual chair election and a Virginia bloc of seven that is the single largest appointing authority. The strategic window is the period immediately following a chair election in which the CEO has cultivated at least a working majority — nine of seventeen — who understand the rationale before the proposal reaches the agenda.³³ A CEO who brings this reorganization to a meeting without that preparation is not managing the political risk; she is creating it. Mapping the appointment schedule and the chair cycle is the project plan that matters more than the org chart itself.

Third, the institutional gravity inside MWAA is real and the proposal must respect it. The Fire and Rescue Department was formed from the FAA ARFF branch in 1987 and has operated as a dual-jurisdiction entity ever since.³⁴ Public Safety carries earned operational credibility and political standing that any reorganization must preserve. The framing that works is "expansion of enterprise capability," not "removal of authority from PSD." The VP for Public Safety needs to be an early partner, not a late objector — and the structure that makes that politically possible is precisely the LAX/ATL hybrid, in which PSD remains a peer to EM under the same senior operations executive rather than being demoted relative to it.

The DCA collision of January 29, 2025 is the live case the Authority will be asked about. The NTSB's January 2026 final report cited "deep systemic failures" and issued recommendations to the FAA, the Army, and DOT.¹¹ The reorganization that elevates EM to peer status with PSD — with authority over the cross-departmental coordination that response actually required — is a credible answer to the question every Congressional committee will eventually ask. Leaving the structure in place is also an answer, and not the one the committee will be satisfied with.

The recommendation that should reach the COO and CEO is the hybrid, not the v1 of either option. EM and Public Safety report as peers to a single senior operations executive — the LAX/ATL configuration. The EM function is staffed at a director level with a duty-officer-anchored program, a defined budget anchored at the Consultant-brief benchmark, explicit convening authority across IT, Facilities, Communications, Finance, and Airline Relations, and a federal grant strategy that returns EMPG, BRIC, and UASI dollars against operating cost. SMS retains its independent line to the Accountable Executive. Engineering Safety stays where it is. Joint duty-officer rotation between EM and PSD preserves the operational interface that NIMS requires. EMAP accreditation becomes a five-year strategic goal, not a precondition. The VP-of-Preparedness consolidation is shelved.

## VII. Edits the Author Should Make Before the Memo Reaches the COO

Four specific edits will move the internal memo from a thoughtful internal argument to a board-ready recommendation.

1. **Drop the Tennessee TEMA citation.** TEMA remains housed within the Tennessee Military Department. The citation is inaccurate as written and a skeptical executive will check it. Replace with MCO/EMAP, the LAX and ATL configurations, and the NACo 90% benchmark.

2. **Drop the consolidated VP-of-Preparedness option from the version that goes to the executive team.** It bundles four functions with different regulatory geometries into a single point of organizational failure, conflicts with SMS independence requirements, and has no peer airport benchmark. Reserve it for a possible Phase 2 conversation three years after the baseline reorganization has performed.

3. **Add an explicit ICS-primacy paragraph and a design-discipline paragraph.** Concede that the Incident Commander runs the scene. Distinguish that from the EOC director's coordination role. Specify the joint duty-officer rotation, joint exercise leadership, and EM-director qualifications (operational background, not planning background) that make the interface work in the first thirty minutes of an incident.

4. **Attach a one-page resource exhibit.** Position cost at the Consultant-brief benchmark. Program staff. Duty-officer rotation. Grant strategy. The exhibit converts the proposal from an aspirational reorganization into a costed structural change the finance and audit committee can approve or amend.

## VIII. Close

In a mid-sized US transit agency a few years ago, a major passenger evacuation produced a 90-minute lag between the operational response and the public messaging — long enough for the local news cycle to run three iterations of misinformation before the agency caught up. The fix was not a new communications tool. It was a reorganization that moved Emergency Management out of Operations and onto a direct reporting line to the CEO, with communications integrated into the EOC by structure rather than by request. The following year's full-scale exercise produced sub-twenty-minute communications activation. No other variable changed.³⁵

The seam-holder problem predates Part 139, and every institution forced to rebuild its emergency management function has rebuilt it in roughly the same shape. The only question MWAA has to answer is whether it learns this lesson before the next event or after it.
