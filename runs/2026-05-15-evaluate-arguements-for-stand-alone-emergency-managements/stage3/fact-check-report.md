## Unverified / Unsourced Claims (Listed Individually)

Each claim below was searched across all eight Stage 1 briefs and could not be located. In the final draft, each has been either removed, softened, or tagged.

**1. "On final approach to Runway 33 at Reagan National." (§I)**
The specific runway is not referenced in any brief. It is widely reported in NTSB and press coverage as Runway 33, but absent from the briefs used for this run. **Action in final draft:** runway designation removed (not load-bearing for the argument).

**2. "Army Black Hawk on a training flight." (§I)**
The training/proficiency nature of the Black Hawk mission is not stated in any brief. Briefs identify the aircraft and confirm 67 fatalities, but do not characterize the mission. **Action in final draft:** "training" removed; aircraft identification retained.

**3. "The diversion plan had finished printing." (§I)**
Rhetorical scene-setting, not a load-bearing claim, but not anchored to any brief. **Action in final draft:** retained as scene-setting — the underlying point (rapid Congressional and family arrival) is implicit in the EM Director brief's first-30-minutes framing.

**4. "An underground electrical fire took the world's busiest airport down in fewer than ninety seconds." (§III)**
The specific 90-second takedown speed is not in any brief. The EM Director brief says only that "an underground electrical fire took out the world's busiest airport for eleven hours." **Action in final draft:** "in fewer than ninety seconds" removed; framing softened to "in minutes" — supported by the underlying event description.

**5. "The backup, routed through the same physical tunnels, was unavailable." (§III)**
The CEO brief states that "both primary and backup power failed" but does not anchor the specific causal mechanism that the backup ran through the same physical tunnels as the primary. This is plausible per public reporting on the AATC tunnel fire but not anchored to the briefs. **Action in final draft:** softened to "the backup system was unavailable on the same failure" — which the briefs do support.

**6. "AATC … was reachable on a Sunday afternoon but not immediately, and not at the level of authority required to open doors." (§III)**
December 17, 2017 was a Sunday (factual), and the EM Director brief documents the AATC access-control gap. The specific framing about "doors" being un-openable is interpretive. **Action in final draft:** retained but trimmed to language closer to the EM Director brief — "AATC, the airline-controlled consortium with access to the electrical infrastructure, was reachable but not immediately operative at the level of authority required."

**7. "For hours afterward, basic questions about who controlled physical access to the electrical infrastructure remained unanswered." (§III)**
The EM Director brief says **days**, not hours: "Days after the event, basic questions about who was in charge had still not been answered publicly." **Action in final draft:** "hours" corrected to "days" to match the source.

**8. "Approximately 2,600 officers from forty-five agencies converged with no staging plan." (§VII, FLL close)**
The "2,600 officers" number is verified (Director of Public Safety brief line 16; EM Director brief line 13, 61). **The "forty-five agencies" figure is not present in any Stage 1 brief.** It is reported elsewhere in public coverage of the FLL incident but cannot be sourced from this run's research. **Action in final draft:** "from forty-five agencies" removed; replaced with "from multiple agencies" — which the briefs do support.

**9. "In the Terminal 2 baggage claim at Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International, a passenger retrieved a checked firearm and opened fire." (§VII)**
The Terminal 2 location is the historically accurate site of the shooting but is not specified in any Stage 1 brief. The briefs only specify that the EOC was in Terminal 3. **Action in final draft:** "Terminal 2" retained but tagged `[UNVERIFIED — HUMAN REVIEW]` because it is a specific factual claim about a load-bearing scene. The narrative function (a coordination failure in a specific location at a specific airport) survives if the terminal is dropped, but the visceral scene is degraded; flagging is the conservative path.

## Suspected Errors (Number in Draft Doesn't Match the Brief)

**E1. "A director-level EM position runs $200,000 to $300,000 before staff and program costs." (§V; endnote 31)**
The Emergency Management Consultant brief (line 66) states: *"A VP of Preparedness at a peer public-sector organization is a $200,000–$300,000 position before staff and program costs."* The brief explicitly anchors the range to a **VP-level** position, not a director-level position. The draft's recommendation in §VI consistently argues for a director-level EM placement under the COO — but then borrows the VP-of-Preparedness compensation range, which the same memo's body language tells the reader is the higher-cost enhanced model. **Action in final draft:** the $200K–$300K figure has been re-attributed honestly — kept in §V but recharacterized as "a VP-level position benchmark per the Consultant brief; a director-level position will sit lower in the range." This is what the briefs support.

**E2. "LAX is structurally identical." (§III)**
The CEO brief (line 62) describes LAX's structure as *EM as one of five direct-report divisions alongside Airside Operations, Landside Operations, Terminal Operations/Guest Services, and Operations Administrative Support.* The ATL structure (CEO brief line 64; Director of Public Safety brief line 44) places EM **alongside PSD** under a single Senior Deputy GM of Operations. These are both peer-under-operations configurations, but they are not structurally identical: LAX makes EM peer to operations sub-divisions; ATL makes EM peer to PSD. **Action in final draft:** "LAX is structurally identical" softened to "LAX uses a similar peer configuration under one operations executive" — which both briefs support.

## Inter-Brief Conflicts the Draft Does Not Surface

**C1. MSP placement.**
The draft (§II, item 2; §III; endnote 6) groups MSP with DFW as airports that "keep EM inside Public Safety." This tracks the Airport CEO brief (line 14, 68). **Three other briefs contradict this:**
- Operations Analyst brief line 15: *"Minneapolis-Saint Paul (MSP) Metropolitan Airports Commission similarly positions its Emergency Manager within Integrated Operations, not in the public safety hierarchy."*
- Airport COO brief line 48: *"Metropolitan Airports Commission (MSP) titles its Emergency Manager under 'Integrated Operations,' not Public Safety."*
- EM Director brief line 35: *"the Metropolitan Airports Commission hired an Emergency Manager in 2023 … placing the role within the Integrated Operations department."*

The weight of evidence in the briefs is that MSP is **not** under Public Safety. The CEO brief is the lone outlier and may reflect an older organizational reading. **Action in final draft:** MSP removed from the "tracks DFW and MSP" comparison. The framing now reads simply "MWAA's structure tracks DFW, which keeps EM inside Public Safety." This is the structurally honest move; including MSP in the comparison materially misstates the peer cohort.

## Missing or Loose Citations (Claim Is Accurate but Source Attribution Is Unclear)

**M1. "The FAA Airports Authority transferred the airports to interstate-compact governance" (§III) and endnote 14.**
The phrasing implies an entity called the "FAA Airports Authority" exists. The historical fact is that the FAA (the federal agency) transferred operating control of DCA and IAD to the newly created Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority under the Metropolitan Washington Airports Act of 1986, effective 1987. **Action in final draft:** rephrased to "the FAA transferred the airports to the newly created Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority" — which the briefs (Reg-Political line 11; EM Director line 115) support.

**M2. "Within thirty minutes" attribution of NTSB and White House Situation Room. (§I)**
The EM Director brief explicitly anchors "the White House Situation Room — all in the first 30 minutes" (line 17). NTSB scene management is referenced as one of the coordination lanes (line 17) but not specifically time-stamped to the first 30 minutes. **Action in final draft:** NTSB retained in the agencies list with the framing "in the first hours" rather than "within thirty minutes" specifically for NTSB; the 30-minute figure preserved for the first-responder agencies anchored directly to the EM Director brief.

**M3. "A standing duty-officer rotation … MWAA does not have one." (§III, with endnote 22)**
The Stage 1 briefs do not contain an explicit factual claim that MWAA lacks a 24/7 EM duty-officer rotation. The EM Director brief (line 45) describes the DCA *Airport Operations* Duty Manager but does not state that EM specifically lacks its own duty officer. **Action in final draft:** softened from a categorical claim ("MWAA does not have one") to a conditional ("an EM-specific duty-officer rotation … is the operational difference; the Stage 1 record does not document one at MWAA"). Tagged `[UNVERIFIED — HUMAN REVIEW]` because it is load-bearing for the operational recommendation.
