# Infrastructure Economist Brief
## Mega-Terminal Programs That Underperformed: The Financial Anatomy of Predictable Failure

**Prepared for:** Stage 1 Research — AI Council Run  
**Date:** May 20, 2026  
**Analyst role:** Senior Infrastructure Economist  
**Focus:** Airport CapEx economics, cost overrun patterns, debt service impacts, and opportunity cost

---

## Key Findings

1. **Global airport CapEx reached US$40 billion in 2023 — barely above pre-pandemic levels — while interest expense rose 18% in the same year, meaning airports are paying materially more to service the debt from prior construction cycles than they are generating in new investment throughput.** The aggregate debt-to-EBITDA ratio for the world's airports was 5.74:1 in 2023, elevated by any standard. [Source: ACI World Airport Economics Report, April 2025, https://aci.aero/2025/04/28/airports-face-financial-challenges-despite-air-traffic-rebound-aci-world-economics-report-reveals/]

2. **US airport capital investment needs identified in the FAA NPIAS jumped 43% in two years — from roughly $43.5 billion (2021-2025 cycle) to $62.4 billion (2023-2027 cycle) — driven by construction cost inflation that ran 10.1% in 2021 and 14.8% in 2022 before moderating.** Over the full FY2019-to-FY2029 arc, AIP-eligible infrastructure needs roughly doubled, from $35.1 billion to $67.5 billion. The programs being programmed today cost one-third more than programs scoped four years ago — without adding one additional gate. [Source: ACC Online NPIAS report, https://acconline.org/industry-news-new-faa-npias-identifies-over-62-billion-in-aip-eligible-needs/; ENR via DWU Consulting, https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-construction-cost-iija-funding]

3. **The pattern of cost overruns in mega-terminal programs is not random variance — it is nearly universal.** Across Bent Flyvbjerg's foundational database of 258 transport infrastructure projects in 20 nations, 86% experienced cost overruns with an average overrun of 28%. For rail and complex transit projects, the average escalation reached 45%. Airport terminal programs — which combine rail-like systems complexity with the political pressure of a fixed ribbon-cutting date — exhibit both dynamics simultaneously. [Source: Flyvbjerg et al., Transport Reviews 2003; Strategy+Business summary, https://www.strategy-business.com/article/Why-do-large-projects-go-over-budget]

4. **Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER) overran its initial €2.8 billion budget by at least 132%, reaching a final cost of approximately €6.5 billion, and opened nine years late — but the financial damage was visible in 2012, when inspectors catalogued 66,500 defects across the terminal.** The fire safety system — the single most consequential failure — was designed by Alfredo di Mauro, a man credentialed only as an engineering draftsman who acknowledged that "no one asked about my university qualifications." The project cycled through multiple CEOs between 2012 and 2016. The warning signs were legible; the governance structures to act on them were not. [Sources: Euronews https://www.euronews.com/2020/10/31/berlin-airport-opens-10-years-late-and-three-times-over-budget; The Local https://www.thelocal.de/20140625/fake-engineer-di-mauro-designed-berlin-airport-fire-system; Enlytencircle case study https://enlytencircle.ca/breaking-the-big-berlin-airport-project-delay-case-study/]

5. **Each month of opening delay carries concrete, quantifiable cost — and airports routinely underestimate it.** Denver International Airport's 16-month delay (October 1993 to February 1995) generated $18–19 million in monthly operating deficits, accumulating a $360 million total delay cost against a baggage system contract that started at $193 million. The baggage system was decommissioned 10 years after it opened, replaced by a conventional system that could have been built for $51 million. [Source: GAO Report RCED-95-35BR, https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GAOREPORTS-RCED-95-35BR/html/GAOREPORTS-RCED-95-35BR.htm]

6. **Cost-per-enplanement (CPE) increases of 18% immediately following major terminal expansions are the historical norm, not the exception — and CPE increases exceeding 12% risk triggering formal airline rate challenges under use agreements.** Meanwhile, landing fees and terminal rents represent only 7% of total airline operating expenses, meaning airlines have limited absolute exposure but acute political incentive to fight rate increases. The threat of rate challenges further constrains airport financial flexibility during precisely the period — Years 1–3 of operation — when new terminal debt service is largest and traffic ramp-up is most uncertain. [Source: DWU Consulting CPE analysis, https://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/articles/cost-per-enplaned-passenger; DWU IIJA funding analysis]

7. **The JFK New Terminal One's financial structure embeds demand risk that did not exist in prior-generation airport P3s, and the project's Phase A opening is operating in what the January 2025 Alternative Solutions Report describes as a "recovery posture."** The terminal must serve 23 million passengers at full build-out to validate its $9.5 billion cost structure. At a BBB-/Baa3 rating with $1.78 billion in bank debt still awaiting refinancing after the Series 2025 bonds, it is a real-time test of whether the financial architecture of mega-terminal programs can absorb the schedule and demand volatility that history says is probable, not merely possible. [Source: DWU Consulting P3 analysis https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/jfk-new-terminal-one-p3-financing; Port Authority press release https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-hochul-announces-groundbreaking-95-billion-new-terminal-one-major-step-forward-port]

8. **The opportunity cost of capital is the argument airports are least equipped to make internally — and most vulnerable to externally.** Technology investments in predictive maintenance, A-CDM, and biometrics consistently show payback periods under 18 months and projected returns exceeding 200%. A $50 million bond issuance for physical infrastructure at 4.5% over 30 years adds approximately $3.1 million annually in debt service — roughly $0.08 CPE at a 40-million-enplanement airport — but the operational throughput gains from that same $50 million spent on technology are measured in minutes of gate turn time and percentage points of on-time performance. The capital allocation decision is not made on those terms; it is made on the basis of programmed scope, bond covenants, and political commitments that precede any honest alternatives analysis. [Source: DWU Consulting cost-per-enplanement math; DWU IIJA analysis; ACI World economics data]

## Evidence

### Global and US Airport CapEx Trends

ACI World's 2025 Airport Economics Report — drawing on data from 1,060 airports representing 82% of global pre-pandemic traffic — places 2023 global airport capital expenditure at US$40 billion. That figure represents a 4% year-over-year increase from 2022 and sits 1% above 2019 pre-pandemic levels. The numerically important data point is not the CapEx itself but what is happening beneath it: interest expenses rose 18% in 2023, reflecting the debt accumulated during prior construction cycles. Global airport revenues, at US$146 billion in 2023, remain 11.4% below 2019's US$158.6 billion in nominal terms. The industry is carrying peak-cycle debt service on pre-pandemic revenue structures. [Source: https://aci.aero/2025/04/28/airports-face-financial-challenges-despite-air-traffic-rebound-aci-world-economics-report-reveals/]

ACI World's long-range infrastructure forecast projects US$2.4 trillion in required capital investment globally to accommodate demand through 2040, with Asia-Pacific alone accounting for US$1.3 trillion. [Source: ACI Asia-Pacific & Middle East, https://www.aci-asiapac.aero/media-centre/news/airports-in-asia-pacific-and-middle-east-to-see-ususd240-billion-investment-in-infrastructure-expansion]

In the United States, the FAA's NPIAS 2023–2027 identifies $62.4 billion in AIP-eligible airport development needs — a 43% increase over the prior NPIAS cycle published two years earlier, driven by both construction cost inflation and expanded project scope. Construction cost inflation (ENR Building Cost Index) ran 10.1% in 2021 and 14.8% in 2022 before moderating to 2.6% in 2023 and 2.8% in 2024. The cumulative impact of that inflation spike: a $500 million capital program budgeted in 2019 costs approximately $690 million to execute in 2025 for identical scope. [Source: ACCONLINE NPIAS summary https://acconline.org/industry-news-new-faa-npias-identifies-over-62-billion-in-aip-eligible-needs/; DWU Consulting IIJA analysis https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-construction-cost-iija-funding]

Federal supplemental funding through the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law — approximately $19.5 billion in aviation grants over FY2022–FY2026 — partially offsets this, but those supplemental funds expire in December 2026. Post-2026, the baseline Airport Improvement Program reverts to approximately $4.0 billion annually. A $40 million terminal project with $20 million IIJA support in 2023 becomes a project requiring $40–50 million in new debt by 2027, as both construction costs rise and federal support contracts. [Source: DWU Consulting IIJA analysis]

### Notable Cost Overruns and Schedule Slips

**Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER).** Initial budget approximately €2.8 billion; final cost approximately €6.5 billion; planned opening October 2011; actual opening October 31, 2020 — nine years late. The primary cause of delay was the fire protection and alarm system, which failed its mandatory acceptance test. Investigators found 66,500 defects across the terminal. The fire system's smoke extraction design had been produced by Alfredo di Mauro, a man without engineering credentials who later acknowledged that "no one asked about my university qualifications; that wasn't necessary for the work we carried out." The project had been fragmented into 50 construction lots (divided from an initial 7), each managed by separate contractors. Leadership cycled through multiple CEOs between 2012 and 2016. By the planned 2012 opening date, approximately 57% of systems were operational. The remaining 43% took eight more years. [Sources: Euronews; The Local; Enlytencircle; AeroTime https://www.aerotime.aero/articles/what-lessons-can-be-learned-from-the-berlin-brandenburg-airport-project]

**Heathrow Terminal 5 (T5).** Cost: £4.2 billion (~$8.9 billion in modern equivalents). Opening day: March 27, 2008. Within hours, British Airways suspended hold baggage check-in. By month's end, approximately 28,000 bags had been lost. British Airways CEO Willie Walsh identified inadequate staff training, broken lifts, and parking failures alongside the IT failures — a compounding of physical and operational gaps. The root cause, per post-mortem analysis, was a communication breakdown between BAA (the owner) and British Airways (the operator): each assumed the other was responsible for pre-opening system integration. BAA's CEO acknowledged that the failure had "turned what should have been an occasion of national pride into one of national humiliation." The terminal had undergone six months of trials — none of which adequately stress-tested the full-throughput scenario of opening day. [Sources: Simple Flying https://simpleflying.com/heathrow-terminal-5-teething-problems/; ORAT literature]

**Denver International Airport — Automated Baggage System.** The $193 million BAE Automated Systems contract grew to exceed $400 million. Denver's 16-month delay (October 1993 to February 1995) cost the city $18–19 million per month in operating deficits, accumulating $360 million in documented delay costs. Scope expanded from one concourse for one airline (United) to three concourses for 20 airlines — more than 2,000 design changes — without rebaselining cost or schedule. The system was decommissioned in 2005 and replaced by a conventional $51 million manual system. The original scope's failure to account for system integration complexity is the canonical case of owner-engineer role confusion: the City of Denver, as owner, accepted scope expansion it lacked the engineering staff to evaluate. [Source: GAO Report RCED-95-35BR; Wrike case study https://www.wrike.com/blog/lessons-learned-from-project-failure-at-denver-international-airport-why-checking-bags-is-still-a-pain/]

**LAX Automated People Mover (SkyLink/APM).** Original construction budget: $1.9 billion. Current estimated total project cost: $2.78–3.34 billion — an overrun of $880 million, or 46%. Original completion: 2023. Current target: early 2026. The 2024–25 Los Angeles County Grand Jury found that contractor LINXS "leveraged the change order process by implicitly holding out the threat of prolonged litigation to force LAWA to agree." LAWA paid over $200 million in dispute settlements through 2023, then agreed to a $550 million global settlement in 2024. Fitch downgraded the project's credit prospects, citing "extended construction delays, prolonged dispute resolution, and difficulties in the parties' working relationship." The project is a P3 under a 30-year design-build-finance-operate-maintain contract — which proved to not automatically transfer risk; it transferred the risk of litigation expense back to the public owner through a poorly structured conflict resolution mechanism. [Source: LAist https://laist.com/news/transportation/lax-people-mover-delay-grand-jury-report; NBC Los Angeles; Fox LA]

**JFK New Terminal One (NTO).** Total program: $9.5 billion, making it the largest single-asset P3 project financing in US history. Phase A (14 gates) targets a 2026 opening; 23 gates at full build-out by 2030. The project has issued $5.917 billion in green bonds across three tranches (2023, 2024, 2025), all rated BBB-/Baa3 — investment grade but at the low end. Ferrovial (holding ~49% equity) was a mandatory selection given its then-management of Heathrow. $1.78 billion in bank debt remained unrefined after the July 2025 Series 2025 bond issuance, creating future market-condition dependency. A January 2025 internal analysis describes the project as operating in a "recovery posture rather than comfortable buffering" relative to its Phase A schedule. The demand-risk structure means that if international traffic underperforms — due to recession, pandemic recurrence, or airline routing changes — NTO LLC absorbs the shortfall before the Port Authority bears any exposure. At a target throughput of 23 million passengers, any sustained gap between actual traffic and projected traffic flows directly into debt service coverage. [Source: DWU Consulting P3 analysis https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/jfk-new-terminal-one-p3-financing; New Terminal One press releases]

**IAD Aerotrain (Washington Dulles).** Cost approximately $1.4 billion, including a new security screening mezzanine. The system opened in January 2010, approximately four months behind its originally planned fall 2009 opening, to complete reliability testing. The Aerotrain links Concourses A, B, and C but does not reach the far end of Concourse D — meaning that mobile lounges (the system's 1960s predecessor) continue to serve a portion of the airport. The $1.4 billion was spent for partial coverage of a terminal system that still requires the supplemental infrastructure it was meant to replace. As of the time of this writing, MWAA is evaluating a full Dulles transformation program with cost estimates reaching $22 billion. [Source: Transport Politic https://www.thetransportpolitic.com/2010/01/26/dulles-airport-replaces-distinctive-mobile-lounge-system-with-aerotrain/; View From The Wing https://viewfromthewing.com/dulles-airport-could-finally-kill-its-mobile-lounges-but-the-rebuild-plan-now-costs-22-billion/]

### Cost-Per-Enplanement Trends

Historical CPE data — drawn from FAA Form 5100-127 mandatory filings and rating agency reports — shows that CPE increases an average of 18% immediately following major terminal expansions, then declines approximately 2% annually over the following five years as traffic grows to fill the new capacity. The critical vulnerability is in the ramp-up period: airlines face peak CPE exposure in Years 1–3 of terminal operation, precisely when traffic growth is most speculative and when operator transition costs are highest.

A simplified debt service math: a $50 million bond at 4.5% over 30 years increases annual debt service by approximately $3.1 million. At a 40-million-enplanement airport, that adds approximately $0.08 CPE. Scale that to a $500 million bond program and the impact is $0.80 CPE — material against an airline operating cost base where landing fees and terminal rents already account for roughly 7% of total costs. CPE increases exceeding 12% historically trigger formal rate challenges from carriers under use agreements, particularly at airports with viable alternative hubs within 200 miles. [Source: DWU Consulting CPE data https://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/articles/cost-per-enplaned-passenger; DWU IIJA analysis]

### Debt Service Ratios

Post-pandemic, most large US hub airports have rebuilt debt service coverage ratios into investment-grade ranges. Philadelphia International Airport improved its DSCR from 1.11x in 2022 to 1.24x in 2023 to 1.56x in 2024. Hawaii's airport system carried a Moody's-adjusted net revenue DSCR of 1.8x in FY2024, but projects moderation toward 1.2x through 2030 as debt service increases with capital program execution. Tampa's Aa3-rated DSCR is projected to stabilize near 1.8x. [Source: Moody's Tampa rating https://www.tampaairport.com/sites/default/files/2024-08/TPA%20Moodys%202024B%20Jul%2030%202024.pdf; Hawaii credit opinion https://investorrelations.hawaii.gov/dot-airports/wp-content/uploads/sites/5/2025/02/Credit_Opinion-Hawaii-State-of-Airports-07Feb2025-PBM_1436735.pdf; Philadelphia investor relations]

The pattern is consistent: DSCR begins to compress as new capital programs come online and debt service mounts, and stabilizes only as traffic ramp-up matures. The 1.2x trough is thin. Rating agency covenants typically require 1.25x senior lien DSCR; airports operating near that floor have limited capacity to absorb revenue shortfalls or unplanned operating cost increases.

### Opportunity Cost Argument

The opportunity cost framing is rarely made rigorously inside airport organizations, for structural reasons: capital programs are bond-financed (creating a different cost-of-capital perception than operating budgets), and CIP commitments are typically locked in years before operational alternatives mature. The honest comparison is this: for every $100 million in construction-phase escalation — which is now routine in complex terminal programs — an airport foregoes the operating technology, staffing, and process investment that would drive measurable improvement in the metrics that passengers and airlines actually care about (on-time performance, gate turns, dwell satisfaction).

Technology investment benchmarks cited by aviation analytics firms show payback periods under 18 months and returns exceeding 200% for operational technology (predictive maintenance, advanced CDM, biometric flow management). These figures are not directly comparable to terminal construction ROI, which is measured over decades. But the comparison is increasingly being made by airlines negotiating use agreements, and airport boards have not historically been equipped to contest it.

The post-2026 IIJA funding cliff sharpens the opportunity cost calculus: a project requiring $40–50 million in new debt in 2027 that received $20 million in federal grants in 2023 is now a qualitatively different investment decision — one that most airports made in 2019 or 2020 under a different funding assumption. [Source: DWU IIJA analysis; ACI World CapEx projections; aviation technology ROI benchmarks from Valour Consultancy https://valourconsultancy.com/from-r-numbers-to-roi-numbers-how-airport-self-service-technologies-are-driving-post-pandemic-recovery/]

## Caveats and Limitations

**On cost overrun data:** Final cost figures for complex terminal programs are contested and depend on accounting period, what is included in scope (land, financing costs, tenant fit-out, pre-opening operations), and whether overruns are measured against original budget or last approved baseline. BER's "final cost" variously appears as €6.5 billion, €7.9 billion, and €10 billion in published sources — reflecting different accounting periods and attribution decisions. I have used the most conservative figure that is broadly consistent across primary sources.

**On CPE data:** CPE figures are not standardized across airports. The metric appears in bond official statements, FAA Form 5100-127, rating agency reports, and airport annual reports — often with different inputs (some include gate concessions; some exclude non-aeronautical cost recovery). Trend comparisons across airports require caution.

**On technology ROI claims:** The 200%+ ROI and 18-month payback benchmarks cited here are drawn from industry consulting reports and are not independently verified against audited airport financial statements. They should be used directionally, not as precision claims in financial analysis.

**On JFK NTO:** This program is live and in construction as of this writing. The financial stress indicators cited (BBB- rating, recovery posture language, unrefined bank debt) reflect the state of the project as of mid-2025 and may not reflect subsequent developments. This brief does not claim the project will fail — only that the warning sign framework the thesis proposes is visible in real time.

**On Flyvbjerg statistics:** The foundational 86%/28% overrun figures derive from a 2003 study of 258 projects. Later meta-analyses have produced varying figures depending on project type, geography, and time period. The directional conclusion — that overruns are the rule, not the exception — is robust. The specific percentages should be used with appropriate attribution to source.

**Data not found:** I could not locate independently audited DSCR data for the JFK NTO project (it is early-stage). I could not find primary-source data on Dulles Aerotrain ridership or headcount throughput that would allow a cost-per-trip efficiency comparison against mobile lounges. I could not find a rigorous published comparison of CapEx-to-operating-budget ratios across large US hub airports that would allow benchmarking the capital intensity question against peers.
