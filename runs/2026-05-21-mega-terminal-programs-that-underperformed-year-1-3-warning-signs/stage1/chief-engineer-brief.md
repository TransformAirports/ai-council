# Chief Engineer Brief: Mega-Terminal Programs That Underperformed — Year 1–3 Warning Signs

**Run:** mega-terminal-programs-that-underperformed-year-1-3-warning-signs  
**Stage:** 1 — Independent Research  
**Agent:** Chief Engineer (25-year airport program delivery)  
**Date:** 2026-05-20

---

## Key Findings

1. **Scope creep is not a planning failure — it is a governance failure with a physical address.** BER's supervisory board approved mid-construction additions of north and south piers plus an inserted retail level. Each scope change reset the critical path in systems that had already been designed, procured, and partially installed. The cost of a scope change made in concrete is roughly two orders of magnitude higher than the same change made on paper during design development. That ratio is not hyperbole; it is the reason BER's construction cost roughly doubled from approximately €2.8 billion to over €7 billion.

2. **Integrated systems testing is the earliest casualty of schedule pressure and the last problem to be discovered.** Every major Year 1 failure mode — BER's smoke extraction, Denver DIA's automated baggage, Heathrow T5's baggage IT — traces directly to a testing regime that was deferred, compressed, or never honestly scoped in the baseline schedule. The testing of complex, interdependent systems (fire detection, smoke extraction, baggage sortation, fueling, access control) cannot be parallelized with construction; it requires a completed, powered, cleaned facility. Owners who schedule ribbon-cutting before commissioning completion have already made their Year 1 decision.

3. **The owner-engineer interface is the single highest-risk boundary on any mega-terminal program.** T5's construction success — delivered on time to its £4.2 billion budget — resulted from BAA operating as a genuine client-integrator, holding all risk on its own balance sheet and managing an integrated supply chain under a bespoke relational contract. BER's failure resulted from a supervisory board of politicians making technical decisions they were unqualified to make. Denver's Great Hall P3 terminated after 14 months because the airport issued enough owner-directed changes to functionally redesign the project mid-construction while simultaneously accusing the contractor of scope creep. The delivery model sets the ceiling on what is achievable; no project management system can compensate for structural confusion about who owns risk.

4. **A Construction Safety and Phasing Plan (CSPP) required under FAA AC 150/5370-2G is not a paperwork exercise — it is the mechanism by which a phased, live-airport program is legally permitted to proceed.** Every change to construction sequencing on a certificated airport that affects runways, taxiways, aprons, or ARFF response routes must go back through the CSPP process. This constraint means that a schedule "optimization" made in the project office does not automatically become executable on the airfield. The sequencing discipline imposed by Part 139 compliance is a structural cost driver, not a variable one.

5. **Lifecycle cost thinking is structurally absent from most mega-terminal business cases.** Industry data is consistent: the initial capital cost of a terminal asset represents 15–20% of its 30-year lifetime cost. Baggage handling system CAPEX accounts for 30–40% of lifecycle cost; OPEX — energy, maintenance contracts, spare parts, labor — consumes 60–70%. A sponsor who optimizes the ribbon-cutting price and defers decisions about maintenance funding, energy performance, and equipment service contracts is pre-loading failure at the 10–15 year mark, when major mechanical systems (baggage sortation, vertical transportation, HVAC, fueling hydrant networks) hit their first major replacement cycle.

6. **Optimism bias in megaproject forecasting is statistically documented, not anecdotal.** Bent Flyvbjerg's database of hundreds of global megaprojects finds that 90% experience cost overruns averaging 80%, a pattern stable for 70 years. Airport terminal programs are not exempt. The Denver DIA baggage system delay alone cost $360 million in operating losses, lost revenue, and remediation by the time GAO measured it in 1994 — before the system was even operational. The monthly operating deficit ran $13–19 million throughout the delay. These numbers appear nowhere in the original project business case.

7. **Interdependencies make single-project evaluation meaningless.** "Add a gate" is a three-year program because the gate touches: apron geometry (FAA AC 150/5300-13A aircraft design group clearances), ground power and preconditioned air (utility extension), hydrant fueling (design, demolition, pipe-in-ground, commissioning), baggage system capacity (additional bag tags through the EDS tunnel), ARFF response geometry (Part 139 response time compliance from the nearest station), and the airline use agreement (terminal cost per square foot redistribution). Evaluating the gate cost without those interdependencies produces a number that will be wrong by a factor of three. Running multiple concurrent mega-programs at one airport — as the Port Authority of New York and New Jersey is managing today with the $19 billion JFK transformation — multiplies labor pool competition, utility outage coordination, and CSPP complexity nonlinearly.

8. **Year 1–3 underperformance is almost always set before the first structural steel goes up.** The warning signs are engineering and governance artifacts, not operational artifacts. They are visible in the completeness of the basis of design, the realism of the commissioning schedule, the clarity of the owner-contractor risk split, and the independence of the project's technical authority from its political authority. By the time the ribbon is cut, the program's outcome has been substantially determined.

## Evidence Section

### BER — Berlin Brandenburg Airport

Construction began September 2006. The original opening target was October 30, 2011. The airport opened October 31, 2020 — nine years and one day late, at a cost exceeding €7 billion against an initial estimate of approximately €2.8 billion. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction_of_Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport]

**Scope creep, physical form:** Mid-construction, airport management requested north and south "piers" added to the main terminal footprint, converting a rectangular plan to a U-shape and substantially enlarging the structure. They then requested insertion of an additional retail and food service level. Both changes were approved by a supervisory board that included the Mayors of Berlin and Brandenburg and the German Federal Finance Minister — politicians, not engineers. [Source: https://interestingengineering.com/science/heres-how-berlin-brandenburg-airport-became-one-of-the-biggest-engineering-failures]

**Systems integration failure, specific:** The fire protection and smoke extraction system was not built to the construction permit. The design required smoke to be pumped downward — reversing hot-gas buoyancy — from ceiling-level collection ducts into basement shafts. This is physically possible but requires exceptional engineering rigor. The system was designed by Alfredo di Mauro, who held himself out as an engineer but was qualified only as an engineering draughtsman. Di Mauro was dismissed in May 2014 with the airport authority citing "serious defects" in his work. Rebuilding the smoke extraction system cost nine-digit figures. [Source: https://www.thelocal.de/20140625/fake-engineer-di-mauro-designed-berlin-airport-fire-system; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction_of_Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport]

**Infrastructure defects at first scheduled opening:** 120,000 documented defects. Approximately 170,000 kilometers of cable dangerously wired — incompatible cable types routed together, including signal cables adjacent to high-voltage conductors. Sixty kilometers of cooling pipe installed without thermal insulation. Seven hundred kilometers of cable duct required replacement due to water infiltration. [Source: https://interestingengineering.com/science/heres-how-berlin-brandenburg-airport-became-one-of-the-biggest-engineering-failures; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction_of_Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport]

**Governance structure:** Prof. Genia Kostka of the Free University of Berlin: "The supervisory board was full of politicians who had no idea how to supervise the project. They were in charge of key decisions." [Source: https://interestingengineering.com/science/heres-how-berlin-brandenburg-airport-became-one-of-the-biggest-engineering-failures] Four CEOs cycled through the project between 2013 and 2017. Corruption allegations included €150,000 in contractor bribes. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction_of_Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport]

### Heathrow Terminal 5

The T5 construction program ran from September 2002 to opening in March 2008. Total cost: £4.2 billion. Nearly 20 years elapsed from first conception to operation. The building was delivered on time and on budget. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heathrow_Terminal_5]

**What made T5 a construction success:** BAA employed a bespoke relational contract — the "T5 Agreement" — under which BAA retained all project risk on its own balance sheet and did not transfer risk to tier-1 contractors. Contractors were incentivized to collaborate and problem-solve rather than to generate change orders and claims. BAA maintained genuine in-house project management capability and operated as the integrating authority across all systems. The delivery model produced remarkable safety statistics alongside on-budget delivery. [Source: https://infrastructuredeliverymodels.gihub.org/case-studies/heathrow-terminal-5/]

**What made T5 an operational failure on Day 1:** Opening day, March 27, 2008. The baggage system contained the largest automated baggage handling installation in the world — five miles of high-speed track, eleven miles of conveyor. Within hours, 34 flights were cancelled and baggage check-in was suspended. Over the following 10 days, 42,000 bags failed to travel with their owners and 500 flights were cancelled.

Root cause: A software filter installed during testing to prevent test data from entering the live system was never removed. The production system therefore could not process bags transferring from other airlines to British Airways. Staff were not trained to the level needed to operate the system under live-operations pressure. Testing had started late, compressing the time available to identify and fix integration failures. [Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/305636422; https://www.mightytravels.com/2024/06/heathrow-terminal-5-revisited-lessons-learned-from-an-operational-nightmare/] Two senior British Airways executives — the director of operations and the director of customer services — were terminated.

T5 is the canonical case for why construction delivery and operational readiness are distinct programs that must be managed with equal rigor. The construction model was exemplary; the operational transition model was not.

### Denver International Airport — Automated Baggage System (1994)

The DIA automated baggage system began as a $193 million contract for a system that would automate bag routing across all three concourses. The technology had not been demonstrated at comparable scale.

**What failed:** During testing, bags were misloaded, misrouted, and ejected from carts — the carts collided, jammed belts, and destroyed luggage. Photocell detection failed to reliably signal belt stops. The system was never delivered as designed; it was ultimately installed only at Concourse B, for United Airlines outbound bags only.

**Cost and schedule impact (GAO documented):** Total delay costs through February 28, 1995: approximately $360 million. Components included $230 million in operating deficits, $37 million in lost income, $86 million for an alternative baggage system and modifications, and $8 million in additional bond issuance. Monthly operating deficit ranged from $13.35 million to $18.75 million during the delay. The airport opened 16 months behind its original October 1993 target. [Source: GAO Report RCED-95-35BR, https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GAOREPORTS-RCED-95-35BR/html/GAOREPORTS-RCED-95-35BR.htm]

The automated system was decommissioned in 2005 and replaced with a conventional manual system — meaning the airport ultimately paid for two complete baggage systems.

### Denver International Airport — Great Hall Renovation (2019)

The Great Hall P3 contract was awarded to Great Hall Partners, led by Ferrovial, at approximately $650 million. By month 11, GHP issued a change order notification claiming $288 million in additional costs. By late July 2019, the overrun estimate had grown to $311 million with delays exceeding three years. DIA terminated the contract in August 2019, 14 months into construction.

**Cause pattern:** The independent report found that the airport engaged in what the report termed "heavy-handed oversight" while simultaneously driving the change order volume that produced the overrun. A city council member who authorized the 2017 vote later called it "the worst vote he'd ever made." Structural concrete defects were discovered mid-construction. Great Hall Partners attributed schedule collapse to "the multitude of Owner Changes being issued on the project." [Source: https://www.constructiondive.com/news/denver-great-hall-project-new-report-what-went-wrong/629795/; https://www.denver7.com/news/local-news/new-report-faults-dia-for-heavy-handed-oversight]

Final cost: at least $2.35 billion — more than three times the original contract price. Completion rescheduled to 2028, seven years beyond original schedule. [Source: https://airlineweekly.skift.com/2022/01/denver-approves-airport-project-with-1-billion-cost-overrun/]

The post-termination independent report concluded the project "may have been better served as a design-bid-build contract or a construction manager/general contractor project" — a finding that indicts the procurement structure, not merely the execution.

### Regulatory Standards That Drive Cost (Non-Optional)

**FAA AC 150/5370-2G — Operational Safety on Airports During Construction:** Requires a Construction Safety and Phasing Plan (CSPP) for every on-airfield construction project at a certificated airport or one receiving Airport Improvement Program funds. The CSPP must account for ARFF response times, runway safety areas, taxiway object-free areas, and airfield lighting continuity. Changes to construction sequence that affect any of these must be resubmitted. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/documentLibrary/media/Advisory_Circular/150-5370-2G.pdf]

**FAA 14 CFR Part 139 — Airport Certification:** Certificated airports must conduct daily self-inspections and post-event inspections. Construction that affects ARFF response geometry or equipment access paths requires coordination with the FAA regional office before proceeding. Non-compliance is a certificate jeopardy event. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/airport_safety/part139_cert]

**FAA AC 150/5300-13A — Airport Design:** Sets aircraft design group clearances, runway object-free areas, and taxiway geometry standards. A new gate pier, a relocated taxiway, or an extended apron must be designed to this standard — which means the gate's geometric footprint drives pavement, marking, and lighting costs in ways that unit-cost estimating (dollars per square foot of terminal) cannot capture.

**ORAT — Operational Readiness, Activation and Transition:** The industry-standard methodology for managing the transition from construction to operations. IATA and ACI both publish guidance frameworks. ORAT begins as a planning function early in design; it includes site acceptance testing, systems integration testing, and operational trials with live staff before opening day. [Source: https://www.aeroclass.org/orat/] The Heathrow T5 opening-day failure is specifically traceable to the ORAT process being compressed and under-resourced relative to the construction delivery program.

## Engineering Synthesis: What the Patterns Mean

The cases above are not outliers. They are a sample from a replicable distribution. The structural failure modes recur because they are embedded in how mega-terminal programs are conceived, governed, and tested — not in how they are constructed.

**Scope creep is irreversible once systems are designed.** The moment a mechanical, electrical, or plumbing system has been designed to a given building footprint and configuration, any change that alters that footprint initiates a cascade of design revisions, permit amendments, contractor change orders, and schedule impacts. A scope change approved in a boardroom takes 12–24 months to manifest fully in the cost and schedule baseline. By that point, the people who approved the change may have left the project. BER is not a German failure. It is what happens when a governing body with political accountability but no engineering authority makes technical decisions.

**Integrated systems testing cannot be fast-tracked.** Fire detection, smoke extraction, baggage sortation, access control, vertical transportation, and building management systems must be individually commissioned and then tested as an integrated whole against failure scenarios. This process requires a complete, operational facility — not a construction site. At BER, the fire system could not pass acceptance testing until the physical system was rebuilt. At T5, the baggage IT system was never run under live-conditions complexity until the day it had to process actual passengers' actual bags. The ORAT methodology exists precisely to prevent this. When it is compressed or underfunded relative to the construction program, the commissioning process gets transferred to Year 1 operations — and passengers, airlines, and regulators pay the tuition.

**The owner-engineer boundary is where projects actually fail.** T5 succeeded in construction because BAA accepted that it owned the risk and built the management apparatus to execute against that ownership. BER failed because political authorities made engineering decisions. Denver's Great Hall failed because neither party to the P3 agreement had a stable definition of the owner's role. When a sponsor cannot clearly define who holds technical authority and who holds contract authority — and when those two are not the same person — the project will spend that ambiguity in change orders and claims.

These warning signs are not hidden. They are visible in the governance structure, the commissioning schedule, the basis of design completeness, and the delivery model — years before the first structural member is erected. The tragedy of every underperforming mega-terminal is not that the warning signs were absent. It is that the institution was not structured to read them.
