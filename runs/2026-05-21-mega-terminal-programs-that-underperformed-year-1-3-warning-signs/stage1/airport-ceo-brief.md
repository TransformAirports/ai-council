# CEO's Brief: Mega-Terminal Governance Failures — The Signals That Were Always There

**Stage 1 Research Brief | Transform Airports AI Council**
**Framed from the airport boardroom. Not the construction trailer.**

---

## Key Findings for the Board Chair

- **The failure mode is not technical; it is institutional.** BER, T5 Heathrow, the DIA baggage system, and O'Hare's stalled $8.5B expansion share a common pathology: a supervisory board or governing body that lacked domain expertise, received selectively curated progress reports, and had no procedural mechanism to force a corrective stop before sunk costs overwhelmed institutional will to act. The fire protection system alone added €1 billion to BER's final bill. That cost was visible in design review years before opening day.

- **The bond covenant is the CEO's first exposure.** A mega-terminal in construction that runs 25–30% over budget on a residual-cost use-and-lease structure flows directly to cost-per-enplanement and coverage ratios. Fitch's explicit band places A-rated airports at 1.25x–1.50x DSCR. An overrun that draws down reserves by $200–400 million on a mid-size hub can push coverage below covenant, triggering additional-bonds test failures and, in the worst case, a downgrade that widens spreads by 30–60 basis points on the next issuance. At a $1.5B bond deal, that is $9–18 million in annualized carrying cost with a 30-year tail.

- **Majority-in-interest clauses are a latent weapon.** In residual-cost agreements — approximately 84% of which contain MII provisions per FAA survey data — a dominant carrier that holds more than 50% of gates can veto or delay capital projects whose scope has changed materially from what appeared in the original use-and-lease agreement. A CEO who allows scope to creep without a formal MII re-authorization process is handing leverage to the carrier at exactly the moment the carrier knows the owner cannot afford to stop.

- **Optimism bias in the official statement is a credit event waiting to happen.** When traffic and revenue projections embedded in the OS were built before scope was fully defined — or before a demand shock — and actuals in Years 1–3 come in below the projected coverage threshold, the rate covenant becomes a liability. The additional-bonds test requires demonstrating future debt service capacity. An airport that missed its Year 2 projection by 15% cannot easily execute a refinancing when it needs one most.

- **Board governance structure predicts vulnerability.** City-department airports — where board members are political appointees without term limits anchored to the capital program, and where annual budget cycles can interrupt multi-year draw schedules — show a documented 1-notch rating disadvantage relative to independent operating authorities per S&P's 2023 peer analysis. BER's supervisory board was composed almost entirely of politicians, civil servants, and union officials. Its chairman, Klaus Wowereit, acknowledged publicly that no one had been selected with construction competency in mind. The board could not evaluate what it could not read.

- **Long-tenure CEOs who got it right aligned the capital program to a renegotiated use-and-lease agreement before breaking ground.** Sean Donohue at DFW closed a 10-year use-and-lease agreement before committing the airport to a $9B capital program. That sequencing is not incidental. It locks carrier cost recovery, limits MII exposure, and gives the bond market a rate-setting framework to underwrite against. CEOs who broke ground without that anchor — DIA's Kim Day on the Great Hall P3, Chicago's successive administrations at O'Hare — found themselves negotiating scope and cost with carriers who had already signed off on a different project.

- **The leading indicators are visible 5–7 years out, and they are not hidden.** Scope creep shows up in change-order logs. Testing deferral shows up in the schedule critical path. Owner-engineer role confusion shows up in contract structure — when the airport is simultaneously the owner, the program manager, and the design reviewer, no one is responsible for systemic risk. A board that demands a quarterly dashboard limited to cost, schedule, and safety misses the organizational warning signs that precede every cost overrun on record.

## Evidence

### The Bond-Rating Lens

Moody's, S&P, and Fitch each publish airport-specific rating methodologies that weigh four financial metrics heavily: debt service coverage ratio (DSCR), days cash on hand (DCOH), cost per enplanement (CPE), and carrier base concentration. Moody's Aa3-or-above airports carried a 2024 median of 72 days cash on hand; A-rated airports typically fall in the 60–75 day range. The DSCR bands that anchor the rating strata are explicit in Fitch's framework: AA airports require 1.50x–2.00x; A airports, 1.25x–1.50x; BBB airports, 1.00x–1.25x. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-bond-ratings]

The practical consequence: a $300M cost overrun on a $1.5B terminal that was originally projected to support a 1.45x DSCR can push post-delivery coverage to 1.18x — inside the covenant floor, triggering a rate-covenant cure period and, if not resolved within 24 months, a technical default on the outstanding bonds. The Burbank Airport Authority's 2024 issuance for a $1.3B terminal replacement illustrates the mechanism without the overrun: Fitch downgraded the authority one notch to A-minus before a shovel entered the ground, solely on the projected leverage impact of the new debt. [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/burbank-airport-terminal-plan-means-higher-debt-load-lower-rating]

On single-carrier concentration: Moody's Factor 1 scoring assigns lower marks when the primary carrier controls more than 60% of enplanements, and the methodology explicitly caps the rating on airports with sub-investment-grade dominant carriers. At a hub airport where one carrier controls 70%+ of gates, a construction-period disruption that depresses that carrier's operations hits both the airport's aeronautical revenue and its carrier-base quality score simultaneously — a double compression. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-bond-ratings]

Rating agencies evaluate construction-period revenue risk through a completion risk lens. P3 structures that isolate construction risk in a special-purpose vehicle — as JFK's New Terminal One consortium did with $2B in Series 2023 Special Facilities Revenue Bonds — remove that risk from the airport's general revenue pledge and transfer it to project finance analysis. The NTO consortium closed $9.0B in bank financing from 48 lenders in June 2022 — the largest bank facility ever arranged for a U.S. airport terminal — precisely because the SPV structure ringfenced the airport's credit. [Source: https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/the-new-terminal-one-at-jfk-concludes-successful-refinancing-with-2-billion-green-bonds-issuance-302007848.html] Airports that instead carry construction risk on the general revenue pledge and allow cost overruns to flow through the rate base do not have that protection.

### Airline Use-and-Lease Exposure

The FAA's survey data on use-and-lease agreements documents that approximately 84% of residual-cost agreements contain majority-in-interest clauses. Under standard MII provisions, airlines representing more than 50% of gate leases can disapprove capital projects whose costs would be included in the rate base. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/airports/aip/airport-business-practices-and-their-impact-on-airline-competition.pdf]

The MII clause is a latent credit instrument that only becomes visible when project scope changes materially from what was in the original agreement. An airport that approved a $1.5B terminal in the use-and-lease agreement but now presents carriers with a $2.2B project — driven by scope changes the carriers did not authorize — is asking them to absorb a higher CPE with no compensating route commitment. The carrier's rational response is to exercise MII rights, negotiate the scope back, or threaten to shift operations. All three outcomes reduce the airport's leverage exactly when construction costs are front-loaded and irreversible.

The O'Hare expansion illustrates this in real time. The $8.5B program unveiled in 2018 has grown by at least $3B, and the city has been renegotiating scope with United and American — the two dominant carriers — since 2023. Both carriers signed off on a revised timetable in May 2024 only after the city agreed to restructure the sequencing of which terminals are built first. [Source: https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2024/05/03/ohare-expansion-american-united-agree-mayor-johnson-timetable-global-terminal-satellite-concourses] The timeline for the program's completion has moved from 2026 to 2032. That six-year deferral is not a construction problem. It is a governance and use-and-lease problem that metastasized into a construction problem.

### Optimism Bias in the Official Statement

The DIA Great Hall P3 is a domestically documented case of optimism bias in the business case. DIA contracted with Ferrovial Airports and Saunders Construction in 2018 for a $1.8B, 34-year DBFOM deal. Within 14 months, the projected cost overrun was $288M and the schedule delay exceeded two years. DIA terminated the contract in August 2019. The city auditor later found that DIA staff had managed the contract as design-bid-build rather than as a DBFOM, inserting the owner into decisions that the contract had expressly delegated to the private partner. CEO Kim Day publicly accepted responsibility in February 2020. She departed in summer 2021. [Source: https://www.cbsnews.com/colorado/news/kim-day-great-hall-project-fiasco-blame-denver-international-airport-dia/] The program was repackaged and ultimately approved at approximately $2.35B — more than triple the original budget. [Source: https://airlineweekly.skift.com/2022/01/denver-approves-airport-project-with-1-billion-cost-overrun/]

The bond rating implication: an OS built on a $1.8B project at projected Year 3 revenues consistent with a 1.40x DSCR has no margin if actuals come in 15% below projection in Year 1 because the terminal opened 24 months late and the cost base is 30% higher than modeled. The rate covenant cure clock starts running while the airport is still in construction.

### Board Governance as a Failure Mode

BER provides the clearest causal chain from board composition to project failure. The supervisory board was composed of the mayors of Berlin and Brandenburg, federal ministers, and union officials — none with megaproject delivery credentials. Board chairman Klaus Wowereit acknowledged that no one had been selected with construction competency requirements in mind. [Source: https://www.euronews.com/2020/10/31/berlin-airport-opens-10-years-late-and-three-times-over-budget] CEO Manfred Körtgen was dismissed in May 2012. His successor Rainer Schwarz was fired in January 2013 after reporting 90% completion when independent auditors found 56% operational readiness. Hartmut Mehdorn, brought in as a turnaround appointment in March 2013, resigned in December 2014 citing excessive political interference. The supervisory board had four different governing configurations in as many years during the critical commissioning phase.

The Hertie School working paper on BER governance identifies a specific mechanism: "big and fundamental changes to scope after construction started" without formal change management matrices, and a procurement process in which the winning bid structure created misaligned incentives between the public owner and its private contractors. [Source: https://www.hertie-school.org/fileadmin/2_Research/2_Research_directory/Research_projects/Large_infrastructure_projects_in_Germany_Between_ambition_and_realities/3_WP_BER_Flughafen_Berlin_Brandenburg.pdf]

The structural difference between governance types matters in bond markets. Independent operating authorities with insulated boards show a documented median 1-notch rating advantage over city-department airports — roughly 15–25 basis points in yield spread — in S&P's 2023 peer analysis. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-governance-models-financial-autonomy] City departments that require city council approval for capital contract modifications — the standard process at Denver, per Denver Municipal Code — add 60–120 days to every corrective action, which is catastrophic when a fire protection system has been designed incorrectly and the opening date is already public.

### Long-Tenure CEO Patterns: Right and Wrong Calls

**Right:** Sean Donohue, DFW (2013–2025), sequenced the use-and-lease renegotiation before committing to the $9B DFW Forward capital program. Terminal F, initially conceived as a $1.6B, 15-gate facility, expanded to a $4B, 31-gate project only after American Airlines voluntarily increased its investment commitment. The scope growth was carrier-driven, not owner-driven, which inverts the risk profile entirely. [Source: https://dallasinnovates.com/dfw-airports-terminal-f-rises-with-record-setting-modular-construction-and-a-scaled-up-4b-plan-to-meet-global-demand/]

**Wrong:** DIA's 1990–1994 baggage system project set the template for every mega-terminal failure that followed. The city rejected three bids that all projected overrun, then awarded the contract to a fourth company at the original price and timeline. The $193M contract grew past $400M; the airport opened 16 months late at a delay cost of $500M; and the system was abandoned entirely in 2005. The failure mode — owner rejected market signals, compressed testing timeline, expanded scope mid-construction — is identical in structure to BER thirty years later. [Source: https://sebokwiki.org/wiki/Denver_Airport_Baggage_Handling_System]

**Wrong:** Chicago's successive administrations at O'Hare unveiled the ORD 21 expansion in 2018 without a carrier agreement locked and without a fully risk-adjusted cost model. Six years later, the program is estimated to be at least $3B over the original $8.5B budget, the opening has moved from 2026 to 2032, and the city is still renegotiating scope with United and American. [Source: https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-03-22/chicago-s-8-5-billion-o-hare-upgrade-falters-as-costs-balloon] The failure is not an engineering failure. The terminals have not been built yet.

**T5 Heathrow (2008):** The parliamentary inquiry found that testing timelines were compressed because construction access was not available until close to opening, and the baggage system's integrated testing was deferred. On opening day, 34 flights were cancelled, 28,000 bags were mishandled, and 15% of BA flights were cancelled over the following week. The inquiry established that staff had not completed training on the baggage system software before opening. Munich's Franz Josef Strauss airport, by contrast, had run continuous 24-hour testing for six months before its opening. [Source: https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/cm200708/cmselect/cmtran/543/543.pdf] The lesson is not that testing should happen — every project plan says testing will happen. The lesson is that integrated systems testing must be non-compressible in the schedule and must be protected by a board-level covenant that cannot be waived by the program manager under schedule pressure.

## Financial and Political Case: For and Against

### The Case for the Thesis (As the CEO Sees It)

The thesis — that mega-terminal failures are governance failures with visible leading indicators — is useful to an airport board because it converts a category of disaster that appears unpredictable into a category of disaster that is preventable. If the signals are consistent across BER, T5, DIA, and O'Hare, a board can build an early-warning framework that is specific enough to act on. That framework is worth building. The alternative is discovering the overrun in Year 4 of a 7-year construction program when the sunk cost ratio is too high to stop and the bond covenant is already at risk.

From the balance sheet: a well-governed terminal program that opens on budget and on schedule generates two financial benefits that compound over the 30-year amortization schedule. First, the airport demonstrates capital-program management capability to the rating agencies, which supports the stable outlook that keeps the next bond spread tight. Second, it preserves carrier confidence in the use-and-lease framework, which is the foundational condition for any residual-cost agreement renegotiation.

### The Case Against (What the Board Chair Will Push Back On)

The thesis risks overfitting. Not every mega-terminal failure has the same root cause, and post-hoc pattern recognition across BER, T5, and DIA can produce a framework that identifies the last war's signals rather than the next one. The specific failure modes — a German fire code revised mid-construction, a British Airways baggage software integration problem, a Denver P3 contract written ambiguously — are different enough that a generic "four signals" framework may generate false confidence in the board that it has checked the right boxes.

The political case against the thesis is sharper. A board that empowers itself to intervene in project execution creates a different governance pathology: micromanagement that undermines management accountability and drives out capable executives who will not work under constant board scrutiny. Mehdorn's resignation at BER was explicitly attributed to excessive political supervision. The cure and the disease can look alike from the outside.

## Specific Data Points for the Strategist

1. **BER cost and schedule overrun, documented:** Original budget €2.8B, final cost €6.5B (132% overrun); original opening October 2011, actual opening October 31, 2020 (nine-year delay). The parliamentary inquiry committee report runs over 1,000 pages. [Source: https://www.euronews.com/2020/10/31/berlin-airport-opens-10-years-late-and-three-times-over-budget]

2. **Fitch DSCR downgrade trigger, Burbank precedent:** Fitch calculated Burbank's DSCR at 4.3x in FY2023 pre-new-debt; post-issuance DSCR was projected at the 1.25x indenture floor — a 344-basis-point compression — resulting in a one-notch downgrade to A-minus before construction began. This is the cleanest available public data point on how a terminal program affects credit profile independent of any overrun. [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/burbank-airport-terminal-plan-means-higher-debt-load-lower-rating]

3. **MII clause prevalence, FAA documented:** 84% of residual-cost use-and-lease agreements contain majority-in-interest clauses per the FAA's airport business practices survey. The clause gives airlines holding 50%+ of gate leases the right to disapprove capital projects included in the rate base. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/airports/aip/airport-business-practices-and-their-impact-on-airline-competition.pdf]

4. **DIA Great Hall P3 — cost tripling with documented governance failure:** $1.8B contracted (2018), terminated after 14 months at $288M projected overrun, repackaged and ultimately approved at approximately $2.35B. City auditor found DIA managed the contract as design-bid-build, not DBFOM. City council approval process added 60–120 days to every material contract modification under Denver Municipal Code. [Source: https://www.constructiondive.com/news/denver-great-hall-project-new-report-what-went-wrong/629795/]

5. **O'Hare expansion — live precedent of schedule slip and MII re-negotiation:** ORD 21 unveiled at $8.5B in 2018; revised estimate by early 2024 is at least $11.5B; completion timeline moved from 2026 to 2032. United and American exercised practical MII leverage — not formal clause rights, but the economic equivalent — by withholding sign-off until the city restructured the build sequence. [Source: https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-03-22/chicago-s-8-5-billion-o-hare-upgrade-falters-as-costs-balloon]
