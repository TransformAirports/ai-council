# Airline-Side Commercial Dynamics: Mega-Terminal Programs and the Carrier Response Problem

**Brief prepared for:** Transform Airports AI Council — Stage 1 Research
**Thesis being tested:** Mega-terminal failures are slow-motion governance failures with leading indicators visible 5–7 years before ribbon-cutting.
**Analytical frame:** No terminal program can succeed if airlines don't perform against the demand assumptions. The airport builds; the airline decides whether to fly. Any business case that does not model airline response as a variable is incomplete.

---

## Key Findings

1. **CPE ranges at major US hubs span a 7:1 ratio, and the geography of that spread explains carrier behavior.** FY2024 data shows Atlanta (ATL) at $3.94, Charlotte (CLT) at $4.74, and Las Vegas (LAS) at $5.36 at the low end — all fortress hubs with dominant carriers. At the high end: Los Angeles (LAX) at $30.16, Chicago O'Hare (ORD) at $29.56, Newark (EWR) at $26.17, and JFK at $25.41. The gap between a carrier's cheapest hub and its most expensive hub is not a number buried in finance — it is the central variable in every growth allocation decision that carrier makes each planning cycle. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/large-hub/cpe]

2. **Terminal cost overruns transmit directly into CPE, and under residual agreements, the airline absorbs the shock.** Under residual ratemaking, all costs not covered by non-aeronautical revenue flow back to signatory airlines as higher landing fees and terminal rents. A terminal that comes in 40% over budget on a residual airport is not an airport finance problem — it is an airline operating cost problem. The carrier response to an unexpected CPE spike is not negotiation; it is capacity reallocation. Pittsburgh (PIT) demonstrates the mechanism: the airport was built to handle 32 million passengers annually, designed to US Airways' specifications, opened in 1992 at a cost tied to $600 million in county bonds — and when US Airways dehubbed in 2004, traffic dropped from 20 million to 8 million passengers, leaving an airport sized for four times its actual traffic. [Source: https://www.governing.com/archive/gov-pittsburgh-international-airport.html]

3. **Airline demand commitments in terminal business cases are almost always softer than they appear.** Letters of intent, memoranda of understanding, and "anchor tenant" press releases are typically non-binding. The distinction between a carrier's public support for a terminal project and its actual legal obligation — documented in a signed use agreement with specific minimum activity levels — is the most consequential gap in most terminal business cases. JFK's New Terminal One illustrates this: 15 carriers are described as tenants, but the revenue model is "traffic-dependent and with no public authority backstop." If traffic underperforms, PANYNJ does not absorb the shortfall — the NTO consortium's debt service coverage deteriorates. Rating agencies flagged demand ramp-up as a material credit risk. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/jfk-new-terminal-one-p3-financing]

4. **The BER case is the clearest example of terminal demand assumptions that were structurally disconnected from airline commercial reality.** The airport was designed for 34 million passengers annually at opening (with 46 million capacity), projected to become a major international connecting hub, anchored by Air Berlin's plan to consolidate its operations there. Air Berlin filed for insolvency on August 15, 2017 — three years before the airport opened. The retail revenue model assumed 50% of airport income from shops serving transfer passengers. The transfer hub model collapsed when the business recognized in 2015 that competition between hubs was too intense and that Berlin's passenger base was price-sensitive, not premium. In Year 1 (2020), BER handled 9 million passengers against a design capacity of 34 million — operating at 26% of design capacity, in the middle of a pandemic that it could not have anticipated but had no resilience to absorb. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport]

5. **Majority-in-interest (MII) clauses give airlines at residual airports structural veto power over capital programs — but at compensatory airports, the airport builds and the airline responds with its feet.** At the 36 of the 66 largest US airports operating under MII agreements, airlines have formally blocked or delayed capital projects. But MII clauses are only in residual agreements. At compensatory airports — including several major P3 terminal projects — the airport or developer can proceed over airline objection, and the carrier then decides at opening how much traffic to commit. The risk transfers from a legal veto to an operational one. The operational veto is harder to see in advance and carries no procedural safeguard. [Source: http://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/articles/airport-capital-review]

6. **Demand forecasting optimism is structural, not incidental.** A 2019 peer-reviewed study (Ryerson and Suh, Transportation Research Part E) established that airport passenger demand forecasts are "prone to optimism bias especially when the use cases of forecasts create incentives to inflate benefits and downplay costs." The incentive structure is obvious: a terminal program that uses realistic demand projections may fail to clear the financial hurdles needed to advance to construction. Forecasters who project conservatively do not get hired again. The result is that virtually every major terminal business case overstates Year 1-3 traffic, and the financial model built on that traffic is then stress-tested against scenarios that do not include the actual downside. [Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1366554518314947]

7. **When a terminal's anchor carrier changes strategy mid-construction, the business case is not revised — it continues forward on the original assumptions.** BER's primary tenant (Air Berlin) ceased to exist before the airport opened. The airport's leadership publicly stated in 2017 that Air Berlin's bankruptcy "would have had no imminent impact on expected traffic volume." That claim conflicted directly with the connecting hub revenue model that the retail and concessions plan was built on. The absence of a mechanism to force a business case revision when anchor carrier strategy changes is a governance failure that the construction schedule makes worse: the terminal is too far along to stop, so the revised assumptions never get formally written. [Source: https://corporate.berlin-airport.de/en/company-media/media-portal/pressemitteilungen/]

8. **The CPE spiral is self-reinforcing once it starts.** When a carrier reduces capacity at a high-CPE airport (or an airport whose CPE rises after an expensive terminal opens), enplanements fall. Because airport costs are largely fixed, the same cost base is now spread over fewer passengers — CPE rises further. The higher CPE makes the airport less attractive for new entrant growth, further limiting enplanements. CVG demonstrates: Delta reduced capacity through the mid-2000s and into the 2010s, and CPE migrated above $10 (from under $5 at hub peak) not because the airport spent more but because the denominator collapsed. A terminal overrun that raises the fixed cost base at a carrier-reduced airport enters this same spiral from a higher starting point. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airports/cvg]

## Evidence Section

### CPE Structure and Carrier Behavior

FY2024 large-hub CPE data, compiled by DWU Consulting from FAA CATS filings, shows the following range across 24 large-hub airports [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/large-hub/cpe]:

| Airport | FY2024 CPE |
|---------|-----------|
| ATL | $3.94 |
| CLT | $4.74 |
| LAS | $5.36 |
| DFW | $13.44 |
| MIA | $16.83 |
| SAN | $17.35 |
| SFO | $22.58 |
| EWR | $26.17 |
| JFK | $25.41 |
| ORD | $29.56 |
| LAX | $30.16 |

Atlanta's CPE of $3.94 is not an accident — it reflects Delta's 73% market share, its aggressive non-aeronautical revenue program, and its structural preference to route capacity through its cheapest hub. The spread between ATL ($3.94) and LAX ($30.16) is not primarily an efficiency story. It is a carrier leverage story: Delta dominates Atlanta and the airport prices to keep Delta growing. Los Angeles cannot make the same offer.

Landing fees and terminal rentals represented 5% of airline operating expenses in 2014. That percentage sounds small — but at a carrier earning $100 per ticket (ULCC) versus $260-270 (legacy), it is the difference between a viable and a marginal route. And for connecting hub economics, CPE is applied to every single enplanement including the connecting passenger who generates less local revenue than an origin-and-destination traveler. At a hub handling 50 million passengers with 60% connecting, a $5 CPE increase costs the carrier $150 million annually in direct airport cost — before any revenue offset from the premium experience the new terminal was supposed to deliver. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/articles/cost-per-enplaned-passenger]

**United at Newark** is the current live demonstration of CPE-driven diversion. EWR CPE of $26.17 — combined with FAA-imposed slot restrictions — has pushed United to shift domestic capacity to Washington Dulles. United cut three Newark routes (Providence, Manchester, Albany) and shifted capacity southward in 2023. United's own network planning team stated publicly it was cutting Newark departures by 10%. The carrier cannot easily exit Newark (its primary East Coast hub), but the direction of marginal growth decisions is clear: away from EWR, toward lower-cost alternatives. [Source: https://simpleflying.com/united-airlines-cuts-3-routes-newark-shifts-capacity-washington-dulles/]

### Use Agreements and Capital Risk Allocation

The three US ratemaking structures allocate construction cost risk fundamentally differently [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrpwebresource6/home/chapter-resources/chapter-7-commercial-service-attracting-airlines-and-transitioning-to-airline-service/7-2-airline-use-agreement/]:

**Residual:** Airlines collectively guarantee cost recovery. Airport financial risk is minimal; carrier financial risk is significant. MII clauses give airlines approval rights over capital projects because they are on the hook for cost overruns. Airlines negotiate hard to limit scope and control timing. In exchange, rates are set at the minimum necessary to meet the rate covenant.

**Compensatory:** Airport bears financial risk; airlines pay for their proportional use of what's actually built. Airlines have limited or no approval rights over capital programs because they are not backstopping cost recovery. The carrier response to an expensive terminal is post-opening: they simply don't commit the traffic the business case assumed.

**Hybrid (most common at large hubs):** Airfield under residual, terminal under compensatory. This structure partially insulates airlines from terminal cost overruns at the rate-setting level but does not create an obligation to fly.

MII clauses exist at 36 of the 66 largest US airports and have historically blocked or delayed projects. In affirmative MII structures, non-response from airlines counts as disapproval. The practical effect: airports with residual/MII structures do fewer speculative capital programs, and when they build, it is usually because the dominant carrier requested the capacity. Airports without MII protection build terminals that carriers can then choose not to use. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/airports/aip/airport-business-practices-and-their-impact-on-airline-competition.pdf]

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: British Airways at Heathrow Terminal 5

**The investment:** Terminal 5 cost £4.2 billion ($8.6 billion at the time) and took 20 years from planning to opening. Construction began September 2002. The terminal was designed to handle 30 million passengers annually, exclusively serving British Airways at Heathrow.

**The opening day disaster (March 27, 2008):** On the first day of operations, BA cancelled 34 flights and suspended baggage check-in after the baggage handling system failed. Staff could not access the car park; security processing broke down; the automated baggage system malfunctioned across multiple failure points. Over the 10 days following opening, approximately 42,000 bags failed to travel with their passengers, and more than 500 flights were cancelled before full operations resumed on April 8, 2008. Citibank estimated the cost to BA at up to $50 million (£25 million). BA's Director of Operations and Director of Customer Services were terminated. [Source: http://edition.cnn.com/2008/BUSINESS/04/04/ba.terminal5/]

**The mechanism of failure:** This was not a construction failure — T5 was built correctly and to specification. It was an integrated systems testing failure. BA ran tests with 500 employees over six months but never conducted a live simulation with the full passenger load and simultaneous baggage, security, and staff access systems operating together under real conditions. The failure mode was exactly what end-to-end testing would have revealed: the system worked in isolation and failed at integration. This is the canonical integrated-systems testing deferral case. [Source: https://hbr.org/2008/04/how-can-british-airways-recove]

**Long-term commercial performance:** Despite the catastrophic opening, BA's long-term performance at T5 vindicated the investment. By 2018, T5 was handling 32.1 million passengers — above the 30 million design capacity. Between 2012 and 2016, it won the Skytrax Best Airport Terminal award for five consecutive years. By 2019, the terminal was processing 90,000–100,000 passengers on peak days. The operational disaster cost BA approximately $50 million and its two most senior operational executives. The terminal cost £4.2 billion. The long-term economics were sound because the demand was real: BA's monopoly at Heathrow T5, the airport's slot-constrained capacity, and the underlying O&D demand of the London market provided structural demand the terminal simply had to be ready to serve. T5 is the case where the commercial case was solid and only the operational launch failed. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heathrow_Terminal_5]

**The strategic distinction:** T5 is a warning on testing discipline, not on commercial assumptions. BA's traffic commitment was genuine — it had no other terminal at Heathrow and no financial interest in underperforming. That constraint does not exist at most US mega-terminals, where the anchor carrier has competing options.

### Case Study 2: BER Berlin Brandenburg Airport

**The timeline:** Original projected opening was October 30, 2011 (cost estimate: €2.83 billion). The opening was postponed six times — to June 2012, then March 2013, then October 2013, then indefinitely. By 2015, total costs had reached €5.4 billion, and Germany had applied to the EU for an additional €2.5 billion spending approval, bringing committed costs to €6.8 billion. Audits in 2017 revealed thousands of incorrectly installed fire suppression and detection devices, nonfunctional ventilation, and substandard electrical installation. By 2017, the budget reached €7 billion. BER finally opened on November 4, 2020 — nine years late, at 2.5x the original budget estimate, during a global pandemic. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport]

**The business case problem:** The original terminal was designed for an initial capacity of 46 million passengers annually, with "projected annual passenger numbers of around 34 million" at opening. The revenue model assumed up to 50% of airport income from retail, predicated on high volumes of transfer passengers from Air Berlin's planned hub operations. Air Berlin required the airport to accommodate Oneworld alliance connecting traffic that Tegel could not provide. The entire commercial model — retail per square foot, food and beverage, premium lounge revenue — was calibrated to a connecting hub scenario, not a low-cost origin-and-destination airport. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport]

**Air Berlin's collapse as a leading indicator:** Air Berlin filed for insolvency on August 15, 2017 — three years before BER opened. The airline had been losing money for years before that; its financial distress was visible in its financial statements from at least 2015. Airport leadership stated publicly that the bankruptcy "would have had no imminent impact on expected traffic volume." That statement was true only in the narrow sense that other airlines (primarily easyJet) took some Air Berlin routes. It was false in the commercially relevant sense: the connecting hub model that underwrote BER's retail and concessions business case was gone. The airport proceeded to open on the original commercial assumptions with its primary commercial rationale already dissolved.

**Actual Year 1-3 performance vs. projections:**
- 2020 (Year 1, partial): 9 million passengers total for Berlin airports, against 35.6 million in 2019. BER operated at roughly 26% of its design capacity.
- 2021: 9.9 million passengers.
- 2022: 19.8 million passengers (recovery year, +99.5% YoY).
- 2023: 23.1 million passengers — still 12.6 million fewer than the pre-pandemic Berlin market, and 31% below the 34 million that the terminal's commercial design was calibrated to.

The 2023 airline mix at BER tells the real story: Ryanair and easyJet together control roughly one-third of total seat capacity (4.8 million and 4.6 million seats respectively). The airport is primarily a low-cost carrier origin-and-destination facility — the opposite of the premium connecting hub that justified the retail revenue model. [Source: https://airserviceone.com/berlin-brandenburg-airport-navigates-return-to-growth-amid-industry-challenges/]

**The structural flaws:** BER's business case failed because: (a) the connecting hub model was contested before construction was complete — Munich and Frankfurt had structural advantages BER could not overcome; (b) the primary anchor carrier's financial distress was visible and was not incorporated into scenario planning; (c) the retail model assumed transfer passenger behavior patterns inconsistent with Berlin's actual traveler mix; and (d) the pandemic opened in the worst possible demand environment, eliminating the demand ramp-up period the debt structure needed. [Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/334842637_Forecast_to_grow_Aviation_demand_forecasting_in_an_era_of_demand_uncertainty_and_optimism_bias]

### Case Study 3: LaGuardia Airport Redevelopment

**The project and cost trajectory:** The LaGuardia redevelopment is the largest complete airport rebuild in US history. Terminal B (LaGuardia Gateway Partners P3): original estimate in 2014 was approximately $3.6 billion; by 2016, costs had escalated to $5.3 billion — a $1.7 billion increase before construction was well underway. The final Terminal B program came in at approximately $5.1 billion including $4 billion in direct construction costs. Terminal C (Delta-led): Delta committed $3.4 billion plus any cost overruns, with PANYNJ contributing $600 million, for a total of $4 billion in the Delta project. Total LGA redevelopment: approximately $8 billion, with some estimates reaching higher when all infrastructure is included. The Delta Terminal C project reportedly came in ahead of schedule at its $2.8 billion airfield phase. [Source: https://www.agcnys.org/articles/cost-to-overhaul-laguardia-airport-soars/]

**Delta's structural commitment:** Delta's use agreement structure for Terminal C is materially different from a speculative terminal developer's position. Delta agreed to absorb cost overruns beyond its committed contribution. This is a genuine risk transfer — Delta has skin in the cost performance, which is precisely why Delta's operational team was embedded in construction management. Delta's market dominance at LGA (roughly 50% of enplanements) and its inability to operate competitive New York service without a functional terminal gave it both the incentive and the leverage to make a real commitment. This is the structure that works: the carrier with the most to gain from the terminal is the one taking construction risk.

**The demand reality:** LGA handled 33.5 million passengers in 2024, approximately 8% above 2019 pre-pandemic levels, and ranked 19th in the US by passenger traffic. The redevelopment was not premised on demand growth; it was premised on replacing functionally obsolete infrastructure serving demonstrably real demand. The business case was fundamentally different from BER's: the market existed, the carrier was committed, and the question was execution quality, not whether passengers would show up. [Source: https://roadgenius.com/statistics/airports/usa/lagaurdia-lga-airport/]

**CPE trajectory post-redevelopment:** Specific LGA CPE figures for 2022-2024 are not published in PANYNJ's public rate schedules at the level of granularity available for FAA-reporting airports. LGA operates under a Port Authority rate-setting framework, and aeronautical revenues from LGA in 2024 totaled $330 million on 33.5 million passengers — implying an average aeronautical yield per enplaned passenger of approximately $9.85. This is lower than many comparable major airports and reflects the volume density of LGA's slot-controlled operation. Whether Delta's actual terminal-specific CPE is higher is not publicly disclosed, but the Delta commitment structure (where Delta pays overruns) creates the right incentives for cost discipline.

### Case Study 4: JFK New Terminal One

**The structure:** JFK's New Terminal One is a $9.5 billion demand-risk P3 developed by a consortium (Ferrovial, Carlyle, JLC Infrastructure, Ullico) known as NTO LLC. It replaces the existing Terminal 1 and former Terminals 2 and 3. The terminal will anchor JFK's south side. Phase A (23 gates) targets opening in 2026; full build-out of 2.6 million square feet by 2030 with capacity for approximately 23 million passengers at completion. [Source: https://www.portauthoritybuilds.com/redevelopment/us/en/jfk/planned-projects/terminal-1.html]

**Financing and demand risk:** The project closed at $9.5 billion with $2.33 billion in sponsor equity and $6.63 billion in bank financing, subsequently refinanced through three bond issuances totaling $5.917 billion. The 2024 issuance of $2.55 billion was the largest airport municipal bond ever sold in the US. All tranches received BBB-/Baa3 uninsured ratings. The critical structural feature: "NTO is a demand-risk P3 with debt secured solely by terminal revenues rather than by PANYNJ's general airport revenue pledge." There is no public backstop. If airlines route traffic elsewhere or global demand contracts, debt service coverage deteriorates before any covenant cure is available. Rating agencies specifically named "demand ramp-up" as a material credit risk. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/jfk-new-terminal-one-p3-financing]

**Airline commitments — the gap:** Fifteen carriers are described as tenants, including Air France, Lufthansa, Turkish Airlines, and Korean Air. These are primarily non-US carriers serving JFK on international routes — carriers for whom JFK is already their only US gateway and whose routing decisions are constrained by bilateral agreements and alliance structures. They are the most captive possible tenant pool: they have nowhere else to go at JFK and limited alternatives in the New York market. However, the NTO use agreements are airline-specific negotiated documents, not a single residual cost backstop. Airlines pay landing fees, terminal rents, and gate use fees — but there is no disclosed minimum activity level commitment. If Air France reduces JFK frequencies (as it has done on some transatlantic routes when fuel costs rise), NTO revenues decline and the fixed cost base remains.

**The traffic model:** NTO's debt structure is modeled to serve 14 million passengers at Phase A opening (2026) and 16 million by 2030. JFK's existing Terminal 1 handled approximately 8-9 million passengers annually before construction began. Nearly doubling traffic volumes to 16 million requires significant growth from the existing tenant pool or new carrier additions — growth that is not contractually guaranteed and depends on the international aviation market sustaining current trajectory. Any recession, geopolitical disruption, or carrier fleet re-routing directly stresses the debt structure with no public-sector buffer.

### Cross-Case Synthesis: Pittsburgh and Memphis as Historical Benchmarks

**Pittsburgh (PIT):** The 1992 terminal was purpose-built for US Airways to handle 32 million passengers annually. In the late 1990s, PIT peaked at 20 million — with 80% connecting traffic. When US Airways entered bankruptcy (twice, 2002-2004) and dehubbed PIT in December 2004, traffic fell from 20 million to 8 million. The airport was left managing facilities built for four times its actual traffic. County bonds worth $600 million, expected to be serviced by the airline's 30-year lease, had to be restructured using state gaming revenue and Marcellus shale mineral leases. The terminal itself — once called "airport of the future" by the New York Times — became the most visible symbol of what happens when a terminal is built to a single carrier's specifications and that carrier's network strategy changes. [Source: https://www.governing.com/archive/gov-pittsburgh-international-airport.html]

**Memphis (MEM):** Northwest Airlines built Memphis into a connecting hub from the mid-1980s. Delta acquired Northwest in 2008. By 2013, Delta had run through its post-merger network rationalization and found that Memphis was redundant to Atlanta — same Southeast origin base, lower-yield connecting traffic, and competing for the same bank of connections. Delta announced the dehub in June 2013 and executed by September. Passenger traffic fell from 11.3 million (2007 peak) to 3.5 million by 2015 — a 69% decline in eight years. MEM's CPE was not the driver; network redundancy was. The airport had made infrastructure investments calibrated to the hub traffic base, none of which could be recovered when that traffic base disappeared overnight. [Source: https://simpleflying.com/the-rise-and-fall-of-delta-air-lines-memphis-hub/]

**The pattern across all five dehubbed airports (CVG, PIT, STL, MEM, CLE):** Every one of them built or expanded infrastructure in the 5-10 years before the dehubbing, calibrated to the hub carrier's traffic projections. Every one of them had their primary carrier signal financial distress before the formal dehub announcement. Every one of them saw their CPE spike after the dehubbing because fixed costs could not be reduced as fast as traffic fell. The airport in each case had invested in the carrier's growth and then been left holding infrastructure the carrier no longer needed.

## Warning Signs Visible 5–7 Years Before Ribbon-Cutting

A commercial strategist watching a mega-terminal program from the airline side looks for the following signals, in order of severity:

**Signal 1 — Anchor carrier financial distress.** If the primary tenant's balance sheet is deteriorating while the terminal is under construction, the business case has a load-bearing assumption that is actively weakening. Air Berlin's losses were public from 2015. Any analyst with access to annual reports could have identified, three years before BER opened, that the connecting hub model was in trouble. The question a capital planner should ask every year of a 5-7 year construction cycle: "Is the anchor carrier's financial position improving or deteriorating, and what does that imply for its commitment to this facility?"

**Signal 2 — Carrier route frequency data diverging from projections.** OAG and Cirium provide scheduled seat capacity data at the city-pair level. If a terminal's business case projects 20% growth in a carrier's frequencies and the carrier's actual schedule filings are flat or declining, the demand assumption is not being validated. This signal is available continuously during construction and is almost never formally incorporated into business case updates.

**Signal 3 — Fleet plan changes at the anchor carrier.** Fleet orders (disclosed in 10-K filings and carrier investor days) tell you what growth is actually funded. If the terminal assumes wide-body growth and the carrier's fleet plan shows narrow-body substitution or fleet retirement without replacement, the passenger projection will miss. Delta's 2024 Investor Day presentation shows the fleet plan discipline that determines where growth actually goes — and it goes to the lowest-CPE hubs first.

**Signal 4 — Alliance or joint venture restructuring.** International terminal business cases are often predicated on connecting traffic from alliance partners. If the anchor carrier's international joint venture partners are reducing capacity, or if a merger creates a redundant transatlantic gateway, the connecting traffic model is broken. BER's connecting hub assumption depended on Oneworld connectivity that Air Berlin's collapse terminated. JFK's NTO depends on SkyTeam carriers (Air France, Korean, Delta connecting traffic) maintaining JFK as their US gateway — a position that is stable but not guaranteed.

**Signal 5 — Use agreement structure that provides no binding traffic floor.** If the terminal's primary tenant has not signed a use agreement with minimum activity levels or minimum revenue guarantees, the "commitment" described in press releases is not a commitment — it is a statement of intent. The standard test: can the airport sue the carrier for breach if the carrier cuts frequency? If the answer is no, the carrier's public support for the terminal is commercial courtesy, not a binding obligation.

**Signal 6 — CPE trajectory of competing airports in the carrier's network.** If the hub carrier's CPE at the subject airport is rising relative to alternatives in its network, marginal growth will be directed elsewhere. This is not a decision that gets announced. It shows up in fleet assignments, in frequency filings, and eventually in ASM data. By the time it appears in enplanement statistics, the pattern has been established for 12-24 months.

**Signal 7 — Connecting traffic as a percentage of total traffic in the business case.** Any terminal business case that relies heavily on connecting passengers is exposed to network rationalization risk in a way that O&D-driven cases are not. Connecting traffic disappears when a carrier decides a hub is redundant — it does not fade gradually. CVG, PIT, STL, MEM, and CLE all lost connecting traffic in step-function declines when the hub carrier made a network decision. The airport had no influence over that decision.

## Analytical Conclusion

The airport builds. The airline decides whether to fly.

That asymmetry is the central commercial reality that every mega-terminal program must confront and almost none formally models. The physical infrastructure is a fixed cost once committed. The airline network decision is a variable that changes with every planning cycle, fuel price, merger, recession, and fleet order.

The cases examined here demonstrate three distinct failure modes:

- **Demand assumption failure (BER):** The business case was built on an airline strategy (Air Berlin's connecting hub) and a passenger mix (premium transfer traffic) that were both wrong before the terminal opened.

- **Operational launch failure with sound commercial case (T5):** The demand was real, the carrier was committed, and only the systems integration failed. This is the recoverable failure — BA absorbed $50 million, fired two executives, and T5 went on to win five consecutive global terminal awards.

- **Carrier strategy change during construction (PIT, MEM, CVG):** The business case was sound at inception; the carrier's network strategy changed during or after construction. The airport had no contractual protection and no mechanism to force a business case revision.

The most important question in any terminal capital program is not "what will we build?" It is "what happens to this business case if the anchor carrier's strategy changes?" If the answer is "we don't have a scenario for that," the program is incomplete.
