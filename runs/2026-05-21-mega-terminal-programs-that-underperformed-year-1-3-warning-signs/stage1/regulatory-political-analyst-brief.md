# Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief
## Mega-Terminal Failures: How Governance Conditions Are Built, Not Discovered

**Stage 1 — Council Run: Mega-Terminal Programs That Underperformed in Years 1–3**
**Analyst Role: Regulatory and Political Forces**
**Date: May 20, 2026**

---

## Key Findings

1. **The PFC cap is the original sin of U.S. airport terminal finance.** Frozen at $4.50 since 2000 — worth roughly $2.72 in 2018 dollars by purchasing-power erosion — it has forced airports into P3 structures, special-facilities revenue bonds, and developer-consortium arrangements that fragment decision rights and make course correction expensive. The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (H.R. 3935) left the cap intact. This is not a regulatory problem; it is a political choice that will remain fixed because airlines lobby effectively against increases and Congress treats any raise as a passenger tax.

2. **BER is not an anomaly. It is a precise demonstration of what happens when political actors own a technical project.** Berlin-Brandenburg Airport opened 9 years late at more than double the original €2.8 billion budget, ultimately exceeding €6.5 billion. The root cause was not the defective fire-safety system — it was the governance structure that permitted an unqualified engineering draughtsman to design the smoke-extraction system and then shielded that fact from regulators for years. The supervisory board was populated with state government politicians, not engineers. When Berlin Mayor Michael Müller became supervisory board head in 2015, he publicly insisted on a 2017 opening that the technical reality could not support. Politicians set schedules; engineers inherit the consequences.

3. **NEPA is a scope-lock mechanism, not merely a delay mechanism.** Once an EIS is complete, changing scope requires either a new environmental study or a finding of categorical exclusion. This creates a structural incentive to maximize scope in the initial EIS — you can always build less than approved, but you cannot easily build more. Major runway projects take an average of fifteen or more years from initiation of environmental review to opening, per a pattern documented across multiple GAO reports (1996, 2003, 2014). The political implication: scope decisions embedded in an EIS carry the same durability as statutory language. Changing them requires mobilizing the same political energy as changing a law.

4. **TSA's checkpoint design authority is an underappreciated mid-construction risk.** TSA must approve checkpoint layouts under the Checkpoint Requirements and Planning Guide and has changed design standards multiple times — CT scanners, automated screening lanes, credential authentication technology — while terminals were in construction or final design. Full-size CT systems require automated bin return, parallel divestiture stations, and high-threat containment, consuming significantly more square footage than 2D X-ray configurations. Airports that locked in checkpoint footprints before TSA's CT rollout discovered the mismatch at delivery. Congress funds TSA staffing below the level airports design for; the staffing gap is not a technical problem airports can solve with capital.

5. **CBP staffing is a federal appropriations ceiling, not an operational variable airports control.** Major international airports including JFK, LAX, and Houston IAH have documented CBP processing times two to three times normal when officers are redeployed or positions are unfilled. The New Terminal One at JFK ($9.5 billion, 23 gates, 2.6 million square feet) has designed its entire arrivals hall around biometric processing partnerships with CBP to reduce the required number of traditional inspection booths — a direct acknowledgment that CBP staffing cannot be assumed at design capacity. Any international terminal business case that models revenue based on CBP-at-design-capacity is wrong about the constraint it is modeling.

6. **P3 structures are a political financing vehicle, not a governance solution.** LGA Terminal B and JFK T1 were both structured as P3s in significant part because public debt capacity was constrained and political actors wanted the projects on their watch without the balance-sheet exposure. The result at LGA: LaGuardia Gateway Partners (Vantage Airport Group, Skanska, Meridiam) received a 35-year lease, with cost-overrun and schedule risk notionally transferred to the consortium. But risk transfer in a P3 concession does not transfer decision rights — when the Port Authority and the Governor's office want project scope expanded or a ribbon-cutting advanced, the contractual risk allocation does not change the political pressure. At JFK T1, the concession structure means phase decisions (Phase B, Phase C) are contingent on traffic thresholds and require additional financing — giving the sponsor consortium effective veto over phasing even when public demand exceeds design assumptions.

7. **The DCA perimeter rule and LGA slot cap are your best available proof of how political decisions calcify.** The perimeter rule at Reagan National originated in 1966 as a noise-management tool; it has since been used by a rotating coalition of regional airline interests, Virginia/Maryland congressional delegations, and mid-continent city advocates to block competition, preserve market share, and extract political concessions. The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 authorized 10 additional beyond-perimeter slot exemptions at DCA — a marginal concession after 60 years of the same argument. At LGA, the slot cap (currently 71 scheduled operations per hour) was directly caused by the AIR 21 reform in 2000, when slot exemptions so overwhelmed the airport that the FAA had to immediately reimpose limits via regulation. Once embedded, neither rule has been removed despite consistent consensus among economists that both are inefficient. The lesson is not that these are unusual — it is that they are typical.

8. **Optimism bias in the business case is not merely psychological. It is structural and rational for the actors presenting it.** Flyvbjerg's research across 16,000-plus infrastructure projects documents that transport infrastructure cost overruns are not random errors — they are systematic and skewed in one direction. Political actors proposing megaprojects have rational incentives to understate costs and overstate benefits, because projects with accurate estimates do not get approved. The BER supervisory board received repeated budget estimates that were politically motivated rather than technically grounded. This is not incompetence; it is the predictable output of a governance structure where approval authority sits with politicians and technical accountability sits with contractors.

## Evidence Section

### FAA Regulatory Forces

Part 139 (14 C.F.R. Part 139) requires airports serving scheduled air carrier operations with aircraft over 9 seats to maintain an FAA-issued Airport Operating Certificate. Certification requirements cover aircraft rescue and firefighting, safety management systems, self-inspection programs, and emergency plans. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/airport_safety/part139_cert] The FAA's Safety Management Systems rule, finalized in February 2023, added a requirement for airports to identify and manage potential hazards systematically — a rule that increases the documentation burden but does not change the political incentives that cause hazards to be under-reported to begin with.

The Airport Improvement Program provides more than $3.18 billion annually in entitlement and discretionary grants. AIP grant conditions restrict eligible uses: capital construction and equipment purchase qualify; operational integration testing, software commissioning, and ORAT programs generally do not receive AIP funding. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/grantapportion_data] The IIJA (Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act) added $5 billion for terminal development ($1 billion annually, FY2022–FY2026) in competitive grants — but the same eligibility bias applies. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/iija/airport-terminals] This is regulation with political origins: Congress defined eligible uses through statute, not through FAA rulemaking, and Congress has not extended eligibility to operational readiness programs.

The GAO's 2020 airport infrastructure report (GAO-20-298) documented that planned infrastructure projects were expected to cost an average of $22 billion annually for FY2019–FY2023, a 19 percent increase over the prior estimate period. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-20-298] ACI-NA's 2025 Infrastructure Needs Study identifies $151 billion in total projects requiring financing over 2023–2028, against combined annual public funding of roughly $12 billion. [Source: https://airportscouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/2025-Infrastructure-Needs-Study.pdf] The gap is not a surprise; it is the predictable consequence of a PFC cap that has lost half its real value.

**NEPA and scope lock.** The FAA's environmental review process requires completion of a NEPA document (EA or EIS) before project construction begins. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/environmental/nepa] The GAO's 2010 report (GAO-10-50) found that airports that incorporated environmental considerations early in planning were better able to navigate the NEPA process — but also that "an airport's concept of delay may include underestimating the time needed to comply with the NEPA process as well as unanticipated delay, such as delays resulting from litigation." [Source: https://www.gao.gov/assets/a309629.html] Major runway construction projects take 15-plus years from environmental study initiation to opening — a timeline that is primarily driven by NEPA and community opposition, not construction complexity. The statutory effect is scope lock: once an EIS establishes a project's footprint and operational parameters, deviating upward requires a supplemental EIS or a new record of decision. Deviating downward does not. This asymmetry is politically rational, technically perverse, and rarely discussed honestly in project planning documents.

### TSA as a Hidden Terminal-Design Regulator

TSA's Checkpoint Requirements and Planning Guide governs checkpoint design and must be followed by airports building or renovating security checkpoints. [Source: https://www.tsa.gov/sites/default/files/checkpoint-requirements-and-planning-guide.pdf] Full-size CT systems, which TSA has been deploying since 2018, require automated bin return, parallel divestiture stations, and high-threat containment — a materially larger footprint than earlier 2D X-ray configurations. TSA awarded $781.2 million in CT procurement contracts in March 2022 and up to $1.3 billion more in April 2023, deploying 678 units to 218 airports nationwide as of that date. [Source: https://www.tsa.gov/news/press/releases/2023/04/12/tsa-awards-13-billion-procure-additional-ct-x-ray-scanners-airport] Airports that completed checkpoint design before 2018 and opened terminals after 2020 faced a design-standard mismatch that was not architectural failure — it was the predictable result of a regulatory agency changing standards mid-construction cycle. TSA has design approval authority; airports do not have approval authority over TSA's design-standard changes.

TSA staffing is a congressional appropriations matter. When Congress does not fund authorized positions, airports open with checkpoints designed for staffing levels that will not be achieved. This is the defining structural tension in terminal passenger-experience design: airports design for the TSA that should exist; they operate with the TSA that Congress funds.

### CBP as a Binding Constraint on International Terminal Capacity

CBP's Federal Inspection Services requirements specify minimum inspection booth counts, primary and secondary processing space, hold rooms, and agricultural inspection areas. These requirements drive international gate design — particularly the ratio of gate count to FIS processing throughput. [Source: https://www.cbp.gov/about/contact/ports/john-f-kennedy-international-airport-new-york-4701] CBP's own staffing model (the Workload Staffing Model) is used to determine staffing allocation, but actual staffing levels are constrained by annual appropriations. [Source: https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GAOREPORTS-GAO-05-663/html/GAOREPORTS-GAO-05-663.htm]

At JFK's New Terminal One, the design team worked directly with CBP to build an arrivals hall premised on biometric processing through CBP's Enhanced Passenger Processing program, explicitly allowing "fewer traditional inspection booths" and using space "more efficiently." [Source: https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/the-new-terminal-one-at-jfk-partners-with-cbp-to-transform-the-international-arrivals-experience-for-us-citizens-302685249.html] This is a rational design adaptation — and also an acknowledgment that traditional FIS staffing cannot be assumed. The risk is that EPP depends on CBP deploying and maintaining biometric infrastructure, which is itself subject to appropriations and program continuity. When CBP officers are redeployed to other functions, major international airports like JFK, LAX, and Houston IAH document processing times two to three times normal. [Source: https://www.lawofficeimmigration.com/blog/tsa-delays-immigration-travel-2026.html] No amount of gate capital solves a staffing appropriation problem.

### The PFC Cap: A Political Choice, Not a Regulatory Finding

The $4.50 PFC cap was established in 2000 under AIR-21 as a compromise between airport interests seeking $6 per segment and airline interests preferring no increase. It has not moved since. By 2018, the purchasing power of $4.50 had declined to $2.72 in 2018 dollars — a 40 percent real reduction. [Source: https://airportscouncil.org/news/independent-research-highlights-the-need-to-increase-the-pfc/] The FAA collected an estimated $3.7 billion in PFCs in 2024 against an annual airport infrastructure need that ACI-NA places at $30 billion-plus. [Source: https://aviationweek.com/air-transport/airports-networks/us-airports-face-significant-infrastructure-funding-shortfall-says]

The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (H.R. 3935), signed May 16, 2024, raised AIP authorization to $4 billion annually beginning FY2025 and streamlined the PFC application process. It did not raise the PFC cap. [Source: https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/house-bill/3935/text] This outcome is not surprising. Airlines for America has consistently characterized any PFC increase as a passenger tax and lobbied effectively against it through six consecutive reauthorization cycles. [Source: https://thehill.com/business-a-lobbying/2573-airlines-airports-take-fight-over-airport-construction-fees-to-hill/] The GAO's 2015 analysis (GAO-15-107) found that raising the PFC cap to $8.50 would reduce Airport and Airway Trust Fund revenues by roughly 1 percent — a finding airlines cite as economic harm and airports characterize as rounding error. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-15-107] Both descriptions are accurate. Neither has moved the political result.

The consequence for terminal finance is structural. Without a higher PFC, airports reaching their general airport revenue bond capacity are forced into special-facilities revenue bonds (SFRBs), private activity bonds, or P3 concession structures. SFRBs, as used at JFK T1, are obligations of a conduit issuer secured solely by terminal revenues — not by the airport's system-wide pledge. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/jfk-new-terminal-one-p3-financing] This shifts credit risk to a single terminal's revenue stream, increases debt service cost, and gives the developer consortium effective control over phasing decisions because future phases require additional financing the sponsor must arrange.

### DCA Perimeter Rule and LGA Slot Cap: The Canonical Cases

The perimeter rule at Reagan National Airport (DCA) limits nonstop service to within 1,250 statute miles of DCA, with statutory exemptions granted in 2000, 2003, 2012, and 2024. [Source: https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IN12504] The 2024 FAA Reauthorization authorized 10 additional beyond-perimeter exemptions — the first expansion since 2012 and the smallest increment in the rule's history. The rule has been in place since 1966, has survived every substantive economic argument made against it, and will not be removed in any foreseeable political environment because the coalition defending it (Virginia and Maryland congressional delegations protecting regional airport usage, airline competitors protecting incumbent slot positions, and community groups concerned about noise and traffic) is more durable than any coalition that has tried to remove it. [Source: https://enotrans.org/article/a-history-of-the-perimeter-rule-at-washington-reagan-national-airport/]

LGA's slot cap (currently 71 scheduled operations per hour) is a direct product of the 2000 AIR-21 legislation, which granted slot exemptions that nearly overwhelmed the airport — forcing the FAA to rapidly re-impose limits. [Source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2020/09/18/2020-18400/operating-limitations-at-new-york-laguardia-airport] The slot cap directly constrains the revenue case for terminal investment at LGA: you cannot grow flights without slots, you cannot get more slots without either a rule change or an exemption, and exemptions require either FAA rulemaking or a statutory act. The terminal investment at LGA was sized against a slot-constrained demand ceiling that the business case treated as a fixed constraint — which it is.

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: BER — Regulatory Approval as the Canary

Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER) opened October 31, 2020 — nine years after its scheduled opening of June 3, 2012 — at a cost exceeding €6.5 billion against an original budget of €2.8 billion. [Source: https://www.euronews.com/2020/10/31/berlin-airport-opens-10-years-late-and-three-times-over-budget]

The proximate cause of the May 8, 2012 postponement — announced 26 days before the scheduled opening — was the failure of the fire protection and smoke-extraction system to pass mandatory acceptance testing by the local Dahme-Spreewald building supervision authority. [Source: https://www.newcivilengineer.com/archive/berlin-airport-opening-delayed-after-failing-fire-regulations-24-05-2012/] The technical failure was real: the smoke-extraction system was designed to pump smoke from the terminal ceiling downward through a basement shaft — reversing the natural behavior of hot air — at a scale and complexity that had never been accomplished anywhere. At the dimensions of the BER terminal, the system had not worked as designed. Inspectors found that in an actual fire, the main smoke vent might implode rather than exhaust.

But the deeper failure was governance, not physics. Alfredo di Mauro, who designed the fire-safety system, was not a licensed engineer — he was an engineering draughtsman. His unqualified status was not discovered until 2014, two years after the system failed testing. [Source: https://www.thelocal.de/20140625/fake-engineer-di-mauro-designed-berlin-airport-fire-system] The supervisory board that should have required professional verification of designer qualifications was composed of politicians: the states of Berlin and Brandenburg and the Federal Republic of Germany are the three shareholders of Flughafen Berlin Brandenburg GmbH, and their political appointees dominated the supervisory board. [Source: https://corporate.berlin-airport.de/en/company-media/fbb-and-subsidiaries/aufsichtsrat.html]

When Berlin Mayor Michael Müller became supervisory board chairman in July 2015, he publicly insisted on a 2017 opening — a date that management could not technically defend but was politically pressured to accept. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport] The airport had 14 postponements over nine years. Each postponement reset the regulatory approval clock; each postponement was preceded by a period of political pressure to announce a new date. The regulatory authority (the building supervision department) did not fail. It performed exactly as designed — it rejected a system that did not meet code. What failed was the governance structure that permitted an unqualified designer to produce the system, kept that fact hidden from the supervisory board, and then allowed political actors to substitute schedule optimism for technical readiness.

The lesson for mega-terminal programs is not "follow fire codes." It is: regulatory approval gates are the last line of defense against governance failures that have been accumulating for years. When a regulatory authority rejects a system 26 days before opening, that is not a regulatory failure — it is a governance failure that took years to build and took one regulatory action to make visible.

### Case Study 2: LGA Terminal B — P3 Structure as Political Product

Governor Andrew Cuomo announced the LaGuardia Terminal B redevelopment on July 27, 2015, joined by then-Vice President Joe Biden. The project was structured as a 35-year P3 concession with LaGuardia Gateway Partners (a consortium including Vantage Airport Group, Skanska, and Meridiam Infrastructure) as the largest transportation P3 in U.S. history at that time. Total program cost: $5.1 billion for the terminal, with the broader LaGuardia redevelopment priced at $8 billion. [Source: https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-cuomo-unveils-new-arrivals-and-departures-hall-terminal-b-part-8-billion-transformation]

The P3 structure was chosen for political and fiscal reasons more than operational ones. The Port Authority had limited room for additional general airport revenue bonds, and the Governor's office needed the project to appear to happen on a 4-year horizon. Structuring it as a demand-risk P3 with cost-overrun risk transferred to the consortium served both interests: it moved the debt off the Port Authority's balance sheet and let the Governor announce a transformative project without full public financing.

The governance consequence is what matters here. When you transfer cost-overrun risk to a private consortium, you also transfer certain decision rights. LaGuardia Gateway Partners held the design, build, operate, and maintain scope. The Port Authority retained oversight, but the practical ability to require design changes, alter scope mid-execution, or accelerate or defer completion is constrained by the concession terms and the consortium's own financing obligations. If the concession agreement is breached, the remedy is legal — which is not the same as operational. When systems integration challenges or phasing problems emerged during construction, the question of "who owns the decision" was answered by the concession agreement, not by the airport's operational needs.

In contrast, Heathrow Terminal 5 was delivered under a fundamentally different philosophy. BAA (the airport owner) retained all risk — paying suppliers their actual cost plus an agreed fixed fee, with no risk transferred to contractors. [Source: https://infrastructuredeliverymodels.gihub.org/case-studies/heathrow-terminal-5/] The integrated project team had aligned incentives because BAA bore the consequences of failure. T5 was delivered on time and within budget during construction. The opening-day baggage system failure — 42,000 bags misplaced in the first days — was an operations failure, not a construction failure, and was attributable to undertrained staff and inadequate operational readiness testing, not to the contract structure. [Source: https://www.mightytravels.com/2024/06/heathrow-terminal-5-revisited-lessons-learned-from-an-operational-nightmare/] The distinction matters: T5's governance structure allowed BAA to identify and correct construction problems because accountability was clear. The ORAT failure happened in the phase where operations transferred to British Airways — a third party — and integration testing was compressed.

The P3 lesson is not that P3s fail. It is that P3 structures chosen for political and financing reasons, rather than operational reasons, embed accountability ambiguities that make course correction expensive. At LGA, the project was ultimately delivered and won Skytrax's Best New Airport Terminal in 2023. But the project's political origins — a governor's office that needed a ribbon-cutting, a Port Authority that needed off-balance-sheet financing, a 35-year horizon that neither party will live to see through — shaped every governance decision downstream.

### Case Study 3: The PFC Cap — A Frozen Number Shapes Every Terminal Financing Structure

The $4.50 PFC cap is not a regulatory requirement. It is a statute. Congress set it in 2000; Congress can change it; Congress will not change it.

The gap between what the PFC generates and what airport terminals cost is now structural. ACI-NA estimates $151 billion in project financing needed over 2023–2028, against annual combined federal and PFC revenue of roughly $12 billion — leaving approximately $18 billion per year of unfunded need. [Source: https://airportscouncil.org/news/independent-research-highlights-the-need-to-increase-the-pfc/] To fill that gap, airports use revenue bonds, special-facilities bonds, private activity bonds, and P3 concessions. Each of these structures introduces governance complexity.

Special-facilities revenue bonds (used for both JFK T1 and LGA Terminal B components) are secured solely by the revenues of the specific facility — not by the airport's broader revenue pledge. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/jfk-new-terminal-one-p3-financing] This means the bond covenant directly constrains how the terminal is operated: if revenues fall below the coverage ratio, the covenant restricts the operator's freedom of action. A terminal that opens with operational problems in Year 1 — reduced enplanements, lower concession revenue, airline disputes over rates and charges — is a terminal whose bond covenant is under stress. The rating agencies are watching. The airport cannot easily change concession structure or operational model without affecting the coverage ratio that secured the bonds.

This is what "political decisions with multi-decade half-lives" means in practice. The PFC cap, frozen in 2000, forced LGA and JFK into financing structures that constrain operational flexibility in 2026 and beyond. Changing the PFC cap would require a coalition capable of defeating airlines' consistent and well-funded opposition — the same coalition that has failed to materialize across six reauthorization cycles, including the 2024 act. That coalition does not exist on any project timeline.

## What Can and Cannot Be Moved, and on What Timeframe

**Cannot be moved on any project timeline (5–15 years):**
- The PFC cap. Six reauthorization cycles, same result. Airline opposition is organized, funded, and consistent. The 2024 FAA Reauthorization is the latest confirmation.
- NEPA scope-lock. Once an EIS is filed and a record of decision is issued, the scope is legally anchored. Supplemental EIS timelines are measured in years, not months.
- DCA perimeter rule and LGA slot cap. Sixty years of the same political fight, marginal adjustments. These rules do not move because the coalition defending them is more stable than the coalition opposing them.
- CBP staffing as a ceiling on international capacity. Appropriations-driven, not capital-driven. No amount of terminal investment solves it.

**Can be influenced, but not on project timelines (3–7 years with organized effort):**
- TSA checkpoint design standards. These do change, but on TSA's deployment schedule, not airports' construction schedules. Airports can work with TSA during design to anticipate standards, but cannot require TSA to freeze requirements.
- AIP grant eligibility. Congress could expand eligible uses to include ORAT and integration testing. This would require a statutory change and consistent advocacy over at least two reauthorization cycles.
- P3 governance structures. The terms of a concession agreement are negotiated before financial close and are extremely difficult to alter after. The window for establishing clear decision rights, scope-change authority, and course-correction mechanisms is pre-close.

**The strategist's working assumption:** Any mega-terminal program that builds its business case on assumptions about CBP staffing, PFC revenue growth, TSA design stability, or political actors staying aligned on scope will be wrong about at least two of those four variables. The programs that have survived — Heathrow T5 construction, LGA Terminal B physical delivery — survived because someone, somewhere, held accountability with enough authority and alignment to resist the political pressure to substitute optimism for evidence. That condition is the exception, not the design.
