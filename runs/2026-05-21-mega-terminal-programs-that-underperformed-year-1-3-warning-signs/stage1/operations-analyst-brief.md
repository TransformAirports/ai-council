# Operations Analyst Brief: Mega-Terminal Failures as Governance Events
## Where the Operational Evidence Points — and What It Tells Capital Planners

**Prepared for:** Transform Airports AI Council — Stage 1 Research  
**Analyst lens:** Airport operations, throughput modeling, delay attribution  
**Date:** May 2026  
**Thesis under examination:** Mega-terminal failures are not black swans. They are slow-motion governance failures with leading indicators visible 5–7 years before ribbon-cutting.

---

## Key Findings

**1. The T5 baggage failure was not a technology failure — it was a compressed testing window that left no time to find what the system didn't know.**
Over the first 10 days after Heathrow Terminal 5 opened on 27 March 2008, more than 42,000 bags failed to travel with their owners, and more than 500 flights were cancelled. British Airways' own post-mortem attributed this directly to testing deferrals: construction delays by BAA meant BA's IT staff could not begin testing until 31 October 2007, just five months before opening. Several trials were cancelled outright, others were reduced in scope because testing staff could not access the full Terminal 5 site. The UK Transport Select Committee concluded the failure stemmed from "testing late and testing less" — not from a fundamentally flawed baggage system. [Source: UK Parliament Transport Select Committee Report, "The Opening of Heathrow Terminal 5," 2008; New Civil Engineer, May 2008]

**2. Berlin Brandenburg's 9-year delay was not an engineering problem — it was a governance vacuum with no one authorized to stop scope changes.**
BER opened 31 October 2020, 14 years after construction started, at a final cost of approximately €7–9.4 billion against an original budget of €2.83 billion — a 250–330% overrun. The proximate cause was a fire safety system that "was not built according to the construction permit and failed the mandatory acceptance test." The structural cause was unchecked scope change: CEO Rainer Schwarz added north and south piers mid-construction, converting the terminal from a rectangle to a U-shape, then inserted a second mezzanine level of retail. The system's designer was later revealed to be a draughtsman, not a licensed engineer. When the fire system first failed its acceptance test in May 2012, inspectors catalogued more than 120,000 defects. The supervisory board — composed largely of politicians — lacked the technical mandate to halt scope changes. [Source: Wikipedia, "Construction of Berlin Brandenburg Airport"; Interesting Engineering; CIO Magazine analysis; New Civil Engineer, May 2012]

**3. Rail passenger demand forecasts overestimate actual demand by 51.4% on average — and airport terminals show the same pattern.**
Flyvbjerg, Holm, and Buhl (2006) analyzed 210 transport infrastructure projects across 14 nations worth US$58 billion and found that actual rail passenger traffic is, on average, 51.4% lower than forecast — equivalent to a 105.6% overestimate. For roads, 50% of projects are wrong on demand by more than 20% in either direction. Nine out of ten megaprojects experience cost overruns; benefit shortfalls of 50% or more are "common, and above 50% not uncommon." BER's design capacity of 27–46 million passengers annually met a first-year (COVID-affected) actual volume of 700,000 passengers in its first 100 days — roughly 10% of 2019 pre-crisis Berlin volumes from the predecessor airports combined. Even adjusting for COVID, the business case demand assumptions were never stress-tested against a phased ramp scenario. [Source: Flyvbjerg, Holm, Buhl, "Inaccuracy in Traffic Forecasts," Transport Reviews, 2006; Flyvbjerg, "The Iron Law of Megaprojects," 2014; Berlin Airport corporate press releases, February 2021]

**4. At LaGuardia Terminal B, the airside geometry change — not the building itself — drove the measurable performance gain.**
The $4 billion Terminal B redevelopment (complete July 2022) recovered 40 acres of airside land and added approximately two miles of usable taxiway, allowing two independent paths to each of 35 gates. Airport-wide flight delays fell from approximately 26.6% four years prior to approximately 20.75% by 2022. Terminal B received the 2023 Skytrax World's Best New Airport Terminal award and the ACI 2023 Airport Service Quality Award for its passenger size category. However, the parallel AirTrain project — originally budgeted at $450 million — was cancelled in March 2023 after estimated costs reached $2.4 billion, five times the initial projection. The airside capacity gain was real; the landside access assumption in the original business case was not. [Source: HOK project documentation; Port Authority of New York and New Jersey; AMNY, March 2023; Skift Airline Weekly, March 2023]

**5. JFK's New Terminal One has reproduced the BER early-warning pattern before it has opened a single gate.**
The first phase of the $9.5 billion New Terminal One, originally scheduled to open summer 2026, was delayed to "no earlier than November 2026" as of May 2026. Moody's Ratings revised the project's senior secured outlook to negative, citing the postponement and "a downward revision in forecasted passenger demand." JLL was engaged for ORAT (Operational Readiness and Airport Transfer) work to begin "next year" from a 2025 announcement — meaning integrated systems testing was still being scoped mid-construction. JFK historically runs a fragmented, per-airline terminal model inherited from a pre-jet era decision that each carrier would build its own facility; peak-hour gate waiting affects 30% or more of arriving aircraft during 3pm–midnight windows. [Source: Bloomberg, May 15, 2026; Moody's/Finexus, May 2026; FAA/ASPM gate-waiting research, George Mason University; Facilities Dive, JLL announcement]

**6. Weather causes 74% of NAS delays over 15 minutes — but the remaining 26% (volume, equipment, runway) is where terminal design decisions show up.**
FAA OPSNET data covering June 2017 through May 2023 attributes 74.26% of system-impacting delays over 15 minutes to weather, 14.92% to volume, 5.96% to runway unavailability, 0.61% to equipment failure, and 4.25% to other factors. Infrastructure and operational coordination account for the entire non-weather portion. Gate unavailability — a direct output of terminal design and turn management — is a primary driver in the volume and runway categories at constrained airports. At airports undergoing active terminal transition, this 26% share becomes the leverage point: the same runway and airspace, different ground geometry and operational protocols, produces meaningfully different delay outcomes. [Source: FAA ASPM/OPSNET delay cause data, 2017–2023, per FAA Weather FAQ]

**7. Construction-while-operating is solvable — but only when the scope is fixed and testing begins before the terminal is declared "built."**
Istanbul Airport's ORAT process ran for more than two years before the October 2018 opening, including participation from Copenhagen and Incheon airport experts and multiple full-scale live trials. The 42km baggage handling system processed up to 28,880 bags per hour; during the first summer peak (2019), system availability reached 99.9%, and average in-system baggage time was approximately 8 minutes against a design specification of 15 minutes — a 47% improvement on specification. Denver's 1995 baggage disaster — where a $193 million automated system grew past $400 million and delayed the airport opening 16 months — was ultimately resolved by abandoning the technology and installing a conventional manual system, at an additional cost of $75 million. The lesson Denver taught, and Istanbul applied, is that integration testing must run long enough to surface what the system does not know yet. [Source: Future Travel Experience, October 2018; Airport Technology, Istanbul baggage system; GAO Report RCED-95-35BR, Denver; Medium/Ketan Keshav analysis of Denver]

**8. The "construction complete" declaration is not the same as "operationally ready" — and the gap between them is where disasters happen.**
IATA's ORAT guidance states that between three months and one year must be added after construction completion to reach operational readiness. At Heathrow T5, the gap was eliminated by construction delays: BA deferred its on-site familiarization program for passenger service and ramp employees by six weeks; trials were cancelled because staff could not access the site. At BER, the gap was occupied by fire safety system reconstruction — the airport received its operational licence only in May 2020 and opened five months later, after a 2019 ORAT approval for the smoke extraction system. The warning sign in every failure case is the same: the ORAT start date gets compressed to protect the construction completion date, rather than the other way around. [Source: IATA ORAT Best Practices document, October 2022; UK Transport Select Committee 2008; Berlin Airport press release, April 2019]

## Evidence Section

### Heathrow Terminal 5 — The Compressed Testing Window

Terminal 5 opened 27 March 2008 after a £4.3 billion construction program lasting six years. The UK Transport Select Committee investigated the opening and concluded that BAA's construction delays prevented BA from beginning integrated systems testing until 31 October 2007 — less than five months before opening. The planned sequence of proving trials was "changed significantly (either reduced in scope or cancelled altogether) because of the inability to access the whole of the T5 site." BA also deferred its on-site familiarization program for passenger service and ramp employees by six weeks.

The operational consequence: on Day 1, baggage check-in was suspended and 34 flights were cancelled. Over the first 10 days, more than 42,000 bags failed to travel with their owners, and more than 500 flights were cancelled over the following weeks. British Airways could not operate its full schedule from Terminal 5 until 8 April 2008 — 12 days after opening. The long-haul transfer from Terminal 4 was further delayed, and BA's director of operations and director of customer services were both terminated.

The technical root cause, per a post-incident IT analysis: a message filter left in place from the testing phase prevented the Terminal 5 baggage system from receiving information about bags transferring from other airlines. The filter had been inserted specifically to prevent test messages from contaminating live systems — but was never removed for go-live. This is a category of failure that only surfaces in integrated, end-to-end testing under live conditions. Compressed testing found the baggage conveyor; it did not find the message routing configuration.

[Source: UK Parliament Transport Select Committee, "The Opening of Heathrow Terminal 5," HC 543, Session 2007-08; New Civil Engineer, "Construction delays blamed for Heathrow T5 fiasco," May 2008; Medium/@pizzaere, "How a Bug Crippled Heathrow"; NPR, March 31, 2008]

### Berlin Brandenburg Airport — The Scope Change That Became a Fire Safety Crisis

BER's timeline: planning began in 1996, construction in September 2006, with an original opening date of October 2011. Actual opening: 31 October 2020. Original budget: €2.83 billion. Final cost: approximately €7–9.4 billion depending on accounting basis for overhead and financing.

The proximate cause of the 9-year delay was fire safety. The terminal's smoke extraction system was designed to pump smoke downward from ceiling ducts into basement shafts — reversing the natural behavior of hot air — described in contemporaneous reporting as "a unique undertaking" that "has not worked as planned." In May 2012, 26 days before the scheduled opening, the system failed its mandatory acceptance test. Inspectors subsequently found more than 120,000 defects. The system's designer, Alfredo di Mauro, was later revealed to hold qualifications as a draughtsman, not as a licensed engineer.

The structural cause was unconstrained scope change. Mid-construction, CEO Rainer Schwarz directed the architect to add north and south piers, converting the terminal from a rectangle to a U-shape and expanding floor area substantially. He subsequently added a second mezzanine level to accommodate retail. These changes were made without a structured change control process or impact assessment on fire safety system design, which had been locked by then. Each scope addition cascaded directly into the fire and smoke extraction design, which had to be re-engineered around a building that was no longer the building it had been designed for.

The governance failure was structural: FBB's 20-member supervisory board was composed primarily of politicians from Berlin, Brandenburg, and the federal government. Internal minutes and timetables were falsified. The board lacked the technical standing to override management's scope decisions. By late 2012, total expenditures had reached €4.3 billion — nearly twice the original budget — and the airport did not open for another eight years.

First-year operational data was deeply distorted by COVID-19. BER handled approximately 700,000 passengers in its first 100 days (November 2020 through February 2021), against a daily peak of roughly 20,000 passengers on 18 December 2020. By February 2021, daily traffic had collapsed to 4,000–8,000. Terminal 5 (the former Schönefeld airport terminal, retained as overflow) was temporarily closed February 2021 for lack of traffic. Full-year 2021: 9.95 million passengers against a design capacity of 27–46 million annually. Even if traffic had been at pre-pandemic 2019 Berlin levels (approximately 35 million across Tegel and Schönefeld combined), BER's terminal configuration and accumulated operational debt from years of deferred readiness work would have faced immediate stress.

[Source: Wikipedia, "Construction of Berlin Brandenburg Airport"; "Berlin Brandenburg Airport"; Berlin Airport press releases 2021; CNN, "Berlin's troubled Brandenburg Airport still in crisis one year after opening," December 2021; International Airport Review, "100 days later," February 2021; New Civil Engineer, May 2012; Interesting Engineering]

### LaGuardia Terminal B — The Airside Win and the Landside Failure

Terminal B's $4 billion construction program ran 2016–2022, with a phased opening strategy that kept the 35-gate terminal operational throughout construction — building "next to, behind and even over the top of the original terminal." The first 11 new gates opened December 2018; the headhouse opened June 2020 (during peak COVID); American's first 7 western concourse gates opened August 2020.

The critical infrastructure gain was geometric, not architectural: the new terminal's positioning recovered 40 acres of airside land and created approximately two miles of additional taxiway, giving aircraft two independent paths to each gate. Airport-wide flight delays fell from approximately 26.6% to 20.75% — a 22% reduction — between the pre-renovation baseline and 2022 completion. The terminal received the 2023 Skytrax Best New Airport Terminal designation and the ACI North America Service Quality Award for its passenger size category.

The landside assumption embedded in the original business case did not survive contact with cost estimating. The proposed AirTrain connection, budgeted at $450 million, was cancelled March 2023 after the estimate reached $2.4 billion — a 433% overrun before a dollar of construction had been spent. The Port Authority substituted an enhanced bus strategy. The operational consequence for passengers is that terminal-side performance improved substantially while access-mode performance remained constrained by road congestion.

[Source: Port Authority of New York and New Jersey; HOK project documentation; Skift Airline Weekly, "LaGuardia: the Queens Miracle," March 2023; AMNY, March 2023; DBIA Owner Spotlight; Airport Technology project brief]

### JFK Terminal 1 — A Pre-Opening Warning Pattern

As of May 2026, the $9.5 billion New Terminal One at JFK has not opened. Its first phase was pushed from summer 2026 to "no earlier than November 2026." Moody's Ratings revised the project's Baa3 credit outlook to negative, citing the delay and a downward revision in demand forecasts. The concern Moody's flagged — "potential for increased leverage required to finance the second phase" if passenger volumes underperform — is precisely the demand shortfall risk Flyvbjerg's research quantifies across 210 comparable projects.

JFK's structural delay vulnerability is relevant context. The airport's fragmented terminal model — each airline historically built its own terminal — produces a coordination tax on ground operations. Gate waiting affects 30% or more of arriving aircraft during the 3pm–midnight window. JFK regularly appears in the top 5–10 worst US airports for delays; in February 2026, 26.9% of flights were disrupted. The new Terminal One will consolidate international operations across a 2.6-million-square-foot, 23-gate facility — the largest at JFK when complete in 2030 — but will open into an airport system still undergoing simultaneous construction across multiple terminals.

ORAT was scoped as JLL's mandate, announced in 2025, covering "operational readiness and transition, start-up support beginning next year." If ORAT work begins in 2026 for a November 2026 opening, the integrated testing window will be measured in months — shorter than the T5 window that produced the 2008 disaster.

[Source: Bloomberg, May 15, 2026; Finexus/Moody's analysis, May 2026; Facilities Dive, JLL announcement; JFK airport operator announcements; Timeout New York, March 2025]

### FAA ASPM Delay Attribution — Where Infrastructure Shows Up in the Data

FAA OPSNET data, covering June 2017 through May 2023, attributes delay causes as follows for NAS delays over 15 minutes: weather, 74.26%; volume, 14.92%; runway unavailability, 5.96%; equipment failure, 0.61%; other, 4.25%. The 25.74% non-weather share is where terminal configuration, gate management, and operational coordination decisions register directly in the delay statistics.

Gate waiting — aircraft holding for an occupied gate — contributes materially to the volume category. Research using ASPM and BTS data (George Mason University CATSR) found that more than 30% of arriving aircraft experienced gate-waiting delays during peak hours at 10 of the FAA's OEP 35 airports in summer 2007. Delta schedules gates at higher than 80% planned occupancy at JFK, producing systematic gate conflicts during peak periods. Airlines industry-wide typically schedule gates for 80% occupancy to buffer for irregular operations; where gate inventory is constrained by terminal design, that buffer disappears.

The policy implication: the non-weather 26% is the portion capital planners and operators can actually influence. New terminal construction aimed at reducing volume delays requires both the physical gate inventory and the operational protocols to manage gate turn times — and the evidence from LGA Terminal B suggests the geometry change (taxiway layout, gate access paths) matters as much as the gate count itself.

[Source: FAA Weather FAQ, citing OPSNET 2017–2023; George Mason University CATSR, "Analysis of Gate-Waiting Delays at Major US Airports," 9th ATIO Conference; FAA ASPM system documentation]

## Counterexamples: When New Terminals Did Deliver

### Singapore Changi Terminal 4 (Opened October 2017)

Changi T4 opened with a fully implemented FAST (Fast And Seamless Travel) biometric suite across all passenger touchpoints — check-in, bag drop, immigration, boarding. The baggage handling system, a 5,000-meter BEUMER CrisBag tote-based ICS, processed 5,400 bags per hour with 100% track-and-trace capability. Changi Airport Group conducted more than 100 trials with over 2,500 staff and 7,500 volunteers before opening — a testing program that extended across multiple months and included live-aircraft trials.

What distinguished T4: the scope was fixed before testing began; the system was designed around a defined passenger experience specification (FAST), not retrofitted to a building that had changed mid-construction; and the ORAT process was led by an operator (CAG) with direct accountability for post-opening performance, not by a construction contractor measuring completion against milestones.

T4 handled 16 million passengers annually at design capacity, against Changi's 2017 total of 62.2 million across all terminals. There is no documented baggage system failure or significant operational incident in Year 1.

[Source: CAAS publication on T4, Issue 24; Airport Technology project brief; BEUMER Group press release; IDEMIA case study; Future Travel Experience, September 2017]

### Istanbul Airport ORAT (Opened October 2018)

Istanbul Airport's ORAT program ran for more than two years before the October 2018 opening, with advisory participation from Copenhagen Airport and Incheon Airport. Multiple live trials were conducted with real Turkish Airlines aircraft and thousands of test passengers and baggage items. The 42km baggage handling system was rated at 28,880 bags per hour; during the first summer peak (2019), system availability reached 99.9% and average in-system time was approximately 8 minutes — against a 15-minute design specification, a 47% outperformance.

What distinguished Istanbul: ORAT was commissioned as a two-year program, not a three-month activation. The testing timeline was set before the construction completion date, not after. Experts from airports with comparable complexity (Incheon handles over 70 million passengers) were brought in specifically to test scenarios the local team had not encountered.

[Source: Future Travel Experience, October 2018; Vanderlande Istanbul Airport case study; SITA/iGA case study]

### Denver International Airport South Terminal Redevelopment (Completed April 2016)

DEN's South Terminal Redevelopment Program — a $544 million addition including a Westin hotel, public transit center, and RTD East Rail connection — was delivered on time and within budget in April 2016. The subsequent Great Hall Program's first two phases were also completed within the original $770 million budget. DEN handled over 82 million passengers in 2024 against a Jeppesen Terminal originally designed for 50 million — demonstrating that adaptive capacity growth is achievable when scope is controlled.

The contrast with BER and T5: DEN's scope was additive (a transit center and hotel alongside an existing operational terminal), not substitutive (replacing a functioning airport system with a new one). The operational risk of scope change was bounded.

[Source: Airport Technology project brief; Denver International Airport project announcements; Axios Denver, February 2025]

## Operational Warning Signs Checklist

Based on the four cases examined, the following leading indicators were visible before opening and predicted operational underperformance. Each was present at T5, BER, and JFK NTO; none were systematically present at Changi T4 or Istanbul.

**1. Testing timeline compressed to protect construction milestone, not operational readiness date.**
The warning sign: ORAT start date is determined by subtracting a fixed interval from the opening date, rather than by the complexity of integrated systems being tested. At T5, the integrated testing window was less than 5 months. At BER, ORAT approval of the smoke system came in April 2019; operational licence followed in May 2020. At JFK NTO, ORAT was scoped in 2025 for a 2026 opening.

**2. Scope changes made during construction without documented impact assessment on life-safety systems.**
The warning sign: change orders that expand floor area, add program elements, or change building geometry after life-safety systems (fire suppression, smoke extraction, emergency egress) have been designed. BER is the canonical case: the U-shape addition and mezzanine floor were inserted after the smoke extraction system was already designed for a rectangular building.

**3. Owner-engineer accountability divided across organizations with no single entity holding integrated systems responsibility.**
The warning sign: the entity responsible for construction completion is different from the entity responsible for operational performance, with no joint accountability mechanism. At T5, BAA held construction responsibility; BA held operational responsibility; they "only held joint meetings after things began to go wrong." At BER, the supervisory board held nominal governance authority but lacked technical standing. At JFK NTO, the developer (Ferrovial-led consortium) holds construction responsibility; JLL holds ORAT responsibility under a separate engagement.

**4. Demand forecast not stress-tested against a phased ramp or demand shortfall scenario.**
The warning sign: the business case presents a single demand curve, not a range. At BER, the design capacity of 27–46 million annually was built against 2009 demand forecasts; the airport opened into a global pandemic and handled under 10 million in Year 1. At JFK NTO, Moody's flagged a demand forecast reduction before the terminal has served a single passenger.

**5. IT and baggage system acceptance testing not completed under integrated, live-conditions before opening.**
The warning sign: system acceptance testing is done in isolation (individual subsystem tests) rather than end-to-end with live airline data, real baggage tags, and interline transfer scenarios. The specific failure mode at T5 — a message filter for transfer baggage not removed after testing — only surfaces in integrated, multi-airline, live-environment testing. Denver 1995 and T5 2008 share this failure mode across different technologies and different decades.
