# The Architecture of Silence
## Mega-Terminal Programs That Underperformed, and the Warning Signs That Were Already in the Room

---

In 1992, engineers at Munich Airport sat down to write a letter. They had recently finished commissioning a fully automated baggage handling system — smaller and simpler than the one Denver was about to build, but new enough that the team had learned things they thought worth sharing. The system, they explained, had required two years of construction. After that came six months of round-the-clock testing. Twenty-four hours a day. Six months. Then it opened.

Denver had set itself a different schedule. The Denver system — 17 miles of track, 4,000 radio-controlled carts, 100 networked computers, 5,000 electric eyes — was several orders of magnitude more complex than Munich's. The city's plan was to design, build, install, integrate, and test it in roughly the same window Munich had spent on testing alone. Three companies had already told Denver the timeline was impossible. The city had rejected all three bids and asked a fourth, BAE Automated Systems, to accept the contract at the original price and schedule. BAE took it. Then the city expanded the scope from one concourse for one airline to three concourses for twenty.

The Munich engineers' letter, in essence, said: this is set up to fail.

The Denver International Airport opened in February 1995, sixteen months late. The Government Accountability Office documented $360 million in delay costs by the time the doors opened. Monthly operating losses had run between $13.35 million and $18.75 million for the entire delay. The automated baggage system never worked at full design — it ran one concourse, one airline, outbound bags only — and was decommissioned in 2005 at a maintenance cost of a million dollars a month. United Airlines replaced it with a conventional manual system that could have been installed at the start for $51 million.

What the Munich engineers wrote in 1992 was a warning sign. It arrived on time. It was specific. It was technical. It was delivered by people who had no incentive to be wrong. And it did not change the outcome.

This is the pattern that recurs across every major terminal failure of the last fifty years, and it is not what most post-mortems describe. The dominant story is that mega-terminal failures are black swans — rare, complex, unforeseeable. The honest story is the opposite. They are slow-motion governance failures with leading indicators visible five to seven years before ribbon-cutting. The signals are not hidden. They are noticed, written down, sometimes formally delivered to the people who could act on them. What fails is not detection. What fails is the institutional capacity to receive the signal and stop.

The thesis of this essay is that the warning signs are real, recurring, and identifiable. The complication is that they are also nearly universal, which is why visibility is the wrong frame. The harder question — the one a capital planner facing a multi-billion-dollar terminal program in 2026 needs to answer — is not whether the signals exist. It is who in your organization has both the information and the standing to halt the program when they appear.

## Executive Summary

1. **The four leading indicators recur across every documented mega-terminal failure of the modern era.** Scope creep approved without re-baselining (Denver 1992, BER 2009), integrated systems testing compressed to protect a fixed opening date (T5 2008, BER 2012), owner-engineer role confusion (Denver's dependence on BAE for technical evaluation, BER's draughtsman designing the smoke extraction system), and optimism bias in the business case (BER's pre-construction projection of 34 million passengers against a Year 1 actual of 9 million). The pattern is documented across at least four decades of post-mortem literature.

2. **The signals are visible years before ribbon-cutting.** BER's fire safety system failed its mandatory acceptance test on May 9, 2012 — 26 days before the planned opening. By that point, inspectors had catalogued 66,500 defects, including 5,845 classified as critical. The smoke extraction system's fundamental design — pumping hot smoke downward into a basement shaft, against the natural buoyancy of heated air — was thermodynamically incoherent before a single duct was installed. It was designed by a man without engineering credentials. None of these conditions arose late. They were locked in during design.

3. **The cost of the failure is quantifiable and routine.** Denver's 16-month delay generated $360 million in operating deficits. BER's nine-year delay drove final program cost from approximately €2.8 billion to over €7 billion. The LAX Automated People Mover, projected at $1.9 billion in 2018, is now $2.78–3.34 billion and more than two years late, with $750 million in dispute settlements paid by Los Angeles World Airports. The Denver Great Hall P3, contracted at $650 million in 2018, was terminated in 2019 and is now projected at $2.35 billion — more than three times the original contract.

4. **The current US terminal construction wave is the largest since 1989–1998.** JFK New Terminal One ($9.5 billion), JFK Terminal 6 ($4.2 billion), O'Hare expansion ($8.5 billion grown to at least $11.5 billion), LAX automated people mover ($4.9 billion), LaGuardia Terminal B ($4 billion completed), Delta's LGA Terminal C ($4 billion), and DFW Terminal F ($4 billion) are executing simultaneously. NPIAS-identified investment needs rose from $43.5 billion in the FY2021–2025 cycle to $62.4 billion in the FY2023–2027 cycle — a 43% increase without a single additional gate scoped, driven by construction inflation that ran 10.1% in 2021 and 14.8% in 2022.

5. **The 2026 federal funding cliff sharpens every business case.** The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act's $19.5 billion in supplemental aviation funding expires in December 2026. Post-cliff, the baseline Airport Improvement Program reverts to roughly $4 billion annually. A $40 million terminal project that received $20 million in IIJA support in 2023 becomes a project requiring $40–50 million in new debt by 2027. Most airports planned in 2019 or 2020 under a different funding assumption.

6. **Recovery from a Year 1 commissioning failure is possible but not absolution.** Heathrow Terminal 5 lost 42,000 bags in its first ten days, cancelled more than 500 flights, fired British Airways' director of operations and director of customer services, and absorbed roughly $50 million in direct cost. By 2018, T5 was processing 32.1 million passengers — above its 30-million design capacity — and had won the Skytrax Best Airport Terminal award five years running. The terminal recovered. The careers and the brand damage did not.

7. **The successful counter-cases share a structural feature.** Istanbul (October 2018) ran a two-year ORAT program with Copenhagen and Incheon advisers and multiple full-scale live trials. Singapore Changi Terminal 4 (October 2017) ran more than 100 trials with 2,500 staff and 7,500 volunteers. Kansas City International's new terminal (2023) built its budget from gate count and functional requirements rather than from drawings, secured airline sign-off before design began, and used early contractor involvement. In each case, scope was fixed before testing started, and a single accountable owner held integrated systems responsibility.

8. **The institutional question is not detection but standing.** At BER, the supervisory board was composed of politicians without engineering credentials. At Denver, the city's project management team lacked the independent technical authority to evaluate BAE's representations. At Denver's Great Hall, the airport managed a 34-year design-build-finance-operate-maintain contract as if it were a traditional design-bid-build. The signals were not missed. They were received by people who could not act on them and filtered before they reached people who could.

## The Argument

### The Munich Warning, and What It Tells Us About Visibility

Return to Denver for a moment. The case is the founding document of this argument, and it is worth dwelling on because it establishes the structural pattern every later failure repeats.

The scope expansion happened in 1992. The system that BAE had been hired to build for United Airlines at Concourse B was expanded by the city to all three concourses and twenty airlines. BAE's senior managers internally estimated the new scope required four years; the contract still said two. More than 2,000 design changes were issued during construction. The city of Denver did not re-baseline cost or schedule. The Project Management Team, by its own later admission, halted system design in May 1992 once it "anticipated responsibility for the integrated system" — a clinical phrase that, translated, means the team realized it was going to own a problem it could not solve.

The independent evaluator the city hired called the plan "too complex." Munich Airport, building a comparable but simpler system, sent direct written notice that Denver's timeline was unworkable. Three bidders for the contract said the same thing in their proposals. The warning was not delivered once; it was delivered four times by four separate parties, in writing, with the names of qualified engineers on it.

The city went ahead anyway. The airport opened in February 1995, sixteen months late, at a delay cost the GAO measured at $33 million per month and $360 million cumulative. The baggage system never functioned at design. It was decommissioned ten years after opening and replaced by the manual conveyor system that could have been built for $51 million in the first place.

This is the part of Denver that matters more than the technology. The technology question — could an automated baggage system of that complexity be built in the early 1990s? — is interesting but secondary. The institutional question is the one to sit with. The city of Denver received four independent, technically qualified, explicit warnings that its plan would fail. It had access to those warnings during the period when corrective action was still possible. The signals were not invisible to detection. They were visible and ignored.

What the Denver case proves is the premise of every subsequent failure: the warning signs are not hidden. They are noticed by engineers, by commissioning staff, by the program manager's deputies, by outside consultants the airport itself hired. They are written down. They reach senior management, or at least the layer below senior management. And they are filtered, softened, or politically inadmissible by the time they reach the people who could stop the program.

The mechanism is not corruption. It is something more banal and more durable: the systematic suppression of bad news by an organizational structure that rewards optimism at every reporting layer. The program manager's incentives are to keep the program green. The design-build contractor's incentives are to close out scope. The bond underwriter's incentives are to get to financial close. Each individual filter is rational. The cumulative effect is that by the time bad news reaches the supervisory board or the airport CEO, it has been softened by four people protecting client relationships and career trajectories.

This is the architecture of silence. It is the load-bearing structure beneath every mega-terminal failure on record, and it is more reliable than any of the four specific signals the failure literature itemizes.

### Berlin's Draughtsman

Berlin Brandenburg Airport is the complete specimen. Every signal manifests at maximum intensity, and the institutional failure is documented in unusual depth because the Bundestag, the federal courts, the city of Berlin, and multiple investigative journalists examined the program in real time.

The original budget was €2.83 billion. The terminal opened on October 31, 2020 — nine years late — at a direct cost exceeding €7 billion. When interest and lost revenue are included, the program reaches €10 billion or more, depending on accounting basis. The proximate cause of the failure was the fire protection and smoke extraction system, which failed its mandatory acceptance test on May 9, 2012, twenty-six days before the planned opening.

Here is what was true at that moment. The smoke extraction system had been designed to pump hot smoke downward from ceiling-level ducts through a basement shaft, because the architect had specified a clean roofline without visible exhaust infrastructure. This required reversing the natural buoyancy of heated air at a scale that had never been demonstrated. At the dimensions of the BER terminal, the system did not work — when the fans built pressure during the May 2012 test, the ducts deformed and the automatic flaps failed to open or close on command. The system's designer, Alfredo di Mauro, was qualified as an engineering draughtsman, not a licensed engineer. His credential status was discovered in 2014. Di Mauro himself later told a Berlin radio station, "No one asked about my university qualifications; that wasn't necessary for the work we carried out."

Read that sentence again. The fire safety system for one of Europe's largest infrastructure projects, the system on which operational certification of the entire terminal depended, was designed by a draughtsman whose credentials no one had verified. That is not a technical failure. That is a governance failure with a physical address.

The institutional context explains how this happened. The supervisory board of Flughafen Berlin Brandenburg GmbH was composed of the mayors of Berlin and Brandenburg, federal ministers, civil servants, and union officials. Its chairman, Klaus Wowereit, publicly acknowledged that no one on the board had been selected for construction competency. The board could not evaluate what it could not read. When mid-construction scope changes were proposed — the addition of north and south piers converting the terminal from a rectangle to a U-shape, the insertion of an additional retail mezzanine — the supervisory body that approved them was a body of politicians making decisions about thermodynamics. Each scope change cascaded directly into the fire safety system design, which had to be re-engineered around a building it had not been designed for.

By the May 2012 test failure, the project was already through one CEO. Manfred Körtgen was dismissed that month. His successor, Rainer Schwarz, was fired in January 2013 after reporting 90% completion when independent auditors found 56.2% operational readiness. Hartmut Mehdorn, a turnaround appointment in March 2013, resigned in December 2014 citing excessive political interference. By the time the airport opened in 2020, the supervisory board had cycled through four governing configurations and the program had passed through what Berlin's investigative press called "a carousel of CEOs."

The wiring inspection by TÜV in May 2018 — six years after the failed acceptance test — documented 863 separate wiring defects in the fire and smoke control systems alone. By that point, 66,500 total defects had been catalogued across the terminal, of which 5,845 were classified as critical. The fire safety system did not receive TÜV approval until April 2019. The first full-systems test rehearsal began August 2019. The airport opened fourteen months later, into a pandemic that masked the most consequential downstream failure — the business case itself.

Because the second collapse at BER was not the building. It was the commercial assumption underneath it.

The terminal had been designed for 34 million passengers annually at opening, with capacity for 46 million. The retail revenue model assumed half of total airport income from concessions, calibrated to the spending behavior of premium connecting passengers — the volume that Air Berlin's planned Oneworld connecting hub was supposed to generate. Air Berlin filed for insolvency on August 15, 2017, three years before BER opened. The carrier's financial distress had been visible in its public filings since at least 2015. Airport leadership publicly stated in 2017 that the bankruptcy "would have had no imminent impact on expected traffic volume." That sentence is technically defensible and commercially absurd. The connecting hub model that underwrote half of BER's retail revenue dissolved with the carrier that was supposed to feed it.

BER's first full year of operations (2021) saw 9.95 million passengers against a design capacity of 27–46 million. By 2023 the airport had recovered to 23.1 million — still below the 34 million the commercial design was calibrated to. The carrier mix had shifted decisively: Ryanair and easyJet together control roughly a third of total seat capacity. BER is now what its actual market always was — a low-cost, origin-and-destination airport — and not what its business case said it would be.

The signals at BER were visible. The draughtsman's credentials. The thermodynamically incoherent smoke extraction design. The supervisory board's composition. Air Berlin's deteriorating balance sheet. The supervisory board's structural inability to act on technical information it could not interpret. Every one of these was knowable and known.

What failed was not detection. It was the institutional capacity to convert detection into intervention.

### Six Months Was What Was Agreed

Heathrow Terminal 5 refines the argument by isolating a single failure variable. T5 cost £4.2 billion and was, by the standards of British construction practice, a triumph. The building was delivered on time and on budget under BAA's bespoke "T5 Agreement," a relational contract in which BAA retained all risk on its own balance sheet and paid contractors actual cost plus a fixed fee. There were no incentives for change orders or claims. The supply chain was integrated under genuine in-house program management. T5 is the canonical case study cited by the OECD and the World Bank for how to make construction work.

It opened on March 27, 2008. Within hours, British Airways suspended hold baggage check-in and cancelled 34 flights. Over the next ten days, more than 42,000 bags failed to travel with their owners and over 500 flights were cancelled. The root cause has been documented in detail by the UK Parliament's Transport Select Committee. It was not the building. It was a message filter, left in place from the testing phase, that prevented the live baggage system from recognizing interline bags transferring from other airlines. The filter had been inserted specifically to prevent test messages from contaminating production systems and was never removed at go-live. Bags arriving from other carriers were automatically routed to manual storage. Staff were not in numbers sufficient to clear the backlog. The system clogged.

This is the kind of failure that only surfaces in integrated, end-to-end testing under live conditions. And the testing window had been compressed. BAA's own specialist systems engineer, Ian Booker, had recommended nine months of operational readiness testing. The program delivered six. Building magazine's post-mortem documented that the testing ran "half as long as recommended." Construction delays consumed the integration window. By the time British Airways could begin on-site testing — October 31, 2007, five months before opening — the proving trials had been reduced in scope or cancelled because the terminal was still an active construction site. BA's on-site familiarization program for ramp and passenger service staff was deferred by six weeks. On opening day, baggage handlers could not access the staff car park. They arrived two hours late. Employee ID systems did not recognize their badges. Eighteen terminal lifts were jammed.

Booker's quote — "I remember suggesting nine months, but six months was what was agreed, and built into the programme" — is the entire argument compressed to a sentence. The recommendation was made. It was overridden. The override was visible in the program schedule from the moment it was made. Anyone reading the project's critical path could see that integrated testing had been compressed below the duration the engineer responsible for testing had requested.

T5 recovered. The brief from the airline commercial side is correct that within eleven months, BA's daily baggage mishandling rate at T5 had fallen to five bags in 1,000. By 2018, the terminal was processing 32.1 million passengers, above its 30-million design capacity. From 2012 through 2016, it won the Skytrax Best Airport Terminal award five years in a row. The terminal that began as a national humiliation became, in operating performance, one of Heathrow's strongest assets.

But recovery is not absolution, and the temptation to read T5 as a soft case should be resisted. BA's director of operations and director of customer services were fired. Citibank estimated direct cost to BA at $50 million. The brand damage was sustained — BA's Heathrow operation was, for a period, an international punchline. And the failure was preventable in a specific, named way. Booker said nine months; the program said six. The decision is on paper.

What distinguishes T5 from BER is not the presence or absence of the signal. The signal was present in both cases. What distinguishes them is whether the institutional structure permitted intervention. BAA, on the construction side, had built a program in which the owner held all risk and could enforce decisions. On the operational side — where construction transferred to BA — there was no equivalent integrating authority. BAA assumed BA owned operational readiness. BA assumed the construction completion date was its activation date. They held joint meetings only after the failure was already unfolding. The owner-operator handoff was the gap.

### Phasing, P3s, and the New Failure Mode

Denver's Great Hall is the case where the contracting structure itself produced the institutional gap. In 2018, Denver International Airport awarded a 34-year design-build-finance-operate-maintain contract to Great Hall Partners, led by Ferrovial Airports, at approximately $650 million. By month eleven, GHP had issued change order notifications totaling $288 million. The airport terminated the contract in August 2019. The independent review found that DIA had managed the contract as if it were design-bid-build — issuing owner changes, inserting itself into decisions that the DBFOM contract had explicitly delegated to the private partner, while simultaneously accusing the contractor of scope creep. Structural concrete defects were discovered mid-construction. A city council member who had authorized the original 2017 vote later called it "the worst vote he'd ever made."

The project was repackaged and ultimately approved at approximately $2.35 billion, with completion now scheduled for 2028 — seven years beyond the original schedule. The post-mortem report concluded that the project "may have been better served as a design-bid-build contract or a construction manager/general contractor project."

That is a finding that indicts the procurement structure rather than the contractor or the airport executive. It is the more troubling result. It says: the institution chose a contract form that did not match its own operating capacity. The choice was made for political and financing reasons — the city wanted the project off its balance sheet, and a 34-year concession provided that — and the operating consequences were absorbed by the airport that still had to manage the program day to day.

This is the new failure mode of the P3 era. Public-private partnerships transfer delivery risk on paper. They can also eliminate the airport's direct technical authority over the program. The contract becomes the governance instrument. When the contract is wrong — when the airport's actual operating model does not match the decision rights the concession agreement assigns — the structure metastasizes into the same paralysis that BER's political supervisory board produced, but for different reasons.

The LAX Automated People Mover is the live counterexample to the proposition that P3 structures discipline costs. Awarded as a 30-year design-build-finance-operate-maintain contract, the project was originally budgeted at $1.9 billion. The current estimated total project cost is $2.78–3.34 billion. The 2024–25 Los Angeles County Grand Jury found that the contractor, LINXS, "leveraged the change order process by implicitly holding out the threat of prolonged litigation to force LAWA to agree." Los Angeles World Airports paid more than $200 million in dispute settlements through 2023, then agreed to a $550 million global settlement in 2024. Fitch downgraded the project's credit, citing "extended construction delays, prolonged dispute resolution, and difficulties in the parties' working relationship." The P3 structure did not transfer the risk. It transferred the risk of litigation expense back to the public owner through a conflict resolution mechanism that the contractor learned to exploit.

JFK's New Terminal One is the live test. The $9.5 billion program is the largest single-asset P3 in US history. SITA serves as Master Systems Integrator within an alliance that includes AECOM Tishman, Schneider Electric, and Faith Group — a structure designed precisely to address the integration risk that has historically broken comparable programs. SITA opened a pre-production testing center near Islip, Long Island, in December 2023, three years before the target opening date. That is the right architecture.

But the warning signs are also visible. The Phase A opening, originally targeted for summer 2026, was pushed to no earlier than November 2026. Moody's revised the project's senior secured outlook to negative in May 2026, citing the postponement and a downward revision in forecasted passenger demand. The January 2025 Alternative Solutions Report described the project as operating in a "recovery posture rather than comfortable buffering." The credit structure embeds material demand risk: 23 million passengers at full build-out is required to validate the cost structure, with no Port Authority general revenue backstop. If the international carriers that make up the tenant base — Air France, Lufthansa, Turkish, Korean — reduce JFK frequency for any reason, NTO's debt service coverage compresses with no public buffer.

And the World Cup is sitting on the timeline. FIFA matches in June and July 2026 create exactly the political opening date that BER's supervisory board kept committing to and that the actual systems work could not meet. JLL was engaged for operational readiness and airport transition work scoped to begin in 2025 for a 2026 opening. If the integrated testing window is measured in months rather than years, the program is testing whether the institutional check — the Port Authority, the Ferrovial-led consortium, SITA, JLL — has the standing to refuse a politically committed opening date the systems aren't ready for. We will know in eight months.

### When the Design Is the Failure

LaGuardia Terminal B introduces a different failure mode that the standard four-signal framework does not catch. Terminal B opened in 2022 on time, won the 2023 Skytrax World's Best New Airport Terminal award and the ACI Service Quality Award, and is by most external measures a success. Airport-wide flight delays fell from approximately 26.6% to 20.75% — a 22% reduction in delay rate that traces directly to the airside geometry change. The new terminal recovered 40 acres of airside land and added approximately two miles of taxiway, giving aircraft two independent paths to each of 35 gates. The gain is real.

And yet. International connection times rose by approximately 25%. The satellite concourse design — two islands connected by 482-foot pedestrian bridges at 60 feet elevation — extended walking distances beyond what the prior terminal required. The drop-off zone, with a single road access, regularly produces wait times above 20 minutes. The proposed AirTrain landside connection was cancelled in March 2023 after estimates rose from $450 million to $2.4 billion — a 433% overrun before a single dollar of construction. The Port Authority substituted an enhanced bus strategy. The landside assumption embedded in the original Terminal B business case did not survive contact with cost estimating.

LGA Terminal B is the case where the building works and the operation it enables does not. The signals the standard framework reads — scope creep, ORAT compression, owner-engineer confusion, optimism bias — do not all appear. What appears instead is a design that traded operational performance for architectural achievement. The walking distances are baked in concrete. By the time ORAT runs, the design failure cannot be undone.

This complicates the thesis. It suggests that the four-signal framework catches the construction-and-commissioning category of failure but misses a quieter, more durable failure mode that runs through design rather than construction. The way you catch this one is not by watching the schedule. It is by having an operating organization with standing to require that someone who will run the building on a Monday morning in February sign off on the circulation design before the architect freezes it. Most terminal programs do not have that organization. Most have only program management firms whose incentive is to close out scope.

### What the Successful Cases Share

Istanbul opened on October 29, 2018. The ORAT program ran more than two years. Advisers from Copenhagen Airport and Incheon Airport, both of which had managed comparable greenfield openings, were brought in specifically to test scenarios the local team had not encountered. Multiple full-scale live trials were conducted with real Turkish Airlines aircraft and thousands of test passengers and baggage items. The 42-kilometer baggage handling system was rated at 28,880 bags per hour. During the first summer peak in 2019, system availability reached 99.9% and average in-system bag time was approximately 8 minutes against a 15-minute design specification.

Singapore Changi Terminal 4 opened in October 2017 after more than 100 trials with 2,500 staff and 7,500 volunteers. The Fast And Seamless Travel biometric architecture was implemented across all passenger touchpoints. Scope was fixed before testing began. The system was designed around a defined passenger experience specification, not retrofitted to a building that had changed mid-construction. There is no documented baggage system failure or significant operational incident in Year 1.

Kansas City International's new single terminal opened in 2023 on time and on budget. The cited differentiators in the post-completion reviews: budget built from gate count and functional requirements rather than from drawings; airline sign-off on functional requirements before design began; early contractor involvement; and a hard external deadline tied to the 2023 NFL Draft.

These cases share a structural feature that none of the failure cases share. Scope was locked before commissioning began. ORAT was funded as a multi-year program with its own budget line. A single integrating authority — Changi Airport Group, IGA, the airport itself — held responsibility for both construction completion and operational performance. The airline customers had signed off on what they were getting before design was frozen.

The distinction is not that the successful cases avoided the four warning signals. Every megaproject of this complexity sees scope pressure, schedule pressure, and optimism in the early business case. The distinction is that the successful institutions had built governance structures that could refuse compromise. Someone with standing could say, two years before opening, that ORAT would run for twenty-four months and that no political opening date would compress it. Someone could say, when the design changed, that the change would be re-baselined or rejected. Someone could insist that the airline tenants sign legally binding commitments before the bond was issued, rather than letters of intent that protected no one.

These are not warning sign detectors. They are warning sign actors. The difference between the failure cases and the success cases is not who could see the signal. It is who could act on it.

## The Counter-Case, Honestly Presented

A serious reader of this argument should not be permitted to leave without being shown its weakest joints. There are four.

The first is that the warning signs are not diagnostic. Bent Flyvbjerg's database of over 16,000 projects across five decades finds that only about 8.5% of megaprojects meet time and budget targets, and only 0.5% deliver on time, on budget, and with promised benefits fully realized. If scope creep, optimism bias, and systems integration deferral appear in 91.5% of megaprojects, they cannot distinguish the failures from the marginal successes. A variable that predicts everything predicts nothing. This is the contrarian's strongest objection, and it has real force. A warning sign is only useful if its absence correlates with success. The thesis as stated does not prove that absence.

The second is that the case set is curated. BER, T5, LGA, and JFK NTO are projects that have entered public consciousness as troubled — or, in JFK NTO's case, as anxious. The terminal programs of the same era that opened without triggering a CNN headline are absent from the analysis. Dallas Love Field, MSP Terminal 1, San Francisco Terminal 3 refresh, San Diego's new Terminal 2 — projects with comparable governance complexity that opened uneventfully — are not in the sample. Without a base rate of how many programs with the same four signals opened successfully, the argument is pattern-matching against a selected sample.

The third is that the four cases are not actually the same failure. BER took nine years to recover. T5 took eleven months. LGA Terminal B opened on time and won an architectural prize. JFK NTO has slipped six months. Calling these the same pattern because they all involve "systems integration deferral" is like saying two patients died of "circulatory failure" — technically accurate, clinically useless. A framework that treats a six-month slip and a nine-year disaster as members of the same set is making a categorical mistake that will mislead the executives it is designed to help.

The fourth is the most uncomfortable. Optimism bias is not a correctable governance failure. It is a rational response to a funding environment that rewards ambition and penalizes candor. Flyvbjerg's research on "strategic misrepresentation" documents that project promoters who present realistic forecasts often do not get funded. The political economy that produces billion-dollar terminal programs also produces the optimism that makes them approvable. Telling airport executives to be less optimistic is, in the contrarian's phrasing, like telling structural engineers to use less gravity. The world in which CEOs present pessimistic business cases to political authorities is not the world we live in. The deeper problem is structural and there is no clean technical fix.

To this should be added a fifth, more political objection. The regulatory and political environment that constrains airport decision-making is not a variable airports can easily move. The Passenger Facility Charge has been frozen at $4.50 since 2000. Its purchasing power in 2018 dollars was $2.72 — a 40% real reduction. Six FAA reauthorization cycles have failed to raise it. CBP staffing is a congressional appropriations matter; major international airports document processing times two to three times normal when officers are redeployed. TSA changes checkpoint design standards mid-construction. The DCA perimeter rule has survived sixty years of economic arguments against it. The PFC cap, frozen in 2000, is what forced JFK NTO and LGA Terminal B into special-facilities revenue bond and P3 structures. The political constraints that produce the financing structures that produce the governance fragmentation are themselves immovable on any project timeline. The thesis's prescription — better governance, better signal-detection, better institutional standing — runs into the wall of what airports cannot actually change.

A reader who agrees with these objections is not wrong. The argument as advanced overreaches at its weakest joints. The honest question is whether the corrections it implies still leave the core thesis standing.

## Why the Counter-Case Is Insufficient

It does. Here is what the corrections actually require.

Concede the first objection at full strength. Yes, the four signals appear in 91.5% of megaprojects. They are not diagnostic in the predictive sense the original thesis implied. A capital planner cannot read the four signals against a project and assign a probability of failure. The signals are universal at the design phase. What is not universal is whether the institution receiving the signal has the standing to act on it. That is the actual variable. The success cases — Istanbul, Changi T4, Kansas City — did not have categorically different signals than the failure cases. They had categorically different institutional architecture for receiving and acting on signals.

This is a sharper formulation than the original thesis, and it implies a different prescription. Do not watch for the four signals as if they were rare. Assume they are present. Then ask, of your own program, who specifically — by name, with what reporting line, with what authority — can refuse a political opening date that the systems work cannot meet. If the answer requires more than one name, or if the answer is "the program manager, who works for the developer," the institutional check is structurally absent. The signals will arrive. They will be filtered. They will be softened. The opening date will be defended until the day the system fails its acceptance test.

Concede the second objection partially. Yes, the case selection is curated. But the curation is not random; it tracks public visibility, which tracks failure scale. The terminal programs that opened uneventfully did so in roughly the conditions the success cases describe — fixed scope, full ORAT, single integrating authority. The base rate problem is real, but the directional finding is robust: programs that compress ORAT below the four-month IATA floor, that change scope mid-construction without re-baselining, that diffuse owner-engineer authority across contract boundaries, and that build business cases on optimistic point forecasts experience Year 1 operational stress at rates substantially above the success cases. The 91.5% Flyvbjerg figure includes minor overruns and modest schedule slips that no operator would describe as failure. The Year 1–3 underperformance the thesis targets is the more severe tail of that distribution, and the four signals do correlate with the tail.

Concede the third objection fully and adjust the argument. BER and T5 are not the same failure. They are different categories of failure that share a common pre-condition — the institutional inability to act on visible signals — but unfold at different scales. BER is a structural failure: the building did not work and could not be made to work without years of remediation. T5 is an operational failure: the building worked but the human and information systems running it failed at integration. LGA Terminal B is a third category: the building works and the operation it enables is partially worse than the operation it replaced, because the design traded operational performance for architectural ambition. The four-signal framework should be understood as a screening test for the underlying institutional condition, not as a predictor of which specific failure category will manifest. A program that compresses ORAT may produce T5; a program that lets scope expand without re-baselining may produce BER; a program that excludes operating staff from design review may produce LGA. The category is downstream of the institutional architecture.

Concede the fourth objection — optimism bias is structural — and recognize what it implies. If the business case is structurally optimistic because the funding environment requires it, then the governance design must be structurally pessimistic to compensate. Reference-class forecasting, the methodology Flyvbjerg developed precisely for this purpose, applies the actual base rate of comparable projects to your project's estimate. The UK Treasury adopted it more than fifteen years ago. Almost no US airport uses it. A board that knows its business case is optimistic and does nothing structural to compensate is choosing to walk into the predictable failure. Acknowledging optimism bias as structural is not a defense; it is an argument for the institutional check that the success cases exhibit.

Concede the fifth objection — the regulatory and political constraints are not movable on project timelines — and accept what it forces. Airport executives cannot raise the PFC, cannot control CBP staffing, cannot freeze TSA checkpoint standards, and cannot eliminate the political pressure for opening dates. They can, however, choose their own governance architecture. They can choose whether the use-and-lease agreement is renegotiated before the bond is issued. They can choose whether ORAT is a budget line with a two-year horizon or a schedule activity that gets compressed when construction slips. They can choose whether the integrated systems testing is built into a contractual covenant the program manager cannot waive under schedule pressure. They can choose, in the language of one of the analysts behind this brief, whether their program has a designated pessimist — a person, with standing, whose job is to find what will break on opening day and surface it eighteen months out, rather than eighteen hours out.

What the counter-case shows, taken seriously, is that the original thesis was right in its observation and incomplete in its prescription. The signals are visible. They are also universal. What predicts the actual outcome is not whether the signals appear but whether the institution has built, in advance, the standing to act on them. Munich's engineers wrote to Denver. Booker said nine months. The TÜV inspectors flagged the BER fire system. The signals arrived. They were not heard by anyone with the authority and the will to halt the program.

This is the harder thesis, and the more useful one. It is also the one the success cases prove. Sean Donohue at DFW closed a ten-year use-and-lease agreement before committing the airport to a $9 billion capital program. Terminal F expanded from $1.6 billion to $4 billion only because American Airlines voluntarily increased its commitment. The scope growth was carrier-driven. Donohue did not stop scope creep; he made scope creep someone else's risk. That is what an institutional check looks like when it is built before construction begins.

The signals are not the framework. The framework is the architecture you build to act on them.

## Implications for the Hubs This Thesis Applies To

This essay was written for an audience that includes the operator of a forthcoming massive capital program. The most relevant case is the Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority and the Dulles transformation now under evaluation, but the analysis applies to every authority operating a multi-billion-dollar terminal program in the 2026–2032 window. The implications are specific and worth naming.

The Dulles Aerotrain is the precedent the authority should reread. The $1.4 billion system opened in January 2010, four months late, linking Concourses A, B, and C — but not Concourse D. The mobile lounges, the 1960s-era passenger transfer vehicles the Aerotrain was meant to replace, are still serving D. MWAA paid $1.4 billion for a system whose coverage is partial and whose supplemental infrastructure persists, fifteen years on, because the original scope did not extend the rails to where the passengers actually go. This is not an Aerotrain failure. It is the scope-and-design failure mode that LGA Terminal B exhibited in a different form: the building works, and the operation it enables is not what the business case described. The $22 billion Dulles transformation cost estimate now in evaluation includes the rebuild of the mobile lounge dependency, fifteen years after MWAA paid to eliminate it.

The first thing the authority should do is not architectural. It is institutional. Before any contracting structure is selected — design-bid-build, CMAR, P3 — the authority should name the person who will hold integrated systems responsibility from the basis-of-design phase through Year 3 of operations. Not a program manager; not a developer. A senior MWAA executive with operational accountability and board-level standing. Denver's Great Hall failed because the airport managed a DBFOM as design-bid-build. The institutional architecture must precede the contract form, not the other way around.

The second thing is to sequence the airline use-and-lease before committing to scope. Donohue did this at DFW. The current MWAA use-and-lease structure is the framework in which the $22 billion program will be priced into cost per enplanement, and the airline carriers — United principally, but the network carriers that route through IAD as a function of slot strategy at DCA — will absorb the cost increase or reroute. The CPE math is concrete: a $500 million bond program adds approximately $0.80 to CPE at a 40-million-enplanement airport, and 12% CPE increases historically trigger formal rate challenges under most use agreements. The use-and-lease cannot be negotiated after the bond is issued and the scope is fixed. It must precede them.

The third is to build the ORAT program before the construction baseline is set. IATA's documented minimum standard is four months of integrated operational trials before opening. The success cases (Istanbul, Changi T4) ran twenty-four months. The failure cases (T5, BER, Denver) compressed to six months or less. The decision to fund ORAT at the success-case duration must be made at the same time the construction schedule is approved, not after construction slippage has consumed the integration window. A board-level covenant — an explicit policy that no opening date will be set within four months of integrated systems testing completion — would be a structurally pessimistic compensation for the structurally optimistic business case the bond market requires.

The fourth is to take the IIJA cliff seriously. The $19.5 billion in supplemental aviation grants expires in December 2026. Post-cliff, baseline AIP reverts to approximately $4 billion annually. Any project scoped in the FY2023–FY2027 NPIAS cycle that assumed continued IIJA-level federal support faces a financing gap that did not exist when the program was conceived. A program that was 40% federally funded in 2023 may be 15% federally funded by 2028. The capital structure must be re-baselined now, not in 2027 when the bond market reads the same news.

The fifth is to build the designated pessimist into the governance structure before construction begins. Not a risk register. A named executive, insulated from the political opening-date commitment, whose performance review depends on surfacing what will break before opening rather than on hitting milestone celebrations. This is the institutional check that the failure cases lacked and the success cases possessed. BER's supervisory board could not perform this function; its members lacked the credentials to read the signals. Denver's project management team could not perform this function; it lacked independent technical authority over BAE. The function does not appear by accident. It must be designed in.

The sixth is to recognize the federal constraints as fixed and design around them. The PFC will not move in this reauthorization cycle. CBP staffing is appropriations-driven and cannot be assumed at design capacity. TSA checkpoint standards will continue to evolve. The international terminal business case must therefore be modeled against the CBP staffing that is funded, not the staffing the design assumes. The biometric architecture must be designed to reduce the booth count required at degraded staffing levels, not to optimize throughput at full staffing. The checkpoint footprint must be sized for the CT scanner standard, not for the 2D X-ray standard that was current when design began. These are constraints the authority can model but cannot move.

The seventh is the discipline most operators resist. Reference-class forecasting against the actual base rate, not against the feasibility study. The base rate for terminal programs of this scale shows cost overruns averaging 80%, schedule slips averaging 25%, and Year 1 demand realization averaging 70% of forecast. The MWAA business case for the Dulles transformation should be modeled against those base rates rather than against the program-specific feasibility numbers. If the financing structure cannot survive a 50% overrun and a 30% demand shortfall in Year 1, the financing structure is not the right one. JFK NTO's "recovery posture rather than comfortable buffering" language is not a warning to JFK alone. It is the description of what most US mega-terminal P3s look like by Year 2 of construction.

These are not exotic recommendations. They are what the success cases exhibit and what the failure cases lack. They are also more difficult to implement than they sound, because each of them runs against an institutional incentive that exists for rational reasons. The airline use-and-lease takes years to renegotiate. The board-level ORAT covenant looks like a constraint on management flexibility. The designated pessimist is uncomfortable to work with. Reference-class forecasting produces estimates that political authorities do not want to publish.

The question MWAA — or any authority sitting before a $20 billion capital decision — has to answer is the one Munich's engineers asked Denver in 1992 and Denver did not answer. If your own commissioning engineers told you, in writing, that the program as scoped would fail, what is the institutional pathway by which that signal reaches the person who can stop it? If the pathway requires more than three handoffs, the signal will not arrive in time. The architecture of silence will absorb it.

There is a photograph from BER's October 2020 opening day. The terminal is largely empty. A handful of passengers move through a vast hall designed for thirty-four million annual travelers. The pandemic is responsible for most of the emptiness, and it is also responsible, perversely, for the fact that the building's first weeks did not produce a Year 1 catastrophe. The systems that had failed for nine years did not have to perform at design load. The retail concessions calibrated for premium connecting passengers were closed. The political pressure that had cycled four CEOs and falsified internal timetables had finally exhausted itself. The terminal had opened. There was no one there to see it.

A capital planner looking at the next decade should ask what the planners in Berlin would have said in 2006 if someone had shown them that photograph. Whether they would have stopped. Whether they could have stopped. And what institutional architecture would have given them the standing to try.
