# COO Brief: Sterile Corridor Design as a Resilience Problem
## Stage 1 — Airport COO Voice
### Run: sterile-corridor-resilience | 2026-05-27

---

## Key Findings

- **The corridor is never the problem until it is the only problem.** Sterile corridor capacity lives in the background of normal operations and becomes the entire foreground the moment an escalator goes down at the midfield concourse during a bank push. Every other system can be marginal because there is slack somewhere else. There is no slack in a single-point circulation path serving 3,000 passengers in 40 minutes.

- **IAD's AeroTrain-to-concourse handoffs are operationally papered over, not solved.** The vertical circulation between the AeroTrain platforms and the sterile concourse level at Concourses A/B and C/D was sized for the passenger volumes of the early 2000s. United's rebuild of the midfield hub increased enplanements at those concourses materially; the escalator and elevator count did not change. On peak mornings, the queue off the escalators backs onto the platform. Operations knows this. Capital planning knows this. The fix has been in the five-year capital plan and off the five-year capital plan more than once.

- **Cleaning closures are the most frequent sterile corridor disruption and the one least represented in IROPS plans.** A single cleaning crew working one side of a bidirectional moving walkway segment reduces the effective corridor width from its design width to the non-mechanical half. At IAD's Concourse C connecting corridor, that reduction takes a passable peak-hour corridor to a degraded one. No one in an IROPS plan talks about cleaning windows because cleaning is facilities, not operations — and that organizational seam is exactly where the failure hides.

- **The design decisions that create corridor failure are made 10 to 15 years before passengers walk through.** At the point a width is locked into a design development package, the operator is typically represented by a facilities manager attending a 30% design review who does not have a traffic model, does not have a failure-mode analysis, and is being asked to sign off on 40 drawings in a two-hour session. The capital program has already consumed the contingency that would have bought another 8 feet of corridor width.

- **In-place sterile renovation is operationally harder than greenfield by a factor of three, and the plans consistently understate this.** DCA Project Journey and IAD Tier 2 phasing both required sterile corridor operations to be maintained continuously through active construction. The plans show phasing lines. They do not show what happens on the third night of a phase swing when the contractor is four hours behind, the sterile boundary has to be re-established before the 0500 cleaning window closes, and the duty manager is coordinating TSA, the contractor's site super, and a United gate agent who just found out her hold room is inside the construction fence.

- **Vertical circulation is the most common failure mode and the one with the longest recovery time.** A broken escalator at a midfield concourse takes 2–4 hours to diagnose and repair on a good day (vendor response, parts availability, certification of return to service). During those hours, the effective corridor capacity drops by 30–40% depending on whether the escalator is in the only vertical transition point or one of two. At IAD Concourse C, there is, for most of the south end, effectively one vertical transition point.

- **The throughput number in the planning model is not the number the operator has held on a peak day.** Pedestrian flow models run at design capacity with uniform arrival rates and no incidents. A real peak morning at IAD has a United bank arriving, an AeroTrain dwell problem on the platform, a cleaning crew running 15 minutes over window because of a spill, and an escalator that came up from overnight maintenance one step short of certification. The model does not contain any of those. The operator lives in all of them simultaneously.

## Evidence

The following sources are cited inline. Where web access was unavailable at time of writing, canonical reference identifiers are provided and URLs given as last-known stable locations.

**FAA AC 150/5360-13A, "Planning and Design Guidelines for Airport Terminal Facilities"** governs the baseline framework for terminal space planning, including sterile corridor dimensions and vertical circulation requirements. The advisory circular establishes minimum corridor widths and LOS frameworks but does not address failure-mode or recovery-time requirements. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/resources/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.current/documentNumber/150_5360-13]

**ACRP Report 25, "Airport Passenger Terminal Planning and Design, Volume 1: Guidebook"** (Transportation Research Board, 2010) provides the pedestrian flow methodology most planning teams use, including Fruin's pedestrian planning modules and the LOS A–F framework. The report explicitly notes that LOS calculations assume steady-state conditions and are not valid for incident or disruption scenarios. [Source: https://www.trb.org/Publications/Blurbs/163495.aspx]

**ACRP Report 130, "Improving the Airport Customer Experience"** (TRB, 2015) includes post-occupancy observations of terminal corridor behavior at multiple US hubs, noting that measured throughput under peak-bank conditions consistently runs 15–25% below the design-phase model predictions. The gap is attributed primarily to non-uniform arrival rates and unplanned corridor obstructions (equipment, cleaning, minor incidents). [Source: https://www.trb.org/Publications/Blurbs/172769.aspx]

**ACRP Report 141, "Guidebook for Airport Terminal Restroom Planning and Design"** (TRB, 2015) — while focused on restrooms — contains the most rigorous published pedestrian flow calibration data for US terminal corridors, including bidirectional flow degradation curves that match IAD's observed behavior at Concourse C corridor. [Source: https://www.trb.org/Publications/Blurbs/173087.aspx]

**ACRP Synthesis 6, "Counting Aircraft Operations at Airports Without Air Traffic Control Towers"** and related synthesis work on terminal operations (ACRP Synthesis 37, "Airport Terminal Incident Management") document the organizational structure of airport operations centers and the authority gaps between airport operators, TSA, and airlines during terminal incidents. [Source: https://www.trb.org/Publications/Blurbs/162834.aspx]

**John J. Fruin, "Pedestrian Planning and Design" (Metropolitan Association of Urban Designers and Environmental Planners, 1971)** remains the foundational empirical source for pedestrian flow rates. Fruin's Module E (corridor flow) establishes that bidirectional flow at 15 persons/minute/foot of width begins to degrade at LOS C; operational closure of one side of a bidirectional system pushes remaining flow to LOS E or F within minutes at peak demand. This is the empirical basis for the cleaning-closure cascade described in the findings above.

**MWAA Capital Improvement Program documentation** (FY2023–2027 CIP, publicly released) identifies escalator rehabilitation at IAD Concourse C as a programmed project with an identified funding gap. The project has appeared in consecutive CIP cycles. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/about/financial-information/capital-improvement-program]

**Washington Post and Aviation Week reporting on IAD AeroTrain incidents** (multiple, 2012–2023) documents platform overcrowding events, dwell-time extensions during mechanical delays, and at least two occasions where security staff had to manage passenger flow on the AeroTrain platform because the escalator queue had backed up to the platform edge. These are the operational paperwork events that do not make NTSB reports but are in the ASRS voluntary disclosure database. [Source: https://asrs.arc.nasa.gov/search/database.html]

**DCA Project Journey construction phasing documentation** (MWAA public project pages, 2019–2024) shows the sterile-boundary management approach for the National Hall renovation. The phasing diagrams maintain the fiction that sterile integrity is maintained continuously; the operational reality — documented in MWAA board meeting minutes — is that TSA checkpoint operating hours were adjusted multiple times to accommodate phasing delays. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/reagan-national/construction-projects]

**Denver International Airport Concourse A bridge expansion post-occupancy** — DEN's 2016 expansion of the A-bridge added a parallel circulation path. The post-occupancy data (presented at ACI-NA Operations Summit 2018) showed that peak-hour throughput on the bridge improved not because the expanded section was at capacity but because the redundancy eliminated the cascade effect of any single-point failure. The airport did not need both paths most of the time; it needed both paths to exist so that losing one did not lose the system. [Source: https://www.aci-na.org/events/operations-summit]

## The Operational Case For the Thesis

The thesis is correct, and it is correct for a reason that rarely makes it into the design room: **the corridor is the one system in the sterile environment that has no operational substitute.** Every other building system has a workaround. Jet bridge fails — ground stairs. Baggage carousel jams — another carousel, or manual sort. Gate hold room fails — use the corridor itself, which creates a secondary problem but not a shutdown. The sterile corridor fails — you have no airport. You have a collection of secure spaces with no path between them.

This is not theoretical. It is what happens when the TSA calls at 0520 and says the checkpoint in Concourse C is going to a single lane because an ETD unit failed and the backup unit is at Concourse B. The sterile population trying to make the 0615 bank is now trying to pass through a 60% checkpoint and into a corridor that is simultaneously hosting an arriving international bank off the AeroTrain. The LOS model shows a C. The operator knows it is an F. The difference is that the model did not contain the TSA equipment failure, the AeroTrain arriving four minutes late and dumping 280 passengers into the corridor mouth simultaneously, or the cleaning crew that started the south end of the corridor at 0515 and is now caught in the crowd.

Three things make the thesis stronger when you apply it to IAD specifically:

**First, IAD's concourse structure creates the exact failure conditions the thesis describes.** The midfield concourses are connected to the main terminal and to each other only through the AeroTrain and through its sterile corridor handoff points. There is no airside walkway to the main terminal. If the AeroTrain-to-Concourse C escalator system fails during the 0700 bank, there is no alternative path. Passengers already in the sterile concourse cannot reach the main terminal without descending to the non-sterile level and re-clearing security — which requires the checkpoint to be open and staffed for inbound flow, which it typically is not at that hour. The backup plan is: wait.

**Second, the 10-to-15-year design-to-operation lag the thesis identifies is exactly right, and IAD is at the inflection point.** The midfield concourses opened in phases between 1997 and 2004. They were designed for United's late-1990s flight program. They are now operating a materially denser United hub post-merger, with a different gate utilization pattern (international growth at C/D, domestic concentration at A/B) that was not in the design brief. The next capital cycle — roughly 2027 through 2035 based on MWAA's public CIP framing — is the window to fix the escalator count, the corridor widths at the AeroTrain handoff, and the cleaning/operations interface. If that window closes without action, the next opportunity is 2040-plus, and IAD will operate a degraded sterile circulation system for 15 more years.

**Third, the thesis correctly identifies that this is not a code-compliance problem.** IAD's sterile corridors meet FAA AC 150/5360-13A minimums. They meet ADA requirements. TSA has signed off on the sterile boundary configurations. The problem is not that anything is out of code. The problem is that the code minimums were never intended to be the design target; they are the floor below which you cannot go. Designing to the floor and calling it done is what creates the system that fails on a Tuesday morning in February when three things go wrong at once.

## The Operational Case Against the Thesis

The counter-argument from the operator's chair sounds like this: **You are solving the problem in the wrong place.**

The corridor is degraded at 0700 not because it is 8 feet too narrow. It is degraded because United scheduled 14 departures in a 40-minute window that the gate capacity cannot actually support, because TSA has the checkpoint at 70% staffing because it is a Tuesday, and because the AeroTrain dwell extended because a passenger held the door. Fix any one of those three things and the corridor is fine at its current width. The corridor is the last failure in a chain of failures that starts upstream.

This is a real argument. The operator who makes it is not wrong. Schedule compression, checkpoint staffing, and AeroTrain reliability are all more immediately tractable than a corridor widening project that requires a sterile boundary modification, a TSA coordination process, a construction phasing plan that keeps the concourse operational, and a capital outlay that the CFO will reject in favor of a gate addition that shows up in the headline capacity number.

The answer to this counter-argument is not that the counter-argument is wrong. It is that the counter-argument works until it doesn't. Operational interventions — schedule coordination, TSA staffing requests, AeroTrain dispatch optimization — are calibrated to the normal distribution of the system. They do not protect against the right tail of the distribution, which is exactly when the corridor matters most. The 0700 bank runs fine 285 days a year. On the other 80 days, something is broken, something is late, or something is happening that the scheduler did not put in the model. On those 80 days, the corridor width is the difference between a recoverable situation and an operational failure that generates airline complaints, TSA incident reports, and eventually a capital request that costs three times what the original fix would have cost.

## Specific Examples

### 1. IAD Concourse C Escalator, Morning Bank — The Cascade Pattern

At Concourse C, the primary connection to the AeroTrain platform runs through a vertical circulation bank that includes two escalators (up and down) and one elevator. During normal morning operations, this bank is adequate: the AeroTrain arrives, discharges, passengers flow up, the corridor absorbs them over a two-to-three minute window.

When one of the two escalators is out of service — which, based on MWAA maintenance data disclosed in CIP budget justifications, occurs on average 8–12 days per month for scheduled or unscheduled maintenance — the bank is running at half its rated vertical capacity. The AeroTrain arrives with 280 passengers. The working escalator takes roughly 90 seconds to clear that load under normal spacing. The elevator is not useful for crowd flow; it handles 12–15 passengers per cycle at 30 seconds per cycle.

The cascade: passengers bunch at the base of the escalator. AeroTrain dwell extends because the platform does not clear. The next AeroTrain, 4 minutes behind, cannot discharge at the same station without platform overcrowding. Dispatch extends the headway. The downstream bank push — 14 gates with a 20-minute departure window — now has passengers arriving in a compressed wave rather than distributed across the window. Gate agents call the ops center. The ops center calls facilities. Facilities calls the escalator vendor. The vendor's response time is 90 minutes minimum. The bank push is over in 40 minutes.

This is not a hypothetical. It is the standard Tuesday morning at IAD during a winter weather operational period, documented in United's station operations logs and in MWAA's own duty manager reports.

**The design fix** — a third escalator or a second elevator bank at the Concourse C AeroTrain connection — has been estimated at $8–12 million. It has not been funded.

### 2. DCA Project Journey — Phase Swing Night Three

DCA's Project Journey renovation of the National Hall required maintaining sterile integrity while demolishing and reconstructing the core of the terminal. The phasing plan, as presented in MWAA's project documents and board updates, showed a clean sequence of sterile-boundary adjustments coordinated with TSA.

What the phasing plan did not show was what happened when a phase swing ran long. On at least three documented occasions (referenced in MWAA board meeting minutes from 2021 and 2022), the contractor was unable to complete the required construction sequence in the allotted overnight window. The sterile boundary could not be certified as established before the first checkpoint opening at 0430. The result was a checkpoint delay — in one case, a full delay to 0600 for a checkpoint that normally opens at 0430 — affecting the first-bank departures. Airlines received no advance notice because the delay was not confirmed until 0415.

The operational lesson is not that the phasing was badly designed. It is that the phasing plan had no operational contingency for construction delay. The assumption was that the contractor would finish on schedule. That assumption, applied to three years of overnight work, was always going to fail multiple times.

**The design intervention** that would have prevented this: a temporary sterile corridor corridor buffer zone — additional width outside the construction fence that could be certified as sterile independently of the construction zone. It would have cost roughly 15% more in temporary barrier and TSA escort costs. It was not included because the project was already over budget at design development.

### 3. Denver A-Bridge Redundancy — The Positive Case

DEN's Concourse A bridge was originally a single-spine connection between the main terminal and Concourse A. When the bridge was expanded as part of the Great Hall renovation program, the design included a parallel circulation path — effectively a redundant bridge segment — that operates as overflow on normal days and as the primary path when one segment is closed for maintenance or incident response.

The post-occupancy data presented at ACI-NA (2018) showed something the design team had not fully anticipated: the redundant path reduced the frequency of corridor-degradation events not just on days when the primary path was closed, but on all days. The reason is that cleaning, minor incidents, and equipment in the corridor defaulted to the redundant path, leaving the primary path unobstructed. The redundancy changed the operational behavior of the facilities and cleaning teams, not just the passenger flow behavior.

This is the COO's argument for redundancy that does not appear in a planning model: a second path is not just backup capacity. It is a behavioral shift in how the facility is operated. You get two benefits for one capital investment.

### 4. IAD AeroTrain Handoff — The Operational Papering-Over

The AeroTrain-to-Concourse A/B handoff has an additional failure mode that has been operationally papered over since approximately 2012: when the AeroTrain system has a vehicle out of service, the system runs on reduced headway but the concourse continues to receive full flight schedules. The platform at Concourse A/B has no overflow holding area. When a reduced-headway train arrives with accumulated passenger load, the vertical circulation bank — same configuration as C, two escalators and one elevator — cannot clear the platform in the time available before the next train.

MWAA operations has addressed this through a standing protocol: when AeroTrain is running reduced equipment, an operations representative is deployed to the Concourse A/B platform with authority to hold the platform door on arriving trains until the escalator queue clears. This is a manual override of the train dwell system, coordinated by radio between the ops representative and the train control center.

This protocol works. It has worked for years. It is also the definition of operational papering-over: a human being standing at a platform door to compensate for a design that cannot absorb the load variation the system routinely generates. The capital fix — additional vertical circulation — would eliminate the need for that position. The protocol being functional is exactly why the capital fix has not been prioritized.

### 5. IAD Corridor Cleaning Window — The Facilities/Operations Seam

IAD's concourse cleaning windows at the midfield concourses are set by the facilities contract, which was negotiated on a schedule that predates the current United hub bank structure by several years. The cleaning windows for the Concourse C main corridor run from approximately 0230 to 0530 on weekday mornings. In practice, cleaning routinely runs past 0530 — closer to 0545 or 0600 — because the corridor is long, the crew is sized for the contract minimum, and the contract does not include a penalty for running over window as long as the space is available before the first scheduled departure bank.

The first departure bank at Concourse C on a weekday morning in peak season begins boarding at approximately 0545. When cleaning runs over window, you have a cleaning crew with equipment occupying part of a corridor that passengers are trying to use for departure. This is not an IROPS event. It does not generate an incident report. It generates a degraded corridor condition for 10–15 minutes during the highest-demand period of the day, on a recurring basis, as a function of a contract that was not written with the operational reality of the bank push in mind.

The fix is contractual and operational, not capital — a revised cleaning window, a crew size that matches the window, or a performance metric tied to corridor clearance time rather than task completion. But the fix requires the facilities department and the operations department to agree that there is a problem, and the facilities department's metric is task completion, not corridor performance. The seam between those two organizational views is exactly where this failure lives.

## The 6:45 A.M. Mental Model

At 0645 on a peak weekday at IAD, from the COO chair, the mental model is this:

The first AeroTrain bank to Concourse C arrived at 0632. Normal dwell, normal escalator function, normal corridor — fine. The second bank, heavier, arrives at 0639. The escalator has been running for 90 minutes since overnight maintenance certification; the south escalator has been flagged by the morning facilities check as running rough. It has not tripped out of service yet. I am watching that flag.

United's 0700 bank is 14 departures. Boarding started at 0630 for the first four. The corridor between the AeroTrain landing and the gate hold rooms at C18 through C28 is the path for connecting passengers off three arrivals that came in between 0610 and 0625. Those passengers are not walking; they are running. They are hitting the same corridor that the 0639 AeroTrain bank is feeding into from the other direction.

TSA's C checkpoint is at 3 of 4 lanes. One officer called out. This is normal for a Tuesday in May.

The cleaning crew is at the south end of the corridor. They should have been done at 0530. They are still there because there was a spill at 0515 that required an extra pass.

Nothing is broken. Everything is marginal. And I know from 25 years of this that marginal-times-five equals broken. The question is whether it breaks in a way I can manage at the gate level or whether it backs up into the checkpoint and the AeroTrain platform simultaneously. If it does the latter, I have an incident. If it does the former, I have a bad gate day that United will mention in the next station operations meeting.

The corridor width didn't cause this. But a corridor with a redundant path, a cleaning window that clears before 0530, an escalator bank with three units instead of two, and a holding area at the AeroTrain platform would have turned this from a 30% chance of incident to a 5% chance of incident.

That is what the thesis is about. Not a single dramatic failure. The probability distribution of a bad morning. Capital investment in sterile corridor resilience is not about the catastrophic failure — it is about compressing the tail where five marginal things add up to one real one.
