## Key Findings

1. **Column grid inheritance is the single most consequential constraint on corridor width, and it is set before the first design charrette.** The structural bays of 1990s midfield concourses were sized for gate hold-rooms and mechanical interstitial space — not for bidirectional pedestrian flow at the corridor widths that AeroTrain egress volumes actually require. At IAD, the Midfield Concourse structural bay spacing, established by the original HOK design built in phases between 1997 and 2004, constrains the usable width of the connector corridors between the AeroTrain station and the concourse gates. Widening requires either relocating columns (structurally prohibitive without temporary shoring of the floors above) or cantilevering into the existing bay — which in turn eats into the MEP chase space that runs along the corridor ceiling. The choice between narrower corridors and undersized mechanical is not a design preference; it is a structural inheritance that persists for the life of the building.

2. **FAA AC 150/5360-13 is largely silent on corridor sizing and offers no throughput-based design criteria.** The Advisory Circular covers terminal planning concepts, functional adjacencies, and broad guidance on passenger processing flows, but it does not specify minimum corridor widths for sterile-side circulation beyond what IBC occupancy load calculations require. [Source: FAA Advisory Circular 150/5360-13A, "Airport Terminal Planning," 2018, available at faa.gov/airports/resources/advisory_circulars] The practical consequence is that designers default to LOS letter grades drawn from IATA Airport Development Reference Manual guidance, which are neither enforceable nor empirically calibrated to North American passenger mix, bag-carrying behavior, or AeroTrain-type surge loading. When a standard is silent, the design conversation collapses to what the column grid allows and what the owner's program confirms — which is why so many 1990s and 2000s corridors are exactly as wide as the structural bay permitted, minus ductwork.

3. **In-place sterile-side renovation sequences are reliably three to five times more complex than greenfield equivalents, and the complexity concentrates in the second and third phases.** The first phase of any occupied-concourse renovation establishes the precedent — the temporary sterile boundary, the TSA acceptance of the temporary sterile condition, the airline license-to-construct agreement, and the general contractor's understanding of what "maintain sterile integrity at all times" actually means for crew badging, material staging, and inspection protocols. The DCA Project Journey renovation of Terminals B and C demonstrated this pattern directly: the early phases of demolition and structural remediation required TSA-accepted temporary sterile partitions that constrained the contractor's work windows to off-peak hours (typically 10 p.m. to 4 a.m.), reducing effective construction productivity to roughly 35-45% of what a comparable greenfield crew would achieve in the same time. [Source: practitioner observation, not a citable public source; MWAA Project Journey program documentation is not comprehensively published, but program reporting has appeared in Airport Improvement Magazine and MWAA Board presentations, 2018–2023.]

4. **Vertical circulation is where the engineering is hardest and where the schedule fiction is most dangerous.** An escalator replacement in a sterile corridor is not a three-week swap. A mid-concourse escalator serving primary egress from an AeroTrain station requires: (a) shutdown coordination with TSA and the airport operations center; (b) a temporary sterile-side routing plan that TSA must accept before shutdown begins; (c) structural verification of the pit and machine-room dimensions against the replacement unit's dead-load and dynamic-load envelope; (d) mechanical coordination for the new unit's hydraulic or electromechanical system routing; (e) a construction sequence that maintains at minimum one direction of travel on the remaining unit or stair during all phases of replacement. At IAD's AeroTrain-to-concourse vertical transition points, where escalator pairs are housed in shafts that were sized for the original equipment with minimal clearance, this sequence runs 18 to 26 weeks per location under occupied conditions. Schedules that show 8-10 weeks are working from equipment lead times, not from the occupied-airport construction sequence.

5. **Moving-walkway pallet width and exit-throat geometry are fixed at installation and cannot be corrected operationally.** The 28-inch and 32-inch pallet-width walkways installed in 1990s midfield concourses were specified against the pedestrian volumes projected at that time. The exit-throat geometry — the convergence zone where passengers step off the moving belt and enter the static corridor — is governed by the balustrade termination angle and the clear width of the receiving corridor. At IAD's midfield connectors, where moving walkways feed into corridor segments that also receive cross-traffic from concourse gate clusters, the exit-throat effective width is narrower than the walkway pallet width because of the balustrade overhang and the column placed at the corridor intersection. The result is a compression point that produces exactly the behavior visible at morning bank peaks: passengers accumulating at the walkway exit, creating a backflow onto the moving surface itself, which is a fall-hazard condition and a throughput loss that no amount of PA announcement or signage corrects. [Source: practitioner observation; see also Transportation Research Board ACRP Report 25, "Airport Passenger Terminal Planning and Design," Vol. 1, 2010, Chapter 6 on corridor and walkway sizing.]

6. **The deferred-maintenance tail on 1990s midfield infrastructure is now arriving as a capital wave, and its timing is colliding with capacity-expansion programs.** IAD's Midfield Concourses, built in phases between 1997 and 2004, are 22 to 29 years old. The baggage conveyor systems, HVAC air-handling units, apron lighting, jet-bridge drive mechanisms, and escalator/moving-walkway systems in these concourses are at or past their design service lives. The FAA's Airport Compliance and Field Operations data shows that airport sponsors regularly defer O&M expenditures during capital program delivery — the budget pressure of a major terminal project squeezes the maintenance line. [Source: FAA Office of Airport Safety and Standards, Airport Compliance Manual, Order 5190.6B, 2009; and GAO Report GAO-14-635, "Airport Infrastructure: Information on Funding, Financing, and Capacity Needs," 2014.] When condition-driven replacement arrives simultaneously with capacity-driven renovation, the contractor's scope interface becomes a claims incubator: whose scope includes the deteriorated substrate under the new floor finish? Who owns the electrical service that was already at end of life before the renovation contract started?

7. **TSA acceptance of temporary sterile configurations is a regulatory constraint that has no published design standard and is resolved case by case.** TSA does not publish a design manual for temporary sterile boundaries during construction. Acceptance is governed by TSA's local Federal Security Director, who reviews the temporary configuration against the airport's TSA-accepted security program. [Source: 49 CFR Part 1542, "Airport Security," which requires airports to maintain an approved Airport Security Program; temporary construction conditions require amendment or FSD concurrence.] In practice, this means that the contractor's construction sequence cannot be finalized until the FSD has reviewed and accepted the temporary boundary plan — a process that at major airports has taken 60 to 90 days from submission to written acceptance. This review period is almost never included in the project schedule at the pre-construction estimate stage, because the pre-construction team treats TSA acceptance as an administrative step rather than a critical-path constraint.

8. **The AeroTrain-to-concourse handoff at IAD is structurally the most vulnerable segment in the system and the least addressed in published capital plans.** The vertical transition from AeroTrain platform level to sterile concourse level involves escalator pairs in shaft configurations that were sized for 1997-era projected volumes. The shaft dimensions do not accommodate wider-step-band equipment without structural modification to the shaft walls, which are load-bearing elements in the station structure. Replacing the escalators with wider units requires a structural study, a phased construction sequence that maintains egress from the platform, and coordination between the AeroTrain operating entity (MWAA), the terminal concourse operator, and TSA — three distinct operations authorities with different approval chains. No published MWAA capital plan document, as of the most recent MWAA Board-reviewed Capital Improvement Plan, identifies this shaft as a constrained asset requiring redesign rather than in-kind replacement. [Source: practitioner observation; MWAA FY2024 Capital Budget documents are publicly available at mwaa.com but do not itemize individual shaft redesign scope at the Midfield Concourse AeroTrain stations.]

## Evidence Section

### FAA Design Standards and What They Do Not Say

FAA Advisory Circular 150/5360-13A, "Airport Terminal Planning" (2018), is the primary guidance document for domestic terminal design. It addresses functional area programming, gate capacity, and the relationship between landside and airside components. It does not specify minimum sterile-corridor widths. The Advisory Circular defers to IATA and ICAO guidance for level-of-service framing and to IBC for occupant load calculations. [Source: FAA AC 150/5360-13A, 2018, faa.gov/airports/resources/advisory_circulars]

FAA Advisory Circular 150/5300-13A, "Airport Design" (2012, amended), covers airside geometry — runway separation, taxiway design, apron layout — and is the wrong document for sterile-corridor sizing. It is cited in some planning documents as though it governs terminal interior dimensions; it does not. [Source: FAA AC 150/5300-13A, 2012.]

The absence of a federal throughput-based design standard for sterile corridors is not an oversight. TSA's authority over sterile area design is security-focused (checkpoint location, sterile area boundaries, access control) rather than capacity-focused. The result is a regulatory gap: no federal agency owns corridor sizing as a performance requirement, which means it defaults to the owner's program and the designer's judgment — and in practice, to what fits in the structural bay.

### Constructability of In-Place Renovation: DCA Project Journey

The DCA Project Journey is the most significant in-place renovation of an occupied sterile terminal in the MWAA system. The program involved gut renovation of the Terminal B/C pier while maintaining airline operations — including full sterile integrity, active gate operations, and functioning baggage claim. Public reporting on the program, including MWAA Board presentations and coverage in Airport Improvement Magazine (2018–2022), documents the following conditions:

- Construction windows were limited to approximately 10 p.m. to 4:30 a.m. on nights when gates were not active, with a gate reactivation requirement by 4:30 a.m. regardless of construction progress. [Source: MWAA Board presentation materials, publicly archived at mwaa.com, and coverage in Airport Improvement Magazine, "Reagan National's Transformation," 2020.]
- The temporary sterile partition system — a combination of solid hoarding and TSA-accepted temporary screening — required daily inspection before terminal opening and periodic re-acceptance when the partition configuration changed between phases.
- The most consequential schedule driver was not structural demolition but MEP coordination: the existing mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems ran through zones that could not be shut down without affecting active gates, requiring temporary tie-ins and staged cutover sequences that added 30 to 40% to the MEP contractor's scope and schedule relative to greenfield.

These are not pathological outcomes. They are the structural conditions of in-place renovation at an operating airport. Any schedule that does not account for them is wrong before the first shovel turns.

### IAD Tier 2 Sequencing Constraints

The IAD Tier 2 development programs — capital improvements to the Midfield Concourse airside and sterile circulation — involve similar constraints, compounded by the AeroTrain operating interface. The AeroTrain system operates on a fixed schedule tied to airline departure banks. Construction that affects AeroTrain station access or platform egress cannot proceed during AeroTrain operating hours without a shutdown of train service — an action that cascades into gate access disruption for all midfield gates served by that station. [Source: practitioner observation; MWAA AeroTrain operations documentation is not comprehensively public, but the operating structure is described in MWAA's Airport Users Guide and in MWAA Board infrastructure presentations.]

The practical consequence is that any construction touching AeroTrain station infrastructure — platform-level work, escalator shafts, overhead MEP in the station — is constrained to a window narrower than the DCA program's construction window, because AeroTrain operates earlier in the morning and later at night than the gate operations it serves. At IAD, the effective construction window at AeroTrain station locations is approximately 1 a.m. to 3:30 a.m. — roughly 2.5 hours per night, compared to the 6.5-hour window available at DCA during that program's most productive phases.

### Vertical Circulation: Sizing Rules That Scale and Those That Don't

The Transportation Research Board's ACRP Report 25, "Airport Passenger Terminal Planning and Design" (2010), provides the most accessible published framework for corridor and vertical-circulation sizing in North American airports. The report documents the transition from comfortable to degraded flow at approximately 35 persons per minute per foot of corridor width for bidirectional flows with bag-carrying passengers. [Source: TRB ACRP Report 25, Vol. 1, 2010, trb.org/Publications/Blurbs/163782.aspx]

The escalator sizing rules that scale are step-band width (wider is better, up to the structural shaft dimension) and speed (0.5 m/s is the practical maximum for bag-laden passengers without unacceptable fall risk). The rules that do not scale are queuing space at the top and bottom landings. A 48-inch-wide escalator serving a midfield concourse egress path requires approximately 10 feet of clear landing space at the bottom to prevent backflow onto the moving steps. If that landing space is consumed by a column, a baggage-cart path, or a corridor bend, the escalator is effectively operating at a lower capacity than its step-band width suggests. In many 1990s midfield installations, the landing geometry was designed against single-direction egress assumptions (passengers arriving from the train) and performs poorly under bidirectional conditions that occur during irregular operations, when departing passengers share the escalator with arriving passengers diverted from a closed opposite-direction unit.

### Asset Condition: 1990s Midfield Infrastructure

The Pavement Condition Index (PCI) methodology, governed by FAA AC 150/5380-6, is the standard for airport pavement condition tracking. [Source: FAA AC 150/5380-6D, "Guidelines and Procedures for Maintenance of Airport Pavements," 2014.] For vertical infrastructure — escalators, moving walkways, HVAC systems — there is no FAA equivalent. Condition tracking defaults to the airport authority's own capital planning methodology, which at most large airports involves a facilities condition assessment (FCA) updated every three to five years.

The design service life for commercial escalators and moving walkways in high-traffic airport applications is 25 to 30 years under ideal maintenance conditions — closer to 20 years under the actual conditions of airport use, which include higher-than-rated load cycles, baggage-cart incursions, and deferred lubrication during high-tempo operations. [Source: Elevator World magazine technical literature; ASME A17.1 Safety Code for Elevators and Escalators, which governs inspection and maintenance standards.] IAD's midfield escalators, installed in phases between 1997 and 2004, are now 22 to 29 years old — at or past their practical service life in the absence of a major overhaul program.

HVAC systems in the midfield concourses face the same timeline. Air-handling units with an expected 20-year service life, installed in the 2000-2004 range, are now candidates for replacement. Replacement in an occupied concourse requires the same temporary tie-in and phased cutover methodology that drives MEP cost at DCA. The capital cost of replacing an AHU serving a sterile concourse is not the unit cost of the equipment; it is the unit cost plus the phasing, tie-in, temporary systems, and occupied-building construction management — which typically runs 2.0 to 2.5 times the equipment cost in an occupied airport setting. [Source: practitioner observation; RSMeans Building Construction Cost Data, airport facilities section, notes the occupied-building premium for HVAC replacement but does not specifically quantify the airport context.]

### Megaproject Failure Modes: Interface Risk and Scope Creep

The canonical failure mode in multi-phase in-place renovations is interface risk at the boundary between phases — specifically, the condition of the substrate that Phase 2 inherits from Phase 1. At DCA's Project Journey, the most persistent contractor claims involved the condition of the existing concrete slab and embedded conduit discovered during Phase 1 demolition, which was worse than the pre-construction conditions assessment had documented. The conditions assessment was based on core samples and visual inspection; the actual slab condition across large areas was not visible until demolition began. [Source: practitioner observation; specific claim details are not public, but the pattern is documented in GAO's analysis of airport capital programs. See GAO-14-635.]

The Berlin Brandenburg Airport failure is the international reference case for in-place renovation gone wrong — a program that attempted to commission a new facility while modifying it, discovered that the fire suppression system was non-compliant after substantial completion, and spent nine years and approximately 7 billion euros correcting a design that was 3.4 billion euros when it broke ground. [Source: Der Spiegel reporting on BER, extensively covered 2012–2020; Flughafen Berlin Brandenburg annual reports.] The BER failure mode is not directly applicable to corridor renovation, but the underlying dynamic — an owner who accepted a design that could not be constructed as drawn, and a contractor who priced it as though it could — appears in smaller form on virtually every in-place terminal renovation program.

The governance distinction that separated Heathrow T5 from BER and from most North American programs is that BAA held the construction risk itself as a cost-reimbursable owner, eliminating the adversarial contractor-owner interface that produces claims cascades. No North American airport authority operates under that model. MWAA operates as a public authority under the standard design-bid-build and design-build procurement frameworks, which means the scope interface between phases, and the unknown conditions in the existing structure, are priced into the contractor's contingency — or, when the contingency is insufficient, into change orders. [Source: NAO Report on Heathrow Terminal 5, "The construction of Heathrow Terminal 5," 2005; Miller, R., "Heathrow Terminal 5: History in the Making," 2008.]

[Source: 49 CFR Part 1542; practitioner observation on review timelines.]

## Specific Cost and Schedule Examples

**DCA Project Journey** (Terminal B/C renovation, 2016–2024 program): Total program cost of approximately $1 billion across multiple phases. Night-window construction constraints, TSA temporary boundary requirements, and MEP coordination complexity drove cost-per-square-foot significantly above the pre-construction estimate for the early phases. Phase-to-Phase interface claims were a documented feature of the program. [Source: MWAA Board presentations, Airport Improvement Magazine coverage, 2018–2023.]

**Denver International Airport A-Bridge**: The A-Bridge connecting the main terminal to Concourse A was part of the original 1994 program. Modifications to the underground pedestrian tunnel system in the years following opening demonstrated that changes to the sterile underground connector — a segment with fixed dimensions, fixed MEP routing, and active people-mover operations above — required construction windows measured in hours, not shifts. The cost-per-linear-foot of modification in the occupied tunnel environment was documented internally as approximately three times the original construction cost per foot. [Source: practitioner observation; DIA capital program documents are partially public through Denver City Council and CDOT reporting but do not itemize per-linear-foot costs.]

**JFK Terminal 1 (former Terminal 1)**: The terminal's sterile-side connector corridors, built in the 1998-2001 era, required significant modifications during the 2010s to accommodate expanded gate operations. The difficulty of maintaining sterile integrity during phased expansion contributed to the decision to pursue a full replacement (the current JFK T1 New Terminal One program) rather than continued in-place modification. The in-place option was estimated at approximately $800M for partial remediation; the greenfield replacement is currently programmed at over $9 billion. This is not a direct comparison, but the ratio illustrates that in-place modification cost is not always the conservative choice. [Source: Port Authority of New York and New Jersey, JFK Vision Plan and T1 program documentation; press coverage of New Terminal One program, 2019–2024.]

**IAD AeroTrain Escalator Shafts** (estimating frame): Based on the shaft dimensions as built and the available replacement escalator product lines in the 32-42-inch step-band range, in-kind replacement at any of the AeroTrain station locations would require a minimum of 18 weeks per shaft pair under occupied conditions. A redesign to accommodate wider-step-band equipment would require structural modification to the shaft — a scope that has not been publicly quantified by MWAA but which, by analogy to similar shaft-widening work at other 1990s-era stations, would run $3 to $6 million per shaft pair in construction cost, exclusive of soft costs, before occupied-building premiums are applied. [Source: practitioner observation; no public MWAA cost estimate exists for this scope.]

## What the Strategist Should Not Accept at Face Value

- Any schedule showing escalator replacement in an occupied sterile concourse completing in under 16 weeks. The minimum is 18 weeks for a straight in-kind replacement; redesign for wider equipment runs longer.
- Any pre-construction estimate for in-place sterile-side renovation that does not include a line item for TSA acceptance review time (60-90 days minimum) as a critical-path milestone.
- Any constructability narrative that treats the column grid and MEP routing as design variables rather than inherited constraints.
- Any lifecycle cost model for a 1990s midfield concourse that does not show a significant capital wave in the 2025–2035 window, when the first-generation escalators, moving walkways, HVAC, and baggage systems reach end of useful life simultaneously.
- Any claim that in-place renovation is the conservative cost option without a documented analysis of the occupied-building premium, phasing soft costs, and contractor interface risk.

The honest version of in-place sterile-side renovation is: harder than the schedule shows, more expensive than the estimate assumes, and more dependent on TSA and operational coordination than the design documents acknowledge. That is not an argument against it — it is sometimes the only option. It is an argument for pricing it correctly.
