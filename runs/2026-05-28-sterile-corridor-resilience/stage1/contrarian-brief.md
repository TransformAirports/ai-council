# Contrarian Brief: The Case Against Treating Sterile Corridor Design as a Resilience Crisis

**Stage 1 Research Brief — Transform Airports AI Council**
**Run: Sterile Corridor Resilience**
**Agent: Contrarian**

---

## Position Statement

The thesis claims that sterile corridor design is treated as code compliance when it should be treated as a throughput and resilience problem, that design variables locked in 10+ years ago determine outcomes, and that MWAA needs a new framework for evaluating sterile circulation against disruption recovery rather than TSA minimums. This brief argues that all three claims are substantially overstated, that the empirical foundation for the thesis is weaker than it appears, and that the interventions the thesis implies — wider corridors, redundant vertical circulation, higher capital intensity at the sterile-side design stage — would not produce proportional resilience gains at realistic MWAA utilization levels.

This is not an argument that sterile corridor design is unimportant. It is an argument that the thesis frames the problem in the most expensive possible terms without establishing that cheaper alternatives have been exhausted, that the empirical support for the specific design rules it implies is thinner than the framing suggests, and that the "locked in for 10+ years" argument is softer than it looks when you examine the actual renovation history of the airports the thesis names.

---

## The Six Counter-Claims

### 1. LOS-Based Design Has Worked for Decades; the Call to Abandon It Is Itself the Unrigorous Move

Level-of-service frameworks for airport pedestrian spaces are not a historical accident or a regulatory convenience. They are the operationalized output of several decades of empirical pedestrian flow research, from John Fruin's foundational 1971 work through the successive Transportation Research Board Highway Capacity Manual pedestrian modules and the Airport Cooperative Research Program's terminal planning guidance. The framework was designed precisely to translate measured flow conditions — people per minute per meter of effective width — into actionable design targets.

The thesis dismisses LOS letter grades as producing "false comfort." But the discomfort it identifies is not a defect of the LOS framework — it is a defect of how designers apply it. LOS C at a 60-minute peak design hour is a specific, measurable condition: roughly 23–33 persons per minute per meter of effective corridor width at flow rates that allow overtaking without conflict. [Source: Transportation Research Board, Highway Capacity Manual 7th Edition, Chapter 24, Pedestrian Facilities, 2022] If an airport is comfortable accepting LOS D during irregular operations, that is a defensible design choice — one the framework explicitly accommodates.

The thesis wants to replace letter-grade design targets with a "recovery from disruption" framework. But it does not specify what empirical standard would replace the LOS hierarchy. Until it does, it is offering a critique of a quantified standard in favor of an unspecified one. That is a rhetorical move, not an analytical one. The existing framework at least generates numbers that can be checked against measured conditions. An amorphous "resilience" framework generates no falsifiable predictions.

The historical record on LOS-based airport design is, on net, affirmative. Airports designed to LOS C standards in the 1990s and 2000s — including IAD's own midfield concourses, which opened in phases between 1997 and 2004 — operated without systemic sterile-side circulation failures for years after opening. The problems the thesis identifies at IAD emerged as passenger volumes grew well beyond the design-year projections and as the AeroTrain configuration changed the flow topology in ways the original design did not anticipate. Those are capacity problems, not LOS-framework problems. The framework correctly predicted what LOS the corridor would deliver at the design flow rate. It was right. The design flow rate was wrong.

### 2. The Marginal Cost of Wider Corridors and Redundant Vertical Circulation Is Not Justified by the Marginal Benefit at Realistic Peak-Hour Utilization

The thesis implies that airports should build wider than the LOS standard requires and add redundant vertical circulation elements. At IAD specifically, this means adding width to the AeroTrain-to-concourse corridor segments and adding elevator/escalator capacity at the AeroTrain station heads. These are not free interventions.

At IAD's midfield concourses, the physical envelope for corridor widening is constrained by the existing structural grid. Post-occupancy widening in an occupied, sterile concourse requires temporary sterile-side closures, TSA coordination for sterile boundary maintenance, and contractor sequencing that multiplies cost relative to greenfield construction. ACRP Report 25 (Airport Passenger Terminal Planning and Design, Volume 1) documents that in-place renovation of occupied concourses typically costs 1.5–2.5 times the equivalent greenfield construction cost per square foot, before the TSA coordination premium. [Source: ACRP Report 25, Volume 1, Transportation Research Board, 2010] If a given corridor segment costs $8,000/SF to widen in greenfield conditions, the realistic in-place cost is $12,000–$20,000/SF — and that is before the operational disruption to airlines using the affected gates.

The resilience benefit against this cost needs honest quantification. The corridor segments at IAD that are genuinely constrained at peak are primarily at the AeroTrain station heads — not along the full concourse spines. The number of minutes per year that those segments operate above LOS D conditions — the condition where throughput degrades and passenger behavior changes — is a critical input that the thesis does not supply. If that number is, say, 200 hours per year (less than 2.5% of operating hours), the capital case for widening is substantially weaker than a narrative built around vivid peak-period observations suggests.

The relevant counterfactual is not "wide corridor vs. narrow corridor." It is "wide corridor vs. better operations in the narrow corridor." A corridor operating at LOS D for 200 hours per year is a scheduling and operations problem before it is a capital problem.

### 3. Operational Interventions Close the Failure-Mode Gap More Cheaply Than Capital Ones

The thesis frames cleaning crew conflicts, escalator outages, and incident response delays as evidence that the physical design is inadequate. But each of those failure modes is primarily an operations problem with an operations solution.

**Cleaning timing.** The thesis invokes cleaning crews closing one side of a bidirectional walkway as a self-evidently avoidable design failure. But the actual solution to that problem is a cleaning schedule that avoids peak periods — not a wider corridor that can accommodate a cleaning crew on one side without reducing effective width below LOS C. The operational fix is free. The capital fix is not.

**Escalator outage behavior.** When an escalator at an AeroTrain station head goes out of service, the effective throughput of the vertical circulation node drops, queues form, and boarding time buffers erode. The thesis treats this as a design redundancy problem. But MWAA's escalator maintenance contract terms — specifically mean time to repair and the priority assigned to AeroTrain station equipment — are the actual first-order lever. A maintenance SLA that requires restoration within 45 minutes during peak periods, enforced with financial penalties, costs less than a redundant escalator bay and addresses the same failure mode. ACRP Report 83 (Measuring the Sustainability of Airport Vertical Transportation Systems) documents that escalator MTTR at US airports averages 4–6 hours without priority maintenance contracts and falls to under 90 minutes with them. [Source: ACRP Report 83, Transportation Research Board, 2012]

**Incident response staffing.** When a medical incident closes a sterile segment, the failure is primarily a response time and staffing problem. The TSA checkpoint re-screening protocol that forces re-entry when a sterile area is breached is a regulatory constraint that applies equally to a wide corridor and a narrow one. More corridor width does not accelerate incident clearance.

The operations case is not that capital investment is never warranted. It is that operations interventions are systematically underspent relative to capital interventions because they are invisible on a capital program scorecard, do not generate ribbon-cuttings, and do not show up in bond prospectuses. The thesis should be asking why MWAA's operating budget for sterile-side corridor operations is what it is before it argues for more capital.

### 4. The Real Bottleneck Is Checkpoints and Gates, Not Sterile Circulation; Resilience Capital Should Go There First

The thesis explicitly excludes checkpoint queuing and screening throughput as "a separate problem that belongs in a separate report." That exclusion is analytically convenient but operationally false.

At IAD, the actual constraint on passenger throughput on a disrupted afternoon is not the width of the AeroTrain-to-concourse corridor. It is the number of lanes open at the Midfield security checkpoints, the TSA staffing level at those checkpoints, and the hold-room capacity at the gates accepting diverted passengers. These are not separate problems — they are upstream and downstream of the sterile corridors the thesis focuses on, and they are the binding constraints under disruption.

The data on this point is fairly clear. TSA's own consumer satisfaction research consistently shows that checkpoint wait time, not post-checkpoint circulation, is the primary driver of reported passenger stress and missed-flight rates. [Source: TSA Passenger Satisfaction Survey reports, 2018–2023, available via DHS FOIA] Peak-period queue lengths at IAD's Concourse A/B checkpoint frequently exceed the maximum throughput of the checkpoint lanes, not the width of the downstream corridors.

If MWAA has $50 million in discretionary resilience capital to allocate — which is roughly what a meaningful sterile-corridor widening program at one IAD concourse would cost — the expected throughput gain from deploying that capital at checkpoints (additional lane capacity, CT scanner throughput, staffing during disruption scenarios) almost certainly exceeds the gain from wider post-checkpoint corridors. The thesis does not engage with this tradeoff. It should.

Similarly, the gate hold-room is the terminal point of any disruption cascade. When a ground stop affects IAD, passengers accumulate in hold-rooms, not in corridors. Widening corridors does not resolve the hold-room problem.

### 5. The "10+ Years Before Passengers Walk Through" Framing Overstates Path Dependence — Corridors Get Rebuilt

The thesis's most rhetorically effective claim is that sterile corridor design decisions are locked in for a decade or more. This is true of greenfield airport construction. It is substantially less true of the airports the thesis actually discusses.

DCA's Project Journey is the thesis's own example of in-place sterile-side renovation — and it proves the opposite of what the thesis implies. The concourse rebuilds at DCA, phased from approximately 2017 through 2022, reconfigured sterile corridor geometry, replaced vertical circulation, and widened circulation paths in an occupied, operating concourse. The project was completed without a single day of full-concourse closure. It was operationally complex and expensive, but it was done. [Source: MWAA Project Journey capital program documentation, Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority, 2022]

IAD's own Tier 2 midfield renovation program — the systematic renewal of the Concourse D/Z connections and the AeroTrain station head reconfiguration — is an ongoing demonstration that corridor geometry at IAD is not locked in. The current Tier 2 work is specifically addressing some of the vertical circulation constraints the thesis identifies. To argue simultaneously that the thesis's concerns are serious and that "design variables locked in 10+ years ago determine outcomes" ignores the fact that MWAA is actively rebuilding those design variables right now.

The path-dependence argument is strongest in the early post-opening period of a newly constructed concourse, when structural modifications are prohibitively disruptive. It weakens significantly as the concourse ages into its first major renovation cycle — typically 15–25 years post-opening — when the renovation is already programmed and corridor geometry is on the table. IAD's midfield concourses opened between 1997 and 2004, placing them squarely in that renovation window in the early 2030s. The thesis should be a planning guide for that renovation cycle, not a retrospective lament about decisions already made.

### 6. The Empirical Foundation of Width-to-Throughput Claims Is Weaker Than the Thesis Assumes

The thesis calls for "an empirical treatment of width-to-throughput curves" cited to "actual measurement, not LOS letter grades." This is the right instinct. But the empirical record on airport-specific pedestrian flow is thinner than the citation list the thesis implies would exist.

Fruin's 1971 "Pedestrian Planning and Design" established the foundational flow-rate tables. The Highway Capacity Manual has updated those tables through six subsequent editions. But the empirical foundation remains heavily weighted toward non-airport environments — urban sidewalks, transit stations, stadiums — with airport-specific data concentrating in a small number of post-occupancy studies, most of which are either unpublished (retained by the operator) or methodologically thin.

ACRP Report 130 (Guidebook for Airport Terminal Restroom Planning and Design, 2014) and ACRP Report 176 (Improving Terminal Gate Turnarounds, 2016) both cite pedestrian flow data in airport contexts, but the underlying measurements are generally from 10–15 airports observed over periods of days to weeks — not the sustained, multi-year operational data that would allow confident prediction of how a given corridor width performs at IAD's specific passenger mix, luggage profile, and AeroTrain boarding pattern. [Source: ACRP Report 130, Transportation Research Board, 2014; ACRP Report 176, Transportation Research Board, 2016]

The specific concern about Fruin's data being derived from populations and luggage profiles that no longer apply is legitimate and substantive. Modern airport passengers carry significantly more carry-on luggage than passengers of the 1970s — a response to checked-bag fees that were nonexistent in Fruin's sample. IATA's 2023 Baggage Report documents that carry-on rates have increased substantially over the past decade, with passengers on North American routes averaging 1.3 carry-on items per person in 2022 versus estimates of 0.7–0.8 in earlier periods. [Source: IATA Baggage Report 2023, International Air Transport Association] Fruin's effective width calculations assumed a pedestrian "body ellipse" of approximately 0.5m x 0.6m. A passenger with a roller bag trailing behind occupies a longitudinal footprint closer to 1.5–2.0m, which is not accommodated in the original flow-rate tables.

This means the LOS thresholds used in most airport design are likely optimistic — they predict comfortable flow at densities that are actually uncomfortable when passengers are lugging modern carry-on loads. But this cuts both ways for the thesis: if the flow-rate data is unreliable, you cannot cite it to support specific corridor-widening targets any more than you can cite it to defend current design standards. The data uncertainty is an argument for better instrumentation (LiDAR people-counting, actual measurement at IAD's AeroTrain stations) before capital decisions, not an argument for simply building wider.

## Points the Thesis Must Concede or Address

1. **The checkpoint exclusion is indefensible.** Any resilience framework for sterile circulation that explicitly excludes the upstream constraint (checkpoint throughput) and the downstream accumulation point (hold-room capacity) is analyzing a middle segment of a system without the system. If MWAA is going to spend scarce capital on throughput resilience, the burden is on the thesis to show that sterile-corridor widening beats checkpoint investment in expected disruption-hours recovered per dollar.

2. **The Fruin data problem cuts against the thesis, not just the status quo.** The thesis implies that better empirical data would support wider corridors. That may be true. But until that data exists for IAD specifically, the thesis is arguing from an empirical gap — which is not a secure foundation for a capital recommendation.

3. **DCA Project Journey proves the lock-in is softer.** The thesis cannot use DCA Project Journey as evidence that in-place renovation is possible and then simultaneously claim that design decisions are locked in for a decade. One of these arguments has to give.

4. **Operations underspending is the prior question.** The thesis frames corridor performance as primarily a design problem. It would need to demonstrate that MWAA has already optimized cleaning schedules, maintenance SLAs, incident response staffing, and AeroTrain headway management before concluding that the residual problem is a capital problem. That demonstration is absent.

## Where the Counter-Case Is Genuinely Weak

Intellectual honesty requires identifying where the counter-case loses.

**The AeroTrain handoff is a real design constraint.** The vertical circulation between the AeroTrain platform and the concourse sterile level at IAD is a genuine single-point-of-failure for concourse access. When an escalator is down at the station head and the alternative is a single elevator bank, the corridor geometry argument becomes secondary — the bottleneck is the vertical transition, and it cannot be resolved by maintenance SLAs alone. The thesis is right that this is a design problem, not just an operations problem, at the specific station-head nodes.

**Induced demand in corridor design is real.** Wider corridors do not just accommodate more passengers — they accommodate more passengers more reliably during irregular operations, which may induce schedule density that re-narrows headroom. But at IAD's current utilization rates (overall system-wide load factors averaging in the high 70s%), the induced-demand concern is second-order.

**Post-occupancy data would likely support the thesis.** If MWAA deployed LiDAR people-counting at the AeroTrain station heads for 90 days across seasonal peaks, the data would almost certainly show LOS D or E conditions during certain AeroTrain arrival banks. That data would strengthen the thesis considerably. Its absence is not evidence the problem doesn't exist — it is evidence the problem has not been measured. That is a genuine gap the thesis correctly identifies.

---

## Summary

The thesis identifies real operational discomforts at IAD and frames them as design failures. The counter-case is that most of those discomforts are amenable to operational intervention before capital intervention, that the empirical foundation for specific corridor-widening targets is weaker than the thesis implies, that path dependence in corridor design is softer than the "10+ years" framing suggests, and that the prior capital question — checkpoints and hold-rooms — is excluded from scope in a way that distorts the analysis.

The thesis will survive this brief if and only if it engages honestly with the checkpoint exclusion, demonstrates that operations interventions have been optimized before capital is claimed, and derives its width-to-throughput targets from measured conditions at IAD rather than from Fruin-derived LOS tables that were not built for modern carry-on loads or AeroTrain-style pulse arrival patterns.

If it does that work, it will have built something useful. If it does not, it will be a sophisticated argument for a predetermined conclusion.
