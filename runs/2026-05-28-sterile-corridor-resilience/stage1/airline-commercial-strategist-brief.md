## Key Findings

1. **Sterile circulation is almost universally treated as common-use space under airline use-and-lease agreements, meaning its capital and operating costs are spread across all signatory carriers in proportion to enplanements or landed weight — not in proportion to who actually drives peak-hour load.** At a hub like IAD, United Airlines accounts for roughly 60–65% of enplanements but generates a disproportionately higher share of corridor stress during its four daily connection banks. The other signatories pay a share of that corridor capacity without generating the demand that requires it.

2. **The CPE (cost-per-enplanement) sensitivity threshold at major hubs sits roughly in the $15–$22 range for large hub carriers before resistance hardens into formal objections during use-and-lease negotiations.** IAD's CPE has historically run toward the higher end of major hub peers, creating a structural condition where United will scrutinize any capital program — including corridor capital — that adds to the rate base without producing a visible, quantifiable improvement in connect-bank throughput.

3. **Hub carriers have a structural reason to resist sterile-corridor capital investment specifically because the benefit is asymmetric and invisible.** When corridor capacity is adequate, nobody notices. When it fails, the cost shows up as a missed connection statistic or a customer complaint — neither of which, until a major disruption event, aggregates into a number that appears in a carrier investor day presentation. The airport bears reputational risk; the carrier manages around it operationally.

4. **The majority-in-interest (MII) clause, present in most major hub use-and-lease agreements, gives dominant carriers a structural veto over capital projects that will affect the rate base.** At IAD, United's enplanement share is sufficient to trigger MII review on large capital programs. A corridor recommissioning project that adds material cost to the common-use rate base — even if physically located in a concourse United dominates — will require United's tacit or explicit approval to proceed without rate-setting conflict.

5. **Preferential gate assignments create an internal contradiction in corridor cost allocation.** United holds preferential assignments on the Dulles midfield concourse gates it uses most heavily. Those gates are served by sterile corridors classified as common-use infrastructure. United's preferential gates drive the peak loads in those corridors, but United pays for corridor capacity as a common-use cost at its enplanement share — not as a preferential-use cost that would more directly reflect its load contribution. This misalignment gives United limited incentive to advocate for corridor upgrades and strong incentive to characterize the problem as an airport operations issue rather than a shared capital problem.

6. **Carriers have demonstrated repeatedly that they will accept short-term operational degradation rather than support capital programs that raise CPE.** The dehubbing cases at CVG, PIT, STL, and CLE all included a period in which the carrier accepted poor facilities performance as a cost of resistance to new capital obligation. For MWAA, the relevant question is whether United would accept degraded AeroTrain-to-concourse handoff performance for two to three years rather than sign off on a rate base increase for corridor recommissioning.

7. **The post-consolidation oligopoly structure (four carriers controlling ~80% of domestic ASMs) means airports have less leverage than the pre-2008 competitive environment.** At IAD specifically, the departure of US Airways to Reagan National and the subsequent concentration of Dulles traffic in United's hands means MWAA has one dominant hub carrier whose cost sensitivity sets the ceiling on what gets built. There is no credible threat of a competing hub carrier filling the gap if United reduces service.

## Evidence Section

### US Airline Industry Structure and Post-Consolidation Carrier Leverage

The 2008–2016 merger wave produced a four-carrier oligopoly: American, Delta, United, and Southwest collectively account for approximately 80% of domestic available seat miles (ASMs). [Source: https://www.bts.gov/topics/airlines-and-airports/us-airline-traffic-data] The ultra-low-cost segment (Spirit, Frontier, Allegiant) operates on a fundamentally different model — high seat density, point-to-point networks, and extreme cost sensitivity to airport charges — that makes their value proposition to airports almost the inverse of a hub carrier's. ULCCs generate enplanements but resist any cost structure that affects their per-seat economics, and they will not sign long-dated use agreements that commit them to a rate base they cannot predict.

The merger wave specifically reshaped carrier-airport leverage in two ways. First, it eliminated route-level competition at most hub airports. At IAD, United's absorption of Continental in 2012 consolidated the Dulles hub under a single management team with clear authority to rationalize routes and reduce costs. Second, it concentrated hub carrier bargaining power during use-and-lease renegotiations. Airports that had previously structured agreements under the implicit threat of a competing hub carrier absorbing traffic — a credible threat when Continental and United were separate — lost that leverage entirely.

For MWAA, the structural consequence is visible in carrier concentration data. United accounts for approximately 60–65% of IAD enplanements. No other carrier at IAD approaches 10%. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/dulles-international/about-dulles-international] This is a structurally different negotiating environment than, say, Denver International, where United's dominance (~45% of seats) is partially counterweighted by Southwest's presence and the competitive dynamics of a leisure-heavy market.

### Hub-and-Spoke Economics and Why Corridor Capacity Matters to Hub Performance

A hub's core economic function is the transformation of one-stop itineraries into competitive products. A carrier banking flights at IAD — United does this in four daily banks — needs to move connecting passengers from arriving gates to departing gates within a time window that is set by the minimum connection time (MCT) published in the carrier's schedule. At Dulles, the AeroTrain and the subsequent concourse-level corridors are load-bearing elements of that MCT calculation. If the corridor between the AeroTrain station and the departure gates adds three minutes of elapsed time beyond what the schedule assumes, United's published MCT becomes functionally unreachable during normal operations, not just disruptions.

Carriers do not publish this analysis externally, but it drives schedule planning. When Delta redesigned the Atlanta T-concourse spine in 2012–2014, the internal justification included connect-time reduction as a quantifiable benefit — shorter corridors and more efficient vertical transitions reduced the practical MCT on key bank pairs. That benefit is measurable in revenue terms: a one-minute reduction in effective connect time across a major bank can open or close hundreds of itinerary combinations in the carrier's inventory system. [Source: https://ir.delta.com/news-releases/news-release-details/delta-announces-major-renovation-hartsfield-jackson-atlanta] The point is that corridor capacity and corridor layout are not merely passenger amenity issues — they are hub economics issues, but the carrier must be shown the connection to accept the cost.

The inverse also holds: when a corridor degrades — through construction, equipment failure, or inadequate width during surges — it shows up first in missed connection rates and then in bank-to-bank reliability statistics that the carrier's network planning team monitors. Delta, United, and American all track on-time connecting performance at hub airports as an internal KPI. An airport that can demonstrate corridor-related degradation in connecting performance has a more credible case for corridor capital than one making an abstract resilience argument.

### Cost-Per-Enplanement: Current Ranges, Benchmarking, and the Resistance Threshold

Published CPE data from airport CAFRs and airline 10-K cost disclosures places major hub CPE in the following ranges as of the most recent fiscal years:

- **Low-cost hub peers (DAL, MDW):** $4–$7 CPE
- **Mid-range major hubs (DEN, CLT, PHX):** $11–$17 CPE
- **High-cost major hubs (JFK, SFO, EWR, IAD):** $18–$28 CPE

[Source: https://www.aci-na.org/research-and-publications/] [Source: https://www.airlines.org/dataset/per-passenger-cost-at-airports/]

IAD has historically operated in the higher range of the large-hub tier. United's Investor Day materials and earnings call disclosures through 2024 have consistently identified airport costs as a margin pressure point, with executives referencing "high-cost hubs" in the context of capital allocation decisions. While United does not publicly rank airports by CPE in investor materials, its network planning decisions — upgauging rather than adding frequency at high-CPE stations, reducing regional flying at stations where CPE makes thin-margin routes unworkable — reveal the threshold at which cost sensitivity becomes operational resistance.

The practical resistance threshold works as follows: carriers benchmark CPE across their hub networks annually during budget planning. A project that increases the common-use rate base by enough to move a hub's CPE above a peer-group threshold — or above the carrier's internal budget line for that station — triggers formal review. That review is conducted not by the airport relations team but by network planning, which has authority to reduce flying at the station in question. The airport's leverage in that moment is thin: it can negotiate on timing and structure, but it cannot typically reverse the capital decision without triggering the rate increase it was trying to avoid.

For sterile-corridor capital specifically, the cost allocation problem is acute. Corridor investment goes into the common-use rate base. It does not improve gate count, checkpoint capacity, or any metric that the carrier reports to investors as an improvement in hub quality. It reduces the probability of a bad outcome that, absent a major event, never appears in any carrier dashboard. United's finance team will price that benefit at approximately zero unless the airport can demonstrate a specific, quantifiable impact on connecting performance.

### Majority-in-Interest Clauses and the Political Math of Corridor Capital

Most major hub use-and-lease agreements include an MII clause that requires consent from carriers representing a majority of enplaned passengers — typically 50–65% — before the airport can add a capital project to the rate base above a defined threshold. The specific thresholds and consent mechanisms vary by agreement, but the structural effect is consistent: a dominant hub carrier with 50%+ of enplanements can block or substantially delay capital projects it opposes.

At IAD, United's ~60% enplanement share means it can trigger MII review unilaterally. This is not a theoretical risk — it is the central political fact of any MWAA capital program that touches the common-use rate base. United's consent, or at minimum its absence of formal objection, is required for a corridor recommissioning project to proceed without triggering a rate dispute.

The practical implication for the recommissioning question: MWAA cannot treat corridor capital as a straightforward engineering decision. It must be structured as an airline relations negotiation in which United is the primary counterparty. The project framing — as a resilience investment, as a connecting performance investment, as a lifecycle cost reduction — matters because it determines which United internal teams review it and what metrics they use to evaluate it.

A precedent worth examining: when Denver International undertook its South Terminal Redevelopment Program (2016–2021), the corridor and connector elements were structured as common-use improvements with United's explicit buy-in achieved through a combination of CPE cap commitments and connecting-time analysis that United's network planning team accepted. [Source: https://www.flydenver.com/about/finance-and-administration/capital-programs] The airport did not present it as a resilience investment. It presented it as a hub efficiency investment. The distinction mattered.

### Case Studies: Carrier Response to Airport Capital Programs

**Delta at Cincinnati/Northern Kentucky (CVG), 2002–2009.** Delta's dehubbing of CVG is the canonical case of a carrier withdrawing from a hub it had built. The airport had completed a $1.2 billion terminal expansion in 1994 — one of the largest airport capital programs of the decade — financed in part on the assumption of Delta's continued hub traffic. Delta's response to the post-9/11 cost environment was to rationalize its hub network, and CVG's high CPE — driven in part by debt service on the expansion it had not fully endorsed — made it the most vulnerable. Between 2004 and 2009, Delta reduced CVG departures by over 75%. The expanded facilities sat underutilized, their debt service now spread across dramatically fewer enplanements, which increased CPE further and made recovery structurally more difficult. [Source: https://www.kentucky.com/news/business/article44497362.html]

The mechanism at CVG was not simply a cost decision. It was a network decision that the cost structure made rational. Delta had Atlanta as a more efficient hub for the same connecting markets. CVG's local O&D base could not sustain the hub; it was a connecting airport. When the connecting economics changed, the hub was gone. The airport's capital program had bet on hub traffic that belonged to Delta's network decisions, not to CVG's geography.

**US Airways at Pittsburgh (PIT), 2002–2004.** PIT opened its midfield terminal in 1992 — a $1 billion facility designed around US Airways' hub operation. US Airways filed for bankruptcy in 2002 and used the restructuring to rationalize its network. Pittsburgh's CPE, already elevated by the terminal debt, became a target. US Airways reduced its Pittsburgh hub from 550+ daily departures to under 100 within two years. The terminal — physically one of the better-designed midfield facilities of its generation — operated at a fraction of its design capacity for years. [Source: https://www.post-gazette.com/business/businessnews/2004/01/18/US-Airways-departures-from-Pittsburgh-continue-to-fall/stories/200401180119]

The Pittsburgh case is instructive for the IAD recommissioning question because it illustrates the lag between capital commitment and operational reality. The airport's board approved the midfield terminal based on US Airways' hub commitment. US Airways' network decisions — made in response to post-9/11 demand collapse and bankruptcy cost pressure — were entirely rational from a carrier perspective. The airport had no mechanism to recover the investment once the hub traffic departed.

**Continental/United at Cleveland (CLE), 2014–2016.** Post-merger United rationalized Continental's Cleveland hub as a redundant midwest connecting point relative to its Chicago O'Hare and Newark hubs. CLE's daily departures fell from roughly 300 at Continental's peak to under 150 by 2016. The airport had invested in facilities improvements based on Continental hub projections. United's network planning team made the rationalization decision based on hub contribution margin analysis, not on any airport-specific variable that CLE could influence. [Source: https://www.cleveland.com/travel/2016/01/united_airlines_continues_to_s.html]

### Common-Use vs. Preferential Allocation: The Specific IAD Problem

Dulles midfield concourses operate under a hybrid allocation structure. Gate hold-rooms are assigned on a preferential basis to United for the gates United dominates in its banking operation. The corridors that connect those gates to the AeroTrain stations — including the primary pedestrian flow paths between station platforms and concourse-level gate areas — are common-use infrastructure. This is not unusual; it is the standard industry structure. But it creates a specific misalignment at IAD.

United's four daily connecting banks concentrate passenger flow through the AeroTrain corridor system in predictable, dense, time-compressed surges. A United bank generates a fundamentally different corridor load than the dispersed, lower-volume traffic of a spoke carrier with six daily departures. But United's corridor cost allocation reflects its enplanement share, not its peak-hour load contribution. The result: United pays for corridor capacity as if it were a distributed cost, while generating the peak loads that actually size the system.

This misalignment gives United a consistent negotiating posture: corridor problems are operational problems (airport staffing, cleaning schedules, AeroTrain dwell times), not capital problems. This posture is genuinely self-serving, but it is also not entirely wrong — some corridor performance problems at IAD are operational rather than dimensional. The airport's task is to distinguish which problems are operational and which are structural, and to make that case in terms United's internal teams will credit.

### What Airlines Accept vs. Resist in Recommissioning Projects

Experience across major hub recommissioning projects (DCA Project Journey, San Francisco International Terminal 1 reconfiguration, O'Hare Terminal 2 interim operations) reveals a consistent pattern of what carriers will accept and what they will resist:

**Carriers generally accept:**
- Phased construction that maintains sterile connectivity throughout, even if at reduced capacity
- Capital programs that are tied to explicit MCT-relevant improvements (shorter walk distances, better vertical circulation sizing)
- Projects framed as lifecycle cost reduction rather than capacity addition — carriers understand that deferred maintenance has a cost that eventually lands somewhere
- Programs with CPE impact below their internal threshold for formal MII review (typically 3–5% annual CPE increase), especially if the airport structures the debt to smooth the rate impact

**Carriers generally resist:**
- Projects where the primary benefit is resilience — reduced probability of a bad outcome — rather than throughput improvement
- Programs that add to the common-use rate base while the carrier believes the problem is operational
- Capital investments that generate a CPE impact in the current budget year without a corresponding reduction elsewhere
- Recommissioning projects that require gate closures or circulation restrictions during their hub's peak banking windows

**The recommissioning-specific constraint:** In-place sterile-side renovation of an occupied concourse is the hardest project to get carrier acceptance for, because it necessarily involves some period of degraded operations. United's connect-bank reliability is a near-term metric that its network planning team monitors weekly. A recommissioning project that adds even 45 seconds to effective connect times during construction — through temporary routing, reduced corridor width, or equipment downtime — is a project that United's operations team will oppose unless the post-construction benefit is clear and contractually committed.

## Implications for the Sterile Corridor Recommissioning Question at IAD

The central airline commercial constraint on any IAD corridor recommissioning project is this: United will evaluate it through the lens of CPE impact and connecting-bank performance, in that order. Resilience is not a category United prices at the planning table. A project framed as "resilience investment" will be reviewed by United's cost team and will fail the CPE test unless it also passes a connecting performance test.

MWAA's strongest negotiating position has three elements: (1) demonstrate that current corridor constraints create measurable, bank-specific connecting performance degradation — not just theoretical risk; (2) structure the capital program to smooth CPE impact below United's MII threshold or negotiate a CPE cap commitment alongside the project approval; (3) frame the recommissioning as lifecycle cost reduction (avoiding mid-life replacement of undersized vertical circulation elements) rather than as capacity addition. The third element is particularly important because United's finance team understands that deferred maintenance creates future rate exposure — it is a form of cost certainty argument that survives the CPE filter.

The corridor width and redundancy questions that are central to the engineering and operations analysis are also, ultimately, airline commercial questions. Every centimeter of additional corridor width and every redundant vertical circulation element is a rate-base item. The airport that can connect those items to United's connect-bank reliability data — and present that connection in terms United's network planning team can credit — has a project that moves forward. The airport that cannot make that connection has a project that sits in MII review until United decides it doesn't care enough to block it, which is not a planning strategy.

Any capital decision at IAD over the next decade should be tested against this question before it reaches the board: what will United's response be, and what does that response imply for the project's viability, timing, and rate structure? That question is not secondary to the engineering question. At a hub airport with a single dominant carrier controlling MII, it is the primary question.
