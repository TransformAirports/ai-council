# Airport CEO Brief: Sterile Corridor Capital as a Board, Bond, and Airline Renegotiation Problem

**Stage 1 — Airport CEO Perspective**
**Run: Sterile Corridor Resilience**
**Prepared: 2026-05-27**

---

## Key Findings (Board Chair Read)

1. **Sterile corridor capacity is common-use infrastructure that produces no revenue, which means it falls into the least-defended quadrant of airport capital budgeting.** Every capital allocation framework a large hub operates under — residual rate protection, signatory airline majority-in-interest thresholds, PFC eligibility screens — structurally disadvantages spending that cannot be tied to a gate, a holdroom, or a check-in position. This is not a design problem. It is a governance and incentive problem.

2. **Rating agencies do not have a line item for corridor resilience. They do have a line item for operating disruptions, litigation exposure, and single-point-of-failure infrastructure risk.** A sterile corridor shutdown that cascades into two hours of concourse closure, misconnects on a hub bank, and a dominant carrier diversion advisory is exactly the kind of event that surfaces in a rating agency call. The connection is real but the translation is not automatic — the CEO has to make it explicitly, before the event, or it won't move capital.

3. **The AeroTrain platform-to-concourse handoffs at IAD are the highest-probability failure points in MWAA's sterile circulation system.** They are the point where two traffic streams converge, where vertical circulation is mechanically dependent, and where there is no redundant sterile path. They were designed for a traffic volume and fleet mix that has since changed. They are also the most expensive to fix in place. [CEO perspective — based on site knowledge; MWAA capital planning documents should be consulted for design specifications.]

4. **The BIL/IIJA Airport Terminal Program is the funding lever that makes this politically survivable.** A capital request structured as a federal-state-airport cost share — with PFC as the local stack — can hold the net airline rate impact to a level that survives Majority-in-Interest review. A request that lands entirely on the airline rate base will not survive the United Airlines counterparty relationship at IAD in the current environment.

5. **The use-and-lease window is the binding political constraint, not the bond market.** MWAA's airline use and lease agreement governs which projects require signatory approval and which do not. A corridor-widening or redundant vertical-circulation project that reclassifies space from exclusive use to common use, or that increases the common-use rate base materially, will trigger MII review. The next agreement renegotiation window is the strategic moment to restructure how corridor capital is classified. Missing that window locks in the current framework for another decade.

6. **No large-hub CEO in the last 25 years has been removed for a corridor failure. Several have survived rating agency downgrades, mid-construction cost overruns, and airline disputes. The incentive structure tells the CEO to defend the credit, protect the airline relationship, and keep the board comfortable — which means corridor resilience capital competes for attention against projects that score better on every short-term metric.** This is the honest framing. The thesis is correct. The CEO incentive problem is also real and needs to be addressed in the brief directly.

7. **The IAD Tier 2 program is both the opportunity and the constraint.** Active construction in the sterile environment is the moment when corridor geometry can be reset. Once Tier 2 is locked and construction contracts are executed, the corridor configuration it produces becomes the configuration MWAA manages for 30 years. The planning decisions being made now — width, redundancy, vertical circulation sizing — will be expressed as operating costs and disruption events for the duration of MWAA's next long bond cycle.

## Evidence Section

### Bond Rating Framework

Moody's, S&P, and Fitch all treat airport revenue bonds as a distinct asset class with specialized methodologies. The core metrics that drive rating outcomes are: debt service coverage ratio (DSCR), with investment-grade large hubs typically carrying 1.4x to 1.6x coverage on senior bonds under residual frameworks; days-cash-on-hand (DCOH), where the agencies target 300–500 days for A-category credits; enplanement concentration (the share of traffic attributable to a single dominant carrier); and debt-per-enplanement, which serves as a proxy for leverage relative to traffic base. [Source methodology documents: Moody's "Rating Methodology: Airports," most recent version as of 2023–2024; S&P "Airport Ratings Criteria"; Fitch "Airport Rating Criteria" — these are publicly available through the respective agency portals and through EMMA/MSRB for bonds that cite them in official statements. Specific document URLs were not accessible during this research session; the researcher should pull current versions directly from Moody's moodys.com, S&P spglobal.com, and Fitch fitchratings.com.]

What the agencies do NOT have: a specific metric for sterile corridor redundancy, vertical circulation uptime, or internal circulation resilience. What they do have — buried in the qualitative sections of the methodology — is an assessment of "concentration risk" (single-point-of-failure infrastructure), "operational disruption exposure," and "capital program execution risk." A CEO who wants to move corridor resilience capital through the rating agency narrative has to translate it into one of those three buckets. The most credible framing is capital program execution risk: a Tier 2 corridor segment that is undersized on Day 1 will require mid-life remediation, which creates unplanned bond issuance, which disrupts the debt service profile. Agencies understand that argument. They are less receptive to "this corridor gets congested at peak."

MWAA carries an A-category rating from all three agencies. [Widely documented in MWAA official statements; confirmed in bond offering documents filed with EMMA. Specific rating levels as of 2025–2026 should be verified against the most current rating action letters.] The Dulles corridor and AeroTrain capital program history — specifically the AeroTrain cost overruns and delivery delay from the 2000s — is a data point rating analysts know. A CEO presenting a new vertical-circulation capital request at IAD is presenting it to analysts who remember the AeroTrain narrative. The framing needs to acknowledge that history and explain why the sizing methodology is different this time.

### Airline Use-and-Lease Structure

MWAA operates under a hybrid use-and-lease framework. The practical effect for capital planning is that projects above a defined dollar threshold, or projects that materially affect the airline cost per enplanement (CPE), require signatory airline review and, in some cases, Majority-in-Interest consent. [Source: MWAA's Airline Use and Lease Agreement is a public document. The specific MII thresholds and consent triggers should be read from the current executed agreement. The researcher should pull the current agreement from MWAA's finance page or from signatory airline public disclosures.]

United Airlines is the dominant carrier at IAD, holding roughly 50–55% of seats and a substantially higher share of connecting traffic. [Widely documented in DOT T-100 market data and MWAA annual reports; precise figures should be verified against current DOT data.] This creates a bilateral negotiation that looks nothing like a multi-carrier negotiation. United has both the economic incentive to push back on common-area cost increases and the political leverage to credibly threaten capacity reallocation. The CEO who takes a corridor capital request into an MII process without United pre-aligned is bringing a live grenade.

The residual vs. compensatory distinction matters here in a specific way: under a residual framework, the airport's net revenue after operating costs and debt service is credited back to the airlines. This means capital spending that produces no new revenue (corridor widening, redundant vertical circulation) must be absorbed by either the rate base or funded through non-airline sources. PFC and AIP grants are non-airline sources. BIL/IIJA Airport Terminal Program grants are non-airline sources. Structuring the corridor capital program to maximize federal and PFC funding — before the airline rate impact is calculated — is the only strategy that keeps the MII process survivable. [CEO perspective on structuring strategy; the specific financial mechanics follow from the framework described in FAA PFC regulations at 14 CFR Part 158 and MWAA's existing PFC applications, which are public record on FAA's website.]

### Federal Capital Stack

The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law allocated $1 billion per year for five years (FY2022–FY2026) to the Airport Terminal Program under the Federal Aviation Administration's discretionary grant program. [Source: BIL Section 40007; FAA Airport Terminal Program grant announcements are published at faa.gov. The researcher should pull the current status of MWAA's ATP applications from the FAA grants database.] ATP grants fund terminal projects, which includes sterile corridor improvements, at a federal share typically in the 80% range for qualifying projects.

The Airport Improvement Program (AIP) formula entitlements provide a separate, annual allocation that MWAA can direct toward eligible projects. AIP eligibility for sterile corridor work depends on how the project is scoped — landside circulation generally qualifies, sterile-side corridor improvements qualify when they are part of a terminal capacity project.

PFC collections at IAD — currently at the statutory cap of $4.50 per enplanement — generate substantial annual revenue given IAD's enplanement base. [The specific annual PFC revenue figure should be verified against MWAA's most recent PFC audit report, publicly available on FAA's PFC database.] PFC can fund corridor capital and does not flow through the airline rate base, which is the crucial characteristic for MII avoidance.

The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 maintained the $4.50 PFC cap despite industry advocacy for an increase. [Source: FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024, Public Law 118-63. The failure to raise the cap is widely reported in aviation trade press.] A PFC cap increase — which has been a persistent lobbying objective of ACI-NA and the airports industry — would materially change the calculus on how much corridor capital can be funded outside the rate base. The CEO's government affairs program should track this as a potential capital unlocking event.

TSA is a federal partner whose decisions arrive on a different calendar than MWAA's capital program. Checkpoint reconfiguration — which affects the upstream load on sterile corridors — is a TSA operational decision that MWAA cannot control but must plan around. The relationship between checkpoint throughput and corridor throughput is real: a checkpoint expansion that increases throughput without a corresponding corridor capacity increase shifts the bottleneck downstream. [CEO perspective on coordination dynamics; this is standard operating reality at any hub with active TSA expansion discussions.]

### Board Governance

MWAA is a federally chartered interstate compact authority, not a city department or a traditional airport district. Its board is politically appointed: the Commonwealth of Virginia appoints a majority of the board, the District of Columbia and Maryland appoint additional members, and the federal government — through the Secretary of Transportation — holds appointment authority over several seats. [Source: MWAA Act of 1986, as amended; the specific current board composition and appointment authorities are documented in MWAA's bylaws and annual report.]

This structure means two things for capital strategy. First, the CEO serves at the pleasure of a board whose composition turns on Virginia gubernatorial politics, not on FAA or airline approval. A governor transition — Virginia holds gubernatorial elections every four years — can shift board priorities faster than a capital program amortizes. A corridor capital commitment that spans two gubernatorial terms needs to be structured so it survives a board chair transition without requiring reauthorization. This means getting it into a bond covenant or a federal grant agreement before the political calendar turns. Second, the interstate compact structure insulates MWAA from direct City of Washington or Arlington County budget authority, which is a structural advantage for long-cycle capital planning. MWAA does not need city council approval to issue bonds or execute capital. It does need to maintain its federal relationships — the Secretary of Transportation retains certain authorities — which is why the congressional delegation relationships matter as much as they do.

### Host Region Political Economy

MWAA's key political relationships are with the Virginia Governor's office, the Maryland Governor's office, the DC Mayor, and the congressional delegations — particularly the Virginia senators and the Northern Virginia congressional delegation whose districts include IAD's service area. The Dulles Toll Road relationship (MWAA operates the DTR) is a persistent political irritant that consumes executive attention in ways that have nothing to do with airport operations. [Widely documented in Virginia legislative history; the DTR toll increases have generated recurring constituent and legislative pushback.]

The business community relationship is primarily through the Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority's stakeholder apparatus and through the Dulles Corridor business community. Amazon's HQ2 in Arlington and the broader Northern Virginia technology corridor generate both traffic demand (important for enplanement growth) and political relationships. A sterile corridor failure at IAD that delays Amazon executives and Northern Virginia technology sector travelers on a Monday morning generates political heat faster than any operating metric.

United Airlines' corporate relationship is both an airline counterparty relationship and a political one. United's advocacy on airport policy — at the FAA, at DOT, and on the Hill — intersects with MWAA's interests often but not always. A sterile corridor capital program that increases CPE at IAD creates a United government affairs problem, not just a use-and-lease negotiation problem.

### CEO Calendar Evidence

At a hub airport of IAD's scale, the CEO's time is roughly allocated as follows, based on documented patterns at peer institutions and widely understood practice in the industry: government relations and federal affairs, 25–30% (FAA, DOT, congressional office visits, grant application advocacy); airline relationship management, 20–25% (regular executive contact with hub carrier leadership, agreement compliance, capital program previewing); capital program oversight, 20% (design review, contractor management, cost escalation, bond market preparation); board and stakeholder management, 15%; media and crisis management, 10%; operations oversight (largely delegated to COO), 10%. [CEO perspective on time allocation; documented CEO calendars at peer hubs are not publicly available, but ACI-NA CEO forums and industry conference proceedings capture the stated priorities of large-hub CEOs.]

The specific IAD overlay: the AeroTrain capital history created a legacy of rating agency skepticism about MWAA's ability to deliver large-scale automated people mover infrastructure on budget and on schedule. The Tier 2 program is, among other things, an opportunity to demonstrate that MWAA has learned from that history. Every rating agency call on a Tier 2 bond issuance will revisit the AeroTrain narrative. The CEO's job is to have a credible answer ready — and the answer has to include a story about how the design methodology for corridor sizing and vertical circulation is different this time.

## The Financial and Political Case — For and Against

### The Case For

The capital case for corridor resilience investment rests on three arguments that survive rating agency and airline scrutiny.

First, the lifecycle cost argument. An undersized corridor segment, or a single-point vertical circulation element, that fails mid-life requires a sterile-environment remediation that is substantially more expensive than getting the sizing right in original construction. Mid-life AeroTrain-to-concourse handoff reconstruction — executed while the concourse remains occupied and sterile — is not a $20 million project. It is a $60–100 million project with significant soft costs, construction schedule risk, and operational disruption costs that are borne by both the airport and the airlines but attributed to no one's capital budget in a way that makes the original design decision visible in retrospect. [CEO perspective on order-of-magnitude estimates; specific estimates require engineering parametric analysis.]

Second, the credit protection argument. The rating agencies reward airports with low operating disruption risk. A sterile corridor system with no redundant path and mechanical dependencies is an operating disruption event waiting to happen. The CEO who presents a proactive corridor resilience investment to a rating agency as "we identified this risk and we are retiring it before it becomes a credit event" gets a different conversation than the CEO who explains why a concourse was shut for two hours last quarter. Proactive disclosure of a mitigated risk is a positive credit narrative. A disruption event that surfaces a previously unidentified single-point-of-failure is a negative one.

Third, the political moment argument. The BIL/IIJA Airport Terminal Program window is not permanent. The federal appropriations that fund ATP are time-limited and subject to congressional reauthorization. The CEO who positions a corridor resilience project as a qualifying terminal capacity investment — and who has the project documentation ready when the next ATP round opens — has access to funding that makes the airline rate impact manageable. The CEO who waits until the political moment has passed is pricing corridor resilience entirely into the rate base.

### The Case Against

The honest case against is also real, and the Strategist needs to hear it.

Corridor capacity is invisible to passengers until it fails. It does not show up in J.D. Power scores. It does not appear in TSA's AOS wait-time data. It does not feature in airline on-time performance metrics because the FAA's on-time definition (departure within 15 minutes of scheduled) does not capture misconnects caused by internal circulation congestion. There is no operating metric that the CEO can report to the board, to a rating agency, or to an airline negotiating counterparty that directly represents "corridor resilience." This is not a measurement problem that better sensors solve. It is a structural invisibility in every accountability framework the airport operates under.

The competing capital uses are real. IAD's capital program — Tier 2 terminal, the concourse D renovation, ongoing airfield work, AeroTrain reliability improvements — is large and the debt service implications of each tranche are real. Adding corridor-specific scope to the Tier 2 program means either more debt, more federal funding, or a longer construction schedule. The CEO has finite capacity to present the bond market with new issuance. Each new issuance requires a roadshow, a rating agency update, and a pricing process. The CEO who goes to market too frequently loses the discipline premium that large-hub credits are supposed to carry.

United's posture on any CPE increase is the third constraint. United is managing its own cost structure in a competitive environment. A corridor resilience project that adds $0.50 per enplanement to the CPE — even on a transitional basis — is a negotiating adversary in every other conversation MWAA has with United. The CEO who picks a fight with United over corridor width is spending political capital that might be needed for the next holdroom negotiation, the next gate reallocation, or the next federal policy fight where United's advocacy matters.

## Specific Data Points and Precedents for the Strategist

**1. AeroTrain delivery and the rating agency memory.** The MWAA AeroTrain (Dulles Airport Mobile Lounge replacement) was completed in phases between 2010 and 2012 at a cost substantially above initial estimates. [Widely documented in MWAA annual reports and bond official statements of that period. The precise cost variance is in the public record.] Moody's and S&P both noted construction execution risk in rating actions during that period. The institutional memory at the agencies is real. Any new mechanical infrastructure capital request at IAD — including vertical circulation elements — will be evaluated in part against that precedent. The CEO's rating agency narrative needs to address it directly.

**2. Denver International A-Bridge as a corridor sizing precedent.** The DEN A-Bridge connecting the main terminal to Concourse A is a widely cited example of sterile corridor design that anticipated long-term traffic growth. DEN's planning methodology — sizing to a traffic scenario 20 years out rather than the forecast at opening — is documentable in DEN's capital planning public record and in ACRP research on terminal planning. [The researcher should pull ACRP Report 25 "Airport Passenger Terminal Planning and Design" and any published post-occupancy evaluation of DEN Concourse A throughput for specific width-to-throughput data.]

**3. LaGuardia Terminal B as the counter-precedent.** The LGA Terminal B redevelopment — a P3 project delivered by LaGuardia Gateway Partners — was celebrated as a design achievement but produced sterile corridor geometries that drew criticism from operators for insufficient width in peak periods. [Documented in trade press and post-occupancy commentary; specific throughput data would need to be pulled from Port Authority of New York and New Jersey public records.] The LGA case is useful because it shows that even a well-funded, high-profile terminal program can produce corridor undersizing when the design process treats LOS letter grades as the target rather than resilience and recovery.

**4. The PFC cap as a capital constraint with a political solution.** The ACI-NA position on PFC cap increases is well documented in their congressional testimony and policy advocacy. A PFC cap increase from $4.50 to $8.50 — the figure that has been under discussion in various iterations of FAA reauthorization — would approximately double the annual non-airline capital available to large hubs. [Source: ACI-NA policy positions and congressional testimony; specific testimony documents are available on ACI-NA's website and through congressional committee archives.] The CEO's government affairs investment in PFC advocacy is directly connected to the feasibility of funding corridor resilience outside the rate base.

**5. The cascade math on a two-hour concourse shutdown.** A two-hour shutdown of one concourse at IAD during a peak morning bank — assume 30–40 departures in a 90-minute window — produces: misconnect costs for connecting passengers, which accrue to United under its contract of carriage; MWAA operating costs for the incident response; potential FAA notification and documentation requirements; and political exposure if the event surfaces in congressional oversight of MWAA. The financial magnitude of a single major corridor incident, properly calculated, exceeds the annualized debt service cost of the redundancy element that would have prevented it. No one has done that calculation in a form that the CEO can take to a board meeting, because no one has an incentive to do it — the costs fall in different budget lines and the capital that would prevent them is in a third budget line that no one is defending. [CEO perspective on cascade math; an infrastructure economist could construct this model from MWAA's own operating data and DOT misconnect statistics.]

## What Binds the CEO's Hands — and What Doesn't

The CEO of MWAA has substantial authority to direct capital within the bounds of the existing use-and-lease agreement, the existing PFC authorization, and the board-approved capital budget. What binds the hands:

- **MII clause:** Projects above the threshold require signatory airline consent. United's consent is effectively required. Getting United pre-aligned before an MII vote is the only strategy that works; surprise MII votes against dominant carriers do not survive.
- **Bond covenant restrictions:** Existing bond indentures limit how new debt is incurred and how it affects coverage ratios. A corridor capital project that pushes pro-forma coverage below 1.25x on senior bonds will not clear the covenant test. [Standard covenant language; MWAA's specific covenant package is in its bond indentures, which are public record on EMMA.]
- **Federal grant conditionality:** ATP and AIP grants come with federal oversight, Davis-Bacon wage requirements, and Buy America provisions. These add cost and schedule complexity to any federally funded corridor project. The CEO who plans a 24-month construction timeline for a federally funded sterile corridor project should plan for 30–36 months.

What does not bind the CEO:

- The board can be educated. The CEO who brings the cascade-cost model to a board committee — translating "corridor resilience" into "credit event avoidance" and "lifecycle cost management" — is doing the governance work the board cannot do for itself. This is the CEO's job.
- The federal grant cycle can be shaped. ATP applications are competitive but they are not blind. MWAA's relationships with FAA and with the Virginia and DC congressional delegations are assets that can be deployed in the grant application process.
- The use-and-lease renegotiation is an opportunity. The CEO who goes into the next agreement renegotiation having already classified corridor capital as common-use infrastructure — with a federal grant stack that reduces the airline rate impact to the threshold of what United will absorb — has a deal. The CEO who goes in without that preparation is going to have a multi-year fight.

## The CEO Test

If the midfield corridor vertical circulation fails at 6:45 a.m. on a Tuesday and cascades into a two-hour shutdown of one concourse, the 7:00 a.m. call is with United's vice president of airport affairs, the FAA's Eastern Region duty officer, and — if MWAA's congressional oversight relationships are active — potentially a staff member from the relevant Senate or House committee. The MWAA board chair will want a briefing by 9:00 a.m.

In that call, the CEO will be asked two questions: what happened, and what are we doing to prevent it from happening again. The second question is the capital question. The CEO who has a documented framework for corridor resilience — a capital plan, a federal funding stack, a board-approved priority — answers the second question from a position of credibility. The CEO who has no such plan is making promises in real time that the capital program cannot support.

That fear — the 7:00 a.m. call — is real. It does motivate CEO behavior. But it motivates reactive capital planning, not proactive capital planning. The thesis of this report is that the design decisions being made in the Tier 2 program right now will determine whether MWAA's CEO is answering that question in 2035 from a position of credibility or from a position of exposure. Those decisions are being made in planning meetings that the CEO is not in. That gap — between the CEO's calendar and the planning room where corridor geometry is being set — is the governance problem the Strategist needs to solve.
