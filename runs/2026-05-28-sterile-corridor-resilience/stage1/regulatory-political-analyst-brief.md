## Key Findings

1. **The FAA's design standards for sterile corridors are advisory, not mandatory — which is both the problem and the alibi.** AC 150/5360-13A (2018) is a reference document, not a regulatory mandate. The FAA cannot cite an airport for having a corridor that is 22 feet wide instead of 30 feet. What this means in practice: designers reach for the AC minimums as a ceiling, not a floor, and the FAA's funding lever (AIP eligibility) rewards projects that can be justified, not projects that are optimized. The gap between "advisory" and "enforceable" is where undersized sterile corridors are born.

2. **TSA's sterile area amendment process is a real constraint, but the 60/90-day figure commonly cited in project planning is a construction of practice, not of regulation.** Under 49 CFR 1542.105 and 1542.107, TSA has 30 days to approve or deny a security program amendment. For construction changes expected to last under 60 days, written notification to TSA is due within 72 hours of the changed condition, and TSA acknowledges in writing. For changes expected to last 60 days or more, the airport must file a formal amendment request within 30 days of discovery. The 60-to-90-day "review window" that planners cite is an aggregation of sequential steps — notification, TSA review, back-and-forth on interim security measures — not a single statutory clock. The practical implication: sterile boundary reconfiguration during construction is not prohibited, but it requires TSA to affirmatively accept interim security measures before work begins. Projects that treat this as a rubber stamp are taking on real schedule risk.

3. **The PFC cap at $4.50 — unchanged since 2000 — is the single most consequential constraint on non-headline terminal capital, and Congress confirmed in the 2024 FAA Reauthorization Act that it will stay there.** P.L. 118-63 (May 2024) retained the $4.50 cap despite years of advocacy from ACI-NA and AAAE to raise it to $8.50 or higher. The real purchasing power of $4.50 in 2024 is roughly $2.70 in 2000 dollars. For MWAA specifically, this means that bond-financed capital backed by PFC revenue is operating with a declining real revenue stream. Sterile corridor capital — which does not show up on a ribbon-cutting dais and does not poll in passenger satisfaction surveys — is exactly the class of investment that loses the internal competition for scarce PFC capacity.

4. **MWAA's governance structure makes it uniquely exposed to multi-directional congressional oversight — and uniquely reluctant to undertake ambitious capital programs that invite scrutiny.** MWAA is a creature of a two-state compact (Virginia and DC, codified in Va. Code § 5.1-152 and D.C. Code § 9-901) that Congress consented to in the Metropolitan Washington Airports Act of 1986, with the airports leased from DOT. The 17-member board includes three presidential appointees. The DOT OIG's 2012 audit found that MWAA awarded 117 of 190 contracts exceeding $200,000 — 62 percent — using categorical exceptions that limited competition, representing $225 million or 40 percent of total contract value. The Silver Line Phase 2 added more than $200 million in contractor cost overruns, eventually settled in 2022. The institutional memory of congressional hearings and OIG audits makes MWAA leadership cautious about capital programs that are difficult to explain and easy to second-guess. Invisible infrastructure — a wider corridor, a redundant escalator bank — is exactly the kind of capital that gets cut when board members are anticipating the next OIG call.

5. **The AIP Handbook and PFC regulations together create a perverse eligibility structure for sterile corridor investment.** AIP funding for terminal projects is generally eligible for "areas directly related to the movement of passengers and baggage in air commerce within the boundaries of the airport" (14 CFR § 158.15(b)(6)). Sterile corridors, as post-security passenger movement space, are nominally eligible. But the AIP Handbook's Appendix N structure distinguishes among landside, sterile, and secured areas — and the discretionary grant competition rewards projects with visible, countable outputs (new gates, new checkpoint lanes, new baggage claim positions). A corridor-widening or redundant vertical circulation project competes against gate additions at every large hub in the country. It will lose.

6. **The empirical foundation for corridor width standards is a 1970s dataset that ACRP itself has acknowledged is no longer valid.** ACRP Problem Statement 24-1058 (2024) explicitly states that "the basic research underlying existing formulas was created in the 1970s" and does not account for "increased prevalence of roll-aboard luggage." The result is that design teams applying AC 150/5360-13A guidance are using pedestrian flow formulas calibrated to passengers without roll-aboards, traveling at speeds no longer representative of the actual mix of ages and mobility profiles in a 2020s terminal. The advisory circular's silence on this gap — it does not flag that its referenced flow-rate data is pre-roll-aboard — is a regulatory failure of omission.

7. **The IAD $22 billion revitalization proposal, floated by the Trump administration DOT in December 2025 and priced by consultants at $21.8 billion in bonds plus $1.1 billion in airport fees, has no confirmed federal funding mechanism.** The DOT's Request for Information (December 2025) was an exploratory process, not a grant announcement. DOT did not respond to press requests for comment on the proposal as of May 2026. The gap between a $22 billion program and the realistic capacity of AIP, ATP, and PFC revenue is too large to bridge without a novel financing structure — privatization, federal appropriation, or a new legislative vehicle — none of which exist as of this writing. Capital programs at this scale tend to proceed on the strength of political commitment, which at MWAA means alignment between the DOT Secretary, the Virginia Governor, and the three presidential board appointees. That alignment is present now; it has a political half-life shorter than the construction timeline.

## Evidence Section

### FAA Design Standards: Advisory, Not Mandatory

FAA Advisory Circular 150/5360-13A (Airport Terminal Planning, July 2018) is the governing design reference for airport passenger terminal facilities, superseding the original AC 150/5360-13. The circular explicitly describes itself as "a reference document and starting point" — not a regulatory mandate. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/resources/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.current/documentNumber/150_5360-13]

This distinction is load-bearing for the thesis. The FAA's regulatory authority over terminal design flows from AIP grant conditions (49 U.S.C. § 47110 on allowable project costs) and Part 139 certification standards for safety-critical airside areas — not from the terminal planning AC. A sterile corridor's width, redundancy configuration, or vertical circulation design is not subject to FAA enforcement action under any current rulemaking. The FAA's leverage is funding eligibility, not citation authority.

AC 150/5300-13B (Airport Design, March 2022) governs airside geometry — runways, taxiways, aprons. It does not address sterile corridors. Designers sometimes import its clearance-distance logic into terminal circulation planning as a conservative heuristic, but this is a professional custom, not a regulatory requirement. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/resources/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.current/documentnumber/150_5300-13]

**Where the AC is silent and where it is misapplied:** AC 150/5360-13A does not specify a minimum sterile corridor width independent of calculated pedestrian flow requirements. It references pedestrian flow capacity in persons per unit width per minute, derived from the Fruin framework (developed in the 1970s). The AC does not flag that its underlying flow data predates roll-aboard luggage proliferation. ACRP Problem Statement 24-1058 (FY2024) directly acknowledges this: "The basic research underlying existing formulas was created in the 1970s, with some efforts to customize these to specific airport environments, though these are over twenty years old and do not account for demographic trends or increased prevalence of roll-aboard luggage." [Source: https://onlinepubs.trb.org/Onlinepubs/acrp/FY2024PS/PDF/24-1058.pdf]

The result in practice: design teams using the AC's flow-rate tables to justify a corridor width are using a model that systematically understates effective width demand. The misapplication is not deliberate — it is the natural result of a guidance document that has not been updated to reflect the operational environment it purports to describe.

**Recommissioning during construction:** The AC addresses construction phasing in general terms but does not provide specific design criteria for maintaining sterile area integrity during active reconstruction of a corridor segment. This gap means that project teams must negotiate phasing protocols with TSA on a project-by-project basis — which is both a design constraint and a source of project-specific schedule risk.

### TSA Regulatory Framework: 49 CFR Part 1542

The operative regulation is 49 CFR Part 1542 (Airport Security). A "sterile area" is defined in 49 CFR § 1540.5 as "a portion of an airport defined in the airport security program that provides passengers access to boarding aircraft and to which the access generally is controlled by TSA...through the screening of persons and property." [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/49/1540.5]

The sterile area boundary is not defined by federal regulation — it is defined in each airport's Airport Security Program (ASP), which is approved by TSA under 49 CFR § 1542.101. Any change to the physical configuration of the sterile area — including temporary boundary alterations during construction — is a change to the ASP.

**The amendment and notification process under 49 CFR §§ 1542.105 and 1542.107:**

Under § 1542.107 (Changed Conditions Affecting Security), airport operators must notify TSA within 6 hours (or per their security program) of any changed condition. For changes expected to last **under 60 days**, written details including interim security measures must reach TSA within 72 hours, and TSA notifies the operator of its disposition in writing. If approved, the written notification becomes a temporary part of the ASP. For changes expected to last **60 days or more**, the airport must file a formal proposed amendment to the ASP within 30 days of discovery.

Under § 1542.105 (Approval and Amendments), TSA's designated official has 30 days to approve or deny an operator-requested amendment, and requests must be filed at least 45 days before the proposed effective date. [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/49/1542.105; https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/49/1542.107]

**What this means for in-place sterile corridor renovation:** The chief-engineer brief's reference to a "60-90 day TSA review process" for temporary sterile boundary acceptance is a reasonable project-planning estimate that reflects the aggregated sequence of the notification and amendment process, not a single statutory clock. The 30-day TSA response window under § 1542.105 is the legal ceiling for each discrete step, but the practical timeline includes the period before the airport files, TSA's back-and-forth on interim security measure adequacy, and any reconsideration. Projects that reach the construction phase without a pre-negotiated TSA construction security plan (sometimes called a Construction Security Plan or CSP, incorporated into the ASP amendment) routinely find that TSA review extends the schedule. This is regulation operating as intended — TSA is not wrong to scrutinize temporary sterile boundaries — but it is a constraint that is systematically underweighted in sterile-side capital planning.

**TSA Information Bulletin / Security Directive references:** TSA Security Directives are Sensitive Security Information (SSI) and are not publicly available. No public-domain Security Directive specifically addressing temporary sterile boundary configurations during construction could be confirmed in this research. Airports navigating this process rely on their TSA Federal Security Director relationships, not published guidance. This is a deliberate feature of the SSI regime, not a gap — but it means that planning teams cannot cite a specific SD in project documentation.

### ADA and IBC: Minimum Widths vs. Operational Reality

The ADA Standards for Accessible Design (28 CFR Part 36, implementing the ADA of 1990) set a minimum accessible route clear width of 36 inches (914 mm). Where the route is less than 60 inches wide, passing spaces of 60 x 60 inches must be provided at intervals not exceeding 200 feet. [Source: https://www.access-board.gov/ada/guides/chapter-4-accessible-routes/]

The International Building Code (IBC) governs minimum egress widths based on occupant load and occupancy classification. Airport terminal corridors, classified as assembly occupancies (Group A), require minimum corridor widths calculated from the occupant load factor — typically 0.2 inches per occupant for corridors in the means of egress. For a large midfield concourse serving peak loads of 3,000 passengers per hour, the IBC egress calculation can produce a minimum width well below what operational flow modeling would require.

**The gap between ADA-minimum and operationally adequate:** A 36-inch ADA-compliant route is not a functioning airport corridor. A 60-inch corridor is technically navigable by two wheelchairs passing, but it fails functionally when a cleaning cart occupies one side. ACRP Report 25 (Airport Passenger Terminal Planning and Design, Volume 1) provides level-of-service frameworks for corridor throughput, but these are planning tools, not design minima with regulatory force. [Source: https://onlinepubs.trb.org/onlinepubs/acrp/acrp_rpt_025v1.pdf]

The practical problem at IAD's midfield concourses is not ADA compliance — those corridors meet code. The problem is that the design standards that produced them treated code compliance as the design target, not the floor. ADA and IBC establish what a corridor must be to avoid a legal violation; they say nothing about what a corridor must be to serve peak flow after an AeroTrain incident reduces throughput by 40 percent for 45 minutes.

### MWAA's Political Economy: The Federal Authority Structure

MWAA is a bistate compact authority (Virginia and DC) created pursuant to P.L. 99-591 (Metropolitan Washington Airports Act of 1986), operating under a long-term lease of federal property from DOT. The board has 17 members: 7 appointed by the Virginia Governor, 4 by the DC Mayor, 3 by the Maryland Governor, and 3 by the President of the United States (subject to Senate confirmation). [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Washington_Airports_Authority]

This structure creates three distinct oversight vectors that operate simultaneously:

**Federal lease oversight.** DOT retains landlord authority and has used it. Following the 2012 OIG audit, DOT committed to pursuing lease amendments to strengthen oversight and enforcement. The OIG audit found that MWAA awarded 62 percent of its contracts exceeding $200,000 through categorical exceptions that limited competition, totaling $225 million or 40 percent of total contract value. [Source: https://www.oig.dot.gov/library-item/28647]

**Congressional scrutiny.** MWAA operates in one of the most watched airport environments in the country. The 2012 OIG audit was triggered by a request from Representatives Frank Wolf and Tom Latham. Congresswoman Eleanor Holmes Norton subsequently introduced legislation to impose Federal Acquisition Regulations on MWAA. The House Transportation and Infrastructure Committee held a hearing on the OIG findings in November 2012. [Source: https://norton.house.gov/media-center/press-releases/after-inspector-general-s-draft-report-finds-major-flaws-in-mwaa-s]

**Silver Line legacy.** The Silver Line Phase 2 required MWAA to settle more than $200 million in contractor cost overruns in 2022, with Dulles Toll Road users absorbing approximately $188 million of the increase. [Source: https://www.washingtonpost.com/transportation/2022/09/08/silver-line-metro-mwaa/] This history shapes how MWAA leadership evaluates risk on the next large capital program. The lesson the institution absorbed is not "manage contractors better" — it is "avoid situations where cost overruns become congressional news."

**Implication for sterile corridor capital:** A capital program that is technically defensible but politically invisible (wider corridors, redundant escalator banks, over-built vertical circulation at AeroTrain-to-concourse handoffs) is precisely the program that gets deferred, descoped, or eliminated when the Silver Line cost-overrun institutional memory is still active. MWAA is not a reckless spender — it is a risk-averse institution in a fishbowl, and that risk aversion is structurally biased against infrastructure that doesn't produce a naming opportunity.

### IAD's $22 Billion Moment: Politics as Funding Risk

The Trump administration DOT issued a Request for Information in December 2025 inviting developers, architects, and engineers to propose enhancements for IAD, with Secretary Duffy specifically highlighting the replacement of mobile lounges. As of May 2026, the plan that emerged from that process — approximately $22 billion, including a $3.75 billion underground subterranean central spine that would expand AeroTrain connectivity and render the mobile lounges obsolete — has no confirmed federal funding mechanism. The financing structure reported is "$21.8 billion in new bonds and $1.1 billion in airport fees," implying primary reliance on MWAA bond issuance against future revenue streams. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/]

DOT did not respond to press requests for comment on the proposal as of the reporting date. The DOT's RFI page does not specify funding mechanisms. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/iad-rfi]

**The political economy problem this creates for sterile corridor investment:** When a $22 billion program is under discussion, everything else — including the sterile corridor resilience investments the thesis advocates — is subordinated to the headline program. A planning process that is waiting on DOT approval for a $22 billion bond program cannot simultaneously prioritize incremental investments in corridor redundancy. The political logic of the moment pushes toward transformational announcements and against incremental hardening of existing infrastructure.

### Federal Funding Pathways: What Is Actually Available

**Airport Improvement Program (AIP).** Under 49 U.S.C. § 47110, AIP funds are available for "airport development" as defined in 49 U.S.C. § 47102. Terminal corridor improvements within the sterile area are nominally eligible as "areas directly related to the movement of passengers and baggage in air commerce." But AIP discretionary grants at large hubs face intense competition. A corridor-widening project that cannot demonstrate a measurable safety or capacity impact — expressed in countable outputs — will not compete effectively against gate additions, runway safety area improvements, or pavement rehabilitation projects. The AIP's formula/discretionary split further constrains this: formula apportionments to large hubs are consumed by the airport's own capital priority list before discretionary grants are relevant. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip]

**BIL/IIJA Airport Terminal Program (ATP).** The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law allocated $5 billion over five years (approximately $1 billion annually, FY2022-FY2026) for competitive terminal grants, with an 80 percent federal cost share at large and medium hub airports. ATP-eligible projects include "safe, sustainable and accessible airport terminals" broadly. Sterile corridor improvements — particularly if framed as ADA accessibility enhancements or resilience improvements — are nominally eligible. The FY2026 Notice of Funding Opportunity had a January 15, 2026 submission deadline. [Source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/12/11/2025-22526/airport-terminal-program-fy-2026-funding-opportunity] The practical constraint: ATP grants have been oversubscribed at every cycle. Projects that advance in the competition have visible outcomes — new checkpoint buildings (as at DCA under Project Journey, awarded $269.8 million against a $1 billion overall program), new concourses, ADA accessibility upgrades. A sterile corridor hardening project without a headline feature will struggle in this competition unless it is bundled into a larger program scope.

**Passenger Facility Charges (PFC).** Under 14 CFR § 158.15(b)(6), PFC funds are eligible for "construction of gates and related areas at which passengers are enplaned or deplaned and other areas directly related to the movement of passengers and baggage in air commerce within the boundaries of the airport." Sterile corridor construction is eligible under this definition. But the $4.50 cap — set in 2000 and confirmed in place by P.L. 118-63 (FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024) — limits total PFC revenue per enplanement. For MWAA, PFC revenue backs bond issuance for Project Journey at DCA and will need to support the IAD capital program. There is no slack in the PFC stack for incremental corridor investment unless it is bundled with a larger financed program. [Source: https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/house-bill/3935; https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/158.15]

**DCA Project Journey as the in-place renovation comparator.** TSA opened two new 50,000-square-foot security checkpoint buildings at DCA on November 9, 2021, as the centerpiece of the $1 billion Project Journey program. The project was funded by MWAA with bonds issued against future PFC revenue. The construction phasing required TSA security program amendments to manage sterile area boundaries during active construction — exactly the 49 CFR 1542.107 process described above. The outcome was successful, but the project was a TSA-facing investment (new checkpoint capacity, new screening lanes), not a sterile circulation hardening project. The political lesson TSA draws from Project Journey is that checkpoint investment is a joint MWAA-TSA win; corridor resilience investment is an MWAA win only. [Source: https://www.tsa.gov/news/press/releases/2021/11/09/reagan-national-airport-opens-two-new-security-checkpoint-buildings]

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: DCA Project Journey and TSA Construction Phasing

Reagan National Airport's Project Journey ($1 billion, completed in phases 2019-2021) required constructing two new 50,000-square-foot checkpoint buildings while maintaining sterile area operations at a facility with three concourses and no shutdown option. The sterile area reconfiguration — moving the security boundary outward to encompass new connecting corridors between concourses — required formal ASP amendments under 49 CFR § 1542.105. The construction phasing was managed through a series of interim security arrangements, each requiring TSA written approval before the physical work could proceed.

The outcome expanded total sterile area square footage, added connecting corridors between Concourses B and C, and increased checkpoint lane capacity from 20 to 23 lanes (with provision for 28). But the project design was checkpoint-centric: the new sterile corridors connecting the buildings to the concourses were sized to clear the checkpoint outflow under normal conditions, not to maintain throughput when one segment of the new configuration is temporarily closed. This is the design failure the thesis identifies — a project that solved the checkpoint problem while embedding a corridor resilience problem that will not become visible until peak-hour disruption exposes it.

**Regulatory lesson:** TSA's construction engagement process under 49 CFR 1542.107 is not a veto — it is a sequencing constraint. Projects that engage TSA early, with a drafted Construction Security Plan ready for incorporation into the ASP amendment, move faster than projects that treat TSA engagement as a late-stage step. The 72-hour notification window for short-term changes and the 30-day amendment process for long-term changes are workable if they are built into the project schedule from the start.

### Case Study 2: MWAA OIG Audit and the Capital Oversight Effect

The 2012 DOT OIG audit of MWAA — triggered by congressional request, resulting in a November 2012 House T&I Committee hearing — found pervasive contracting failures and governance weaknesses. The institutional response was not primarily procedural reform; it was institutional risk aversion. The board's appetite for large capital programs contracted for several years following the audit.

The Silver Line Phase 2 cost overruns (more than $200 million settled in 2022) reinforced this pattern. The political and reputational cost of a cost overrun at MWAA is disproportionate to the cost itself because MWAA operates under a microscope that most local airport authorities do not. The result is that MWAA's capital program systematically favors projects with clear, auditable, countable outputs — gate counts, lane counts, square feet of new terminal — over projects whose value lies in resilience and redundancy. Wider corridors and redundant escalator banks do not produce auditable output metrics that withstand OIG scrutiny. "We over-built this corridor so that when one segment fails, operations continue" is not a narrative that survives the next congressional oversight cycle if a committee member decides the extra width looks like waste.

**Political lesson:** The 2012 audit and its aftermath are not ancient history at MWAA. The institutional memory is current. Any capital program advocating for resilience investment in non-headline infrastructure must be legible in terms that survive the OIG audit framework — which means specifying measurable outcomes, not just operational arguments.

### Case Study 3: PFC Cap as a Permanent Constraint on Non-Headline Capital

The Passenger Facility Charge cap of $4.50 per enplanement was set in the Wendell H. Ford Aviation Investment and Reform Act for the 21st Century (AIR-21, P.L. 106-181, 2000). In real terms, $4.50 in 2024 is equivalent to approximately $2.70 in 2000 dollars — a 40 percent erosion of purchasing power over 24 years. Every FAA reauthorization cycle since 2000 has included proposals to raise the cap; every one has failed. The 2024 FAA Reauthorization Act (P.L. 118-63) did not raise the cap, despite sustained advocacy from ACI-NA and AAAE seeking an increase to $8.50 or higher, and despite airline opposition from Airlines for America (A4A) which successfully framed any increase as a "hidden tax" on passengers. [Source: https://aviationweek.com/air-transport/airports-networks/us-airports-face-significant-infrastructure-funding-shortfall-says-aci-na-ceo]

The political economy is straightforward: airlines have a concentrated interest in opposing PFC increases (every dollar of PFC revenue is a dollar they cannot capture in ticket price), while airports have diffuse interests in supporting them (the revenue benefits are spread across hundreds of projects over decades). Airlines are better organized, make larger campaign contributions, and have a cleaner political message. This is not going to change.

**What it means for corridor investment:** MWAA's bond capacity backed by PFC revenue is finite and spoken for. A $22 billion capital program financed primarily by bonds against PFC revenue leaves no margin for additional bond issuance for corridor resilience projects that are not already inside that program scope. The PFC cap is not a constraint that will be resolved on any construction project timeline.

## What Can and Cannot Be Moved

**Fixed constraints (political half-life: multi-decade):**
- PFC cap at $4.50. Not moving. Congress confirmed this in 2024. Airlines' structural advantage in the PFC debate will not shift absent a political realignment that does not appear imminent.
- TSA's Security Program amendment process. The process is workable but cannot be shortened without TSA discretion. The 30-day review windows are statutory minimums; emergency procedures exist for genuine emergencies, not construction scheduling convenience.
- MWAA's board structure and oversight exposure. The bistate compact and DOT lease are not subject to modification on project timelines. Congressional scrutiny of MWAA capital programs is a permanent condition, not a phase.

**Movable but slow (political half-life: 5-10 years):**
- FAA updating AC 150/5360-13A to incorporate current pedestrian flow research. ACRP Problem Statement 24-1058 is the predicate. If funded and completed, a new ACRP report could provide empirical corridor-width formulas that MWAA planners can use as a design floor rather than a ceiling. The FAA would then need to revise the AC. This cycle typically takes 5-8 years from problem statement to AC revision.
- ATP (BIL/IIJA) program continuation beyond FY2026. The program expires with the BIL authorization. Continuation would require a new appropriation or authorization. The political environment for infrastructure spending in 2026 is uncertain.

**Movable with the right framing (project-timeline possibilities):**
- TSA engagement on sterile boundary phasing. Projects that engage TSA at the concept stage — before design development — can negotiate construction security plans that are incorporated into the ASP amendment process efficiently. This is not "moving" the regulatory framework; it is using it correctly. The difference between a project that treats TSA as a late-stage approver and one that treats TSA as a co-designer of the phasing plan is measured in months of schedule risk.
- AIP and ATP project framing. Corridor resilience investment that cannot win a grant competition on its own terms may win if bundled inside a larger program scope with headline outputs. DCA Project Journey's corridor improvements were funded as part of a checkpoint replacement program. The same logic can work at IAD if the sterile corridor investment is inside the AeroTrain expansion program scope, not a standalone request.
