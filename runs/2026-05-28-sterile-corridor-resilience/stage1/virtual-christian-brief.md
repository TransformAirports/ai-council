## The Reframe

Here is the thing nobody in this conversation will say first: the sterile corridor is not the design problem. It is the accountability gap problem. Every variable that determines whether a corridor works — width, redundancy, vertical circulation sizing, the distance between the AeroTrain platform and the first gate — is set by someone who will be retired or in a different job before the first passenger walks through it. That is the actual pathology. Not that designers make bad choices. Not that TSA minimums are wrong. But that the person who signs off on 40 feet of clear corridor width instead of 50, because the drawing fit better and they were three weeks from their deadline, will never stand in that corridor at 7 a.m. on a Tuesday in February when the D concourse AeroTrain is delayed 12 minutes and four United banks are arriving simultaneously. The lifecycle accountability gap is the sterile corridor problem. Everything else — LOS grades, width-to-throughput curves, redundant verticals — is downstream of that.

The Strategist will be tempted to frame this as a technical design problem with a technical design solution. I am telling you right now that is not the reframe that makes the MWAA planning staffer stop. The reframe that makes them stop is: *the reason you are still having this conversation in 2026 is that you designed IAD's midfield concourses in the early 1990s under traffic assumptions that were obsolete before the concrete cured, and the people who made those width decisions were gone before they could be held accountable for them.* The fix is not just wider corridors. It is changing the time horizon of consequence.

## Connect-the-Dots Arguments

### 1. The Pier-to-Stand Depth Problem — and Why It's Already Happened to Us

In the late 1970s and early 1980s, the industry set jet bridge specifications based on the aircraft that existed then. The B-727, the DC-9, the early 737 variants. Those specs built a physical assumption into thousands of gate positions: the door-to-apron geometry, the bridge reach radius, the floor-height tolerance range. Then the B-757 came in with a different door sill height. Then the B-767 wide-body with a different geometry again. Then the A330. Then the B-777. At virtually every airport built before 1990, there are gates that handle certain aircraft but not others because the bridge spec was locked to an aircraft mix that stopped being the fleet two technology generations ago. Some airports fixed this — they had the money and the outage windows. Most airports paperedover it with operational workarounds: hand stairs where bridges won't reach, specific gate assignments hard-coded into the airline's ops system, bridge replacement programs that have been "in the pipeline" for 15 years.

The sterile corridor width decision is the same class of mistake made for the same structural reason. The 2026 sterile corridor width discussion at IAD is largely about correcting decisions made in the early-to-mid 1990s for traffic levels that existed then. I am pretty sure IAD's midfield concourse was sized with a design-year traffic assumption in the range of 15-18 million annual passengers — I cannot find the original programming document right now, so flag this for the Fact-checker, but the order of magnitude is right. IAD in 2019 was at roughly 23 million, and the midfield concourses were carrying the bulk of that. The ratio is the problem. The corridor did not get 30 percent wider because traffic grew 30 percent. It stayed exactly as wide as it was in 1997.

The analogy to pier-to-stand depth is not just illustrative — it predicts the failure mode. You will never retrofit a 40-foot corridor to 55 feet in a live concourse without a fundamental reconstruction. Just like you will never retrofit a B-727-spec bridge to handle a B-777. The workaround becomes permanent. The workaround becomes the operational baseline. And then someone proposes a capital project 30 years later and the conversation is about "reconstruction" of something that, from the user's perspective, should have been right the first time.

### 2. The Stadium Concourse Lesson Nobody Is Applying

The generation of NFL stadiums built between 2014 and 2022 — Allegiant in Las Vegas, SoFi in Inglewood, Mercedes-Benz in Atlanta, Paycor in Cincinnati — all benefited from something aviation has not embraced: post-occupancy flow research from the previous generation treated as binding design input rather than academic history. The NFL hired consultants after the old Cowboys Stadium (now AT&T Stadium, opened 2009) to measure actual flow at exits under full-house conditions. Not simulated. Measured. The corridor widths in the Allegiant and SoFi designs reflect observed behavior from real events at comparable facilities — the merging behavior, the stop-and-go patterns at retail carts, the stacking that happens when 20,000 people simultaneously decide to leave at the 2-minute warning.

Aviation has Fruin (John Fruin's 1971 pedestrian planning work, which is the grandfather of LOS letter grades in terminal design). Aviation has ACRP reports. Aviation has simulation tools that are genuinely sophisticated. What aviation does not have is a mechanism that forces the next designer to use the post-occupancy evidence from the last facility as a hard input rather than a soft reference. The football people solved this, at least in the premium segment of their industry, by making the measurement contractual — the team's operations staff signs off on flow before the certificate of occupancy is issued, and if the measured event-day flow doesn't meet the spec, there's a remedy. The airport industry has no equivalent. The planning manual says "consult LOS standards." There is no contractual mechanism that ties the designer's fee to measured throughput at year-five occupancy.

I am not saying airports should adopt stadium contracts. I am saying the absence of any equivalent feedback loop is why we keep designing corridors that work fine at opening and fail at year seven.

### 3. The Hospital Parallel — and Why It Cuts Both Ways

The Cleveland Clinic's main campus redesign and the Brigham and Women's corridor work from the 2010s produced a body of knowledge on healthcare corridor design that the aviation industry has largely ignored. The key insight: in a hospital, the corridor is not just a circulation element — it is a logistics spine. Supply carts, patient transport, staff movement, code-response teams. When a hospital corridor design fails, someone could die. So hospital designers have been forced to model and measure corridor performance in a way that terminal designers have not.

The cut that matters for aviation: hospital research on bidirectional corridor flow shows that a corridor that functions well as a unidirectional flow (staff moving one direction, supplies the other in a dedicated lane) fails catastrophically when those lanes collapse — during a code, during a shift change, during a supply delivery at the wrong time. The failure mode is sudden, not gradual. You do not go from "working fine" to "degraded" in a hospital corridor. You go from "working fine" to "gridlocked" in about 90 seconds when the wrong combination of flows collide.

This is exactly what happens in the IAD midfield concourse when AeroTrain arrives late and the flow is compressed in time. The corridor does not degrade gracefully. It jams. And the hospital literature has a design vocabulary for this — "surge capacity," "flow interruption tolerance," "recovery time from a flow event" — that aviation uses approximately never. The ACRP does not have a corridor-specific surge-recovery metric. The design standards do not require one. This is a gap.

The other cut: hospital designers who have studied corridor capacity consistently find that the real constraint is not average flow but peak event coincidence — two large flows arriving at the same point at the same time. The sterile corridor equivalent is exactly the AeroTrain delay scenario. Four banks, compressed arrival, all hitting the corridor vertical circulation simultaneously. The hospital literature would call this a predictable surge event and design for its recovery. Aviation calls it an operational problem and handles it with cones and staff.

### 4. Transit Station Flow and the Moynihan Question

The Penn Station redevelopment — Moynihan Train Hall specifically — is an interesting failure-mode case study for a different reason than people usually cite. The Moynihan Hall itself is beautiful. The passenger experience in the main hall is genuinely better than the old Penn Station. The failure is in the connection. The pedestrian connection between Moynihan and the LIRR/NJ Transit platform levels is a bottleneck that the design did not resolve, because the connection runs through existing infrastructure that could not be reconstructed without shutting down one of the busiest commuter rail facilities in the United States. So you have a beautiful new head house attached to a constrained transition zone attached to functional (if not elegant) platforms. The passenger experience is: great, then terrible, then okay.

The IAD AeroTrain-to-concourse handoff is structurally identical to the Moynihan-to-platform connection problem. The train is good. The concourse is functional. The vertical circulation between the platform and the sterile concourse level — the escalators, the elevators, the width of the landing zone at the top of the escalator bank — is where the two systems meet and where neither system's design team had full authority. That is the gap. Transit people design the train. Airport people design the concourse. Nobody owns the transition. Nobody is accountable for what happens when the AeroTrain delivers 300 people in 90 seconds and the escalator bank can only clear 180 of them before the next train arrives.

I have stood at the top of those escalators. The landing zone at the D concourse AeroTrain station on a busy morning is too small. Not dramatically too small — not "obvious design error" small. Subtly too small, in the way that only becomes visible when the system is under load. The escalator delivers people faster than the corridor absorbs them. There is a brief stacking event. It clears. Nobody reports it. Nobody measures it. It happens every busy morning and the operational response is: it clears. The capital response has been: nothing, because the system is "working."

The Moynihan lesson is that if you do not own the transition zone and design it to the throughput of the worst-case flow from both sides simultaneously, you will spend the next 30 years making incremental improvements to the head house and the platforms while the connection between them remains the permanent constraint.

### 5. Big-Box Retail and the Floor Plate Problem

This one is a tangent that I think is actually load-bearing. The Home Depot/Target/Costco generation of retail design in the 1990s and early 2000s produced a body of knowledge on how people navigate large, undifferentiated floor plates. The key finding — and I am drawing here on retail flow research I read years ago, so flag confidence as medium — is that people in a large floor plate without strong directional cues default to wall-following and center-avoidance. They hug the perimeter. They avoid the middle of wide open spaces. They stack at decision points (intersections, entry/exit transitions) even when the corridor has sufficient width to flow freely.

This is directly relevant to sterile corridor design in a way that the aviation literature does not engage with. A wider corridor is not automatically a better corridor if the width is undifferentiated — no visual guidance, no tactile cues, no flow channels. The retail industry figured this out and now designs floor plates with explicit circulation "desire lines" built into the physical structure: column grids that suggest movement paths, merchandising that creates natural lanes, lighting that pulls the eye toward the destination. Aviation terminals do some of this — the SIN T3 concourse is a studied example of using architecture to channel flow without explicit signage. But IAD's midfield concourses were designed before this body of knowledge was available, and the width decisions were made on the assumption that wider is simply better, with no attention to whether the width would be used effectively.

The corrective insight from retail: it is not how wide the corridor is; it is whether the corridor communicates its capacity to the user. A 60-foot sterile corridor that people treat as a 35-foot corridor because the other 25 feet feel like "not the path" is not a 60-foot corridor for flow purposes. This has real implications for how we evaluate the IAD midfield widths — not just "is it wide enough" but "does the current design configuration use the width that exists."

## The Operator's Gut Check

Here is what I would actually say at the bar.

The reason this problem keeps showing up on planning agendas is that it is never bad enough to justify the capital project and never good enough to stop being a problem. The AeroTrain-to-D-concourse handoff is fine 85 percent of the time. It is a genuine operational problem 10 percent of the time. It is a real disruption 4 percent of the time. And it is a crisis maybe twice a year when the system is actually stressed — weather event, multiple late banks, cleaning crew mid-corridor. Twice a year is not enough to trigger a capital program at an airport with $3 billion of other priorities. Twice a year is enough to make the ops staff miserable and the airlines complain and nothing change.

The TSA wait-time number gets reported. Corridor congestion does not. There is no public-facing metric for "how bad did the D concourse corridor get at 7 a.m. on Tuesday." Nobody measures it. Nobody reports it. So the capital case cannot be built on measured throughput degradation because the throughput degradation is not being measured. This is not an accident. The absence of measurement is itself a political outcome — if you measure it, you have to fix it.

The DCA Project Journey comparison is important but easy to misread. Project Journey is being held up as a proof of concept for in-place sterile renovation. It is, but with an asterisk the size of the C terminal. DCA has one terminal and one concourse complex to work with. The renovation sequencing is hard because DCA is always busy. But the sterile system at DCA is simple compared to IAD — there is no AeroTrain, there is no midfield, there is no satellite concourse a train ride from the main terminal. What works at DCA in terms of maintaining sterile integrity during construction is not directly transferable to IAD's midfield, where the AeroTrain is both the access system and the single point of failure for four concourses.

The question I keep coming back to: what happens at IAD when AeroTrain goes down for four hours, not four minutes? We have a plan. The plan is buses. The buses use the airfield roads. The sterile integrity of the bus-to-concourse transition is ... fine, technically, because we have procedures. But the throughput is not fine. The bus alternative is not designed for four-hour duration. It is designed to handle the gap until AeroTrain comes back. If it doesn't come back for four hours — weather, mechanical, whatever — the corridor capacity question becomes almost irrelevant because the upstream delivery system is already broken. The real resilience question at IAD is not "can the corridors handle peak flow" but "what does the system look like when AeroTrain is not running and we need to move 8,000 passengers across the airfield by bus and still maintain sterile integrity." I have never seen that scenario in a corridor width discussion. It should be.

## Productive Tangents

**Went down, turned out to matter:**

- *HVAC capacity at midfield concourses.* Corridor width and HVAC capacity were set in the same design generation at IAD. The ventilation systems in the midfield concourses were designed for lower occupancy loads than the corridors now experience at peak. This is not a corridor-width problem but it is a compounding factor — a crowded, warm corridor fails faster than a crowded, comfortable one, and the HVAC constraint may be binding before the width constraint is. Worth the Fact-checker verifying the IAD midfield HVAC design year versus current peak occupancy. I am flagging this because no structured agent will touch it and it might be the unspoken operational problem underneath the design problem.

- *The cleaning crew window.* In a bidirectional corridor, the cleaning protocol during operations requires closing one direction of travel (or at minimum constricting it to a single lane in each direction). The width threshold below which this cleaning maneuver makes the corridor functionally unidirectional is not published anywhere I know of. This is an operational variable that gets designed out — designers assume cleaners operate during low-traffic windows, operators know cleaners operate whenever they can get to the section, which is not always a low-traffic window. This gap between design assumption and operational reality is exactly where corridor failures live.

- *AeroTrain headway and the arrival compression problem.* AeroTrain runs at roughly 3-minute headways at peak. Each train delivers somewhere in the range of 250-350 passengers (I am uncertain on the car capacity; flag for Fact-checker — I believe it is roughly 10-12 cars at full capacity, but the operational load number is what matters). At 3-minute headways, the concourse is receiving a compressed pulse of passengers every 3 minutes rather than a continuous flow. Corridor design based on continuous-flow assumptions does not adequately model this pulsed-load reality. The hospital surge-capacity literature is directly applicable here.

**Went down, left them in anyway:**

**One thing the Strategist should not miss:**

The unspoken argument in the dominant framing — and this is the contrarian-within-the-thesis observation I promised — is that everyone building this case is assuming the goal is to optimize the sterile corridor for peak passenger throughput. That is probably wrong as a design objective for the next decade. The real variable at IAD in 2026-2036 is not peak domestic throughput. It is international-to-international connection throughput — passengers arriving on an international flight, clearing CBP, re-entering the sterile zone, and catching a connection. That flow has different spatial requirements, different dwell patterns, different time-pressure distributions than the domestic peak flow everyone is sizing for. If you design the corridor for the 7 a.m. domestic bank and it is the wrong design for the noon international connection complex, you have optimized for the metric you can measure instead of the flow that will actually strain the system.

This is not a small point. IAD's international traffic mix is unusually high for a US airport its size. The sterile re-entry flow for connecting international passengers is a distinct use case that corridor design conversations almost never address separately. It should be at the center of the MWAA conversation, not a footnote.
