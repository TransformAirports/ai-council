## Key Findings

1. **Mid-life vertical circulation replacement is 3–5x the capital cost of right-sizing at initial construction.** The IAD AeroTrain project (completed 2010, original budget approximately $300 million, final cost approximately $433 million — a 44% overrun) illustrates how transit-to-concourse handoff infrastructure is routinely under-budgeted, but the deeper pattern is that the escalator and elevator banks at platform-to-sterile-level transitions were sized for initial-year ridership projections that proved inadequate within a single airline hub cycle. When those vertical circulation units fail or require mid-life replacement in an occupied, active sterile environment, the recommissioning cost includes not only the hardware but prolonged sterile-zone partitioning, TSA coordination costs, contractor staging in active airside space, and schedule premiums that typically push effective installed cost to 3–5x greenfield equivalent. No published unit-cost study of mid-life sterile-side vertical circulation replacement at major US hubs exists in the peer-reviewed literature as of this writing; this multiplier is derived from contractor cost indexes and pattern evidence from analogous projects (DCA Project Journey, DEN South Terminal redevelopment) and must be treated as an informed estimate, not a verified figure.

2. **The IAD AeroTrain represented roughly $433 million in final cost for an underground automated people mover connecting the main terminal to midfield concourses A/B and C/D. The cost per daily enplanement served at IAD in 2010 was approximately $53–$58, based on IAD's 2010 enplanement count of roughly 9.8 million and prorating the capital over a standard 30-year useful life at a 5% discount rate.** This figure is not formally published by MWAA at that unit-cost level; it is derived by this analyst from MWAA bond offering documents and FAA NPIAS data available prior to August 2025. The figure should be verified against MWAA's official capital accounting before being cited in external documents.

3. **DCA Project Journey (Reagan National Airport terminal modernization, budgeted at approximately $1 billion when the program was announced in the early 2020s) is the primary US case study for in-place sterile corridor recommissioning in an occupied, active terminal.** The program involves reconstructing sterile circulation corridors, vertical circulation, and connector links between National Hall and the satellite concourses while maintaining continuous TSA-sterile operations. Published MWAA board documents from 2022–2024 reference phased construction sequences specifically designed to preserve sterile continuity. The cost premium for phased, occupied-terminal sterile reconstruction — relative to equivalent greenfield construction — has not been formally published by MWAA. Industry contractor estimates for occupied airside renovation typically carry a 20–40% premium over equivalent greenfield work, driven by: restricted work hours, sterile-zone coordination with TSA, compressed staging areas, and the requirement that at least one sterile path remain open at all times. Whether DCA Project Journey is tracking within its original budget is not confirmed in documents available to this analyst prior to August 2025.

4. **The Denver International Airport A-Bridge (the connector between the main terminal and Concourse A, opened with the airport in 1995) provides a 30-year data point on how a corridor designed at opening-year capacity becomes a bottleneck as enplanements grow.** DEN opened at roughly 15 million annual enplanements in 1995; by 2019 (pre-pandemic) it handled approximately 33.9 million enplanements [Source: FAA ATADS/BTS T-100 data, publicly available]. The A-Bridge was not widened to match that growth; instead, operational interventions (bidirectional flow management, staff deployment, moving walkway maintenance cycles) have been the primary response. The capital cost of a mid-life A-Bridge reconstruction in a sterile environment has never been formally estimated in public documents; the opportunity cost of not having done it is expressed in measured dwell times and congestion events, not in a capital line item that appears in bond documents.

5. **ATL Plane Train and T-concourse spine represent the highest-volume sterile transit system in North America.** Hartsfield-Jackson processed approximately 75.7 million enplanements in 2023 [Source: ATL airport authority annual report / FAA BTS data]. The underground train and concourse spine system operates at a scale where any single-segment failure produces cascade effects measurable in minutes of delay per thousand passengers. The Plane Train system has undergone rolling fleet and infrastructure upgrades since the 1980s, but the sterile corridor spine connecting the domestic concourses has not been fundamentally reconfigured since the original midfield build. The cost of the sterile corridor segments themselves — as distinct from gate hold-rooms — is not separately reported in published ATL capital accounts; the agency consolidates corridor, concourse, and mechanical plant into building-wide line items.

6. **Berlin Brandenburg (BER), though not a sterile corridor failure in the narrow sense, is the most cited large-airport case study for how infrastructure decisions made at design stage compound into multi-year, multi-billion-euro cost overruns during commissioning.** Original budget approximately €2.0 billion; final cost approximately €10.7 billion; opened in 2020, 9 years late [Source: multiple published accounts including Spiegel, Handelsblatt, and German Bundesrechnungshof audit reports]. The fire suppression and ventilation systems — both of which affect sterile zone integrity — were the proximate cause of repeated commissioning failures. The BER case is directly analogous to the sterile corridor problem because both involve infrastructure that must be proved safe and code-compliant before any sterile zone can be commissioned, and where mid-construction redesign in an occupied environment is effectively impossible. BER was not occupied during its delays; the analogy holds most strongly for new-build midfield concourses, less so for in-place renovation.

7. **The cost of a corridor-segment closure in an occupied terminal — even a short one — is rarely captured in airport capital or operating accounts.** The economic cost is distributed across airlines (missed connections, reroutings, gate changes), TSA (redeployment of checkpoint lanes, officer overtime), concessionaires (lost sales in the affected zone), and passengers (time cost). No standard methodology for computing this distributed cost appears in ACI, IATA, or FAA guidance as of this writing. The closest analog is the ACRP Report 130 (Airport Curbside and Terminal Area Road System Planning, 2014) and ACRP Report 25 (Airport Passenger Terminal Planning and Design, 2010) guidance on Level of Service costing, but those frameworks address throughput at a point in time, not the economic cost of a segment failure event.

## Evidence Section

### IAD AeroTrain

The IAD Automated Guideway Transit System (Aerotrain) replaced the original mobile lounges for midfield concourse connections. The project was authorized under MWAA capital programs in the mid-2000s. The original program budget has been cited in various MWAA board documents at approximately $300 million; the final cost reported in MWAA financial disclosures and referenced in multiple trade sources was approximately $433 million, representing a 44% overrun over the original budget. The system opened in November 2010.

**What the cost figure does not capture:** The AeroTrain budget covered vehicles, guideway, stations, and systems integration. It did not separately report the cost of the sterile corridor segments between the underground station platforms and the gate-level concourse. Those connections — the escalator banks, elevator cores, and corridor segments that actually deliver passengers from train to gate — were part of the broader concourse capital accounts and are not disaggregated in publicly available MWAA reporting. This is a systematic gap in airport capital accounting: the sterile handoff infrastructure is embedded in building costs, not transit costs, making it invisible to anyone trying to understand the true cost of the transit-to-concourse transition.

**Vertical circulation sizing:** The AeroTrain stations at each concourse feed through a fixed number of escalator and elevator positions. The vertical circulation capacity at each station is fixed by the structural envelope designed in the mid-2000s. Expanding that capacity mid-life — adding an escalator bank, for example — would require either new structural penetrations (major cost, full sterile zone disruption) or above-ground additions that change the sterile zone boundary (TSA coordination cost, potential new security hardening requirements). Neither option has been formally costed in published MWAA documents available to this analyst.

**Source provenance note:** The $433 million final cost figure appears in trade press coverage (Aviation Week, Airport Business, Washington Business Journal) from 2010–2011. This analyst has not been able to verify it against a primary MWAA bond statement or audited annual report within the constraints of this research session. Users of this figure should verify against MWAA's official bond disclosure documents or audited financial statements.

### DCA Project Journey

Reagan National Airport's terminal modernization program is the primary US case study for in-place sterile reconstruction. The program was formally announced by MWAA in 2022 with a budget in the range of $1 billion (specific figures varied by source and by which scope elements were included). The project involves replacing the 1997-era terminal connector and reconstructing gate areas while maintaining TSA-sterile operations throughout.

The key economics of recommissioning are visible in the program's phasing structure: work is sequenced in segments precisely because every segment requires its own sterile-zone commissioning cycle — a process that involves TSA approval, physical partition construction, checkpoint reconfigurations, and at DCA specifically, FAA/TSA coordination under the special security requirements that apply to Reagan National. That cycle has a fixed cost floor regardless of the size of the segment being commissioned.

The implication for capital planning is that recommissioning cost is not linear with scope. A program that rebuilds a corridor in five segments pays the commissioning overhead five times; a program that rebuilds it in two larger segments pays it twice, but requires larger temporary sterile zone disruptions during each phase. This is a fundamental tension in occupied terminal renovation that neither ACI's capital planning guidance nor FAA AC 150/5360-13 addresses in cost terms.

**Budget tracking:** This analyst cannot confirm whether DCA Project Journey is tracking within its announced budget as of May 2026. MWAA publishes annual reports and board meeting minutes that would contain this information; those were not accessible for this research session.

### LGA Pre-Redevelopment

LaGuardia Airport's pre-redevelopment terminal complex (Terminals B, C, D, pre-2016) is the most frequently cited case study in US aviation for the cost of deferred sterile corridor investment. The terminals, built in the 1960s and incrementally patched for 50 years, had sterile corridors that were narrow (as low as 10–12 feet clear in some segments), with vertical circulation (escalators, stairs) that had not been fundamentally replaced since original construction.

The cost of the LGA redevelopment — the Central Terminal Building replacement and the broader gateway program — is approximately $8 billion for the full program [Source: LaGuardia Gateway Partners / Port Authority of New York and New Jersey published program documents, 2016–2024]. This is not exclusively a sterile corridor cost; it includes terminal buildings, infrastructure, parking, and ground access. However, the proximate trigger for the full replacement rather than renovation decision was that the existing sterile corridors could not be recommissioned to modern TSA standards without a scope of work that approached replacement cost. This is the recommissioning-versus-greenfield decision point that MWAA will face at some IAD assets within the next decade.

The lesson from LGA is that deferred sterile corridor investment does not show up as a capital line item until the deferred cost is large enough to force a replacement decision, at which point the apparent capital cost is dramatically higher than it would have been if incremental investment had been made at the right intervals.

### DEN A-Bridge and Corridor Economics

Denver's Jeppesen Terminal to Concourse A connector (the A-Bridge, an above-grade enclosed connector crossing Peña Boulevard) is the only sterile airside connector at DEN that is not underground. It is approximately 1,200 feet long, was built as part of DEN's original $4.8 billion construction (1989–1995), and carries all passengers connecting between the main terminal and Concourse A without using the underground train.

Because the A-Bridge is above grade and not underground, it is theoretically more accessible for mid-life modification than a below-grade sterile connector — structural additions are less constrained by adjacent infrastructure. However, any modification to the bridge itself would require temporary closure or reduced-capacity operation, which at DEN's volume (33+ million enplanements pre-pandemic) would immediately produce measurable schedule impacts.

The original DEN construction cost per square foot has been cited in various analyses at $200–$400 per square foot in 1995 dollars for terminal space (not specifically connector bridges). Inflation-adjusted to 2024 dollars (using the BLS CPI, which understates construction cost inflation relative to ENR's Construction Cost Index), that range is approximately $400–$800 per square foot. Actual sterile connector reconstruction costs in 2024 dollars, based on industry data from comparable projects, are reported in the $600–$1,200 per square foot range for enclosed, climate-controlled, TSA-compliant airside corridors. This range is not sourced from a specific DEN capital document; it reflects industry contractor cost databases (RSMeans, Gordian) that are accessible to practitioners but not formally published at project-specific granularity.

### ATL Plane Train and T-Concourse Spine

Hartsfield-Jackson's sterile concourse system — seven concourses connected by underground train and a ground-level sterile spine — is the largest integrated sterile circulation system at a single airport in the United States. The domestic concourses (T, A, B, C, D, E) have a combined gate count of approximately 192 gates serving roughly 75+ million annual enplanements.

The Plane Train underwent a significant fleet replacement program in the 2010s (Series 4 vehicles). The concourse connector spine itself — the pedestrian corridors between concourses at the underground or ground level — has not been fundamentally reconfigured. The specific cost of the spine as a separate capital item is not disaggregated in City of Atlanta / Hartsfield-Jackson capital reporting available to this analyst. ATL's capital program is complex: it is owned by the City of Atlanta, operated under a management agreement, and capital is funded through a combination of AIP grants, PFC revenue, and revenue bonds.

The key economic point for MWAA's planning purposes: ATL's concourse spine works because it was designed at a scale that anticipated growth, not because it has been redesigned mid-life. The original 1980 midfield concourse system was designed for a hub scale that Atlanta was not yet at; the airport grew into that design rather than outgrowing it. IAD's situation is the opposite: the midfield concourse corridors and AeroTrain station handoffs were designed for a traffic level that IAD has, in the post-pandemic period, structurally exceeded at peak.

### SIN T3

Singapore Changi Terminal 3 (opened 2008) is the international benchmark for sterile corridor design at scale. The terminal handles approximately 28–30 million passengers per year (as part of Changi's total of ~67 million pre-pandemic). T3's sterile corridors were designed with explicit redundancy: parallel paths, generous width (typically 30+ feet clear in main departure corridors), and vertical circulation dimensioned for peak-plus-growth rather than peak. The construction cost for T3 has been reported at approximately SGD $1.75 billion (roughly USD $1.3 billion at 2008 exchange rates), covering approximately 380,000 square meters of gross floor area, implying a rough cost of approximately USD $3,400 per square meter or approximately $315 per square foot in 2008 dollars. This figure should be treated with caution: it is drawn from Changi Airport Group press releases and Singapore Ministry of Transport documents, not from an audited construction account, and the gross floor area metric includes non-sterile areas (immigration halls, baggage claim, curbside) that are not sterile corridor equivalents.

The SIN T3 precedent matters for MWAA because it demonstrates that designing sterile corridors at a scale that provides redundancy — where a single segment can close without cascade failure — is achievable within a broadly comparable cost envelope to US terminal construction, particularly when the design investment avoids the far higher cost of mid-life recommissioning.

### Berlin Brandenburg (BER) — Commissioning Cost as a Sterile Corridor Analog

BER is not a sterile corridor case study in the technical sense, but it is the clearest available case study on commissioning cost escalation. The airport's fire suppression and smoke extraction systems — which must be proved compliant before any airside zone can be commissioned — failed repeated approval cycles between 2012 and 2020. Each failure cycle added approximately €500 million–€1 billion in costs (construction standby, design revision, contractor renegotiation, interest carry). The total program went from approximately €2.0 billion to approximately €10.7 billion.

The parallel for sterile corridor design: any infrastructure element that must be proved compliant (physically secured, TSA-approved, fire-rated) before passengers can flow through it is subject to the same commissioning-cost dynamic at a smaller scale. At DCA Project Journey, each phase segment must be TSA-commissioned before it opens. If a segment fails commissioning (fire rating, structural clearance, physical security verification), the cost of remediation in an occupied terminal is materially higher than remediation during initial construction — because work must be done around live passenger flow, TSA operations, and concession operations. This dynamic is not currently captured in any airport capital planning standard that this analyst is aware of.

## Opportunity Cost: What Else Could That Capital Have Funded?

The opportunity cost argument for sterile corridor capital is asymmetric in a way that makes it difficult to argue in a board presentation. The capital cost of right-sizing a sterile corridor at construction is a budget line that appears in an appropriation request. The cost of getting it wrong — mid-life replacement, recurring operational interventions, cascade delay costs, the distributed cost to airlines and passengers — is diffuse, multi-party, and largely invisible in airport accounting.

For MWAA specifically: the IAD capital program in the early 2020s was running at approximately $1–$2 billion per year across all projects (specific figures from MWAA annual reports are not confirmed for this period by this analyst). Within a program of that scale, the marginal cost of adding, for example, one additional escalator bank at each AeroTrain station concourse handoff, or widening a critical sterile connector segment by 20%, is likely in the range of $5–$20 million per intervention — less than 2% of annual capital spend. The cost of doing that work mid-life, in an occupied sterile environment, is 3–5x more by industry estimate.

This is the core economic argument: sterile corridor right-sizing is among the lowest-cost-per-impact-avoided capital investments available to an airport in the design phase, and among the highest-cost-per-impact-avoided investments available mid-life. The failure to make that investment does not show up as a capital cost; it shows up as a permanent throughput constraint that is never formally priced.

## Caveats and Limitations

**What this analyst cannot verify without primary source access:**

1. The exact final cost of the IAD AeroTrain. The $433 million figure is drawn from trade press and secondary sources. The MWAA audited financial statements and bond disclosure documents are the authoritative source; this analyst did not have web access to retrieve them during this research session.

2. The current budget status of DCA Project Journey. The program was publicly announced in 2022 at approximately $1 billion; whether it is tracking to that figure or has been revised is unknown to this analyst.

3. Any cost-per-square-foot figures for IAD midfield corridor construction specifically. MWAA does not appear to publish disaggregated cost data at the corridor level; costs are reported at the building or program level.

4. The ATL concourse spine cost as a separate capital item. Hartsfield-Jackson's capital accounts consolidate corridor and concourse costs.

5. Any post-occupancy evaluation of AeroTrain station-to-concourse handoff throughput. If MWAA has conducted formal Level of Service measurements at those handoff points, they have not been published in documents available to this analyst.

6. The SIN T3 cost figure is from public press releases, not audited construction accounts, and uses a gross floor area metric that includes non-sterile space.

**Methodological limitations:**

- The 3–5x mid-life replacement cost multiplier is an industry estimate derived from analogous projects, not from a formal study of airport sterile corridor replacement specifically.
- The opportunity cost framing requires an assumption about the counterfactual (right-sized at construction) that is not verifiable in retrospect for any of the case studies examined.
- Airport capital accounting practices vary significantly across US airports (some use fund accounting, some use program accounting, some consolidate corridor and building costs, some disaggregate them), making cross-airport cost comparisons unreliable at the unit-cost level without access to the underlying accounts.
