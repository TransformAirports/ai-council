## I. The Cleaning Crew at 6:45 a.m.

At 6:45 a.m. on a Tuesday in February, the Concourse C escalator bank at Dulles International is doing the work it does most mornings — moving roughly two hundred passengers from an AeroTrain that arrived at 6:39 up to the sterile concourse level, where four United banks are converging on a forty-minute departure window. One of the two escalators is running rough. Facilities flagged it on the 0500 walk; it has not tripped out yet. A cleaning crew is at the south end of the corridor, fifteen minutes past their scheduled clearance, because somebody spilled coffee at 5:15 and the second pass took longer than the contract assumes. The TSA checkpoint upstream is at three of four lanes. One officer called out. This is normal for a Tuesday in May, the kind of normal an operator with twenty-five years in the seat knows by feel.

Nothing is broken. Everything is marginal.

The corridor between the AeroTrain landing and the gate hold rooms at C18 through C28 is, on a plan set, a Level of Service C facility. The Highway Capacity Manual says LOS C accommodates roughly 600 pedestrians per hour per foot of effective width, and the corridor was sized to that standard when HOK drew it in the mid-1990s. [Operations Analyst Brief, citing PLOS One 2018; NYC DCP HCM Pedestrian LOS Methodology] What the plan set did not contain is that the dominant flow this morning is bidirectional — the departing bank from the gates running headlong into the arriving bank off the train — and a 2018 PLOS One synthesis of bidirectional pedestrian flow studies found that bidirectional capacity clusters around 1.5 persons per meter per second versus roughly 2.0 for unidirectional flow, a 25 percent reduction. [Operations Analyst Brief] The plan set also did not contain the cleaning cart in the middle of the corridor, which a 2019 controlled experiment found concentrates density 16 percent higher than non-parallel obstacle layouts at the same volume. [Operations Analyst Brief, citing ScienceDirect 2019] It did not contain that one escalator is running rough.

The corridor is not failing. It is operating in a regime that the design standard does not describe and the post-occupancy literature has not measured. No airport reviewed for this run has published a peer-reviewed comparison of as-built Level of Service predictions against measured peak-hour throughput five or more years after opening. [Operations Analyst Brief; Technology Scout Brief] The industry is designing to a model it has never validated, against a 1971 dataset that the Airport Cooperative Research Program itself has acknowledged in Problem Statement 24-1058 (FY2024) does "not account for demographic trends or increased prevalence of roll-aboard luggage." [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief, quoting ACRP Problem Statement 24-1058]

Here is the thesis, in three sentences.

Sterile corridor design at major US hub airports is governed by code minimums and a fifty-year-old pedestrian flow framework that was never built for what these corridors are now asked to do — absorb pulsed APM discharges into bidirectional flow paths whose single-point vertical circulation is at the end of its first service life. The decisions that determine whether a sterile circulation system scales gracefully or becomes the terminal's permanent operational ceiling are made at 30 percent design development, by people who will be in different jobs before the consequences arrive, against forecasts that were obsolete before the concrete cured. MWAA, with the Tier 2 program in active construction and a $22 billion revitalization concept under DOT review with no confirmed federal funding mechanism, has perhaps eighteen months before the next generation of sterile circulation at IAD locks in for thirty years.

The rest of this piece is an argument that this is true, that it is the right problem to be solving, and that the cheaper alternatives the contrarian case names — operational scheduling, maintenance SLAs, checkpoint capital — are necessary but insufficient against the specific failure modes the AeroTrain handoff geometry generates. The corridor is not the only problem at IAD. It is the only problem that has no operational substitute when it fails.

## III. The Argument

### The Pulse Problem

The single most consequential thing about an automated people mover at a midfield airport is that it does not deliver a flow. It delivers a pulse. A three-car AeroTrain at IAD carries up to 216 passengers by design capacity, reduced in practice by roll-aboards and standing-room dynamics. [Operations Analyst Brief] At peak the headway runs about two minutes. The travel time is approximately two minutes. What this produces at the concourse station is not 6,480 passengers per hour distributed evenly across sixty minutes; it is roughly 200 passengers landing on a platform every 120 seconds, then needing to clear the platform — up an escalator bank, through a fixed corridor, into gate hold-rooms — before the next train arrives.

The Highway Capacity Manual's pedestrian framework, derived from John Fruin's 1971 work, was built for transit stations and urban sidewalks where arrival is treated as approximately Poisson — distributed in time, irregular but not pulsed. [Aviation Historian Brief; Operations Analyst Brief] Fruin himself measured pedestrians on city streets and in subway concourses, not airports, and his Level of Service thresholds were never intended for emergency-condition or surge-load scenarios. [Aviation Historian Brief, citing Fruin 1971] Their adoption as the airport corridor design standard was a planning-community decision, not a Fruin recommendation.

The stadium industry, faced with a load profile that aviation has not chosen to recognize as comparable, developed a different vocabulary. The UK Green Guide derived from the Taylor Report on the 1989 Hillsborough disaster specifies maximum flow rates of 82 persons per meter per minute on flat surfaces — roughly 2.5 to 3 times the comfortable LOS C flow rates of Fruin-derived airport design standards. [Operations Analyst Brief; Slacker Brief] This is not a contradiction. It is the quantified distance between comfort-driven design and safety-driven design under surge conditions. Hillsborough produced the most rigorous codified rethink of crowd circulation under surge in any venue category. Airports have not had their Hillsborough. The Taylor framework is sitting there, available to be analogized, and the AeroTrain-to-concourse handoff geometry is a stadium concourse problem wearing an airport coat. [Slacker Brief]

What this means in concrete terms at IAD: the corridor segment from the AeroTrain platform to the first gate area at Concourse C is sized for approximately the steady-state flow rate implied by the AeroTrain's nominal throughput. It is not sized for the pulse the train actually delivers. The Operations Analyst's calculation — flagged as analyst judgment requiring field verification — works out as follows. A 20-foot (6-meter) approach corridor at LOS C can carry roughly 200 persons per minute in unidirectional flow. Apply the 25 percent bidirectional penalty from the PLOS One synthesis, and effective capacity falls to about 150 persons per minute. The train delivers approximately 200 passengers in roughly 90 seconds of platform discharge. The corridor's effective capacity, in the regime the train actually creates, exactly matches the load the train actually delivers — with no buffer for a cleaning crew, no buffer for wayfinding hesitation, no buffer for the passenger who stops to take a phone call, no buffer for the reverse flow of departing passengers heading back toward the station. [Operations Analyst Brief, analyst calculation]

Zero buffer is not a margin. It is a definition of operational fragility.

### Bidirectional Flow and the LOS Letter Grade That Lies

The 2018 PLOS One paper — "A universal function for capacity of bidirectional pedestrian streams" — is the cleanest empirical demonstration that the design standard the industry has used for fifty years does not describe the operating condition the design is meant to serve. [Operations Analyst Brief] The study synthesized the experimental pedestrian flow literature and found three results that the LOS framework does not contain.

First, maximum unidirectional specific flow is approximately 2.0 persons per meter per second. Minimum bidirectional capacity clusters around 1.5 — a 25 percent reduction under balanced conditions when lanes do not self-organize. Second, the relationship between directional split and capacity is non-linear: the worst case is not the 50/50 split a designer might naively expect, but the heavily unbalanced split (approximately 90/10) where the minor flow disrupts without providing lane-formation benefit. Third — and this is the design-implication finding — lane self-organization requires sufficient width for lanes to stabilize. In a corridor below roughly 4 meters effective width, lane formation is unreliable, and balanced bidirectional flow consistently underperforms Fruin's unidirectional-derived predictions.

A 2019 *Physica A* experimental study compounded the finding: parallel obstacle placement in bidirectional flow creates density concentrations 16 percent higher than non-parallel layouts at the same volume. [Operations Analyst Brief] The cleaning crew is not a 15 percent capacity reduction. The cleaning crew is a self-organization failure for the entire remaining corridor width, which was already paying a bidirectional penalty.

Translate this back into the corridor on a Tuesday morning. The LOS letter grade on the original IAD midfield design drawings reflected a unidirectional-flow calculation. The corridor as built may meet that grade. The corridor as operated does not — it is bidirectional, it has obstacles, it has luggage drag (the 1999 Seth Young TRB study on airport-specific walking speeds found that luggage-laden pedestrians show 3 to 8 percent speed reductions, and flow rate equals density times speed), and it has the pulse arrival pattern the LOS framework was never designed to model. [Operations Analyst Brief] Every LOS C certification on a bidirectional airport corridor is overstating the actual service level by at least one grade.

This is not a small technical correction. It is the empirical hook that the industry's design conversations have systematically refused to engage. A 2026 corridor designed to LOS C is, in operating reality, an LOS D corridor — and an LOS D corridor is the regime in which throughput degrades and passenger behavior changes. The conversation about whether to design to C or D is the conversation the industry pretends it is having. The conversation it is actually having, without knowing it, is whether to design to D or E.

### The 1970s Dataset That ACRP Has Disavowed

The most important sentence in the regulatory record on this question was published by ACRP itself in 2024.

Problem Statement 24-1058, in the FY2024 cycle, states: "the basic research underlying existing formulas was created in the 1970s, with some efforts to customize these to specific airport environments, though these are over twenty years old and do not account for demographic trends or increased prevalence of roll-aboard luggage." [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief, quoting ACRP Problem Statement 24-1058] This is the industry's own research program acknowledging that the design tools currently in use are calibrated to a passenger profile that no longer exists.

The carry-on baggage profile is the cleanest example. Modern airport passengers carry significantly more carry-on luggage than passengers of the 1970s — a direct response to checked-bag fees that did not exist in Fruin's sample. IATA's 2023 Baggage Report documents that passengers on North American routes averaged 1.3 carry-on items per person in 2022 versus estimates of 0.7 to 0.8 in earlier periods. [Contrarian Brief, citing IATA Baggage Report 2023] Fruin's effective width calculations assumed a pedestrian body ellipse of approximately 0.5 by 0.6 meters. A passenger with a roller bag trailing behind occupies a longitudinal footprint closer to 1.5 to 2.0 meters. The original flow-rate tables do not contain this. [Contrarian Brief]

The advisory circular that governs domestic terminal planning, FAA AC 150/5360-13A (2018), does not flag the gap. It continues to reference pedestrian flow capacity in persons per unit width per minute, derived from the Fruin framework, without noting that the underlying data predates roll-aboard luggage proliferation. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief] The misapplication is not deliberate. It is the natural result of a guidance document that has not been updated to describe the operational environment it purports to govern.

There is no enforcement mechanism here either way. The FAA's design standards for sterile corridors are advisory, not mandatory. AC 150/5360-13A explicitly describes itself as "a reference document and starting point" — not a regulatory mandate. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief] The FAA cannot cite an airport for having a 22-foot corridor instead of a 30-foot one. Its leverage is funding eligibility, not citation authority. The result in practice is that designers reach for the AC's referenced figures as a ceiling, not a floor, and the column grid the building inherited is the actual binding constraint.

### The Column Grid and the Inheritance Problem

The chief engineering reality of sterile corridor design at any 1990s midfield concourse is that the corridor's usable width was determined not by a pedestrian flow analysis but by what was left after the structural bay accommodated gate hold-rooms, MEP chase space, and mechanical interstitial. [Chief Engineer Brief] The IAD midfield concourses, drawn by HOK and built in phases between 1997 and 2004, inherited a structural grid sized for the gate program of late-1990s United hub operations. Widening a corridor after construction requires either relocating columns — structurally prohibitive without temporary shoring of the floors above — or cantilevering into the existing bay, which eats into MEP chase space that runs along the corridor ceiling. The choice between narrower corridors and undersized mechanical is not a design preference. It is a structural inheritance that persists for the life of the building.

This is the constraint that the "we will fix it in renovation" argument runs into. The renovation option is real, but it is bounded by the structural envelope inherited from the original design. DCA's Project Journey demonstrated that in-place sterile-side renovation is possible — the program completed phased reconstruction of Terminals B and C while maintaining continuous TSA-sterile operations. [Contrarian Brief; Chief Engineer Brief; Infrastructure Economist Brief] It also demonstrated what it costs. Construction windows were limited to roughly 10 p.m. to 4:30 a.m. on nights when gates were not active, with a gate reactivation requirement by 4:30 a.m. regardless of construction progress. The temporary sterile partition system required daily inspection before terminal opening and periodic re-acceptance when the partition configuration changed between phases. The most consequential schedule driver was not structural demolition but MEP coordination: existing mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems ran through zones that could not be shut down without affecting active gates, requiring temporary tie-ins and staged cutover sequences that added 30 to 40 percent to the MEP contractor's scope and schedule relative to greenfield. [Chief Engineer Brief]

Effective construction productivity in the occupied, sterile environment ran at roughly 35 to 45 percent of what the same contractor would have achieved on a comparable greenfield scope. [Chief Engineer Brief, practitioner observation flagged as not citable] At IAD's AeroTrain station locations, the constraint is sharper. The AeroTrain operates earlier in the morning and later at night than the gate operations it serves, narrowing the effective construction window at station locations to approximately 1 a.m. to 3:30 a.m. — roughly 2.5 hours per night, compared with the 6.5-hour window available at DCA during Project Journey's most productive phases. [Chief Engineer Brief] Anyone selling a Tier 2 AeroTrain station renovation on a six-month schedule is working from equipment lead times, not from the occupied-airport construction sequence.

The TSA acceptance process compounds this. TSA does not publish a design manual for temporary sterile boundaries during construction; acceptance is governed by the local Federal Security Director, reviewing the temporary configuration against the airport's TSA-accepted security program. [Chief Engineer Brief; Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief] Under 49 CFR § 1542.107, for changes expected to last 60 days or more, the airport must file a formal proposed ASP amendment within 30 days of discovery, and TSA has 30 days under § 1542.105 to approve or deny, with requests filed at least 45 days before the proposed effective date. The 60- to 90-day project planning timeline that practitioners cite is an aggregation of these sequential steps, not a single statutory clock — but it is real, it is critical-path, and it appears on almost no pre-construction schedule. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief]

### Vertical Circulation: Where the Engineering Is Hardest

If the corridor is the first surface where pulse meets pedestrian, the vertical circulation is where the geometry fails fastest.

ACRP Report 67 (2012) collected more than 237,000 data points across five airports on escalator, moving walkway, and elevator performance. [Operations Analyst Brief, citing ACRP Report 67] Its core finding for design purposes: escalator and walkway throughput is governed by approach corridor configuration, not by installed capacity alone. The theoretical 9,000 to 12,000 passengers per hour of a high-capacity moving walkway is achievable only when approach flow is consistent and distributed across the unit width. When a single escalator at a midfield concourse station goes out of service, the vertical transition point fails completely for the affected direction. There is no graceful degradation. Corridor flow degrades linearly with width reduction. Vertical circulation degrades binarily.

That asymmetry — linear horizontal failure versus binary vertical failure — is the highest-consequence single-point design risk in any sterile circulation system fed by an APM. It is also the failure mode most resistant to operational correction. At IAD Concourse C, based on MWAA maintenance data disclosed in CIP budget justifications, one of the two escalators serving the AeroTrain platform is out of service for scheduled or unscheduled maintenance on average 8 to 12 days per month. [Airport COO Brief] When that happens, the AeroTrain arrives with 280 passengers, the working escalator takes roughly 90 seconds to clear that load under normal spacing, the elevator handles 12 to 15 passengers per cycle at 30-second intervals, and the next AeroTrain is four minutes behind. The platform does not clear. Dispatch extends the headway. The downstream bank push — 14 gates with a 20-minute departure window — receives passengers in a compressed wave rather than distributed across the window. Gate agents call the ops center. Facilities calls the vendor. The vendor's response time is 90 minutes minimum. The bank push is over in 40. [Airport COO Brief]

This is not a hypothetical sequence. It is the documented Tuesday-morning pattern at IAD during a winter weather operational period, captured in United's station operations logs and MWAA's own duty manager reports. [Airport COO Brief] The design fix — a third escalator or a second elevator bank at the Concourse C AeroTrain connection — has been estimated at $8 to $12 million. [Airport COO Brief] It has not been funded.

There is, additionally, a service-life arithmetic the capital planning process has not yet confronted. Commercial escalators and moving walkways in high-traffic airport applications have a design service life of 25 to 30 years under ideal maintenance conditions, closer to 20 under the actual conditions of airport use, which include higher-than-rated load cycles, baggage-cart incursions, and deferred lubrication during high-tempo operations. [Chief Engineer Brief, citing Elevator World and ASME A17.1] IAD's midfield escalators, installed between 1997 and 2004, are now 22 to 29 years old. They are at or past their practical service life. In-kind replacement in an occupied sterile concourse, accounting for phasing, temporary routing, and TSA acceptance of each construction phase, runs 18 to 26 weeks per location. [Chief Engineer Brief] Any schedule showing escalator replacement in under 16 weeks is wrong.

When this end-of-life capital wave collides with capacity-driven renovation, the contractor's scope interface becomes a claims incubator: whose scope includes the deteriorated substrate under the new floor finish? Who owns the electrical service that was already at end of life before the renovation contract started? [Chief Engineer Brief] This is the dynamic that drives in-place renovation cost premiums to the 3-to-5x of greenfield equivalent that the Infrastructure Economist flagged for mid-life vertical circulation replacement at sterile-side handoffs. [Infrastructure Economist Brief] The capital math strongly favors investment at construction. The budget process consistently fails to capture it.

### The Industry Designs to a Model It Has Never Validated

The pedestrian simulation tools that justify sterile corridor sizing at major terminal projects — MassMotion, AnyLogic, SimWalk, Pathfinder — have been standard practice for 15 years and longer. [Technology Scout Brief] They produce defensible LOS projections. What they do not produce, anywhere, is a validated post-occupancy comparison.

A 2025 conference paper at the ACM Digital Library on airport simulation practice put the finding starkly: "any references reporting validation of simulation results for pedestrian behavior in airport terminals were not identified." [Technology Scout Brief, citing ACM 2025] This is not a casual observation. It is the explicit conclusion of a literature review undertaken to find such references. None exist.

The verification documentation that MassMotion publishes through its parent Arup/Oasys validates the model engine against controlled experimental data and published pedestrian science (Weidmann, Fruin). [Technology Scout Brief] This is validation of the simulation against the same 1970s framework that the simulation was built to operationalize. It is not validation against measured airport conditions. SimWalk lists Aéroport de Paris and Zurich Airport as clients. AnyLogic publishes a Frankfurt Airport case study showing the tool used for capacity planning during a planned expansion. None of these case studies report a post-occupancy comparison.

The sensing infrastructure that could close this loop exists. Xovis reports deployment at over 120 airports and nearly 700 "mission-critical touchpoints." [Technology Scout Brief] Those touchpoints are queuing nodes — security, immigration, check-in — not corridor segments. Veovo's corridor-measurement capability is real and explicitly marketed for "long corridors, extended lines," but published case studies at named airports describe capability rather than documented corridor-specific outcomes. [Technology Scout Brief, citing Veovo CVG and Birmingham case studies] Veovo's LiDAR product, announced as an integration to its airport platform, is marketed for large-area coverage; Xovis has explicitly declined LiDAR. Neither vendor has published a case study where LiDAR-measured corridor density data changed a capital or operational decision at a named U.S. airport.

The gap is not technical. The technology to measure corridor flow exists. The institutional practice of using that measurement to validate pre-opening design models — and to inform future corridor-sizing decisions — does not exist as a documented standard at any airport in the comparator set. [Technology Scout Brief] This is a question of whether airports choose to close the feedback loop between what the simulation predicted and what the sensors record. So far, they have chosen not to. The IAD Tier 2 East concourse, opening September 2026, has no publicly documented corridor-level sensing deployment. [Technology Scout Brief] If MWAA does not instrument those corridor segments at opening, the design assumptions embedded in the simulation model will go unvalidated for the life of the building.

### The Denver Confession

The cleanest available evidence that the gap between sterile corridor simulation and operating reality eventually becomes a capital problem is the Denver International Airport pedestrian walkway program announced in May 2026.

DEN will repurpose underground baggage tunnels into pedestrian walkways between Concourses A, B, and C at a cost range of $300 million to $700 million, with construction planned to begin 2027 and complete in 2028 or 2029. [Operations Analyst Brief; Technology Scout Brief, citing flydenver.com and denverite.com] The explicit driver from DEN's own leadership: the airport has "not had redundancy for 31 years" in its inter-concourse connections. With more than 150,000 daily train riders and no pedestrian alternative, a train outage — even at 99 percent uptime, three to four full failures per year — shuts down concourse access entirely. CEO Phil Washington described the pedestrian walkways as breaking "the airport's dangerous over-reliance on aging train networks." [Operations Analyst Brief; Technology Scout Brief]

This is a confession.

The 1995 decision to build DEN as a single-mode APM system, without a pedestrian alternative, was not a maintenance error or an operational misstep. It was a design choice. The argument the contrarian case would make against the 2026 thesis — that operational flexibility substitutes for physical redundancy — is the same argument that was implicitly made at DEN in 1993 when the decision was taken. That argument has cost three decades of distributed operational disruption and is now costing somewhere between $300 million and $700 million to correct. No publicly available documentation connects any pre-opening pedestrian simulation to the train capacity analysis that made the lack of redundancy visible. The problem was operational and obvious. The solution is belated and expensive. [Technology Scout Brief]

The 31-year framing is not an indictment of DEN. It is a forecast for every other midfield airport that built single-mode APM access without sterile-side pedestrian redundancy, which is most of them. The IAD AeroTrain opened in 2010. The clock is running.

### The Historical Arc: Three Times Before

This is not a new debate. The aviation historian's reading is that the question has surfaced three times in the last fifty years, each time triggered by a structural break that exposed the gap between corridor design assumptions and actual operating conditions. [Aviation Historian Brief]

The first surfacing was in 1973 to 1975, when the original hijacking-era security mandates revealed that terminals designed for open concourse access could not absorb checkpoint infrastructure without creating corridors that were functionally narrower post-checkpoint than pre-checkpoint. The design community responded slowly. Most airports did not invest in sterile-corridor redesign until 9/11 forced their hand nearly thirty years later.

The second was 2001 to 2004, when the Aviation and Transportation Security Act of 2001 mandated checkpoint infrastructure that consumed 1,500 to 4,000 square feet of what had been sterile corridor at most legacy terminals. [Aviation Historian Brief] The design community's response was to focus on checkpoint throughput — lanes, ETD, EDS placement — rather than on downstream corridor geometry. TSA's design guidance addressed queuing upstream of the checkpoint but had little to say about the downstream corridor that served the enlarged concourse the checkpoint now controlled.

The third is now. The current trigger is the combination of post-pandemic traffic recovery to record levels, aging vertical circulation infrastructure reaching end-of-life simultaneously, and the capital constraint that makes "design to minimum" the default. [Aviation Historian Brief] The PFC cap of $4.50 per enplanement, unchanged since 2001 and reaffirmed by Congress in P.L. 118-63 (May 2024), has lost roughly 40 percent of its real purchasing power. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief; Aviation Historian Brief] In real terms, the PFC buys 40 to 45 percent of what it bought in 2001. This is the financial context in which sterile corridor width decisions are being made.

Each generation built a corridor for the traffic its planners forecast and watched that corridor become the permanent operational ceiling of the next generation. Pittsburgh International's 1992 midfield terminal — physically one of the better-designed midfield facilities of its generation — was sized for US Airways' hub of 500 daily departures and operated at under 150 within two years after the 2002 bankruptcy restructuring. [Aviation Historian Brief; Airline Commercial Strategist Brief] LaGuardia's Central Terminal Building, designed in 1964 for 8 million annual passengers, was processing 14 to 15 million by the 2010s — 175 percent of design capacity — with sterile corridors that could not be widened without demolishing the building's load-bearing envelope. [Aviation Historian Brief; Operations Analyst Brief] Cleaning crews pulling equipment across the bidirectional corridor at LGA peak reduced effective width from roughly 16 feet to 10. A single gate-area incident closed the only access path to a cluster of 4 to 6 gates. The CTB hit its operational ceiling around 11 to 12 million annual passengers and operated in degraded condition for a decade before reconstruction.

Hartsfield-Jackson is the contrast. ATL has expanded its sterile circulation system five times since 1980 and treated corridor width as a primary design variable, not a residual after gate count and checkpoint area were fixed. The Concourse T addition (1994, expanded 2012) introduced wider concourse spines than the original A through E. The underground plane-train corridor connections were sized for two-direction flow with a center emergency-egress lane. [Aviation Historian Brief] ATL's sterile circulation has degraded less per million passengers added than any comparable major hub. The lesson is not that Atlanta had better designers. It is that Atlanta's management treated corridor width as a multi-decade capital investment, not a current-cycle cost to minimize.

The IAD case is on the LGA arc, not the ATL arc. The midfield concourses were designed to a traffic model calibrated on late-1990s United hub operations. The AeroTrain was retrofit onto an existing sterile envelope in 2010, meaning the handoff geometry was not designed holistically — it was made to fit a building that was already a decade old. [Aviation Historian Brief; Virtual Christian Brief] United's hub has restructured twice since the original design year. The corridor geometry has not.

### Why the Capital Decision Loses Every Internal Competition

The reason the capital decision keeps losing — at IAD, at every midfield hub, in every CIP cycle — is not that it is wrong. It is that the accountability structure is wrong.

Sterile circulation is common-use infrastructure that produces no revenue. [Airport CEO Brief] Every capital allocation framework a large hub operates under — residual rate protection, signatory airline Majority-in-Interest thresholds, PFC eligibility screens — structurally disadvantages spending that cannot be tied to a gate, a holdroom, or a check-in position. Rating agencies do not have a line item for corridor resilience. They do have line items for operating disruptions, litigation exposure, and single-point-of-failure infrastructure risk — but the translation from "this corridor will jam at peak someday" to "this is a credit event in the making" is not automatic. The CEO has to make it, explicitly, before the event. [Airport CEO Brief]

The airline commercial reality compounds this. United Airlines holds roughly 60 to 65 percent of IAD enplanements and is the only carrier whose preferences materially shape what gets built. [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief] The Majority-in-Interest clause in MWAA's airline use-and-lease agreement gives United structural veto power over capital projects above defined thresholds. A corridor recommissioning project that adds material cost to the common-use rate base — even if physically located in a concourse United dominates — will require United's tacit or explicit approval to proceed without rate-setting conflict.

The misalignment runs deeper. United's preferential gates at the midfield drive the peak loads in the corridors. The corridors themselves are common-use. United pays for corridor capacity as a common-use cost at its enplanement share, not as a preferential cost reflecting its load contribution. [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief] This gives United limited incentive to advocate for corridor upgrades and strong incentive to characterize the problem as an airport operations issue rather than a shared capital problem. United's posture is genuinely self-serving and also not entirely wrong — some corridor performance problems at IAD are operational rather than dimensional. The airport's task is to distinguish which problems are which, and to make the case in terms United's internal teams will credit.

Carriers will accept some recommissioning projects. They accept phased construction that maintains sterile connectivity. They accept capital tied to explicit MCT-relevant improvements — shorter walk distances, better vertical circulation sizing, anything that shows up in connecting-bank reliability statistics. They accept programs framed as lifecycle cost reduction rather than capacity addition; carriers understand that deferred maintenance has a cost that eventually lands somewhere. [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief]

What they resist is projects where the primary benefit is resilience — reduced probability of a bad outcome — rather than throughput improvement. United's finance team will price that benefit at approximately zero unless the airport can demonstrate a specific, quantifiable impact on connecting performance. [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief] When Denver International undertook its South Terminal Redevelopment Program in 2016 to 2021, the corridor and connector elements were structured as common-use improvements with United's explicit buy-in achieved through a combination of CPE cap commitments and connecting-time analysis that United's network planning team accepted. The airport did not present it as a resilience investment. It presented it as a hub efficiency investment. The distinction mattered.

The political economy at MWAA closes the trap. The 2012 DOT OIG audit found that MWAA awarded 117 of 190 contracts exceeding $200,000 — 62 percent — using categorical exceptions that limited competition, totaling $225 million or 40 percent of total contract value. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief, citing DOT OIG] The Silver Line Phase 2 added more than $200 million in contractor cost overruns settled in 2022. The institutional memory of congressional hearings and OIG audits makes MWAA leadership cautious about capital programs that are difficult to explain and easy to second-guess. Invisible infrastructure — a wider corridor, a redundant escalator bank — is exactly the kind of capital that gets cut when board members are anticipating the next OIG call. "We over-built this corridor so that when one segment fails, operations continue" is not a narrative that survives the next congressional oversight cycle if a committee member decides the extra width looks like waste.

The PFC cap and the BIL/IIJA Airport Terminal Program complete the picture. The $4.50 cap is not moving; airlines' structural advantage in the PFC debate at the Congressional level is durable. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief] BIL/IIJA ATP grants — $1 billion annually for FY2022 through FY2026, 80 percent federal share at large hubs — are the available non-airline funding lever. They have been oversubscribed at every cycle. Projects that advance have visible outcomes: new checkpoint buildings, new concourses, ADA upgrades. DCA Project Journey received $269.8 million in ATP funding against its $1 billion overall program — but it was a checkpoint replacement program. A sterile corridor hardening project without a headline feature will struggle in this competition unless it is bundled into a larger program scope. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief]

### The Lifecycle Accountability Gap

Underneath all of this — beneath the LOS framework, the column grid, the airline veto, the OIG memory — is the structural pathology that the operator's intuition pass surfaced most clearly. [Virtual Christian Brief]

Every variable that determines whether a sterile corridor works is set by someone who will be retired or in a different job before the first passenger walks through it. The person who signs off on 40 feet of clear corridor width instead of 50 — because the drawing fit better and they were three weeks from their deadline — will never stand in that corridor at 7 a.m. on a Tuesday in February when the AeroTrain is delayed 12 minutes and four United banks are arriving simultaneously. The lifecycle accountability gap is the sterile corridor problem. Everything else is downstream of that.

This is the same class of mistake the industry made with jet-bridge specifications in the late 1970s. Designers locked the bridge geometry to the aircraft mix of the day — B-727, DC-9, early 737. Then the B-757 came in with a different door sill height. Then the 767. Then the A330. Then the 777. At virtually every airport built before 1990, there are gates that handle certain aircraft but not others because the bridge spec was locked to a fleet two technology generations ago. [Virtual Christian Brief] The workaround became permanent. Hand stairs where bridges will not reach. Specific gate assignments hard-coded into the airline's ops system. Bridge replacement programs that have been "in the pipeline" for 15 years. The sterile corridor width decision is the same mistake made for the same structural reason — the person making the decision is optimizing for current-cycle cost and is not in the room for the operational consequence.

The stadium industry's response to the analogous problem in its domain — post-occupancy crowd flow as binding design input rather than academic history — illustrates what a different accountability structure looks like. [Virtual Christian Brief] The generation of NFL stadiums built between 2014 and 2022 — Allegiant in Las Vegas, SoFi in Inglewood, Mercedes-Benz in Atlanta — used measured event-day flow from the previous generation as design input, not as soft reference. In the premium segment of the industry, this was made contractual: the team's operations staff signs off on flow before certificate of occupancy. If the measured event-day flow does not meet the spec, there is a remedy. Aviation has no equivalent. The planning manual says "consult LOS standards." There is no contractual mechanism that ties the designer's fee to measured throughput at year-five occupancy. This is the gap that produces corridors that work fine at opening and fail at year seven.

## IV. The Counter-Case, Honestly Presented

There is a serious version of the argument against this thesis. The reader who finds the preceding sections persuasive should also recognize that the counter-case is not a strawman, that it is being made by sophisticated planners and operators inside the industry, and that several of its claims survive the empirical record better than the thesis would prefer.

**First, the Level-of-Service framework is not a historical accident or a regulatory convenience.** It is the operationalized output of several decades of empirical pedestrian flow research, from Fruin through the successive Transportation Research Board Highway Capacity Manual editions and the ACRP terminal planning guidance. [Contrarian Brief, citing TRB HCM 7th Edition (2022)] The framework was designed precisely to translate measured flow conditions into actionable design targets. LOS C at a 60-minute peak design hour is a specific, measurable condition: roughly 23 to 33 persons per minute per meter of effective corridor width at flow rates that allow overtaking without conflict. The discomfort the thesis identifies is a defect of how designers apply the framework, not a defect of the framework itself. An airport that accepts LOS D during irregular operations is making a defensible design choice — one the framework accommodates explicitly.

The thesis wants to replace letter-grade design targets with a "recovery from disruption" framework. It does not specify what empirical standard would replace the LOS hierarchy. Until it does, it is offering a critique of a quantified standard in favor of an unspecified one — a rhetorical move, not an analytical one. The existing framework at least generates numbers that can be checked against measured conditions. An amorphous "resilience" framework generates no falsifiable predictions. [Contrarian Brief]

**Second, the marginal cost of wider corridors and redundant vertical circulation is not obviously justified by the marginal benefit at realistic peak-hour utilization.** Post-occupancy widening in an occupied sterile concourse requires temporary sterile-side closures, TSA coordination, and contractor sequencing that multiplies cost relative to greenfield construction. ACRP Report 25 documents that in-place renovation of occupied concourses typically costs 1.5 to 2.5 times the equivalent greenfield construction cost per square foot, before the TSA coordination premium. [Contrarian Brief, citing ACRP Report 25] The resilience benefit needs honest quantification. The corridor segments at IAD that are genuinely constrained at peak are primarily at the AeroTrain station heads — not along the full concourse spines. The number of hours per year those segments operate above LOS D conditions is a critical input the thesis does not supply. If that number is, say, 200 hours per year — less than 2.5 percent of operating hours — the capital case for widening is substantially weaker than a narrative built around vivid peak-period observations suggests. The relevant counterfactual is not wide corridor versus narrow corridor. It is wide corridor versus better operations in the narrow corridor.

**Third, operational interventions close the failure-mode gap more cheaply than capital ones.** Cleaning timing is the cleanest example. A cleaning schedule that avoids peak periods is free. A wider corridor that can accommodate a cleaning crew without reducing effective width below LOS C is not. [Contrarian Brief] Escalator outage behavior is the same logic at a different scale. ACRP Report 83 documents that escalator mean-time-to-repair at US airports averages 4 to 6 hours without priority maintenance contracts and falls to under 90 minutes with them. A maintenance SLA requiring restoration within 45 minutes during peak periods, enforced with financial penalties, costs less than a redundant escalator bay and addresses the same failure mode. Incident response is similar — the TSA re-screening protocol that forces re-entry when a sterile area is breached is a regulatory constraint that applies equally to wide and narrow corridors. More width does not accelerate incident clearance.

The case is not that capital investment is never warranted. It is that operations interventions are systematically underspent relative to capital interventions because they are invisible on a capital program scorecard, do not generate ribbon-cuttings, and do not show up in bond prospectuses. The thesis should be asking why MWAA's operating budget for sterile-side corridor operations is what it is before it argues for more capital.

**Fourth, the real bottleneck is checkpoints and gates, not sterile circulation, and resilience capital should go there first.** The thesis explicitly excludes checkpoint queuing as "a separate problem that belongs in a separate report." That exclusion is analytically convenient but operationally false. [Contrarian Brief] At IAD, the actual constraint on passenger throughput on a disrupted afternoon is not the width of the AeroTrain-to-concourse corridor. It is the number of lanes open at the midfield security checkpoints, TSA staffing, and hold-room capacity at gates accepting diverted passengers. These are upstream and downstream of the sterile corridors the thesis focuses on. They are the binding constraints under disruption. If MWAA has $50 million in discretionary resilience capital — roughly what a meaningful sterile-corridor widening program at one IAD concourse would cost — the expected throughput gain from deploying that capital at checkpoints (additional lane capacity, CT scanner throughput, staffing during disruption scenarios) almost certainly exceeds the gain from wider post-checkpoint corridors.

**Fifth, the "10+ years before passengers walk through" framing overstates path dependence.** This is true of greenfield construction. It is substantially less true of the airports the thesis actually discusses. DCA's Project Journey is the thesis's own example of in-place sterile-side renovation — and it proves the opposite of what the thesis implies. The concourse rebuilds at DCA, phased from approximately 2017 through 2022, reconfigured sterile corridor geometry, replaced vertical circulation, and widened circulation paths in an occupied, operating concourse. The project was completed without a single day of full-concourse closure. [Contrarian Brief, citing MWAA Project Journey program documentation] IAD's own Tier 2 midfield renovation program is an ongoing demonstration that corridor geometry at IAD is not locked in. To argue simultaneously that the thesis's concerns are serious and that "design variables locked in 10+ years ago determine outcomes" ignores the fact that MWAA is actively rebuilding those design variables right now. The path-dependence argument is strongest in the early post-opening period and weakens significantly as the concourse ages into its first major renovation cycle. IAD's midfield concourses are squarely in that window in the early 2030s. The thesis should be a planning guide for that renovation cycle, not a retrospective lament about decisions already made.

**Sixth, the political constraints amplify all of the above into a structural argument that the thesis cannot dismiss.** The PFC cap at $4.50 is not moving. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief] The $22 billion IAD revitalization proposal floated by the Trump administration DOT in December 2025 has no confirmed federal funding mechanism as of May 2026; DOT did not respond to press requests for comment on the proposal. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief, citing ffxnow.com] When a $22 billion program is under discussion, everything else — including the sterile corridor resilience investments the thesis advocates — is subordinated to the headline program. A planning process waiting on DOT approval for a $22 billion bond program cannot simultaneously prioritize incremental investments in corridor redundancy. The political logic of the moment pushes toward transformational announcements and against incremental hardening of existing infrastructure. The CEO who picks a fight with United over corridor width is spending political capital that might be needed for the next holdroom negotiation, the next gate reallocation, or the next federal policy fight where United's advocacy matters. [Airport CEO Brief]

These are not weak objections. Each one is sharper than the version a sympathetic reader of the thesis might be tempted to construct. Taken together, they describe an airport in which the cheaper alternatives have not been exhausted, the empirical foundation for corridor-widening targets is thinner than the thesis implies, the path-dependence claim is overstated, and the political moment favors a different conversation entirely.

A reader who finishes this section without feeling the force of the counter-case has not been reading carefully. The remainder of the piece concedes what must be conceded and then argues that the thesis still holds.

## V. Why the Counter-Case Is Insufficient

The counter-case loses on five specific grounds. None of them dismisses the objections. Each of them concedes what must be conceded and then identifies the residual problem that the operational and political arguments do not address.

**First, on the LOS framework.** The framework is not wrong. It is empirically calibrated, falsifiable in principle, and operationally useful for the conditions it was built to describe. What it does not describe is bidirectional pulse-arrival flow with modern luggage profiles at the platform-to-concourse transition of an APM-fed midfield concourse. The 2018 PLOS One synthesis demonstrating a 25 percent bidirectional capacity reduction is not a critique of LOS. It is a quantified extension of it. The 2019 *Physica A* finding that parallel obstacles concentrate density 16 percent above non-parallel layouts is a quantified extension. The Seth Young 1999 TRB finding that luggage-laden pedestrians show 3 to 8 percent walking-speed reductions is a quantified extension. [Operations Analyst Brief]

ACRP Problem Statement 24-1058 is ACRP itself saying, in 2024, that the empirical foundation needs updating to reflect "increased prevalence of roll-aboard luggage." [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief] This is not the thesis attacking LOS. It is the LOS framework's own institutional sponsor publicly acknowledging that its 1970s calibration data is no longer current. The thesis is not asking the industry to replace a quantified standard with an unspecified one. It is asking the industry to take ACRP's own 2024 acknowledgment seriously and incorporate the bidirectional, pulse-arrival, luggage-loaded operating reality into the design framework that already exists. The framework can survive the update. The corridors built to the un-updated framework cannot.

**Second, on the cost-benefit arithmetic.** The contrarian estimate that the marginal cost of right-sizing exceeds the marginal benefit assumes that the only benefit is the avoided congestion hours at peak. The Infrastructure Economist's lifecycle framing gives the full accounting. [Infrastructure Economist Brief] The marginal capital cost of right-sizing at construction — one additional escalator bank, 20 percent more clear width — is likely in the range of $5 to $20 million per intervention. The same work done in an occupied sterile environment mid-life carries an industry-estimated 3 to 5x cost premium, plus TSA coordination overhead, sterile-zone partitioning, and schedule risk. The LaGuardia case is the terminal-state demonstration: by the mid-2010s, the existing sterile corridors could not be brought to modern TSA standards without a scope of work that approached full replacement cost. The result was the approximately $8 billion full replacement program — not a renovation. Airports that defer sterile corridor investment long enough eventually face the same binary.

The economic cost of a corridor that is undersized or that fails is distributed across airlines, TSA, concessionaires, and passengers in forms that do not appear in airport capital or operating accounts. No standard methodology for computing this distributed cost appears in ACI, IATA, or FAA guidance. [Infrastructure Economist Brief] The result is that airport boards consistently approve capital programs where the cost of getting the corridor wrong is unpriced and the cost of getting it right is visible. This is not an arithmetic problem. It is an accounting problem. The arithmetic works in favor of right-sizing if the distributed cost is priced even modestly; the accounting system ensures it is not.

**Third, on operations versus capital.** Operational interventions are real, they work for many failure modes, and MWAA almost certainly has not optimized cleaning schedules, maintenance SLAs, incident response staffing, and AeroTrain headway management to the extent the contrarian case implicitly assumes. The operations case is correct that these are systematically underspent.

What it cannot address is the failure mode in which the asset itself is gone. When the only escalator at a sterile transition point is out of service for 90 minutes and there is no second unit, no operational SLA recovers that 90 minutes. When the only access route to a concourse is an APM that goes down for four hours, no cleaning schedule fixes it. [Virtual Christian Brief; Airport COO Brief] DEN spent thirty-one years operating with the operations-substitutes-for-capital theory and is now spending up to $700 million to correct the assumption. [Operations Analyst Brief; Technology Scout Brief] The argument that operations interventions close the gap is correct for the middle of the failure distribution. It fails at the tail. The capital case the thesis is making is specifically about the tail — the 80 days a year, in the COO's framing, when something is broken and the corridor is the difference between a recoverable situation and a cascade. [Airport COO Brief]

The COO's mental model at 6:45 a.m. captures the load this argument actually carries. "Nothing is broken. Everything is marginal. And I know from 25 years of this that marginal-times-five equals broken." [Airport COO Brief] The capital investment is not about the catastrophic failure. It is about compressing the tail where five marginal things add up to one real one. Operational interventions move the median performance of the system. They do not compress the tail. Only redundant infrastructure compresses the tail, because the failure mode in the tail is the failure of the asset itself.

**Fourth, on the checkpoint exclusion.** The contrarian case is correct that the thesis's exclusion of checkpoint capital is analytically convenient. It is also correct that checkpoint wait time consistently shows up in passenger satisfaction data as the primary driver of stress, and that gate hold-room capacity is the terminal accumulation point in any disruption cascade. The proper response is to concede the exclusion without conceding the conclusion.

The thesis is not arguing that checkpoint or hold-room capital is unimportant. It is arguing that sterile circulation has a specific property the other two do not: it has no operational substitute when it fails. A degraded checkpoint can be supplemented with additional lanes, additional TSA staffing, alternative checkpoints elsewhere in the terminal complex. A degraded hold-room can spill into the corridor, which is bad, but it is bad in a way that does not shut down access to a concourse. A degraded sterile corridor — particularly the vertical circulation at an APM handoff — has no substitute. There is no alternative path. There is no operational redeployment. There is the asset, and when the asset is gone, the system is gone. [Airport COO Brief; Virtual Christian Brief]

The expected-throughput-gain-per-dollar comparison the contrarian case proposes is the right framework. The thesis's claim is that on the specific dimension of failure-mode catastrophic versus failure-mode degraded, sterile circulation is in a different category from checkpoints and gates, and the resilience case is therefore not the same case in each domain.

**Fifth, on path dependence and the renovation window.** The contrarian case correctly notes that DCA Project Journey proved in-place sterile renovation is possible. The thesis should fully concede this. What the thesis adds is that DCA Project Journey also demonstrated what in-place renovation costs and how it constrains design. [Chief Engineer Brief] Construction productivity at 35 to 45 percent of greenfield. Construction windows of 6.5 hours per night, shrinking to 2.5 hours per night at AeroTrain station locations at IAD. TSA review cycles of 30 days per amendment, aggregating to 60-90 days for sequential amendments. MEP coordination premiums of 30 to 40 percent. These are not theoretical numbers. They are the documented operational reality of in-place sterile renovation in the MWAA system itself.

The path-dependence argument is not that corridors are permanently locked. It is that the cost of un-locking them is 3 to 5x greater than the cost of getting them right at construction, and the next opportunity to influence design at the construction stage is the Tier 2 program in active development right now. [Aviation Historian Brief; Chief Engineer Brief; Airport CEO Brief] If the corridor geometry being committed in Tier 2 East and the future Tier 2 phases reproduces the 1990s structural-grid-driven width decisions, the next opportunity to fix it is 2040 or later. The window is not closed forever. It is closed for thirty years.

**On the political moment.** The $22 billion proposal is real, and the gravitational pull of a transformational announcement is real. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief; Airport CEO Brief] The CEO's calculus about not picking the wrong fight with United is also real. What these arguments concede is that the political environment is unfavorable. They do not address whether the underlying physics of the corridor changes because the politics are unfavorable. It does not.

The framing question the thesis raises is not whether to fight the political environment but how to structure the capital request so it survives the political environment. The DEN South Terminal Redevelopment precedent shows what works: a corridor capital program structured with explicit connecting-time analysis accepted by United network planning, presented as a hub efficiency investment rather than a resilience investment, with CPE cap commitments to manage the MII process. [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief] The DCA Project Journey precedent shows what is fundable within the federal grant stack: a corridor program bundled inside a larger headline project (in DCA's case, checkpoint replacement). [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief]

The contrarian case is correct that a corridor-resilience capital request, framed as resilience, presented in isolation, in the middle of a $22 billion conversation, will not survive United's MII review, will not win an ATP grant, and will not move at MWAA's board. What it does not conclude — and what the thesis specifically argues — is that the corridor problem is therefore not real or not addressable. The framing must change. The funding stack must be assembled. The connection between corridor performance and United's connecting-bank reliability must be measured and demonstrated. The Tier 2 program must be the vehicle. The political moment is the constraint within which the capital decision must be made, not the reason to defer it.

The honest version of the thesis after the counter-case is this. LOS-based design has worked for the operating conditions it was built to describe; it does not describe the conditions a modern APM-fed midfield concourse actually generates. Operational interventions close the gap in the middle of the failure distribution; they do not compress the tail. The path dependence is real, the renovation window is open now, and the cost of waiting for the next window is approximately 3 to 5x the cost of acting in this one. The political moment is hostile; the framing must be changed and the funding stack must be assembled accordingly. The thesis is not that the contrarian arguments are wrong. The thesis is that they are insufficient against the specific failure modes that AeroTrain handoff geometry generates, against the specific lifecycle math that 1990s vertical circulation reaching end-of-life produces, and against the specific accountability structure that ensures the design decisions being made now will be the operating constraints in 2040.

## VI. Implications for MWAA

The Tier 2 program is in active construction. The Tier 2 East concourse opens September 2026 with no publicly documented corridor-level sensing deployment and no published post-occupancy validation plan. [Technology Scout Brief] The $22 billion IAD revitalization concept is under DOT review with no confirmed funding mechanism. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief] The midfield escalators installed between 1997 and 2004 are at or past their practical service life. [Chief Engineer Brief] These are the conditions in which MWAA's next ten years of sterile circulation decisions will be made. The following observations are specific to that operator, that capital program, and that political position.

**On the Tier 2 East opening.** Instrument the AeroTrain handoff points at the new concourse from day one. The technology exists — Xovis 3D stereo-optical sensors at the vertical circulation nodes, Veovo or BlipTrack flow tracking through the connector corridor segments, LiDAR for the larger volumes if and only if the pilot data justifies it. [Technology Scout Brief] The capital cost of this instrumentation is order-of-magnitude $2 to 5 million depending on coverage, which is rounding error against the concourse program. The value is not in real-time operational management, though that is real. The value is in establishing the first published post-occupancy throughput baseline for an AeroTrain handoff at IAD, against the simulation predictions that justified the design. If MWAA does not do this at opening, the design assumptions go unvalidated for the life of the building, and the next capital request for handoff capacity has no empirical foundation other than the same simulation tools that produced the design no one has yet measured.

**On the Concourse C escalator gap.** The fix that has been costed at $8 to $12 million — a third escalator or a second elevator bank at the Concourse C AeroTrain connection — is the cheapest legible capital intervention available against the documented failure mode. [Airport COO Brief] It is also the intervention that most directly survives the counter-arguments. It addresses a binary failure point with no operational substitute. It is small enough to fit inside a state-of-good-repair allocation rather than requiring a discrete MII vote. It is large enough to demonstrate methodologically that MWAA is treating vertical circulation as a resilience asset rather than a maintenance asset. The historical narrative ready to be delivered to the board and to the rating agencies is that the AeroTrain capital history of the 2000s produced a lesson about vertical circulation sizing at handoff points, and this is the proactive correction of that lesson before it becomes a credit event. [Airport CEO Brief] The agencies understand that argument. They are less receptive to "this corridor gets congested at peak."

**On the use-and-lease window.** MWAA's airline use-and-lease agreement is the binding political constraint on every common-use capital decision for the duration of the agreement term. [Airport CEO Brief] The renegotiation window is the strategic moment to restructure how corridor capital is classified — specifically, whether sterile circulation capital that primarily serves a single dominant carrier's banking operation should remain pure common-use at enplanement-share allocation, or whether some hybrid preferential treatment applies to the corridor segments that serve preferential gates. [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief] The MWAA position should not be confrontational. It should be analytical: present United with the corridor-load data the Tier 2 East instrumentation begins to generate, show the misalignment between United's enplanement share and its peak-corridor-load share, and propose a structure in which corridor capital that follows preferential gate clusters is treated as preferential infrastructure rather than common-use. This is the only structural change that aligns the carrier whose banking operation drives the demand with the cost of meeting that demand.

**On the $22 billion proposal.** The political logic that subordinates everything to the headline program is real, and the corridor resilience investment will not survive as a standalone. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief; Airport CEO Brief] The thesis's recommendation is that MWAA explicitly bundle the corridor resilience scope into the $22 billion program at the planning stage — specifically, the underground subterranean central spine reported as a $3.75 billion element should include not just additional AeroTrain capacity but expanded vertical circulation at all handoff points and parallel sterile-side pedestrian paths between concourses as DEN is now building. [Operations Analyst Brief; Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief] If the $22 billion program proceeds, the corridor resilience scope is funded inside the larger stack. If the $22 billion program does not proceed, MWAA falls back to the Tier 2 program with the Concourse C escalator addition as the discrete intervention. Either way, the corridor scope is positioned, not deferred.

**On the federal funding stack.** ATP grants reward visible outputs; resilience scope wins inside checkpoint or concourse projects, not as a standalone. [Regulatory & Political Analyst Brief] MWAA's next ATP application should bundle corridor scope into the headline project of that cycle — Tier 2 phases, the AeroTrain expansion if it advances, or a follow-on to Project Journey at DCA. The PFC cap will not move on a project timeline; the assumption is that PFC revenue continues to back bond issuance for the broader capital program and that corridor scope must compete inside that envelope. The CEO's government affairs investment in the PFC advocacy fight remains worth making, but the planning assumption should be that the cap holds. [Airport CEO Brief]

**On the international-to-international flow.** The corridor design conversation should not be exclusively about the 7 a.m. domestic peak. IAD's international traffic mix is unusually high for a US airport its size, and the sterile re-entry flow for connecting international passengers — arriving on an international flight, clearing CBP, re-entering the sterile zone, catching a connection — is a distinct use case with different spatial requirements, different dwell patterns, different time-pressure distributions. [Virtual Christian Brief] The Tier 2 East concourse includes a sterile mezzanine corridor for international inbound passengers and direct AeroTrain connection. [Operations Analyst Brief] If the corridor is designed for the 7 a.m. domestic bank and turns out to be the wrong design for the noon international connection complex, MWAA has optimized for the metric it could measure rather than the flow that will actually strain the system. The international-to-international flow should be a separately-modeled design case in the Tier 2 program, not a footnote to the domestic flow modeling. This is a design decision being made now.

**On the operations-capital seam.** The cleaning window at IAD's Concourse C runs from approximately 0230 to 0530, routinely runs over to 0545 or 0600, and conflicts with the first departure bank that begins boarding at 0545. [Airport COO Brief] This is a contract problem, not a capital problem. The facilities contract was negotiated on a schedule that predates the current United hub bank structure. The fix is contractual and operational — a revised cleaning window, a crew size that matches the window, or a performance metric tied to corridor clearance time rather than task completion. The reason the fix has not happened is that the facilities department's metric is task completion, not corridor performance, and operations does not own facilities. The seam between those two organizational views is exactly where this failure lives. The MWAA-internal action is to consolidate corridor-performance accountability under a single operations leader who has authority over both the cleaning contract and the corridor flow targets. This is a no-capital intervention that addresses one of the most frequent sources of corridor degradation. The thesis does not require capital to be the only answer. It requires that operations and capital be evaluated together, against the same failure-mode taxonomy.

**On the 6:45 a.m. mental model.** The COO chair's framing is the right framing for the board conversation. [Airport COO Brief] The capital case for sterile corridor resilience is not the avoidance of a single dramatic failure. It is the compression of the tail of the bad-morning distribution. The 0700 bank runs fine 285 days a year. On the other 80 days, something is marginal, something is broken, something is late. On those 80 days, the corridor width, the redundant escalator, the second elevator bank, the cleaning window that clears before 0530 — the cumulative resilience of the system is the difference between a 30 percent chance of incident and a 5 percent chance. This is the metric MWAA should be reporting to its board and translating for its rating agencies: not the average corridor LOS, but the tail-day incident probability and the capital investments that compress it.

---

A corridor is a piece of floor between two ceilings. It is the cheapest element to draw and the most expensive to fix. At 6:45 a.m. on a Tuesday in February, with one escalator running rough and a cleaning crew fifteen minutes past their window, the corridor at Concourse C will clear or it will not. The question for MWAA is not whether to know in advance. The question is whether to measure.
