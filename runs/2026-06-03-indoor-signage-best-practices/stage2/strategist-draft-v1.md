# The Cheapest Lever

## A Report on Airport Wayfinding as a Governance Discipline

*Transform Airports AI Council — Indoor Signage Best Practices Run*
*Stage 2 — Strategist Draft v1*
*June 3, 2026*

---

## I. The Decision Point

At six in the morning at Washington Dulles, a passenger steps off a United mainline aircraft at Concourse C with a connection to Concourse D. She has thirty-five minutes — United's published minimum, the same number Delta promises at Atlanta. She has never been to IAD before. She walks up the jet bridge into the holdroom, past gate signs branded in United blue, and looks for the sign that will tell her what to do next.

The sign she needs has to answer a specific question. Not "where are the gates" — she knows where the gates are. Not "where is ground transportation" — she is not going to ground transportation. The question is: *which mode of underground transit will get me to Concourse D?* The answer is not the AeroTrain. The AeroTrain runs to Concourses A, B, and C. Concourse D is still served by Mobile Lounges — the strange, two-story buses Eero Saarinen designed in 1962, which depart every five minutes and can take up to fifteen minutes to reach the concourse. United's own airport guide tells passengers this. The airport's signage, layered across decades of construction, tells them less reliably.

If she reads the right sign, she has twenty minutes of buffer for an outbound boarding. If she reads the wrong sign — if she boards an AeroTrain and rides to Concourse C station, then has to come back, then has to walk to the Mobile Lounge bay, then has to wait — she is, with high probability, on a later flight. United's MCT calculation assumes the best-case path. The signage system assumes that any passenger arriving in the decision zone can resolve a two-mode transport choice at a single decision point. The signs were installed under at least three different design standards across at least three construction eras. Some are 1990s vintage. Some were added when the AeroTrain opened in 2010. Some came in with the Silver Line connection in 2022. They do not agree with each other about colors, hierarchies, terminology, or what the passenger is presumed to already know.

Multiply that decision point by every connecting passenger, every day, at every American hub. That is the cost of bad airport wayfinding.

This report argues three things. **Wayfinding is the lowest-cost, highest-leverage operational intervention available to a US hub airport.** The reason most US hubs wayfind poorly is not budget; it is the absence of a governance structure with the authority to enforce a single hierarchy against the airlines, the federal agencies, the concessionaires, and the airport's own capital programs, which each tend to add signs faster than the system can absorb them. The exemplars cited in every wayfinding case study — Changi, Munich, Zurich, Helsinki — wayfind well not primarily because they spent more, but because a single design authority owned the standard across decades of expansion, with explicit performance criteria, and refused to negotiate. MWAA, having accumulated three decades of contradictory signage decisions at IAD and DCA, has the opportunity to do the same. The question is not whether the standard exists. The standard exists. The question is whether the authority to enforce it exists.

## II. Executive Summary

This is the report in miniature, for the reader who will not finish it.

**1. Wayfinding is a rounding error in any large airport capital program, but it taxes the efficiency of every square foot the program builds.** MWAA's 2025–2039 Capital Construction Program totals $9 billion. Heathrow's five-year wayfinding consultancy framework, scaled to MWAA's traffic, suggests an eight-million-dollar comprehensive program — under 0.2 percent of the capital plan [Infrastructure Economist Brief]. The asymmetry between the cost of signage and the value of what signage governs is the structural case for treating wayfinding as a discipline rather than as a line item.

**2. The decision-point methodology — what the research actually says — is forty years old, federally adopted, and routinely ignored.** Romedi Passini's 1984 framework reframed wayfinding as spatial problem-solving: identify each point where the passenger must choose, and provide the minimum information needed to resolve that choice. ACRP Report 52 (2011), adopted into FAA Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F (2013), codified three principles — continuity (no passenger loses sight of a directional cue across more than thirty meters), connectivity (cues chain across the full journey), and consistency (the same system at every decision point) [Operations Analyst Brief; Chief Engineer Brief]. The standard is testable. Most US hub airports have never been tested against it.

**3. The dynamic-versus-static debate is the wrong frame and produces wrong answers.** Dynamic digital displays do specific work that static signage cannot — flight information, gate changes, baggage carousel assignments, security wait times. Static signage does specific work that digital cannot — fixed orientation, directional confirmation, decision-point answers under stress. Eye-tracking studies at Heathrow and Munich found that passengers stop reading digital displays mid-corridor when the content rotates between navigation and advertising; the noise trains them to filter out the signal [Technology Scout Brief]. The error airports make is treating digital and static as a binary technology choice rather than matching the medium to the information-change rate at each location.

**4. Airport apps are not the substitute for fixed signage.** Only seven percent of airport passengers use the departure airport's own app. The trajectory of phone-based navigation is real, but Google and Apple indoor mapping are not substitutes for a coherent physical sign system at the decision points that matter — exit from a jet bridge, exit from an escalator, the transition from one transport mode to another. The passenger who most needs wayfinding help is the one least likely to navigate by app [Technology Scout Brief; Contrarian Brief]. Designing for the digital native is designing for the population that does not need the help.

**5. The governance vacuum, not the design vocabulary, is the binding constraint.** At a typical US large-hub airport, the airport authority controls common areas. Airlines control leased gate areas. TSA controls the checkpoint zone. CBP controls the FIS hall. Concessionaires control advertising positions. No federal regulation requires any of them to coordinate. The "committee design problem" is the predictable output of this structure — every stakeholder adds signs expressing their own priority, and no one has the authority to subtract [Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief]. The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey spent the better part of a decade building a unified standards manual covering JFK, LGA, and EWR; it remains in prototype rollout years after publication. The PANYNJ case is the most documented US success story and also the clearest evidence of how hard the implementation is [Operations Analyst Brief; Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief].

**6. Contradiction debt is the operational cost airports do not track but are paying.** At IAD, six distinct construction events from 1962 through 2022 have deposited overlapping signage layers — Saarinen-era, AeroTrain-era, Silver Line-era — that use different colors, hierarchies, and terminology. At DCA, the June 2022 renaming of more than 1,000 signs from a three-terminal to a two-terminal structure was executed in a five-day window, layered onto signage debt already accumulated [Aviation Historian Brief; Airline Commercial Strategist Brief; Chief Engineer Brief]. The cost of this stratigraphy is not in sign replacement; it is in the cognitive load on passengers who must hold multiple mental models simultaneously, in the eighty percent of customer service agent time consumed by directional questions, and in the staff-driven verbal redirections that compound inconsistency rather than resolve it [Operations Analyst Brief].

**7. Live-terminal construction premium and historic-preservation constraints make remediation more expensive than scope documents recognize.** Airport construction runs forty to fifty percent above conventional commercial work because it must schedule around flight operations, typically in overnight windows; signage projects routinely discover that the condition of existing substrates, conduit, and ADA compliance gaps is worse than assumed [Chief Engineer Brief]. Ontario International Airport budgeted a signage project at $485,000 and finished at $1.3 million — a 168 percent overrun driven by unknown field conditions [Infrastructure Economist Brief]. The IAD Main Terminal carries National Register of Historic Places designation; relocating overhead pendant signs in the Saarinen catenary-cable roof requires structural analysis and preservation review on every move [Chief Engineer Brief].

**8. MWAA's current allocations are study budgets, not implementation budgets, and the carrier-response question is unaddressed.** The 2021 capital allocations of $514,000 for an IAD Signage and Wayfinding Masterplan and $4.87 million for the DCA equivalent are appropriate for studies — they are not implementation programs [Operations Analyst Brief; Chief Engineer Brief]. The 2025–2039 Airport Use and Lease Agreement, under a hybrid residual rate-setting structure, gives United (which carries 49.9 percent of IAD mainline enplanements, or roughly two-thirds including United Express) and American (which controls 53 percent of DCA flights) practical influence over any capital improvement that enters the rate base [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief]. A wayfinding program structured as a capital expenditure will face majority-in-interest scrutiny. Structured as an operating standard with capital classification reserved for infrastructure-grade elements — sign substrates, digital backbone, asset management platform — it can be moved without triggering it.

The rest of this report shows the work behind these eight claims, then takes the strongest counter-arguments seriously, then explains what the counter-arguments are missing.

## III. The Argument

### The Decision Point Is the Unit of Analysis

The fundamental act of bad airport signage is to answer a question the passenger is not asking. Romedi Passini, the Canadian environmental psychologist who wrote *Wayfinding in Architecture* in 1984, framed it precisely. A passenger entering a terminal is not executing a pre-loaded route. She is solving a series of nested problems. Each problem requires information at a specific moment and a specific place. The job of the sign system is not to publish the airport's full directory; it is to answer the next question.

What Passini did was reframe wayfinding from an aesthetic question to an operational one. What does the passenger need to decide *at this point*, and does this sign answer that question? Per Mollerup, the Danish designer who built the wayfinding systems for Copenhagen, Oslo, and Stockholm Arlanda between 1989 and 1998, coined "wayshowing" to drive the distinction home: wayfinding is what the passenger does; wayshowing is what the environment does. Design committees, Mollerup observed, optimize for the wrong variable. They design for beauty or brand rather than for the specific navigation decision the sign must resolve at the specific point where a passenger encounters it.

Mollerup's nine wayfinding strategies catalog how passengers actually behave under uncertainty. The ninth, social navigation — asking a staff member — is the failure mode. Every time a passenger approaches a customer service agent to ask for directions, the environment has failed to answer a question it should have answered without speech. At a typical US hub airport, eighty percent of customer service agent time is consumed by directional questions [Operations Analyst Brief]. That number is a proxy for system failure. When the signage works, agents handle exceptions. When it fails, they become the system. The cost is not just labor. The cost is the introduction of inconsistency, because verbal directions do not replicate, and the next agent's answer to the same question may differ in detail from the previous one's.

In 2011, the Transportation Research Board published ACRP Report 52 — *Wayfinding and Signing Guidelines for Airport Terminals and Landside*. The report, co-developed with the Texas Transportation Institute, the Society for Environmental Graphic Design, and the Airport Sign Managers Network, operationalized Passini's framework into a formal methodology. Identify each segment of the passenger journey. Map the decision points within each segment. At each decision point, define the minimum information required to resolve the choice. Enforce three rules: continuity (no passenger should lose sight of a directional cue across more than thirty meters of their journey), connectivity (cues chain to each other across the full journey), consistency (the same system, the same language, the same hierarchy throughout) [Operations Analyst Brief; Chief Engineer Brief; Aviation Historian Brief]. In 2013, the FAA adopted ACRP 52's substance into Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F. The standard, in other words, is federal. The standard is not enforced. The Advisory Circular is advisory; compliance is not required for AIP grant eligibility [Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief].

The standard is also testable. Tampa International's Wayfinding Standards Manual — a 482-page document developed with Gresham Smith — includes a Journey Segment Evaluation Tool requiring that every new capital project be evaluated against the existing sign hierarchy at each decision point it touches, so that individual project decisions cannot silently degrade the system [Operations Analyst Brief]. Tampa is operating ACRP 52 as a checklist. Most US hub airports are not.

### What Eye-Tracking Has Actually Told Us

In December 2022 and January 2023, Heathrow Terminal 5 ran a formal eye-tracking study using Tobii Pro Glasses 3 on 108 participants moving through arriving, departing, and connecting journeys. The methodology — recording actual gaze data rather than surveying passengers about what they remember — is the most rigorous source of empirical evidence on airport signage behavior available in the public literature. What it found is embarrassing to designers and operators alike.

The failures concentrated at transitional points — escalator exits, entries to large halls, multi-path junctions. Not the mid-corridor sections where most airports place their largest signs. Two specific failure modes dominated. The first: signs that passengers simply did not notice, often because they were positioned outside the natural sightline at the moment the decision had to be made. The second: oversaturation of signs at the same point, where competing information increased cognitive load and reduced the probability that the passenger registered any of it [Technology Scout Brief; Operations Analyst Brief]. Heathrow acted on the data, decluttered the decision points, reconfigured the immigration entrance, and revamped onward travel signage. The terminal's wayfinding satisfaction score had declined during COVID — when operational necessity prompted sign proliferation — and reversed after the intervention.

A parallel Munich Airport study using the same methodology over four days reached a finding that ought to be hung on the wall of every airport design office: *passengers largely ignored information on navigation signage that wasn't relevant to their journey*. The implication is not just that sign overload confuses. The implication is that sign overload trains passengers to stop reading signs altogether. The system that adds more signs to communicate more information communicates, on average, less.

The 2025 study in the *Journal of Asian Architecture and Building Engineering* — a controlled laboratory experiment with sixty participants varying information density, format, and time pressure — confirmed the mechanism. High information density and text-only formats increased cognitive load and reduced decision confidence; text-plus-graphic formats delayed the onset of cognitive overload [Operations Analyst Brief; Technology Scout Brief]. Passengers under time pressure exhibited physiologically measurable resource strain — larger pupil diameters, lower reported decision confidence. The operational implication is that the same sign that performs adequately for a leisurely traveler becomes actively misleading for a connecting passenger with twenty minutes.

The Heathrow and Munich studies are commercial partnerships with Tobii, which means they are applied research rather than independent. The methodology is sound, the findings replicate the laboratory evidence, and the airports that have taken them seriously have measurable improvements to show for it. Manchester Airport's documented wayfinding improvement, under the direction of a single Head of Visual Environment with a clear directive to reduce sign volume, can be summarized in five words from the manager herself: *We're vastly reducing the number of signs on site* [Technology Scout Brief]. The lesson is not subtle. Most US hubs have learned the opposite lesson.

### The Wayshowing Distinction and the Committee Problem

Mollerup's reframing of design as "wayshowing" is more than terminological. It locates the failure mode at the design committee. A signage committee, by structural disposition, optimizes for stakeholder representation. The airline operations team wants its boarding information visible. The retail tenant wants directional signage to its space. TSA wants its prohibited-items notice prominent. CBP wants its currency declaration warning. The fire marshal wants the egress signs lit. The ADA coordinator wants the tactile and Braille requirements met at every doorway. The aviation marketing office wants the airport name and city brand visible to arriving passengers. Each stakeholder is acting reasonably. Each represents a legitimate interest. None of them, by definition, is responsible for the cumulative cognitive load on the passenger.

The committee dynamic explains why every US hub airport's signage looks roughly the same — additively dense, hierarchically flat, visually crowded — even though every US hub has hired some of the same wayfinding consultants. The consultants produce competent design. The committees absorb the design and then add to it. The system degrades to its governance floor: the weakest enforcement point becomes the system's effective standard.

The international exemplars — Frankfurt (1972), Singapore Changi (1981), Munich (1992), Zurich, Helsinki — all share a feature that the US largely lacks: a single airport authority with explicit veto power over commercial signage, federal-agency signage, airline tenant signage, and concessionaire signage. Otl Aicher's 1972 Frankfurt system embedded the rule in the airport's operating architecture: commercial advertising was spatially separated from wayfinding signage, on different surfaces, in different formats. The separation was structural, enforced as a contract term, not negotiated case by case [Aviation Historian Brief]. Aicher solved the design problem fifty-four years ago. What the US has not built is the governance to sustain such a system across multiple construction eras and stakeholder cohorts.

The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey, in commissioning Paul Mijksenaar to extend the Schiphol system to the JFK, LGA, and EWR campus, attempted the closest US equivalent. The 2020 PANYNJ Airport Standards Manual for Pedestrian Signing and Wayfinding designates Aviation Customer Experience in the Central Office as the enforcement authority, with a Program Manager for Wayfinding and Connections Solutions serving as principal reviewer of all signage project submittals. The lease agreements bind terminal operators to compliance [Operations Analyst Brief; Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief]. The program took the better part of a decade from initiation to adoption, required extraordinary political capital, and remains in prototype rollout at JFK years after the manual was finalized. The PANYNJ effort is at once the proof that the governance approach can be built in a US context and the proof that building it is harder than any prior airport authority has appreciated.

### Contradiction Debt Is the Hidden Operating Cost

If the design problem was solved in 1972 and the methodology was federally codified in 2013, what does an unaddressed US wayfinding system actually look like in the field? It looks like a stratigraphy of layered decisions, each correct at the moment it was made, each contradicting the others now.

At Charlotte Douglas International, before remediation, passengers consistently overestimated walking time durations because inconsistent wayfinding cues induced uncertainty, which produced hesitation. The intervention — consolidating five alpha-coded security zones into three numeric zones, with color-coded portals and vertical beacons — reduced perceived complexity without physical reconstruction [Operations Analyst Brief]. The operational lesson is one that runs counter to design instinct: *consistency is a separate variable from quality*. A uniformly mediocre system outperforms a partially excellent one where the excellent and mediocre elements contradict each other. A passenger who has learned the rules of the system in one zone is being punished for that learning when she enters the next zone and the rules have silently changed.

The IAD case carries six distinct construction events: the Saarinen Main Terminal (1962), the Midfield Concourses (1996), the underground walkway (2004), the AeroTrain (2010), the Concourse C expansion (2016), and the Silver Line Metro connection (2022) [Aviation Historian Brief]. Each event introduced new passenger circulation modes with no predecessor signage. Each one required new signage to explain a new option, layered onto signage explaining earlier options that many passengers still used. By 2022, IAD passengers could choose among four modes to reach the concourses — mobile lounges, the underground walkway, the AeroTrain, and the Metro connection — with each mode partially served by signage from a different construction era, in different fonts, with different terminology for the same destinations.

The DCA case is more compressed but structurally identical. Three terminal eras — Terminal A (pre-1941 and 1958), the César Pelli Terminal B/C (1997), and the post-9/11 emergency checkpoint zone — accumulated signage vocabularies produced by different designers under different standards [Aviation Historian Brief]. Project Journey, MWAA's $1 billion DCA capital program, included a five-day window in June 2022 during which more than 1,000 signs were converted from terminal letter-names to terminal numbers and from numeric gate designations to alphanumeric [Chief Engineer Brief; Airline Commercial Strategist Brief]. The coordination required airline FIDS systems, boarding pass systems, WMATA Metro station entrance signage, parking garage digital systems, and roadway signs to update on a synchronized timeline. It was an extraordinary operational achievement and also a reminder of how high the friction of any system-wide signage change has become.

What does the contradiction debt cost? Most airports cannot answer the question directly because no one routes operational exceptions back to the wayfinding system. Missed flights are logged against carrier delays. Gate holds for late passengers are logged against boarding inefficiency. Information desk volume is not tracked against wayfinding performance at most US airports. The measurement gap is itself a governance failure: if no one owns wayfinding as a performance system, no one measures its failures [Operations Analyst Brief].

What we can document, even partially: traditional airport sign maintenance across a mid-sized terminal system exceeds $740,000 annually before any improvement is funded [Infrastructure Economist Brief, Ponemon 2023 figure cited from vendor literature]. Information desk staff time consumed by directional questions runs around eighty percent at large hubs [Operations Analyst Brief]. Industry analyses, sourced from customer experience vendors and properly discounted, suggest that up to $700 million is lost annually across the US system in concession revenue attributable to wayfinding-related passenger experience failures [Operations Analyst Brief; Infrastructure Economist Brief — vendor figure, directional only, not used as load-bearing]. The directional point underneath the soft number has support from independent research: a peer-reviewed 2024 study of 89 US airports found that a 10 percent increase in passenger dwell time was associated with a 6 percent increase in retail revenue and an 8 percent increase in food and beverage revenue [Infrastructure Economist Brief]. Wayfinding-driven dwell time improvement — by reducing navigation anxiety, eliminating head-down rushing to gates, extending comfortable pre-board time — is among the few interventions that convert fixed terminal area into incremental non-aeronautical yield.

For MWAA specifically, the yield gap is measurable. The airports reported combined in-terminal concession revenue of $110 million in 2023 across approximately 52 million passengers — about $2.12 per passenger, materially below the North American non-aeronautical average of $8.68 per passenger (which includes parking and is therefore not a perfect comparison) [Infrastructure Economist Brief]. At a conservative one percent improvement in dwell time, the elasticity implies $660,000 to $880,000 in incremental annual revenue per percentage point gained. The arithmetic is not subtle. A $5–$10 million one-time wayfinding investment at MWAA, if it produced a one to two percent dwell-time improvement across the system, would pay back in years, not decades, before any reduction in service recovery costs or information desk labor is counted.

### Why Digital Is Not the Answer the Vendors Sell

The dynamic-versus-static debate, as airports usually have it, is the wrong frame for the question. The better frame, drawn from the eye-tracking literature and the empirical record on what passengers actually do, is to match each medium to the information-change rate of the location it serves.

Dynamic digital displays earn their cost where the information is time-variant. Flight Information Display Systems for gate assignments, departure times, delay status, and baggage carousel assignments. Security wait times at checkpoint approaches. Gate area concourse directories at hubs with frequent changes [Operations Analyst Brief; Technology Scout Brief]. The global FIDS market was $527 million in 2024, growing at 7 percent CAGR — settled technology, settled value, no meaningful controversy [Technology Scout Brief]. Changi Terminal 2 retired its iconic split-flap boards in February 2024 after twenty-one years; nobody mourned the operational loss.

Static signage earns its cost where the information does not change. Which direction is Terminal C. Where are the elevators. Which way is ground transportation. The Munich eye-tracking study found that passengers learn to filter content that changes unpredictably; a screen that rotates between *Gates A1–A30 →* and a car rental advertisement trains passengers to discount it as a navigation resource [Technology Scout Brief]. Decision-point under time pressure is exactly the moment when a static answer outperforms a rotating one. Denver International's 2013 signage overhaul understood this: it replaced 23 roadside and 22 overhead signs with retroreflective static alternatives, saving an estimated $3 to $4 million in avoided structural replacement costs, while establishing pilot standards for digital displays in the terminal proper [Operations Analyst Brief; Chief Engineer Brief]. Retroreflective overhead signs are lower maintenance, lower power, and — critically — always on when there is a power event.

The lifecycle math is more sobering than capital programs typically recognize. Static backlit signs run a realistic 15- to 20-year useful life with LED lamp replacement at years 8 to 10. Digital display panels in 24/7 airport environments run 5 to 7 years; media players run 3 to 5 years [Chief Engineer Brief]. A complete FIDS hardware-plus-software deployment runs $8,000 to $15,000 per display node, with premium gate-area units reaching $20,000 to $30,000. An estimated 35 to 40 percent of FIDS units currently installed at US commercial airports are operating beyond their stated lifecycle. The strategic error airports make is funding digital installations in capital programs while treating refresh-cycle hardware replacement as an O&M problem — then deferring O&M when budgets tighten. Airports are now carrying digital signage fleets installed in the early 2010s that have never been refreshed.

The case for app-based wayfinding is weaker still. Only 7 percent of airport passengers use the departure airport's dedicated mobile app [Technology Scout Brief; Contrarian Brief]. The gap is being filled, to some degree, by Apple Maps indoor mapping at more than thirty US airports and by Google Maps Live View at Zurich and Sydney. But the substitution argument has a hole the Contrarian brief itself partially acknowledges: the passenger who most needs wayfinding help — the unfamiliar traveler, the first-time international visitor, the elderly passenger, the connecting passenger under time pressure — is, by definition, the passenger least likely to have installed a third-party indoor navigation tool and least able to consult it under stress. Designing for the digital native is designing for the population that does not need the help.

### The Governance Architecture That Actually Exists

The reason this debate keeps recurring at US airports is not that the designers have run out of ideas. It is that the governance structure makes the design irrelevant. The Regulatory and Political Analyst brief catalogs the constraint stack precisely: the FAA has no enforceable authority over indoor terminal wayfinding (Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F is guidance, not regulation). ADA creates a genuine regulatory floor, but compliance is often implemented as a parallel sign layer that competes with the wayfinding hierarchy rather than integrating with it. TSA's Checkpoint Requirements and Planning Guide (May 2025) requires that all signage within the checkpoint zone be approved by TSA headquarters' Office of Signage Guidelines. CBP's Airport Technical Design Standards govern signage in Federal Inspection Services halls. Local fire marshals govern egress signage under NFPA 101. Concessionaires hold advertising display rights under contracts that typically run 10 to 15 years.

The result, at a major international hub, is that three federal regulatory authorities govern the same square foot of terminal floor and do not coordinate with each other. The airport's own standard loses jurisdiction precisely at the two highest-stress moments in the passenger journey — security and customs [Chief Engineer Brief]. And the contractual cage is the binding constraint: airline use and lease agreements grant carriers the right to brand their gate areas, ticket counters, and jetways, with airport approval authority that is structurally broad in theory and rarely exercised in practice [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief; Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief].

The committee design problem is, in plain terms, the structural output of distributed veto with no point of authoritative override. Naming it as such matters, because it locates the lever where it actually sits: not in design talent but in the institutional decision to centralize wayfinding authority and then defend that authority against every plausible objection from every stakeholder who will produce one.

## IV. The Counter-Case, Honestly Presented

A good thesis does not benefit from a straw-man opposition. The strongest case against the cheapest-lever argument deserves to be stated at full strength, because a reader who finds it persuasive will not be argued out of it by a summary dismissal.

**The smartphone has changed the navigation environment in ways that fixed signage cannot recover.** Only 7 percent of passengers use the departure airport's dedicated app, which sounds like a navigation vacuum but is not. The vacuum is being filled. Apple Maps provides indoor mapping at more than thirty US airports, including Dulles, Reagan National, JFK, LaGuardia, Newark, LAX, SFO, SEA-TAC, Denver, and Miami. Google's Live View indoor navigation — using the phone camera to overlay directional arrows in real time — is live at Zurich (one of the exemplars cited by the thesis) and at Sydney. IATA's 2025 Global Passenger Survey found that 78 percent of passengers want a single smartphone device to combine digital wallet, digital ID, loyalty cards, and airport navigation. Airline app usage has risen to 23 percent of passengers, up 7 percentage points in five years. The trajectory of passenger navigation is unambiguously digital and phone-centric. A wayfinding strategy in 2026 built around static sign hierarchy is optimizing for a passenger behavior model that is actively shrinking as a share of traffic [Contrarian Brief].

**The international exemplars are capital success stories, not governance success stories.** Helsinki's celebrated single-terminal wayfinding clarity followed a €1 billion terminal expansion that consolidated all operations under one roof. Zurich's "Airport 2000" fifth construction phase cost CHF 2.4 billion and produced a purpose-built Airside Centre with unified hub geometry. Munich's Terminal 2 is a joint venture between Flughafen München GmbH and Deutsche Lufthansa AG — a co-ownership arrangement described as unique in Europe. Changi is investing S$3 billion over six years and S$5+ billion in Terminal 5. The conclusion the thesis draws — that these airports wayfind well because of governance — has the causal arrow backward. They wayfind well because billions of dollars created unified physical environments in which a single design authority could operate without contractual contradictions. US airports cannot replicate the result without replicating the capital base [Contrarian Brief].

**The single-authority governance model the thesis implicitly recommends is illegal under US lease structures.** Airlines at US airports hold contractual approval rights over capital development under their use and lease agreements. Thirty-six of the 66 largest US airports have majority-in-interest provisions that give signatory carriers the right to review and, at affirmative MII airports, to block capital projects above defined thresholds [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief; Contrarian Brief]. Signage in airline-leased concourses is, by contract, airline signage. TSA controls checkpoint signage by federal mandate. CBP controls FIS signage by federal mandate. The FAA's own Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F draws an explicit distinction between "systems typically installed by an airport operator" and "those typically installed by individual airlines." A "single wayfinding authority with veto power over tenants, TSA, and airlines" is not a governance innovation that US airports have failed to adopt out of managerial weakness. It is a legal construct that does not exist and cannot be conjured without renegotiating leases, federal agency memoranda, and security protocols simultaneously. The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey is the most sophisticated US operator with the most documented attempt at this, and its 2021 manual remains in prototype rollout at JFK five years later — not because PANYNJ lacks discipline, but because the implementation runs through fourteen terminals, multiple federal partners, and dozens of carrier leases [Contrarian Brief; Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief].

**The digital wayfinding path is the correct investment category, and it does require capital.** The airports demonstrating measurable navigation improvement are deploying dynamic systems: interactive kiosks at Philadelphia International, multilingual digital assistance at Hamad International in 20 languages, AR wayfinding at Amsterdam Schiphol and Hong Kong. One major airport's deployment of 500 LED wayfinding displays — vendor-reported, properly discounted — produced a 37 percent reduction in information desk inquiries, a 40 percent reduction in average navigation time, and a 25 percent improvement in gate-reaching speed [Infrastructure Economist Brief]. Digital systems can be updated in real time from a content management system. Static sign corrections require a work order, a fabricator, and a night crew. The thesis is directionally right about the importance of wayfinding but wrong about the tool. The correct argument is for digital capital investment, not committee discipline [Contrarian Brief].

**The "cheapest lever" claim is asserted, not demonstrated, and the competition is real.** A comprehensive signage rationalization at a major US hub involves cataloging thousands of existing signs (the Port Authority documented over 10,000 across its three-airport system), eight to twelve months of planning and design, fabrication of custom sign types in airport-rated materials, installation during active operations, and a permanent enforcement mechanism. Meanwhile, PA system improvements, gate agent positioning, information desk placement, and FIDS refresh-rate adjustments cost tens of thousands of dollars and can be deployed in weeks without airline consent. The thesis conflates "high impact" with "low cost" without rigorously pricing either claim [Contrarian Brief].

**The "discipline not dollars" framing risks letting capital decision-makers off the hook.** If the Council publishes a finding that wayfinding dysfunction is a governance problem rather than a resource problem, the institutional takeaway at most US airports will be: form a committee. The airport director will convene a wayfinding working group that will produce a design standards document that will sit in a drawer while the airlines continue to hang their own signs and TSA continues to staple up its own stanchion-mounted notices. The governance-first framing becomes, in practice, an excuse for under-investment in digital systems that would actually work. It gives boards a low-cost way to appear to act — approve the style guide — while avoiding the capital appropriation that would produce results [Contrarian Brief].

These six points constitute the strongest version of the case against the thesis. A reader who finds them persuasive will, by default, conclude that this report is an essay in the wrong decade.

## V. Why the Counter-Case Is Insufficient

The counter-case is partly right. It is not right enough to overturn the thesis. The work of this section is to concede what must be conceded, by name, and then to explain why what remains still holds.

### What the smartphone substitution argument gets right and what it misses

The trajectory is real. Apple Maps and Google Maps have done meaningful work to extend indoor navigation into airports, and the IATA passenger preference data is what it is — 78 percent want a single phone for the whole journey. A wayfinding strategy that ignores the trend is, on its face, foolish.

But the substitution claim has two specific failures that the thesis turns on. First, the population for which the substitution holds — frequent flyers comfortable with phone-based navigation, often with the airline app already installed, often familiar with the specific airport — is the population that does not generate the wayfinding cost. The cost is generated by the unfamiliar traveler, the first-time international visitor, the elderly passenger, the connecting passenger who has just stepped off a long-haul aircraft and is six time zones from home. Those passengers are not pulling out a phone to consult an indoor mapping product at the bottom of an escalator. They are looking up. The signage answers the question, or it does not.

Second — and this is the substantive point — the eye-tracking evidence is not about whether passengers can navigate with help. It is about what they do under stress at decision points. The Heathrow and Munich studies, the Tandfonline 2025 lab study, the Shanghai Pudong research all converge on the same finding. Under time pressure, passengers exhibit measurable cognitive load increases, scan signs in predictable patterns, demonstrate "reconfirmation behavior" — re-reading a sign they have already acted on — and require text-plus-graphic formats to maintain decision confidence [Operations Analyst Brief; Technology Scout Brief]. This is not a problem a phone solves. It is a problem the environment solves at the moment the decision is made. The phone is for planning the journey before it starts and for tracking the bag after it ends. The decision point in front of the escalator at 6 a.m. is not phone work.

The smartphone substitution argument is, ultimately, an argument that the airport need not optimize the environment because the passenger will optimize for the airport. The eye-tracking data says that the high-stakes passenger will not.

### What the capital-base argument gets right and what it misses

Helsinki's €1 billion terminal expansion is real. Zurich's CHF 2.4 billion fifth construction phase is real. Changi's S$3 billion six-year program is real. These airports built unified physical environments. Their wayfinding clarity is in part the result.

But the causal argument runs in only one direction unless it can be tested against a case where the governance discipline was present without the matching capital event. That case is Frankfurt, 1972. Otl Aicher's Frankfurt Central Terminal system imposed wayfinding discipline on an existing terminal building. The discipline was built into the contracts: spatial separation of commercial advertising from wayfinding signage, restriction of commercial format, enforced not by aspiration but by lease term [Aviation Historian Brief]. Aicher solved the wayfinding problem inside a constrained capital envelope. The mechanism he used — contractual enforcement of a hierarchical standard against the most aggressive contaminating force in the environment — is the mechanism PANYNJ is attempting to operationalize fifty years later. It is the mechanism Tampa International built into its Journey Segment Evaluation Tool. It is, in a less complete form, what the LAWA Design and Construction Handbook does at LAX. The capital was not the variable in any of these cases; the governance was.

The counter-case requires the reader to believe that no governance solution is possible without a billion-dollar capital event preceding it. The historical record does not support that claim. The PANYNJ case, in particular, supports the more interesting one: governance reform is possible, the implementation timeline is long, and the airports that have begun the work are visibly further along than the airports that have not.

### What the lease-structure argument gets right and what it misses

The contractual cage is real. Majority-in-interest clauses are real. The FAA's acknowledgment of the divided-authority structure is real. Renegotiating a use agreement against airline objection is a multi-year political act with material airline leverage. None of this is in dispute.

But the lease-structure argument concedes the point it most needs to defeat. The airport operator has unambiguous authority over common areas — ticketing halls, security queue approaches, baggage claim, ground transportation curbs, escalators, transit stations, the transition zones between airline-controlled concourses. These are the most critical decision points in the passenger journey. They are where the eye-tracking data says wayfinding failures concentrate. They are where the airport's authority is contractually unquestioned. A signage moratorium and rationalization in these zones requires governance discipline, not capital, and not lease renegotiation. The MII clause does not apply to operating standards classified appropriately; the FAA's distinction between "airport-installed" and "airline-installed" systems is a constraint on the airline-controlled zones, not on the airport's authority in the rest of the building [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief; Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief].

This is also why the airline commercial strategist's framing of the question — *will the dominant carrier support a wayfinding standard that covers its concourse?* — is the right question for the airline-controlled zones and the wrong question for the rest of the building. United will resist any standard that constrains its Polaris lounge environment, its gate holdroom branding, or its proprietary gate display technology. American will resist any blanket standard that asks it to subordinate the $1 billion in capital it has just installed at DCA. Those resistances are real and substantial. They do not extend to the AeroTrain station, the National Hall, the baggage claim floor, the escalator at the bottom of which the connecting passenger is making her decision. Those are MWAA's. The standard has authority there.

The PANYNJ implementation timeline is not, as the counter-case reads it, evidence that the governance approach fails. It is evidence that the governance approach takes time and political capital. A program that produces a unified standard manual in five years and reaches full enforcement across all terminals in ten is a program that, in industry timeframes, is moving fast. The alternative — every terminal renovation generating a new signage layer, every carrier installation contradicting the last one, every TSA reconfiguration carving out another exempt zone — is not a faster path. It is the path MWAA has been on for thirty years.

### What the digital-investment argument gets right and what it misses

Dynamic digital systems do real work. FIDS is settled technology. Interactive kiosks at known high-confusion points add value. Real-time security wait time displays reduce checkpoint anxiety. Multilingual digital assistance at international transfer zones reaches passengers that English-language static signs cannot. The thesis does not deny any of this. The thesis denies that *more digital* is, by itself, the answer.

The vendor literature consistently overpromises. The 37 percent and 40 percent and 25 percent figures from the unnamed major airport's 500-display LED deployment come from the display manufacturer's marketing material. They have not been independently audited. The $700 million annual revenue-loss figure that recurs in airport wayfinding decks is sourced to Adoreboard, a customer-experience analytics vendor; the original methodology is not traceable [Technology Scout Brief; Infrastructure Economist Brief]. Properly discounted, these are directional claims, not findings. The structurally valid point underneath them — that disoriented passengers under-spend and over-complain — has independent support, but the magnitude is not what the vendor decks claim.

More important, the case for digital depends on the same governance structure the thesis is arguing for. A digital wayfinding network with no enforcement of content hierarchy, no constraint on advertising co-location, and no single owner of the standard becomes a more expensive version of the same problem. Manchester Airport's documented improvement under a single Head of Visual Environment did not come from installing more screens. It came from explicitly choosing not to use signage real estate for advertising in navigation-critical locations [Technology Scout Brief]. Without that discipline, digital displays trained passengers to filter the signal as noise — the Munich eye-tracking finding restated as a technology procurement principle. Digital infrastructure is not an alternative to governance. It is governance with higher capital exposure.

### What the form-a-committee risk gets right, and how to answer it

The Contrarian brief's sharpest point may be the procedural one. If the Council publishes a finding that wayfinding is a governance problem, the institutional reflex at most US airports will be to form a committee, produce a design standards document, and use the production of the document as evidence of action. The document will sit in a drawer. The airlines will continue to hang their own signs. TSA will continue to staple up its own notices. The capital programs will continue to layer new signage onto the old, and the contradiction debt will continue to compound.

This is a real risk, and any honest version of the thesis has to name it. The answer is that the deliverable is not a design document. The deliverable is an enforcement protocol. Tampa's Journey Segment Evaluation Tool is the model: a mechanism that requires every new capital project to be evaluated against the existing sign hierarchy at each decision point it touches, with a designated reviewer who has actual veto authority over project documents. PANYNJ's Aviation Customer Experience structure is the same idea at scale: a Program Manager for Wayfinding and Connections Solutions named in writing, with authority embedded in the lease agreements, supported by an asset management platform (SignAgent at Denver International tracks ten thousand signs as a queryable database) that makes enforcement auditable [Operations Analyst Brief; Chief Engineer Brief; Aviation Historian Brief].

The committee risk is real for an airport that treats wayfinding as a design exercise. It is mitigated for an airport that treats wayfinding as an asset class with a named owner, a published lifecycle plan, and an enforcement protocol tied to every capital approval gate. The choice between "form a committee" and "buy more screens" is a false choice. The right choice is to build governance with teeth, which has a higher institutional cost than either alternative but produces durable outcomes that neither does.

### The cheapest-lever claim, rigorously

The Contrarian brief asks the thesis to defend its strongest assertion — that wayfinding is the cheapest operational lever — against rigorous comparison. The defensible version is more careful than the slogan.

Wayfinding is among the few operational interventions at a US hub airport where the order-of-magnitude ratio between cost and value can be reasonably argued at 100:1 or better, with an implementation path that does not require lease renegotiation in the most critical zones and does not require federal regulatory change. A $5 to $10 million one-time wayfinding investment at MWAA — calibrated against Heathrow's £10 million five-year framework, scaled by passenger volume — is on the order of 0.1 to 0.2 percent of the 2025–2039 capital program [Infrastructure Economist Brief; analyst calculation from brief-cited components]. The same investment, against the dwell-time elasticity findings from the 2024 89-airport study, has plausible incremental concession revenue in the $1 to $5 million per year range, plus reductions in customer service labor of unknown but non-trivial magnitude, plus reductions in maintenance-line spending currently running at $740,000 annually on the legacy system, plus modest reductions in service-recovery cost from passengers reaching gates on time [Infrastructure Economist Brief; Operations Analyst Brief]. The payback case is durable.

The other low-cost operational interventions the Contrarian brief names — PA system improvements, gate agent positioning, information desk placement, FIDS refresh rates — are valuable. They are also, with the partial exception of FIDS, complementary to a coherent wayfinding system rather than substitutes for it. A staffed information desk is the system's exception-handling layer; better placement of the desk does not address the structural inconsistency that drove eighty percent of agent time toward directional questions in the first place. A signage rationalization that reduces that load by half is doing the higher-order work.

The strict version of the thesis: wayfinding is among the highest-yield, lowest-incremental-cost capital interventions available, with the lowest external-stakeholder dependency for the work that produces the most of the value, and with measurable returns whose elasticities are documented in peer-reviewed literature. It is not the only cheap lever. It is the cheapest lever that the airport can pull without negotiating with someone who can say no.

## VI. Implications for MWAA

This is a council writing for MWAA's specific position. The generic recommendations are uninteresting. The question is what MWAA should do, given what MWAA actually has.

What MWAA has, today, is two airports under a single authority, operating across the most stratified signage history in the US large-hub inventory. IAD carries six construction eras and two ground-transport modes serving the same connection use case. DCA is fresh off Project Journey's $1 billion capital program, including the five-day, 1,000-sign renaming, and is operating new American Airlines concourses built to American's specifications. The 2025–2039 Airport Use and Lease Agreement is a hybrid residual structure with a 1.40x debt service coverage target and a 1.25x bond covenant floor. Moody's projects MWAA's adjusted debt per O&D enplanement will peak at approximately $400 in 2028, with net revenue DSCR declining from 1.63x in 2024 to roughly 1.3x over the next five years [Infrastructure Economist Brief]. United carries 49.9 percent of IAD mainline enplanements, or roughly two-thirds with United Express. American controls 53 percent of DCA flights. The $9 billion capital program is set. The $514,000 IAD Signage and Wayfinding Masterplan budget and the $4.87 million DCA equivalent are study budgets, not implementation programs [Operations Analyst Brief; Chief Engineer Brief].

Five observations follow from this position, in the order they would have to be addressed.

**One. Treat wayfinding as a named asset class with a single owner reporting at the executive level.** Not a committee. Not a working group. A Sign Standards Authority — one named position, reporting to a senior executive, with explicit veto authority over signage in MWAA-controlled common areas at both airports — and a published Wayfinding and Signing Standards Manual covering both IAD and DCA on a single set of rules. The PANYNJ structure is the closest US precedent and the most directly applicable: Aviation Customer Experience in the Central Office, Program Manager for Wayfinding and Connections Solutions as principal reviewer. MWAA's Design Manual currently runs to seven volumes, with separate tenant design standards for IAD's Main Terminal and Concourse B — two parts of the same airport [Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief]. The volume count is structural evidence of the problem. A forward standard collapses this into a single hierarchy with two-airport scope.

**Two. Move the masterplan budgets from study to implementation, and structure the program to avoid the MII trap.** The $514,000 and $4.87 million allocations are appropriate for the study phase. The implementation phase is missing. A defensible scope estimate, calibrated against the Heathrow framework and against the Ontario International experience (where a $485,000 signage project finished at $1.3 million on field condition discovery), runs $15 to $25 million for a phased rationalization across both airports — within the range that, treated as an operating standard with capital classification reserved for infrastructure-grade elements (sign substrates, digital backbone, asset management platform), need not trigger formal MII review at affirmative-MII thresholds [Infrastructure Economist Brief; Airline Commercial Strategist Brief]. The airline commercial strategist brief is explicit on this point: the structuring is not just accounting; it is governance strategy. United and American will scrutinize any capital improvement that enters the rate base. They will not scrutinize an operating standards manual published by the airport authority over the spaces the authority controls.

**Three. Solve the IAD transport-mode wayfinding problem at the decision point, not at the gate.** The AeroTrain serves Concourses A, B, and C. The Mobile Lounges serve Concourse D. Connecting passengers must distinguish between the two modes before they can read the FIDS that tells them their outbound concourse. The signage solution at the AeroTrain station / Mobile Lounge interface has to break the dependency chain: the sign must answer the question *which mode do I take?* by pointing to the FIDS first, and only then routing the passenger. This is a single decision point at a single physical location. It is solvable inside MWAA's authority. United's preferred solution — completing the AeroTrain loop to Concourse D — is a multi-hundred-million-dollar capital program; a signage-only intervention is second-best at best [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief]. But second-best at one percent of the cost of the first-best, while the first-best is unfunded, is the right call.

**Four. Account for the Saarinen constraint, and use the IAD-Concourse D Mobile Lounge connection to test the discipline.** The IAD Main Terminal carries National Register of Historic Places designation. Overhead pendant sign installation in the catenary-cable roof requires structural analysis and preservation review on every move [Chief Engineer Brief]. This is not a constraint to argue against; it is a constraint to design around. The IAD signage standard should specify that overhead pendant signage in the Main Terminal is a constrained inventory — every existing pendant sign is presumed to be in its final position, and additions require a formal exception process. Floor-mounted directional cues, wall-mounted directories, and digital displays at landside-airside transition points should carry the navigation load that would, in a less constrained building, be carried by overhead pendants. The Pittsburgh 2025 design principle — *the new building is expansive with great natural flow that doesn't need extensive wayfinding; instead, it needs larger, recognizable moments* — does not apply to a 1962 historic terminal in the way it applies to a 2025 greenfield. But the embedded principle does apply: use the architecture to do the navigation work the signage cannot easily do in a constrained envelope.

**Five. Budget for eye-tracking validation before and after implementation, and fund an asset-management platform on the SignAgent model.** A terminal-scale eye-tracking study using the methodology Heathrow and Munich applied runs $40,000 to $80,000 [Technology Scout Brief]. The validation cost is a rounding error against the implementation budget. Any wayfinding program installed without pre-and-post validation is a program that does not know whether it worked. Denver International's SignAgent deployment — tracking 10,000 signs as a queryable asset database, reducing work-order generation from 4–5 days to minutes — is the operational platform that turns a standards manual from a document into a system [Technology Scout Brief; Chief Engineer Brief]. MWAA's existing Pointr indoor positioning contract (six-figure, 2019) is data without a frame [Operations Analyst Brief]; an asset management platform is the frame.

What this is not. It is not a typeface recommendation. The graphic designers will produce competent design. The competent design is not the problem MWAA has. The problem MWAA has is that the design, once produced, has historically been undermined by every adjacent stakeholder and every subsequent capital project. The forward standard MWAA needs is the one that the next capital project, the next federal mandate, and the next carrier renegotiation cannot quietly displace. That standard is built on three things: an owner with named authority, an asset management discipline that makes the system auditable, and an enforcement protocol embedded in the gate review process for every project that touches a sign. The pieces are commercially available. The decision is institutional.

---

The connecting passenger from the opening of this report will, if MWAA does the work, step off her aircraft and find the answer she needs at the first decision point she encounters. She will not know whose name is on the standards document. She will not know that the sign she is reading is a different generation from the one above her gate. She will read it, choose the right mode, and make her connection.

If MWAA does not do the work, she will be on a later flight, and we will continue to call it a carrier delay.
