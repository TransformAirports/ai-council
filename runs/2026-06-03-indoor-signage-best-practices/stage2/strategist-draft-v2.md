## I. The Decision Point

At six in the morning at Washington Dulles, a passenger steps off a United mainline aircraft at Concourse C with a connection to Concourse D. She has thirty-five minutes — United's published minimum, the same number Delta promises at Atlanta. She has never been to IAD before. She walks up the jet bridge into the holdroom, past gate signs branded in United blue, and looks for the sign that will tell her what to do next.

The sign she needs has to answer a specific question. Not "where are the gates" — she knows where the gates are. Not "where is ground transportation" — she is not going to ground transportation. The question is: *which mode of underground transit will get me to Concourse D?* The answer is not the AeroTrain. The AeroTrain runs to Concourses A, B, and C. Concourse D is still served by Mobile Lounges — the strange, two-story buses Eero Saarinen designed in 1962, which depart every five minutes and can take up to fifteen minutes to reach the concourse. United's own airport guide tells passengers this. The airport's signage, layered across decades of construction, tells them less reliably.

If she reads the right sign, she has twenty minutes of buffer for an outbound boarding. If she reads the wrong sign — if she boards an AeroTrain and rides to Concourse C station, then has to come back, then has to walk to the Mobile Lounge bay, then has to wait — the buffer can collapse to negative. The thirty-five-minute MCT is constructed for the best-case path [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief]. The signage system at the decision point determines which path she takes. The signs at IAD were installed under at least three different design standards across at least three construction eras. Some are 1990s vintage. Some came in with the AeroTrain in 2010. Some arrived with the Silver Line in 2022. They do not agree with each other about colors, hierarchies, terminology, or what the passenger is presumed to already know.

This is one passenger at one decision point at one US hub on one morning. The institutional question is what happens when no one owns the standard that resolves it.

This report argues three things. **Wayfinding is the lowest-cost, highest-leverage operational intervention available to a US hub airport.** The reason most US hubs wayfind poorly is not budget; it is the absence of a governance structure with the authority to enforce a single hierarchy against the airlines, the federal agencies, the concessionaires, and the airport's own capital programs, each of which tends to add signs faster than the system can absorb them. The exemplars cited in every wayfinding case study — Changi, Munich, Zurich, Helsinki — wayfind well not primarily because they spent more, but because a single design authority owned the standard across decades of expansion, with explicit performance criteria, and refused to negotiate. MWAA, having accumulated three decades of contradictory signage decisions at IAD and DCA, has the opportunity to do the same. The question is not whether the standard exists. The standard exists. The question is whether the authority to enforce it exists.

## II. Executive Summary

This is the report in miniature, for the reader who will not finish it.

The asymmetry is the first thing to see. MWAA's 2025–2039 Capital Construction Program totals $9 billion — roughly $600 million per year [Infrastructure Economist Brief]. Heathrow's 2013 wayfinding consultancy framework cost £10 million across five years for an 80-million-passenger airport; inflation-adjusted to 2026 and scaled by MWAA's traffic, a comparable program runs eight to thirteen million dollars [Infrastructure Economist Brief; analyst calculation]. That is one to two percent of a single year's MWAA capital spend — less than one part in a thousand of the fifteen-year program — to govern the efficiency of every square foot the program builds. The asymmetry between the cost of signage and the value of what signage governs is the structural case for treating wayfinding as a discipline rather than as a line item.

The methodology exists, has existed for forty years, was federally codified thirteen years ago, and is largely ignored. Romedi Passini's 1984 framework reframed wayfinding as spatial problem-solving: identify each point where the passenger must choose, and provide the minimum information needed to resolve that choice. ACRP Report 52 (2011), adopted into FAA Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F (2014), codified three principles — continuity (no passenger loses sight of a directional cue), connectivity (cues chain across the full journey), and consistency (the same system at every decision point) [Chief Engineer Brief]. The Advisory Circular is guidance, not regulation; compliance is not required for AIP grant eligibility [Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief]. Most US hub airports have never been tested against the standard they have nominally adopted.

The dynamic-versus-static debate is the wrong frame, and it produces wrong answers. Dynamic digital displays do specific work that static signage cannot — flight information, gate changes, baggage carousel assignments, security wait times. Static signage does specific work digital cannot — fixed orientation, directional confirmation, decision-point answers under stress. The Munich Airport eye-tracking study (Tobii Pro Glasses 3, four days of real travelers) found that passengers largely ignored navigation signage irrelevant to their journey — overload trains them to filter rather than confirm [Operations Analyst Brief; Architectural Historian Brief]. The error airports make is treating digital and static as a binary technology choice rather than matching each medium to the information-change rate at the location it serves. Airport-branded apps are not the substitute either — only seven percent of departing passengers use one [Technology Scout Brief]. Apple Maps and Google Maps Live View are filling part of the navigation gap at more than thirty US airports and at Zurich, but the passenger who most needs wayfinding help — the unfamiliar traveler, the first-time international visitor, the elderly passenger, the connecting passenger under time pressure — is, under stress at the decision point, looking up at the sign rather than down at the phone.

The binding constraint is governance, not design vocabulary. At a typical US large-hub airport the airport authority controls common areas; airlines control leased gate areas; TSA controls the checkpoint zone; CBP controls the FIS hall; concessionaires control advertising positions. No federal regulation requires any of them to coordinate [Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief]. The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey spent more than a decade building the closest US analog to a unified standard — the 2021 PANYNJ Airport Standards Manual for Pedestrian Signing and Wayfinding, with Aviation Customer Experience as enforcement authority — and remains in prototype rollout years later. PANYNJ is the proof that the governance approach can be built in a US context and the proof that building it is harder than any prior airport authority has appreciated [Operations Analyst Brief; Contrarian Brief].

The cost of governance failure is what the briefs collectively call contradiction debt. At IAD, six distinct construction events from 1962 through 2022 have deposited overlapping signage layers — Saarinen-era, AeroTrain-era, Silver Line-era — that use different colors, hierarchies, and terminology. At DCA, Project Journey's June 2022 conversion of more than 1,000 signs across six calendar days was layered onto signage debt already accumulated [Aviation Historian Brief; Chief Engineer Brief]. The cost shows up not in sign replacement but in cognitive load, in customer service agent time spent on directional questions (a vendor estimate puts it at roughly eighty percent of agent time at large hubs; treat the number as directional, but the pattern is real), in staff-driven verbal redirections that compound inconsistency rather than resolve it [Operations Analyst Brief].

Pittsburgh, 1992–2025, is the cleanest empirical demonstration of what unmanaged contradiction debt costs over time. PIT was built in 1992 as a US Airways hub with wayfinding optimized for connections. When the hub collapsed in 2004 — enplanements fell from twenty million to under eight, the traffic profile inverted from sixty percent connecting to ninety-eight percent O&D — the wayfinding system remained connection-optimized [Aviation Historian Brief]. For two decades, PIT operated with signs calibrated for a passenger population that no longer existed. The system was not fixed until the $1.5 billion greenfield terminal opened in November 2025. The lesson is sobering: a wayfinding system correct for one operational model and wrong for another stays wrong until someone has the governance authority and the capital to reset it. MWAA's terminals are currently in Chapter 2 of this story.

Live-terminal construction premium and historic-preservation constraints make remediation more expensive than scope documents anticipate. Airport construction runs forty to fifty percent above conventional commercial work because it schedules around flight operations [Chief Engineer Brief]. Ontario International budgeted a signage project at $485,000 and finished at $1.3 million — a 168 percent overrun on unknown field conditions [Infrastructure Economist Brief]. The IAD Main Terminal carries National Register of Historic Places designation; overhead pendant signs in the Saarinen catenary-cable roof require structural analysis and preservation review on every move [Chief Engineer Brief].

MWAA's current allocations are study budgets, not implementation budgets, and the carrier-response question is unaddressed. The 2021 capital allocations of $514,000 for an IAD Signage and Wayfinding Masterplan and $4.87 million for the DCA equivalent are appropriate for studies; they are not implementation programs [Chief Engineer Brief]. The 2025–2039 Airport Use and Lease Agreement, under a hybrid residual rate-setting structure, gives United (which carries approximately two-thirds of IAD enplanements including United Express) and American (which controls 53 percent of DCA flights) practical influence over any capital improvement that enters the rate base [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief]. A wayfinding program structured as a capital expenditure will face majority-in-interest scrutiny. Structured to keep capital-classified spend below MII thresholds — with the operating-standard component carrying most of the program — it can be advanced without triggering formal MII review, though the classification itself will be a negotiation.

The rest of this report shows the work, then takes the strongest counter-arguments seriously, then explains what those counter-arguments are missing.

## III. The Argument

### The Decision Point Is the Unit of Analysis

The fundamental act of bad airport signage is to answer a question the passenger is not asking. Romedi Passini, the Canadian environmental psychologist who wrote *Wayfinding in Architecture* in 1984, framed it precisely. A passenger entering a terminal is not executing a pre-loaded route. She is solving a series of nested problems. Each problem requires information at a specific moment and a specific place. The job of the sign system is not to publish the airport's full directory; it is to answer the next question.

What Passini did was reframe wayfinding from an aesthetic question to an operational one. What does the passenger need to decide *at this point*, and does this sign answer that question? The Danish designer Per Mollerup, who built the wayfinding systems for Copenhagen, Oslo, and Stockholm Arlanda between 1989 and 1998, coined "wayshowing" to drive the distinction home: wayfinding is what the passenger does; wayshowing is what the environment does. Design committees, Mollerup observed, optimize for the wrong variable. They design for beauty or brand rather than for the specific navigation decision the sign must resolve at the specific point where a passenger encounters it [Architectural Historian Brief].

Mollerup's nine wayfinding strategies catalog how passengers actually behave under uncertainty. The ninth, social navigation — asking a staff member — is the failure mode. Every time a passenger approaches a customer service agent to ask for directions, the environment has failed to answer a question it should have answered without speech. Industry estimates from wayfinding-software vendors put the share of customer service agent time consumed by directional questions at roughly eighty percent at large hubs; the number is directional, not audited, and should be treated as a proxy for system failure rather than a measured rate [Operations Analyst Brief]. The deeper evidence is the ACI ASQ data: across the global Airport Service Quality survey, wayfinding ranks consistently in the top six satisfaction drivers, and at the top eight US gateway airports surveyed for ACRP Report 161, finding their way was the leading concern passengers reported above amenity satisfaction [Architectural Historian Brief; Operations Analyst Brief]. Wayfinding is not a background variable. It is what the passenger remembers.

In 2011, the Transportation Research Board published ACRP Report 52 — *Wayfinding and Signing Guidelines for Airport Terminals and Landside*. The report operationalized Passini's framework into a formal methodology. Identify each segment of the passenger journey. Map the decision points within each segment. At each decision point, define the minimum information required to resolve the choice. Enforce three rules: continuity (no passenger should lose sight of a directional cue), connectivity (cues chain to each other across the full journey), consistency (the same system, the same language, the same hierarchy throughout) [Chief Engineer Brief]. In 2014, the FAA adopted ACRP 52's substance into Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F — *Airport Signing and Graphics*. (AC 150/5340-18H covers airfield marking signs only — runways and taxiways — and is a common confusion in airport scope documents; the two ACs address entirely different environments [Chief Engineer Brief].) The 12F standard is federal. It is not enforced. The Advisory Circular is guidance; compliance is not required for AIP grant eligibility [Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief].

The standard is also testable. Tampa International's Wayfinding Standards Manual — a 482-page document developed with Gresham Smith — includes a Journey Segment Evaluation Tool requiring that every new capital project be evaluated against the existing sign hierarchy at each decision point it touches, so that individual project decisions cannot silently degrade the system [Operations Analyst Brief]. Tampa is operating ACRP 52 as a checklist. Most US hub airports are not.

### What Eye-Tracking Has Actually Told Us

In December 2022 and January 2023, Heathrow Terminal 5 ran a formal eye-tracking study using Tobii Pro Glasses 3 on 108 participants moving through arriving, departing, and connecting journeys. The methodology — recording actual gaze data rather than surveying passengers about what they remember — is the most rigorous source of empirical evidence on airport signage behavior available in the public literature. What it found is embarrassing to designers and operators alike.

The failures concentrated at transitional points — escalator exits, entries to large halls, multi-path junctions. Not the mid-corridor sections where most airports place their largest signs. Two specific failure modes dominated. The first: signs that passengers simply did not notice, often because they were positioned outside the natural sightline at the moment the decision had to be made. The second: oversaturation of signs at the same point, where competing information increased cognitive load and reduced the probability that the passenger registered any of it [Technology Scout Brief; Operations Analyst Brief]. Heathrow acted on the data, decluttered the decision points, reconfigured the immigration entrance, and revamped onward travel signage. The terminal's wayfinding satisfaction score had declined during COVID — when operational necessity prompted sign proliferation — and reversed after the intervention.

The Munich Airport study, using the same methodology over four days with real travelers, reached a finding that ought to be hung on the wall of every airport design office: *passengers largely ignored information on navigation signage that wasn't relevant to their journey*. The implication is precise. Information density that does not serve the passenger's current decision is not neutral noise; passengers learn to filter against it, and the filter generalizes. A passenger trained to filter irrelevant content on one sign is more likely to filter relevant content on the next [Architectural Historian Brief; Operations Analyst Brief]. The system that adds more signs to communicate more information communicates, on average, less.

The 2025 study in the *Journal of Asian Architecture and Building Engineering* — a controlled laboratory experiment with sixty participants varying information density, format, and time pressure — confirmed the mechanism. High information density and text-only formats increased cognitive load and reduced decision confidence; text-plus-graphic formats delayed the onset of cognitive overload [Operations Analyst Brief]. Passengers under time pressure exhibited physiologically measurable resource strain — larger pupil diameters, lower reported decision confidence. The operational implication is that the same sign that performs adequately for a leisurely traveler becomes actively misleading for a connecting passenger with twenty minutes.

The Heathrow and Munich studies are commercial partnerships with Tobii, which means they are applied research rather than independent. The methodology is sound, the findings replicate the laboratory evidence, and the airports that have taken them seriously have measurable improvements to show for it. Manchester Airport's documented wayfinding improvement, under the direction of a single Head of Visual Environment with a clear directive to reduce sign volume, can be summarized in five words from the manager herself: *we're vastly reducing the number of signs on site* [Technology Scout Brief]. The lesson is not subtle. Most US hubs have learned the opposite lesson.

### The Wayshowing Distinction and the Committee Problem

Mollerup's reframing of design as "wayshowing" is more than terminological. It locates the failure mode at the design committee. A signage committee, by disposition, optimizes for stakeholder representation. The airline operations team wants its boarding information visible. The retail tenant wants directional signage to its space. TSA wants its prohibited-items notice prominent. CBP wants its currency declaration warning. The fire marshal wants the egress signs lit. The ADA coordinator wants the tactile and Braille requirements met at every doorway. The aviation marketing office wants the airport name and city brand visible. Each stakeholder is acting reasonably. Each represents a legitimate interest. None of them, by definition, is responsible for the cumulative cognitive load on the passenger.

The committee dynamic explains why every US hub airport's signage looks roughly the same — additively dense, hierarchically flat, visually crowded — even though every US hub has hired some of the same wayfinding consultants. The consultants produce competent design. The committees absorb the design and then add to it. The system degrades to its governance floor: the weakest enforcement point becomes the system's effective standard.

The international exemplars — Frankfurt (1972), Singapore Changi (1981), Munich (1992), Zurich, Helsinki — share a feature that the US largely lacks: a single airport authority with explicit veto power over commercial signage, federal-agency signage, airline tenant signage, and concessionaire signage. Otl Aicher's 1972 Frankfurt system embedded the rule in the airport's operating architecture: commercial advertising was spatially separated from wayfinding signage, on different surfaces, in different formats. The separation was enforced as a contract term, not negotiated case by case [Aviation Historian Brief]. The honest qualifier: Aicher built this discipline into a new terminal at the design stage, not as a retrofit onto an existing fragmented system. The case proves that the contractual mechanism works when written into the building's program. It does not prove that the same mechanism can be imposed on three decades of accumulated stratigraphy without political capital that no US authority has yet been willing to spend.

The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey, in commissioning Paul Mijksenaar to extend the Schiphol system across the JFK, LGA, and EWR campus, attempted the closest US equivalent of the Aicher approach as a retrofit. The 2021 PANYNJ Airport Standards Manual for Pedestrian Signing and Wayfinding designates Aviation Customer Experience in the Central Office as the enforcement authority, with a Program Manager for Wayfinding and Connections Solutions serving as principal reviewer of all signage project submittals. The lease agreements bind terminal operators to compliance [Operations Analyst Brief; Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief]. The program took the better part of a decade from initiation to adoption, required extraordinary political capital, and remains in prototype rollout at JFK years after the manual was finalized. The PANYNJ effort is at once the proof that a retrofit can be built in a US context and the proof that building it is harder than any prior airport authority has appreciated. It is the case the strategist's argument actually depends on. Frankfurt is the historical precedent. PANYNJ is the present-day evidence.

### Contradiction Debt Is the Hidden Operating Cost

If the design problem was solved in 1972 and the methodology was federally codified in 2014, what does an unaddressed US wayfinding system actually look like in the field? It looks like a stratigraphy of layered decisions, each correct at the moment it was made, each contradicting the others now.

At Charlotte Douglas International, before remediation, passengers consistently overestimated walking time durations because inconsistent wayfinding cues induced uncertainty, which produced hesitation. The intervention — consolidating five alpha-coded security zones into three numeric zones, with color-coded portals and vertical beacons — reduced perceived complexity without physical reconstruction [Operations Analyst Brief]. The operational lesson runs counter to design instinct: *consistency is a separate variable from quality*. A uniformly mediocre system outperforms a partially excellent one where the excellent and mediocre elements contradict each other. A passenger who has learned the rules of the system in one zone is being punished for that learning when she enters the next zone and the rules have silently changed.

Pittsburgh International is the cleanest case in the US record of what contradiction debt costs across an operational regime change. The 1992 hub terminal was built for US Airways as a connection machine — an X-shaped airside concourse with 75 gates, every intersection point equidistant from connecting flights, signage optimized for the connecting passenger with limited time. The system worked for its stated purpose. In November 2004, US Airways collapsed the Pittsburgh hub. Enplanements fell from approximately twenty million to under eight million. The traffic profile inverted from sixty percent connecting to ninety-eight percent O&D [Aviation Historian Brief]. The wayfinding system remained the connection-optimized system. The passenger population it had been designed for no longer existed. For two decades, PIT operated with signs calibrated to a hub that wasn't there. The cost is not directly measured in the public literature. But the operational shape of it — a passenger navigating a near-empty connection concourse to find a single O&D departure gate — is exactly what the eye-tracking research predicts produces hesitation, reconfirmation behavior, and over-reliance on staff redirection. The system was not corrected until the $1.5 billion greenfield terminal opened in November 2025. Two decades of mismatch. No mechanism for resetting it short of a billion-dollar capital event. MWAA's terminals are in Chapter 2 of the same story.

The IAD case carries six distinct construction events: the Saarinen Main Terminal (1962), the Midfield Concourses (1996), the underground walkway (2004), the AeroTrain (2010), the Concourse C expansion (2016), and the Silver Line Metro connection (2022) [Aviation Historian Brief]. Each event introduced new passenger circulation modes with no predecessor signage. Each one required new signage to explain a new option, layered onto signage explaining earlier options that many passengers still used. By 2022, IAD passengers could choose among four modes to reach the concourses — mobile lounges, the underground walkway, the AeroTrain, and the Metro connection — with each mode partially served by signage from a different construction era, in different fonts, with different terminology for the same destinations.

The DCA case is more compressed. Three terminal eras — Terminal A (pre-1941 and 1958), the César Pelli Terminal B/C (1997), and the post-9/11 emergency checkpoint zone — accumulated signage vocabularies produced by different designers under different standards [Aviation Historian Brief]. Project Journey, MWAA's $1 billion DCA capital program, included a five-day window in June 2022 during which more than 1,000 signs were converted from terminal letter-names to terminal numbers and from numeric gate designations to alphanumeric [Chief Engineer Brief]. The coordination required airline FIDS systems, boarding pass systems, WMATA Metro station entrance signage, parking garage digital systems, and roadway signs to update on a synchronized timeline. It was an extraordinary operational achievement and also a reminder of how high the friction of any system-wide signage change has become.

What does the contradiction debt cost? Most airports cannot answer the question directly because no one routes operational exceptions back to the wayfinding system. Missed flights are logged against carrier delays. Gate holds for late passengers are logged against boarding inefficiency. Information desk volume is not tracked against wayfinding performance at most US airports. The measurement gap is itself a governance failure: if no one owns wayfinding as a performance system, no one measures its failures [Operations Analyst Brief].

What we can document: customer-experience vendor literature claims that up to $700 million is lost annually across the US system in concession revenue attributable to wayfinding-related passenger experience failures. The figure is widely cited and not independently audited; the original methodology is not traceable; both the Infrastructure Economist and Technology Scout briefs flag it as vendor-sourced and unreliable. Treat it as a number that exists in the industry conversation rather than as evidence. The directional point underneath it has support from independent research: a peer-reviewed 2024 study of 89 US airports found that a 10 percent increase in passenger dwell time was associated with a 6 percent increase in retail revenue and an 8 percent increase in food and beverage revenue [Infrastructure Economist Brief]. The same brief flags the necessary caveat: dwell time has multiple drivers — security throughput, lounge access, retail mix, scheduling, wayfinding — and wayfinding is one share of the total, not the whole. Properly discounted, wayfinding-driven dwell-time improvement may capture twenty to thirty percent of the elasticity, which still leaves it as one of the few interventions that converts fixed terminal area into incremental non-aeronautical yield without renegotiating a lease or expanding a footprint.

For MWAA specifically, the yield gap is measurable. The airports reported combined in-terminal concession revenue of $110 million in 2023 across approximately 52 million passengers — about $2.12 per passenger, materially below the North American non-aeronautical average of $8.68 per passenger (which includes parking and is therefore not a perfect comparison) [Infrastructure Economist Brief]. At the brief's calibrated elasticity, a one percent improvement in dwell time implies $660,000 to $880,000 in incremental annual revenue per percentage point gained. Allocating twenty percent of that to a wayfinding-driven share — the upper end of plausible — implies $130,000 to $175,000 per percentage point. Multiply by a plausible three- to five-point dwell-time gain across a multi-year program and the payback math is in the $400,000 to $875,000 annual range. Modest, real, and one component of a multi-stream return that also includes reduced customer-service load, deferred sign-maintenance cost, and reduced service-recovery exposure. The case does not rest on any single revenue line.

### Why Digital Is Not the Answer the Vendors Sell

The dynamic-versus-static debate, as airports usually have it, is the wrong frame. The better frame, drawn from the eye-tracking literature, is to match each medium to the information-change rate of the location it serves.

Dynamic digital displays earn their cost where the information is time-variant. Flight Information Display Systems for gate assignments, departure times, delay status, and baggage carousel assignments. Security wait times at checkpoint approaches. Gate area concourse directories at hubs with frequent changes [Operations Analyst Brief; Technology Scout Brief]. The global FIDS market was $527 million in 2024, growing at 7 percent CAGR — settled technology, settled value, no meaningful controversy [Technology Scout Brief]. Changi Terminal 2 retired its iconic split-flap boards in February 2024 after twenty-one years; nobody mourned the operational loss.

Static signage earns its cost where the information does not change. Which direction is Terminal C. Where are the elevators. Which way is ground transportation. Decision-point under time pressure is exactly the moment when a static answer outperforms a rotating one. Denver International's 2013 signage overhaul understood this: it replaced 23 roadside and 22 overhead signs with retroreflective static alternatives, saving an estimated $3 to $4 million in avoided structural replacement costs, while establishing pilot standards for digital displays in the terminal proper [Operations Analyst Brief; Chief Engineer Brief]. Retroreflective overhead signs are lower maintenance, lower power, and — critically — always on when there is a power event.

The lifecycle math is more sobering than capital programs typically recognize, and the implication is not "less digital." It is "fund the refresh discipline." Static backlit signs run a realistic 15- to 20-year useful life with LED lamp replacement at years 8 to 10. Digital display panels in 24/7 airport environments run 5 to 7 years; media players run 3 to 5 years [Chief Engineer Brief]. A complete FIDS hardware-plus-software deployment runs $8,000 to $15,000 per display node, with premium gate-area units reaching $20,000 to $30,000. An estimated 35 to 40 percent of FIDS units currently installed at US commercial airports are operating beyond their stated lifecycle. Airports are now carrying digital signage fleets installed in the early 2010s that have never been refreshed. The strategic error airports make is funding digital installations in capital programs while treating refresh-cycle hardware replacement as an O&M problem — then deferring O&M when budgets tighten. The correct response is not to deploy less digital. It is to fund digital with a published lifecycle plan and a refresh budget locked to the panel and media-player replacement cycle, governed by the same standards authority that owns the static system. Digital infrastructure without that discipline becomes a more expensive version of the contradiction debt it was supposed to solve.

The case for app-based wayfinding is more complex than the vendor pitch admits. Only 7 percent of airport passengers use the departure airport's dedicated mobile app [Technology Scout Brief]. The gap is being filled, to some degree, by Apple Maps indoor mapping at more than thirty US airports and by Google Maps Live View at Zurich and Sydney. The substitution argument has a hole that the eye-tracking data illuminates: the question is not whether the unfamiliar passenger has installed an indoor mapping product. Apple Maps is installed by default on every iPhone. The question is whether the passenger under time pressure at a decision point will pull out the phone, open the app, identify their location, and read the directional overlay — or whether she will look up at the sign overhead. The Heathrow and Munich data say she will look up. The decision point at the bottom of the escalator at 6 a.m. is not phone work [Operations Analyst Brief; Technology Scout Brief].

### The Governance Architecture That Actually Exists

The reason this debate keeps recurring at US airports is not that the designers have run out of ideas. It is that the governance arrangement makes the design irrelevant. The FAA has no enforceable authority over indoor terminal wayfinding; Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F is guidance, not regulation [Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief]. ADA creates a genuine regulatory floor, but compliance is often implemented as a parallel sign layer that competes with the wayfinding hierarchy rather than integrating with it. TSA's Checkpoint Requirements and Planning Guide (May 2025) requires that all signage within the checkpoint zone be approved by TSA headquarters' Office of Signage Guidelines. CBP's Airport Technical Design Standards govern signage in Federal Inspection Services halls. Local fire marshals govern egress signage under NFPA 101. Concessionaires hold advertising display rights under contracts that typically run 10 to 15 years.

The result at a major international hub is that three federal regulatory authorities govern the same square foot of terminal floor and do not coordinate with each other. The airport's own standard loses jurisdiction precisely at the two highest-stress moments in the passenger journey — security and customs [Chief Engineer Brief]. And the contractual cage is the binding constraint: airline use and lease agreements grant carriers the right to brand their gate areas, ticket counters, and jetways, with airport approval authority that is broad in theory and rarely exercised in practice [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief; Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief].

The committee design problem is the predictable output of distributed veto with no point of authoritative override. Naming it as such matters, because it locates the lever where it actually sits: not in design talent but in the institutional decision to centralize wayfinding authority and then defend that authority against every plausible objection from every stakeholder who will produce one.

## IV. The Counter-Case, Honestly Presented

A good thesis does not benefit from a straw-man opposition. The strongest case against the cheapest-lever argument deserves to be stated at full strength, because a reader who finds it persuasive will not be argued out of it by a summary dismissal.

**The smartphone has changed the navigation environment in ways that fixed signage cannot recover.** Only 7 percent of passengers use the departure airport's dedicated app, which sounds like a navigation vacuum but is not. The vacuum is being filled. Apple Maps provides indoor mapping at more than thirty US airports, including Dulles, Reagan National, JFK, LaGuardia, Newark, LAX, SFO, SEA-TAC, Denver, and Miami. Google's Live View indoor navigation — using the phone camera to overlay directional arrows in real time — is live at Zurich (one of the four exemplars the thesis depends on) and at Sydney. IATA's 2025 Global Passenger Survey found that 78 percent of passengers want a single smartphone device to combine digital wallet, digital ID, loyalty cards, and airport navigation. Airline app usage has risen to 23 percent of passengers, up 7 percentage points in five years. The trajectory of passenger navigation is unambiguously digital and phone-centric. A wayfinding strategy in 2026 built around static sign hierarchy is optimizing for a passenger behavior model that is actively shrinking as a share of traffic [Contrarian Brief].

**Zurich's celebrated wayfinding may owe part of its reputation to the Google overlay, not the sign hierarchy.** This is the Contrarian's hardest punch. Zurich is one of the four exemplars on which the thesis rests; if Zurich's reputation is partly a digital overlay rather than a governance achievement, the historical argument weakens accordingly [Contrarian Brief].

**The international exemplars are capital success stories, not governance success stories.** Helsinki's celebrated single-terminal wayfinding clarity followed a €1 billion terminal expansion that consolidated all operations under one roof. Zurich's "Airport 2000" fifth construction phase cost CHF 2.4 billion and produced a purpose-built Airside Centre with unified hub geometry. Munich's Terminal 2 is a joint venture between Flughafen München GmbH and Deutsche Lufthansa AG — a co-ownership arrangement described as unique in Europe. Changi is investing S$3 billion over six years and S$5+ billion in Terminal 5. The conclusion the thesis draws — that these airports wayfind well because of governance — has the causal arrow backward. They wayfind well because billions of dollars created unified physical environments in which a single design authority could operate without contractual contradictions. US airports cannot replicate the result without replicating the capital base [Contrarian Brief].

**The single-authority governance model the thesis implicitly recommends does not exist under US lease structures.** Airlines at US airports hold contractual approval rights over capital development under their use and lease agreements. Thirty-six of the 66 largest US airports have majority-in-interest provisions that give signatory carriers the right to review and, at affirmative MII airports, to block capital projects above defined thresholds [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief]. Signage in airline-leased concourses is, by contract, airline signage. TSA controls checkpoint signage by federal mandate. CBP controls FIS signage by federal mandate. The FAA's own Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F draws an explicit distinction between "systems typically installed by an airport operator" and "those typically installed by individual airlines." A "single wayfinding authority with veto power over tenants, TSA, and airlines" is not a governance innovation that US airports have failed to adopt out of managerial weakness. It is a legal construct that does not exist and cannot be conjured without renegotiating leases, federal agency memoranda, and security protocols simultaneously. The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey is the most sophisticated US operator with the most documented attempt at this, and its 2021 manual remains in prototype rollout at JFK years later — not because PANYNJ lacks discipline, but because the implementation runs through fourteen terminals, multiple federal partners, and dozens of carrier leases [Contrarian Brief; Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief].

**Capital renewal of aging signage is unavoidable, with or without a governance reform.** The Contrarian brief's depreciation point is sharper than the strategist's prior framing acknowledged. Existing sign infrastructure is aging; tactile and Braille ADA components have a finite life; backlit cabinets fail; pendant blades dent. The question is not whether capital will be spent on signage. The question is whether it will be spent as patchwork inside individual capital projects or as a coherent program against a published standard. The thesis treats wayfinding as if the alternative were no spending. The Contrarian correctly notes that the alternative is reactive spending, terminal by terminal, which is what every US airport is already doing [Contrarian Brief].

**Staff-based interventions can deliver passenger experience improvements faster and cheaper than a sign rationalization, without crossing the MII threshold.** PA system improvements, gate agent positioning, information desk placement, and FIDS refresh-rate adjustments cost tens of thousands of dollars and can be deployed in weeks. A comprehensive signage rationalization at a major US hub involves cataloging thousands of existing signs (the Port Authority documented over 10,000 across its three-airport system), eight to twelve months of planning and design, fabrication of custom sign types in airport-rated materials, installation during active operations, and a permanent enforcement mechanism. The Contrarian asks whether the strategist has rigorously priced one against the other [Contrarian Brief].

**The "discipline not dollars" framing risks letting capital decision-makers off the hook.** If the Council publishes a finding that wayfinding dysfunction is a governance problem rather than a resource problem, the institutional takeaway at most US airports will be: form a committee. The airport director will convene a wayfinding working group that will produce a design standards document that will sit in a drawer while the airlines continue to hang their own signs and TSA continues to staple up its own stanchion-mounted notices. The governance-first framing becomes, in practice, an excuse for under-investment in systems that would actually work. It gives boards a low-cost way to appear to act — approve the style guide — while avoiding the appropriation that would produce results [Contrarian Brief].

These constitute the strongest version of the case against the thesis. A reader who finds them persuasive will, by default, conclude that this report is an essay in the wrong decade.

## V. Why the Counter-Case Is Insufficient

The counter-case is partly right. It is not right enough to overturn the thesis. The work of this section is to concede what must be conceded, by name, and then to explain why what remains still holds.

### On smartphones, and what the unfamiliar passenger actually does

The trajectory is real. Apple Maps and Google Maps have done meaningful work to extend indoor navigation into airports, and the IATA preference data is what it is — 78 percent want a single phone for the whole journey. A wayfinding strategy that ignores the trend is, on its face, foolish.

But the substitution claim has two failures that the thesis turns on. First, the population for which substitution holds — frequent flyers comfortable with phone-based navigation, often with the airline app already installed, often familiar with the specific airport — is the population that does not generate the wayfinding cost. The cost is generated by the unfamiliar traveler, the first-time international visitor, the elderly passenger, the connecting passenger who has just stepped off a long-haul aircraft and is six time zones from home. The phones are installed; the maps are present. The behavior at the decision point is not.

Second — and this is the substantive point — the eye-tracking evidence is not about whether passengers *can* navigate with help. It is about what they *do* under stress at decision points. The Heathrow and Munich studies, the *Journal of Asian Architecture* 2025 lab study, the Shanghai Pudong research all converge on the same finding. Under time pressure, passengers exhibit measurable cognitive load increases, scan signs in predictable patterns, demonstrate "reconfirmation behavior" — re-reading a sign they have already acted on — and require text-plus-graphic formats to maintain decision confidence [Operations Analyst Brief; Technology Scout Brief]. The decision point in front of the escalator at 6 a.m. is not phone work. The phone is for planning the journey before it starts and for tracking the bag after it ends. The substitution argument is, ultimately, an argument that the airport need not optimize the environment because the passenger will optimize for the airport. The eye-tracking data says that the high-stakes passenger will not.

### On the Zurich-Google question directly

The Contrarian's claim that Zurich's wayfinding reputation is partly a Google Live View overlay is the sharpest single objection in the brief, and it deserves a direct answer.

Two parts to it. First: Zurich's wayfinding reputation predates Google Live View. Zurich's signage discipline and tenant-enforcement structure were documented in the European airport literature in the early 2000s, more than a decade before Google's indoor camera-based navigation reached the airport. Helsinki, Munich, Changi, and Frankfurt — also exemplars — wayfind well without a corresponding Google deployment of equivalent quality. The pre-digital record stands [Aviation Historian Brief; Architectural Historian Brief].

Second, and more interestingly: the Contrarian is partially right about the present-day overlay, and the thesis is strengthened rather than weakened by saying so. Google Live View at Zurich is doing real work for a subset of passengers. It works because it is layered onto a coherent physical signage hierarchy that does not contradict the digital overlay. A digital overlay onto a contradictory physical system would produce dueling sources of authority — which is what passengers at US hubs experience when their phone tells them one thing and the overhead sign tells them another. The "match the medium to the information-change rate" frame the thesis adopts in Section III is the same frame that explains why Google Live View works at Zurich and would not, by itself, fix IAD. Digital overlays inherit the governance quality of the physical system beneath them. The Contrarian's observation is correct; the inference is wrong.

### On capital base, and on the Aicher question

Helsinki's €1 billion terminal expansion is real. Zurich's CHF 2.4 billion fifth construction phase is real. Changi's S$3 billion six-year program is real. These airports built unified physical environments. Their wayfinding clarity is in part the result. The Architectural Historian brief is direct: spatial legibility cannot be retrofitted with signs. *A spatially incoherent terminal will defeat any sign system.* The Contrarian's capital-base point is in part also the Architectural Historian's spatial-legibility point.

The honest concession follows. A signage program at IAD cannot make Eero Saarinen's Main Terminal into Norman Foster's Stansted. The Mobile Lounge / AeroTrain transport-mode mismatch is not a sign problem at root; it is a circulation-architecture problem that the Aviation Historian brief describes correctly as a hub-and-spoke operational model retrofitted onto a building designed for a different circulation logic. United's preferred answer — extending the AeroTrain to Concourse D — would solve it; signage cannot.

What signage can do is order the decision chain inside the existing architecture so that each link works rather than fails. The thesis is not that better signs make IAD into Stansted. The thesis is that better signs make IAD's existing geometry as legible as it can be, and that the alternative is the geometry plus the contradiction debt — which is strictly worse. Frankfurt 1972 is supporting evidence: Aicher's contractual mechanism for spatial separation of commercial and wayfinding signage was governance work, and the mechanism could in principle have been retrofitted onto an existing terminal even though it was applied at design stage. The cleaner present-day evidence is PANYNJ: a multi-airport retrofit, underway, visibly further along than any peer authority that has not undertaken the work. Five years from manual publication to prototype rollout across fourteen terminals is, in industry timeframes, fast. The counter-case requires the reader to believe that no governance solution is possible without a billion-dollar capital event preceding it. PANYNJ's actual progress does not support that claim.

### On the lease structure and what the airport actually controls

The contractual cage is real. Majority-in-interest clauses are real. The FAA's acknowledgment of the divided-authority structure is real. Renegotiating a use agreement against airline objection is a multi-year political act with material airline leverage. None of this is in dispute.

But the lease-structure argument concedes the point it most needs to defeat. The airport operator has unambiguous authority over common areas — ticketing halls, security queue approaches, baggage claim, ground transportation curbs, escalators, transit stations, the transition zones between airline-controlled concourses. These are the most critical decision points in the passenger journey. They are where the eye-tracking data says wayfinding failures concentrate. They are where the airport's authority is contractually unquestioned. A signage moratorium and rationalization in these zones requires governance discipline, not capital, and not lease renegotiation. The MII clause does not apply to operating standards classified appropriately; the FAA's distinction between "airport-installed" and "airline-installed" systems is a constraint on the airline-controlled zones, not on the airport's authority in the rest of the building [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief; Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief].

This is also why the airline commercial strategist's framing of the question — *will the dominant carrier support a wayfinding standard that covers its concourse?* — is the right question for the airline-controlled zones and the wrong question for the rest of the building. United will resist any standard that constrains its Polaris lounge environment, its gate holdroom branding, or its proprietary gate display technology. American will resist any blanket standard that asks it to subordinate the $1 billion in capital it has just installed at DCA. Those resistances are real and substantial. They do not extend to the AeroTrain station, the National Hall, the baggage claim floor, the escalator at the bottom of which the connecting passenger is making her decision. Those are MWAA's.

The PANYNJ implementation timeline is not, as the counter-case reads it, evidence that the governance approach fails. It is evidence that the governance approach takes time and political capital. The alternative — every terminal renovation generating a new signage layer, every carrier installation contradicting the last one, every TSA reconfiguration carving out another exempt zone — is not a faster path. It is the path MWAA has been on for thirty years.

### On depreciation, staff interventions, and the false binary

The Contrarian's depreciation point is correct and friendly to the thesis. Capital will be spent on signage regardless. The question is whether it is spent reactively, accumulating as patchwork inside individual capital projects, or spent strategically, accumulating as a coherent system against a published standard. Treating wayfinding as a governance discipline does not mean spending zero. It means spending the same money against a standard rather than against the local preferences of the project at hand. Reframed this way, the depreciation argument strengthens rather than undermines the governance case. Every dollar of reactive signage spend across the next fifteen-year capital cycle is a dollar that could have purchased a system instead of a patch.

The staff-intervention point is also correct, and the answer is *both, in parallel*. Staff-based interventions — better PA system tuning, gate agent positioning, information desk placement, FIDS refresh-rate calibration — are valuable, fast, and below any reasonable MII threshold. They should be funded. They are not substitutes for a signage rationalization for two reasons. First, a staffed information desk is the system's exception-handling layer; better placement of the desk does not address the structural inconsistency that produces the exceptions in the first place. Second, the cognitive load eye-tracking documents at decision points is a property of the physical environment, not of the staff layer. A passenger at the bottom of an escalator with twenty seconds to choose a path is not helped by a customer-service agent positioned thirty yards away. The two interventions do different work. Funding both is correct.

### On digital, and the refresh discipline

Dynamic digital systems do real work. FIDS is settled technology. Interactive kiosks at known high-confusion points add value. Real-time security wait time displays reduce checkpoint anxiety. Multilingual digital assistance at international transfer zones reaches passengers that English-language static signs cannot. The thesis does not deny any of this. The thesis denies that *more digital* is, by itself, the answer.

The vendor literature consistently overpromises. The widely cited $700 million annual revenue-loss figure is not independently auditable and should be set aside, even when it appears in third-party decks. The structurally valid point underneath it — that disoriented passengers under-spend and over-complain — has independent support from the 89-airport peer-reviewed elasticity study, properly discounted for wayfinding's share of total dwell time. That is the number the case should rest on.

More important, the case for digital depends on the same governance structure the thesis is arguing for. A digital wayfinding network with no enforcement of content hierarchy, no constraint on advertising co-location, no single owner of the standard, and no funded refresh cycle becomes the contradiction debt at higher capital intensity. Manchester Airport's documented improvement under a single Head of Visual Environment came in part from explicitly choosing not to use signage real estate for advertising in navigation-critical locations [Technology Scout Brief]. The lifecycle data — 5–7 year panel life, 3–5 year media-player life, 35–40 percent of US FIDS fleet beyond stated lifecycle — argues for funded refresh discipline as a precondition for digital investment, not for less digital. The point is the discipline, not the technology choice. Digital with the discipline is part of the system. Digital without it is a more expensive version of the same problem.

### On the form-a-committee risk

The Contrarian's sharpest procedural point is that the institutional reflex at most US airports, given a finding that wayfinding is a governance problem, will be to form a committee, produce a design standards document, and use the production of the document as evidence of action. The document will sit in a drawer. The airlines will continue to hang their own signs. TSA will continue to staple up its own notices. The capital programs will continue to layer new signage onto the old. The contradiction debt will continue to compound.

This is a real risk, and any honest version of the thesis has to name it. The answer is that the deliverable is not a design document. The deliverable is an enforcement protocol. Tampa's Journey Segment Evaluation Tool is the model: a mechanism requiring every new capital project to be evaluated against the existing sign hierarchy at each decision point it touches, with a designated reviewer who has actual veto authority over project documents. PANYNJ's Aviation Customer Experience structure is the same idea at scale: a Program Manager for Wayfinding and Connections Solutions named in writing, with authority embedded in the lease agreements, supported by an asset management platform — Denver International's SignAgent deployment tracks 10,000 signs as a queryable database, reducing work-order generation from 4–5 days to minutes [Operations Analyst Brief; Chief Engineer Brief]. The committee risk is real for an airport that treats wayfinding as a design exercise. It is mitigated for an airport that treats wayfinding as an asset class with a named owner, a published lifecycle plan, and an enforcement protocol tied to every capital approval gate.

### The cheapest-lever claim, rigorously

The Contrarian asks the thesis to defend its strongest assertion against rigorous comparison. The defensible version follows the brief math, not the slogan.

Wayfinding is among the few operational interventions at a US hub airport where the order-of-magnitude ratio between cost and value can be reasonably argued at single-digit to low-double-digit multiples on a ten-year horizon, with an implementation path that does not require lease renegotiation in the most critical zones and does not require federal regulatory change. The Infrastructure Economist brief's framing — *an IRR that most capital programs cannot match* — is the right one. An $8 to $13 million one-time program at MWAA (Heathrow's £10 million 2013 framework inflated to 2026 dollars at roughly 50 percent UK construction inflation, scaled by passenger volume) is on the order of one to two percent of a single year's MWAA capital spend, less than one part in a thousand of the fifteen-year program [Infrastructure Economist Brief; analyst calculation]. Properly discounted wayfinding-driven dwell-time revenue is $130,000 to $175,000 per percentage point gained, plus reductions in customer-service load of unknown magnitude, plus deferred sign-maintenance costs (the most widely cited number — $740,000 annually — is a Ponemon study cited only in vendor literature; treat as directional only [Infrastructure Economist Brief]), plus modest reductions in service-recovery cost from passengers reaching gates on time. The cumulative case clears the IRR threshold without depending on any single revenue line.

The strict version of the thesis: wayfinding is among the highest-yield, lowest-incremental-cost capital interventions available *in the airport's common-use areas*, with the lowest external-stakeholder dependency for the work that produces the most of the value, and with returns whose elasticities are documented in peer-reviewed literature. It is not the only cheap lever. It is the cheapest lever the airport can pull, in its common-use areas, without negotiating with someone who can say no.

## VI. Implications for MWAA

This is a council writing for MWAA's specific position. The generic recommendations are uninteresting. The question is what MWAA should do, given what MWAA actually has.

What MWAA has, today, is two airports under a single authority, operating across the most stratified signage history in the US large-hub inventory. IAD carries six construction eras and two ground-transport modes serving the same connection use case. DCA is fresh off Project Journey's $1 billion capital program, including the five-day, 1,000-sign renaming, and is operating new American Airlines concourses built to American's specifications. The 2025–2039 Airport Use and Lease Agreement is a hybrid residual structure with a 1.40x debt service coverage target and a 1.25x bond covenant floor. Moody's projects MWAA's adjusted debt per O&D enplanement will peak at approximately $400 in 2028, with net revenue DSCR declining from 1.63x in 2024 to roughly 1.3x over the next five years [Infrastructure Economist Brief]. United carries approximately two-thirds of IAD enplanements including United Express. American controls 53 percent of DCA flights. The $9 billion capital program is set. The $514,000 IAD Signage and Wayfinding Masterplan budget and the $4.87 million DCA equivalent are study budgets, not implementation programs [Chief Engineer Brief].

Five observations follow from this position. They are presented as prose rather than as a numbered list because the structure of the argument is not separable into discrete recommendations; each implication depends on the one before it.

### A named owner with executive authority

Treat wayfinding as an asset class with a single owner reporting at the executive level. Not a committee. Not a working group. A Sign Standards Authority — one named position, reporting to a senior executive, with explicit veto authority over signage in MWAA-controlled common areas at both airports — and a published Wayfinding and Signing Standards Manual covering both IAD and DCA on a single set of rules. The PANYNJ structure is the closest US precedent and the most directly applicable: Aviation Customer Experience in the Central Office, Program Manager for Wayfinding and Connections Solutions as principal reviewer. MWAA's Design Manual currently runs to seven volumes, with separate tenant design standards for IAD's Main Terminal / Concourse Z (the international arrivals level, which interacts directly with CBP signage) and Concourse B — two parts of the same airport published as two distinct standards documents [Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief]. The volume count is evidence of the problem. A forward standard collapses this into a single hierarchy with two-airport scope.

### Move from study to implementation, and structure to live under MII

The $514,000 IAD allocation and the $4.87 million DCA allocation are appropriate for the study phase. The implementation phase is missing. A defensible scope estimate, calibrated from the Heathrow framework and the field experience of recent US programs, runs in the $15 to $25 million range for a phased rationalization across both airports. The math: Heathrow's 2013 £10 million five-year framework, inflated to 2026 at roughly 50 percent UK construction inflation, is approximately £15 million or $19 million; scaled down by MWAA's 52 million passengers against Heathrow's 80 million gives a base figure of roughly $12 million; the Chief Engineer's 40–50 percent live-terminal premium adds another $5 to $6 million; the Ontario International overrun multiplier (2.7x on a single project, properly discounted across a multi-project program) implies a $4 to $5 million contingency reserve. The total range is illustrative, not committed; precise scoping is downstream of the masterplan studies already budgeted [Infrastructure Economist Brief; Chief Engineer Brief; analyst calculation].

A $15 to $25 million program will cross the typical $5–$10 million MII threshold by any reasonable accounting. The right strategy is not to claim the program automatically sidesteps MII; it is to classify aggressively. Capital classification should be reserved for genuinely infrastructure-grade elements — sign substrates, structural attachments, digital backbone, asset management platform. The dominant share of program cost — design, standards governance, sign fabrication, installation, change management — is appropriately operating expense and should be funded from the airport's operating budget rather than the capital plan. The structuring is aggressive but defensible. Airlines may challenge it. The structuring strategy is the work; the standard is downstream of the structuring.

The PFC cap, unchanged since 2001 and now worth roughly half its 2001 purchasing power, is the structural reason discretionary capital will not appear for a wayfinding program of this size [Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief]. Funding from operating budget against a multi-year operating program is not a workaround. It is the only path available. Treating wayfinding as an operating discipline is partly a governance choice; it is also a funding mechanics necessity.

### The ADA 2027 deadline as forcing function

ADA Title II's compliance date for digital accessibility was extended to April 26, 2027 for larger entities — approximately ten months from the date of this draft [Chief Engineer Brief]. MWAA's existing FIDS, BIDS, dynamic directional, and interactive kiosk inventory will require accessibility compliance by that date. The deadline turns "wayfinding is overdue" into "wayfinding is timed against an external compliance forcing event." Any digital-component refresh undertaken in 2026 to comply with the 2027 deadline that is not coordinated with a standards manual will produce a new contradiction layer that the standards manual will then have to retire. The same money spent inside a forward standard produces a system. The compliance deadline is the lever. It will not return.

### The IAD transport-mode decision chain

Solve the IAD transport-mode wayfinding problem at the decision point, with the honest concession that signage alone cannot fully resolve it. The Airline Commercial Strategist brief is precise: the AeroTrain–Mobile Lounge transition is a decision chain, not a single decision point, and each link is a wayfinding problem [Airline Commercial Strategist Brief]. The signage solution orders the chain so each link works rather than fails; it does not eliminate the underlying mode mismatch. The intervention is to position the FIDS-first wayfinding decision at the transit-mode interface, route the passenger by mode rather than by concourse, and provide consistent directional confirmation along the Mobile Lounge bay. The first-best fix is extending the AeroTrain to Concourse D — United's preferred solution, a multi-hundred-million-dollar capital program, currently unfunded. The signage intervention is second-best, at less than one percent of the cost of the first-best. Second-best at one percent of the cost, while the first-best is unfunded, is the right call.

The Saarinen catenary-cable roof is a constraint to design around rather than argue against. The IAD signage standard should specify that overhead pendant signage in the Main Terminal is a constrained inventory — every existing pendant is presumed to be in its final position, and additions require a formal exception process. Floor-mounted directional cues, wall-mounted directories, and digital displays at landside-airside transition points should carry the navigation load that would, in a less constrained building, be carried by overhead pendants. The Architectural Historian brief is direct: the Saarinen processional logic was broken when the AeroTrain replaced the Mobile Lounge as the dominant transport mode; the IAD wayfinding problem is the residue of that change. The forward standard cannot restore the processional logic. It can prevent the next capital event from depositing another contradictory layer on top of it [Architectural Historian Brief].

### Validation and the asset platform

Budget for eye-tracking validation before and after implementation, and fund an asset-management platform on the SignAgent model. A terminal-scale eye-tracking study using the Tobii methodology runs $40,000 to $80,000 [Technology Scout Brief]. The validation cost is a rounding error against the implementation budget. Any wayfinding program installed without pre-and-post validation is a program that does not know whether it worked. Denver International's SignAgent deployment — 10,000 signs as a queryable asset database, work-order generation reduced from 4–5 days to minutes — is the operational platform that turns a standards manual from a document into a system [Chief Engineer Brief]. MWAA's existing Pointr indoor positioning contract (six-figure, 2019) is data without a frame [Operations Analyst Brief]; an asset management platform is the frame.

What this is not. It is not a typeface recommendation. The graphic designers will produce competent design. The competent design is not the problem MWAA has. The problem MWAA has is that the design, once produced, has historically been undermined by every adjacent stakeholder and every subsequent capital project. The forward standard MWAA needs is the one that the next capital project, the next federal mandate, and the next carrier renegotiation cannot quietly displace. That standard is built on three things: an owner with named authority, an asset management discipline that makes the system auditable, and an enforcement protocol embedded in the gate review process for every project that touches a sign. The pieces are commercially available. The decision is institutional.

---

The connecting passenger from the opening of this report will, if MWAA does the work, step off her aircraft and find the answer she needs at the first decision point she encounters. She will not know whose name is on the standards document. She will not know that the sign she is reading is a different generation from the one above her gate. She will read it, choose the right mode, and make her connection.

If MWAA does not do the work, the next capital project will deposit one more layer, the next federal mandate will create one more exempt zone, and the building she walked into will become marginally harder to read than the one her predecessor walked into ten years ago. The arithmetic of contradiction debt is not subtle. It compounds.
