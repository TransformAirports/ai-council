# Stage 1 Research Brief: Public Safety and Emergency Response
## Director of Public Safety — Hub Airport Operations

**Run Thesis:** Wayfinding governance at US airports is a life-safety deficit, not merely a passenger experience failure. The same discipline gap that produces confusing terminal navigation produces fatal evacuation failures, unreachable 911 callers, and command-and-control breakdowns under incident conditions.

---

## Key Findings

1. **Compliance with NFPA 101 exit signage requirements does not produce functional evacuation paths.** Legally compliant exit signs mounted at code-mandated intervals in a heavily retail-cluttered concourse are invisible to passengers under stress. The standard requires signs; it does not require that those signs work as a system. Most large US hub airports satisfy the letter of Section 7.10 while constructing, through accumulated retail build-out and ad-hoc stakeholder signage, an environment in which passengers cannot identify an egress path under normal cognitive load — let alone panic.

2. **The Fort Lauderdale 2017 after-action record is the clearest proof that terminal layout and wayfinding gaps directly produce mass-casualty-scale secondary harm.** Five people were killed in the initial shooting. Approximately 36 additional people were injured — not by the shooter — but in the uncontrolled self-evacuation that followed. Passengers with no path guidance ran onto active aprons. No unified command was established. No terminal evacuation plan existed that could be executed. The signage environment gave fleeing passengers exactly one instinctive option: run through whatever opening appeared. That opening was the airfield.

3. **Post-security sterile areas have a structural wayfinding gap with no regulatory backstop.** Every sign in a standard US post-security concourse is designed to move passengers toward gates — inbound flow only. When an incident requires reverse evacuation, passengers have no sign-supported path back through checkpoints. TSA's exit lane controls are designed to prevent unauthorized sterile-area entry, not to route an emergency outbound flow. The result in active incidents has been crowd compression at checkpoint choke points, officer-passenger conflict at exit lanes, and the airfield incursion problem documented at FLL.

4. **FIDS-based digital signage networks are not wired to incident command.** Most US large-hub airports operate FIDS networks under a commercial/operational authority — typically the airfield operations or airline systems team — entirely separate from the emergency operations center. During an incident, the incident commander cannot override terminal display systems in real time without a pre-planned integration that almost no airport has built. NFPA 72 Chapter 24 provides the framework for integrated emergency communication systems with visible notification appliances; the majority of US airports are not compliant with its performance intent for large assembly occupancies.

5. **Inconsistent space addressing — gate numbering, pier vs. concourse labeling, room identification conventions — is a direct command-and-control problem, not an inconvenience.** When airport police dispatch receives "Gate B17," the responding officer needs to know without hesitation which pier, which level, which terminal, and what the nearest access point is. At airports where gate numbering resets between concourses, where pier labels don't match gatehouse signage, or where room numbers are non-unique across levels, multi-unit response is degraded from the first radio call. This is not theoretical — the LAX 2013 after-action found that 20-plus agencies responding to a single terminal event could not maintain a common operating picture in part because they were not sharing consistent spatial references.

6. **TSA checkpoint approach queues are the highest-density predictable soft target in US commercial aviation, and wayfinding discipline directly affects how long and how dense those queues become.** The 2016 Brussels and 2016 Istanbul attacks both exploited pre-security public areas where crowd density was high and exit routes were unclear. TSA's own Public Area Security National Framework (May 2017) acknowledges the risk explicitly. Efficient queue routing and clear approach-lane wayfinding that moves passengers through these areas faster and more dispersed is not a customer service metric — it is a vulnerability reduction.

7. **The NG911 location problem inside terminal buildings is unsolvable without consistent zone addressing already built into the physical environment.** A passenger calling 911 from a jet bridge provides a cellular fix that may resolve to the airport's street address — or to the wrong terminal — or to a neighboring property. NG911's dispatchable location requirement depends on validated GIS sub-address data: building, floor, zone, room. An airport that has never enforced consistent internal addressing has no usable indoor GIS layer, and its dispatchers are working off voice description. Every minute of location ambiguity is compounded by every inconsistency in how spaces are labeled.

8. **The asymmetric liability calculus is straightforward.** A mass-casualty incident with a documented wayfinding-related evacuation failure — passengers who couldn't find exits, couldn't reverse egress through a checkpoint, or ran onto an airfield because nothing told them not to — is a federal investigation, congressional hearing, and multi-defendant wrongful death docket. The FLL 2017 litigation named the airport operator, the airlines, and three security contractors. Wayfinding governance costs a fraction of that exposure. Most airports have not run this calculation.

## Evidence

### Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport, January 6, 2017

The Police Foundation's after-action report (the most comprehensive independent review produced) documents the following sequence: gunman fired in Terminal 2 baggage claim; five killed, six shot; approximately 90 minutes later, unsubstantiated radio reports of additional shots caused a secondary mass panic; uncontrolled self-evacuation drove passengers onto active aprons and ramps across multiple terminals; approximately 10,000 passengers were ultimately bused to Port Everglades because the airport was functionally inoperable; 36 additional people were injured in the panic itself — not in the shooting.

The report identified directly: no unified command was ever established; the terminal evacuation plan was inadequate; the absence of coordinated mass notification left passengers with no authoritative directional guidance. When the only information available was noise and crowd movement, passengers followed the crowd through whatever opening existed. The apron access points were open. Nothing in the signage environment — compliant with NFPA 101 for normal egress — provided reverse-flow guidance.

[Source: https://www.policinginstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/Ft.-Lauderdale-Hollywood-International-Airport-AAR.pdf]

The secondary panic spread to Terminal 1 via radio traffic and social media, not via confirmed threat. This is critical: the signage environment in Terminal 1 also provided no shelter-in-place, hold-position, or directed-evacuation information. Passengers in all terminals were receiving unfiltered noise and responding to it.

The litigation filed after the incident named the Broward County Commission (the airport operator), Delta Air Lines, and multiple security contractors. Multiple wrongful death and negligence claims remain active in the public record.

[Source: https://www.cbsnews.com/miami/news/lawsuit-retirement-party-security-fort-lauderdale-airport-shooting/]

### Los Angeles International Airport, November 1, 2013

The LAWA after-action report (83 pages, released March 18, 2014) documented that the shooting produced a terminal evacuation in which employees — not passengers — were the primary navigation problem: lower-level employees had not been trained for evacuation and did not know what to do or how to direct passengers. The radio interoperability failure between LAWA police, LAPD, LAFD, and the 20-plus responding agencies meant no common spatial reference was maintained. Police and fire set up separate command posts that did not unify for 45 minutes.

The most operationally significant finding: TSA Officer Hernandez lay shot approximately 20 feet from an exit but did not receive medical care for 33 minutes because no coordinated protocol existed for law enforcement to clear and medical personnel to enter. The inability to confirm whether the terminal was secure — compounded by spatial ambiguity from multiple agencies working without common reference — directly contributed to that 33-minute gap.

The report's approximately 50 recommendations included mandatory active shooter training and bi-annual evacuation drills. Signage-specific recommendations were not the focus, but the spatial coordination failure — agencies could not agree on what "Terminal 3" meant or where the hazard boundary was — is a wayfinding-architecture problem at its root.

[Source: https://www.lawa.org/news-releases/2014/news-release25]
[Source: https://www.nbcnews.com/news/investigations/report-communications-breakdown-after-lax-airport-shooting-n55686]

The Gerardo Hernandez Airport Security Act of 2015 (Public Law 114-50) was passed directly in response to this incident, requiring TSA to mandate active shooter training, install duress alarms at checkpoints, and conduct a review of interoperable communications capabilities. The law did not address wayfinding or evacuation path design — a gap that the FLL 2017 event subsequently exposed.

[Source: https://www.congress.gov/114/plaws/publ50/PLAW-114publ50.pdf]

### Brussels Zaventem, March 22, 2016, and Istanbul Ataturk, June 28, 2016

Both attacks targeted pre-security public areas — departure hall approach queues and check-in areas — where crowd density is highest and wayfinding to exits is most contested by commercial and retail signage. The Brussels attack killed 32 and injured more than 300; Istanbul killed 45 and injured more than 230. In both cases, after-action commentary from security professionals identified the pre-checkpoint approach zone as an inherently high-density, low-awareness environment where passengers have no instinctive exit orientation because all the visual information in that space is designed to direct them toward a destination (check-in, security), not toward exits.

The security debate following both attacks focused on whether to move screening perimeters outward — and concluded that doing so would simply displace the soft-target queue rather than eliminate it. The operationally relevant point is that the same commercial signage competition that defeats emergency egress under fire conditions also defeats defensive crowd dispersal in a security incident.

[Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_Brussels_bombings]
[Source: https://www.nbcnews.com/storyline/istanbul-ataturk-airport-attack/istanbul-attack-reopens-airport-security-debate-n545551]

### NFPA 101 and the Compliance-vs-Competence Gap

NFPA 101 Section 7.10 requires exit signs in plain legible letters not less than 6 inches high, with a directional indicator where the direction of travel to the nearest exit is not apparent, at intervals not exceeding 100 feet. Section 7.9 requires emergency lighting at not less than 0.2 foot-candles for a minimum of 90 minutes on battery backup.

These requirements are achievable — and routinely met — while producing an exit signage environment that is functionally invisible. In a modern US airport concourse, 100-foot sign spacing produces one exit sign per retail bay, mounted at ceiling height, competing with illuminated storefront signage, hanging banners, digital advertising displays, and wayfinding signs for airlines, ground transportation, restrooms, and ticketing. Under normal conditions, passengers habituate to the environment and follow directional cues. Under evacuation conditions, passengers regress to familiar-path preference and social herding — neither of which is guided by ceiling-mounted NFPA-compliant exit signs.

[Source: https://usmadesupply.com/resources/building-codes-standards/emergency-life-safety/nfpa-101]

### NFPA 72 and Emergency Communication Integration

NFPA 72 Chapter 24 covers Emergency Communications Systems (ECS), including mass notification systems with both audible and visible notification requirements. The Emergency Command Center, under Chapter 24, is required to be capable of interfacing with and controlling other notification systems, including visual control signs. The standard explicitly requires that evacuation signals and emergency digital signage must be manually silenced or reset by authorized emergency personnel.

The gap at most US airports is not a reading of the standard — it is that FIDS networks and commercial digital display systems are managed as separate enterprise systems with no real-time interface to the EOC or incident command position. The architecture exists; the integration contract has not been written. During an incident, the IC cannot push "evacuate north concourse" to the display network without a pre-planned connection that most US airports have not built.

[Source: https://ifmacentralohio.org/images/meeting/101316/nfpa_chapter_24__emergency_communication_systems.pdf]
[Source: https://www.alertus.com/blog/nfpa-72-mass-notification-requirements-guide]

### TSA Checkpoint Queue as Soft Target

The TSA Public Area Security National Framework (May 2017) acknowledges explicitly that pre-security public areas — including checkpoint approach queues — represent a security vulnerability that became operationally documented through the 2016 Brussels and Istanbul attacks, and the FLL 2017 shooting. The framework calls for queue monitoring, crowd management, and law enforcement positioning in public areas as mitigation.

The PARAS 0013 report (Program for Applied Research in Airport Security) addresses minimizing congestion that could be a viable target for acts of violence, recommending dispersal strategies, virtual queuing, and technology-assisted queue management — all of which depend on wayfinding infrastructure that routes passengers efficiently and reduces dwell time in high-density zones.

[Source: https://www.tsa.gov/sites/default/files/pass_national_framework.pdf]
[Source: https://www.sskies.org/images/uploads/subpage/PARAS_0013.MinimizingCongestion.FinalReport-Final.pdf]

### Evacuation Behavior Research

John Bryan's foundational research on human behavior in fire (summarized in the SFPE Handbook of Fire Protection Engineering) established that following initial alarm notification, people delay evacuating by an average of 50 to 220 seconds — and that when they do evacuate, they preferentially use familiar routes over nearest routes. This is directly relevant to airports, where the vast majority of passengers enter through one of two or three familiar paths and have no familiarity with secondary exits or reverse-checkpoint egress.

Guylène Proulx's work at Canada's National Research Council on evacuation behavior identified that the factors driving successful evacuation are evacuation efficiency, information supporting wayfinding, and emotional stability — in that order. Her research is cited in the theoretical framework for individual wayfinding decisions under stress published by ScienceDirect (2023), which confirms through meta-analysis that people's likelihood of choosing the exit indicated by enhanced signage is more than 18 times higher than their likelihood of choosing other exits. The practical implication: well-designed, prominent, hierarchically consistent exit signage dramatically improves outcomes. Poorly positioned or visually competing signage provides no practical benefit under panic conditions.

Edwin Galea's work at the University of Greenwich Fire Safety Engineering Group on evacuation simulation — including the EXODUS model — documents that social influence (following the crowd), place affiliation (familiar routes), and exit visibility all interact in ways that produce herding toward choke points when exit path hierarchy is unclear.

John Fruin's Level of Service framework for pedestrian design establishes that at density above 2-3 persons per square meter, free movement breaks down and crowd flow dynamics dominate individual decision-making. Pre-checkpoint queues at major US hub airports routinely reach Fruin Level F (breakdown conditions) during peak periods — the exact condition under which individual wayfinding stops functioning and crowd panic dynamics take over.

[Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/367078062_Individual_wayfinding_decisions_under_stress_in_indoor_emergency_situations_A_theoretical_framework_and_meta-analysis]
[Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ed_Galea]
[Source: https://www.gkstill.com/Support/crowd-flow/fruin/Fruin1.html]

### NG911 Location and Internal Addressing

FCC rules require wireless E911 to deliver dispatchable location — street address plus sub-address information adequate to identify the caller's location — for indoor calls. For large terminal buildings, this requires validated GIS sub-address data mapping building, floor, zone, and room. Without that layer, a 911 call from Gate C22 resolves to the airport's street address. The PSAP dispatcher asks "where are you?" and the passenger says "I don't know — I'm at a gate."

The Esri-published framework for NG911 GIS integration documents this directly: without valid civic addresses and indoor sub-address data, 911 calls cannot be routed to the correct PSAP, and first responders are navigating by voice description. An airport with consistent, maintained zone addressing — the same discipline required for effective wayfinding — can build the indoor GIS layer. An airport that has allowed gate numbering, pier labeling, and room identification to accumulate inconsistencies across decades cannot.

[Source: https://www.esri.com/en-us/industries/blog/articles/ten-ways-gis-extends-the-value-of-indoor-maps-and-ng911]
[Source: https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DOC-365168A1.pdf]

## The Public Safety Case for the Wayfinding Thesis

The affirmative case has three independent lines of argument, each sufficient on its own.

**First, the evacuation case.** Normal-ops wayfinding design and emergency evacuation wayfinding are not the same discipline, but they depend on the same physical infrastructure: sign placement, visual hierarchy, competing-information density, and passenger spatial orientation. An airport that has allowed its normal-ops signage environment to degrade into stakeholder-driven visual clutter has also destroyed the substrate on which any emergency evacuation system must operate. You cannot retrofit a functioning evacuation path hierarchy onto a concourse where retail installations, airline branding, government wayfinding, and operator navigation signs are competing at equal visual weight. The FLL 2017 airfield incursion was not primarily a trained-response failure — it was an environmental failure. Passengers who could not identify a directed path followed the only path available.

**Second, the command-and-control case.** First responders navigating a large terminal depend on consistent, unambiguous space identification. Inconsistent gate numbering, non-standard pier and concourse labels, and non-unique room numbering degrade multi-unit response from the first dispatch. The spatial reference problem identified in the LAX 2013 after-action is the same problem that surfaces in every large-scale airport exercise: 20 agencies arrive and they are not working from the same map. An airport with a rigorous wayfinding governance standard — one that enforces consistent space naming as a condition of any tenant improvement permit — creates the spatial data layer that both emergency response and NG911 depend on. An airport without that standard cannot build that layer retrospectively without a full survey.

**Third, the soft-target case.** Pre-checkpoint approach queues are the densest predictable crowds in US commercial aviation. Efficient queue routing — the product of clear, hierarchically consistent wayfinding — reduces dwell time and crowd density in those areas. TSA and DHS have acknowledged this connection in post-Brussels and post-Istanbul analysis. This is not a theoretical security argument; it is a documented finding from two mass-casualty events. Wayfinding governance that moves people through these areas faster and with less confusion is a vulnerability reduction measure with no dedicated cost center.

The ACRP Report 52 framework — decision-point analysis, sign hierarchy, the three Cs of continuity, consistency, and connectivity — provides the technical standard. What it lacks, and what the thesis correctly identifies as the core deficit, is governance: a mechanism to prevent every subsequent stakeholder from degrading the hierarchy that the standard establishes.

[Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrpwebresource2/acrp-report-52-wayfinding-and-signing-guidelines-for-airport-terminals-and-landside/]

## The Honest Counter-Case

The public safety argument for wayfinding investment is weakest in three places.

**Dedicated emergency signage systems can substitute for good normal-ops wayfinding.** Dynamic LED egress path systems — floor-level lighting strips, photoluminescent path markers, overhead dynamic signage systems integrated with the fire alarm — exist and work. A modern, purpose-built emergency egress system can provide effective evacuation guidance independent of the normal-ops sign environment. The 2014 International Airport Review article on airport emergency evacuation makes this argument explicitly and cites a Transportation Research Part A study suggesting airports with clear, illuminated emergency signage experience 30 percent shorter evacuation times. If the argument is strictly about evacuation performance, a capital investment in a dedicated emergency signage layer may deliver more reliable results than a governance-discipline campaign against stakeholder sign proliferation.

**Training and procedures can substitute for environmental wayfinding.** Well-trained staff who know the evacuation plan and can physically direct passengers are more reliable under panic conditions than any signage. The LAX 2013 finding that employees did not know what to do is a training failure, not a signage failure. An airport with a strong staff training program and a functioning mass notification system that delivers voice direction can compensate for a poor sign environment.

**The real problem is operations, not signs.** The FLL 2017 after-action's primary findings were about unified command, common operating picture, and mass notification — not about exit signs. An airport that builds unified command, pre-positions resources, and has a functional mass notification system connected to the PA network will likely outperform an airport with excellent wayfinding governance but poor emergency operations. The signage argument can become a distraction from the more fundamental organizational and operational work.

The honest synthesis: these counter-arguments are real but incomplete. Dedicated emergency signage systems and training programs still perform better in a spatially coherent environment than in a visually cluttered one. The research on familiar-path preference under stress confirms that environmental design shapes behavior even when systems and people are in place. The counter-arguments argue for redundancy, not for abandoning wayfinding governance — which is approximately the position the evidence supports.

## Specific Incident References and Operational Facts for the Strategist

1. **FLL 2017 secondary injury count.** Five people were killed by the shooter. Approximately 36 additional people were injured in the evacuation panic that followed — not by the shooter — including injuries from the airfield incursion. This is the quantifiable cost of an unmanaged self-evacuation in a terminal with no effective emergency wayfinding. [Source: Wikipedia / Police Foundation AAR]

2. **LAX 2013, 33-minute medical delay.** TSA Officer Gerardo Hernandez lay shot approximately 20 feet from a terminal exit but did not receive medical care for 33 minutes because no protocol existed for confirming the terminal clear and routing EMS. Two separate command posts — police and fire — did not unify for 45 minutes. The spatial ambiguity between agencies sharing no common reference for "Terminal 3" contributed directly. [Source: LAWA After-Action Report, March 2014]

3. **NFPA 101 Section 7.10 sign spacing vs. visual environment.** Code requires exit signs at no more than 100-foot intervals in exit access paths. A standard US airport concourse retail bay is 60 to 80 feet wide. Code-compliant sign spacing means one exit sign per bay, competing with illuminated storefronts and hanging banners. The code minimum produces legally defensible documentation and operationally marginal performance. This is the compliance-vs.-competence gap stated in regulatory terms.

4. **NG911 dispatchable location failure mode.** FCC rules require indoor wireless 911 calls to deliver a dispatchable location — building, floor, zone. Without an indoor GIS sub-address layer validated against the physical space, the PSAP receives a street address. At a large hub airport with multiple terminals and piers, a 911 call from a jet bridge on Concourse D may route to the wrong terminal's response zone. The fix requires the same spatial consistency discipline that rigorous wayfinding governance produces. [Source: FCC E911 fact sheet; Esri NG911 indoor mapping framework]

5. **Brussels 2016: "No staff to tell anyone what to do."** A passenger account from the Zaventem departure hall describes the PA announcing "evacuate" with no physical or visual guidance on where to go. Staff reported they had never trained for this. The departure hall is architecturally identical in its wayfinding problem to a US hub terminal pre-security area: all directional information points toward check-in and security, none toward exits. [Source: TIME eyewitness accounts, Brussels Zaventem bombing]

6. **ACRP Report 52 and the governance gap.** The TRB's own guidance acknowledges that the three Cs (continuity, consistency, connectivity) require a managing authority willing to enforce hierarchy against stakeholder pressure. The report was used by Philadelphia International, Fort Lauderdale, and Singapore Changi — notably, FLL applied it for passenger experience and still produced the evacuation environment that failed in 2017. The technical standard existed. The governance to maintain it against subsequent tenant improvements did not. [Source: ACRP Report 52 / TRB]
