## 1. Gut Take
*(Written before any research. Not revised afterward, even where research contradicted it.)*

The thesis is right about the disease and slightly overconfident about the cure.

The governance diagnosis nails it. Wayfinding failure at US airports isn't a design-knowledge problem — everyone involved knows what a clear sign looks like. It's an authority problem. The reason American airports accumulate contradictory signage across decades is that nobody has standing to say no. TSA wants their checkpoint reminder hanging from the ceiling. United wants their gate cluster banner. The concessionaire wants an arrow pointing at TGI Friday's. The airport authority wants a heritage plaque. Nobody has veto power, and so the ceiling fills up like a refrigerator after a decade of leftover takeout menus.

What's suspicious: every exemplar airport in the thesis — SIN, ZRH, MUC, HEL — was either built greenfield under unified authority or substantially rebuilt in a single design campaign. Singapore Changi is government-owned and managed as a single entity. MUC opened in 1992 as a purpose-built replacement airport. ZRH is run by Flughafen Zürich AG as an integrated operation. HEL just opened a new terminal. The "discipline" these airports show may be inseparable from the conditions that allowed that discipline in the first place. Calling it a governance virtue when it might be a greenfield advantage deserves a paragraph.

The joke: The problem with airport signage is that the person who designed it always knows exactly where everything is. 

That joke is not actually a joke — it is the methodological problem at the heart of wayfinding. Signs get approved by people who already know the answer. The test should be someone who doesn't. The entire "decision-point analysis" methodology the thesis recommends is essentially a formalization of this observation: stop walking the path as an insider and start walking it as someone who just landed in a foreign country with a connection in 40 minutes and bladder pressure rising.

The MWAA-specific framing is interesting because IAD and DCA are genuinely different wayfinding problems. DCA is cramped, historically constrained, and just completed a Terminal 1 project. IAD has vast distances, the AeroTrain, and a corridor system that puzzles first-timers for good reason. Treating them as one unified "MWAA contradiction-debt problem" may paper over the fact that they need different interventions — not the same masterplan applied twice.

*Note after research: confirmed that MWAA actually authorized $514K for an IAD Signage and Wayfinding Masterplan in 2021, meaning the study referenced in the thesis already exists (or nearly so). The report should probably reference this rather than treating MWAA as starting from zero.*

## 2. Tangent Finds

**1. Search:** "Passini wayfinding cognitive research decision points"
**Found:** Romedi Passini (1981, 1984) framed wayfinding as spatial problem-solving with three phases: decision-making, decision execution, and information processing. Intersections are the critical nodes — the moment of maximum cognitive load.
**Why it might matter:** The decision-point methodology the thesis recommends isn't new; it has a 40-year academic pedigree that the report should cite, not just invoke. Passini's work grounds the methodology in cognitive science rather than design intuition.
URL: https://cognitiveresearchjournal.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s41235-018-0098-3

---

**2. Search:** "Paul Mijksenaar airport wayfinding design methodology"
**Found:** Mijksenaar created the Schiphol visual identity system and later codified it — including developing ratified standards with the Port Authority of New York and New Jersey. His agency's work at JFK is a direct predecessor. The "Schiphol standard" was subsequently updated by his own firm and has been cited as an industry reference.
**Why it might matter:** The US is not actually without a good precedent — there's a Mijksenaar-designed JFK standard that presumably still exists somewhere in the PANYNJ design manual. Worth asking whether anyone followed it after he left.
URL: https://www.mijksenaar.com/

---

**3. Search:** "Per Mollerup wayshowing signage hierarchy airports"
**Found:** Mollerup coined "wayshowing" (the sign's job) vs. "wayfinding" (the passenger's job) — a distinction that clarifies who's responsible for what. He designed wayfinding systems for Copenhagen (1989), Oslo (1996), and Stockholm Arlanda (1998) — all high-performing Scandinavian airports.
**Why it might matter:** The terminological distinction is useful: the airport shows the way, the passenger finds it. When systems fail, they usually fail at the showing, not at the finding. This reframes the blame correctly.
URL: https://www.designworkplan.com/read/wayshowing-wayfinding-book

---

**4. Search:** "hospital wayfinding research patient stress anxiety signage"
**Found:** Hospital staff in the US spend 30 minutes weekly per person on wayfinding assistance, costing an estimated $12 billion annually in productivity losses. The healthcare design literature on wayfinding is extensive because the stakes are obvious: lost patients in a hospital are a safety event, not just an annoyance.
**Why it might matter:** Airports should steal from this literature shamelessly. The research base is denser, the outcome metrics are more concrete, and the design principles translate directly. The "lost passenger" is emotionally closer to the "lost patient" than either community typically admits — both are stressed, time-pressured, and navigating unfamiliar spaces.
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11345034/

---

**5. Search:** "London Underground TfL signage standards central authority wayfinding governance"
**Found:** TfL publishes comprehensive, public-facing design standards manuals for signs, pictograms, interchange signage, and product standards. The "Legible London" system extended this into the surrounding city, developed in partnership with boroughs and disability groups. The LU Signs Manual is a real document with real enforcement teeth.
**Why it might matter:** TfL is the closest public-sector comparator for the MWAA governance question. Multi-operator, politically complex, old infrastructure — and yet coherent standards. The mechanism worth examining is whether veto authority sits with a named role (TfL's Design function) or just with policy documents. Policy documents without named enforcers are decoration.
URL: https://foi.tfl.gov.uk/FOI-0092-2526/lu-signs-manual.pdf

---

**6. Search:** "eye tracking wayfinding signage airports glance time research"
**Found:** Munich Airport's eye-tracking study (Tobii) found that passengers largely *ignored* navigation information not relevant to their current journey. Heathrow's 108-participant study found wayfinding was generally good in T5 but highlighted the need to eliminate "temporary tactical signage below head height" and consolidate sign volume. The "Signage Attention" metric measures time-to-first-fixation on a target sign.
**Why it might matter:** "Passengers ignore irrelevant signs" is a direct empirical argument against the stakeholder-addition problem. Every irrelevant sign competes for the same glance budget as the one sign the passenger actually needs. Adding signs doesn't help lost passengers; it helps the organization that wanted the sign.
URL: https://www.tobii.com/resource-center/customer-stories/munich-airport-wayfinding-study

---

**7. Search:** "ACRP airport wayfinding signage best practices report TRB"
**Found:** ACRP Report 52 (Wayfinding and Signing Guidelines for Airport Terminals and Landside) is the canonical US industry guidance document. Philadelphia, Fort Lauderdale, and Changi all applied it. Follow-on reports cover international passengers (Report 161, 2016) and aging/disability (Report 177, 2017).
**Why it might matter:** The US airport industry already has a credible, TRB-backed wayfinding standard that most airports cite but few implement systematically. The question is why the guidance exists and the performance doesn't — which lands back on governance.
URL: https://nap.nationalacademies.org/read/13640/chapter/2

---

**8. Search:** "Singapore Changi airport wayfinding system governance ownership single authority"
**Found:** Changi's Terminal 1 signage and wayfinding system was designed by NACO (Netherlands Airport Consultant Company) under Changi Airport Group, the single government-owned operator. The system introduced "'less is more' to Asia's airport signage ecosystem, built on a clear hierarchy of pictorial information and logical guiding flow."
**Why it might matter:** The Changi "less is more" language is exactly what the thesis recommends — and it was achievable because the client was one entity with one budget and one set of priorities. The design principle is not the lesson; the client structure is.
URL: https://www.naco.nl/en/projects/enabling-40-years-of-world-class-customer-experience-at-changi-airport

---

**9. Search:** "Kevin Lynch Image of the City legibility wayfinding urban design"
**Found:** Lynch's five elements — paths, edges, districts, nodes, landmarks — were published in 1960 and remain the cognitive foundation for environmental wayfinding theory. "A legible city would be one whose districts or landmarks or pathways are easily identifiable and are easily grouped into an overall pattern."
**Why it might matter:** Airport terminals are small cities. Lynch's node concept maps directly onto Passini's decision-point concept. An airport that deploys Lynch's framework consciously would design for landmark-based navigation rather than sign-density navigation. Most US airports have eliminated landmarks in favor of generic commercial concourses — removing a natural wayfinding tool and replacing it with an inferior one.
URL: https://urbandesignlab.in/the-image-of-the-city-by-kevin-lynch/

---

**10. Search:** "National Park Service Unigrid sign standards wayfinding authority enforcement"
**Found:** Massimo Vignelli designed the NPS Unigrid system in 1977. The NPS Director's Order 52C governs park signs. The NPS Arrowhead is a registered trademark controlled and "policed" by the agency. Signs produced outside the system cannot carry the Arrowhead.
**Why it might matter:** This is the closest US precedent for a distributed public organization achieving consistent signage through a combination of design standards, named enforcement authority, and a trademark control mechanism that creates consequences for non-compliance. MWAA doesn't have equivalent consequences for tenant or TSA signage that violates standards.
URL: https://www.nps.gov/subjects/policy/upload/DO_52C_9-29-2003.pdf

---

**11. Search:** "shared space road design remove signs improve flow Hans Monderman"
**Found:** Dutch traffic engineer Hans Monderman's insight in the 1980s: when you remove road signs, traffic slows and safety improves because drivers must negotiate rather than follow rules passively. Poynton, England implemented shared space at a 26,000 car/day intersection; accidents fell and pedestrian activity rose.
**Why it might matter:** This is the most counterintuitive tangent in the brief — and possibly the most useful. Monderman's principle ("my method is always to remove something") is the exact opposite of how airport wayfinding committees work. The sign-addition reflex is not neutral; it actively degrades performance. A sign-removal audit would be more useful at IAD than a sign-addition plan.
URL: https://www.pps.org/article/hans-monderman

---

**12. Search:** "ISO 7001 AIGA pictogram airport symbol performance comprehension"
**Found:** ISO 7001 requires an 80/10 rule: a pictogram passes only if 80% interpret it correctly and fewer than 10% interpret the exact reverse. Specific failures: the "Arrivals" aircraft-descending symbol scored only 22.5% comprehension in one study. The rental car symbol was poorly understood with no better alternative found. The "EXIT" concept is described as "a difficult message to convey via a pictogram."
**Why it might matter:** The thesis's recommendation to rely on AIGA/ISO 7001 pictograms needs caveats. The standard is better than nothing, but several critical airport symbols actually fail the standard's own threshold. An airport that treats pictogram compliance as a wayfinding solution rather than a floor is making an empirically unsupported assumption.
URL: https://medium.com/@Follow_the_arrow/the-curious-case-of-the-failed-pictogram-4457de62fdfe

---

**13. Search:** "MWAA IAD signage wayfinding masterplan 2021"
**Found:** MWAA authorized $514,000 in March 2021 for an IAD Signage and Wayfinding Masterplan to "establish an allowance for future implementation of wayfinding and signage improvements based on the findings and recommendations of a Wayfinding and Signage Master Plan Study" completed around 2019.
**Why it might matter:** MWAA has already done the study. Before this report recommends that MWAA commission a decision-point analysis, someone should read what the 2019/2021 study found and whether any of its recommendations were actually implemented. Starting from scratch without engaging the prior work would be a waste.
URL: https://www.mwaa.com/public-notice-march-2021

---

**14. Search:** "Schiphol airport signage governance Total Design"
**Found:** Schiphol's original signage system was designed in the early 1960s by Total Design (Ben Bos, Wim Crouwel's studio) — yellow for main routes, green for secondary. Mijksenaar updated it decades later. The Schiphol Regulations (currently Version 33.2) govern all activities in the airport area under a private-law relationship between the airport operator and everyone present.
**Why it might matter:** Schiphol's governance mechanism — binding private-law regulations that apply to all parties including tenants — is the structural instrument the thesis is actually recommending when it talks about "refusing to let every stakeholder add their own sign." The question for MWAA is whether their lease agreements and design manual have equivalent enforceability, or whether they're guidelines that concessionaires can ignore.
URL: https://assets.ctfassets.net/biom0eqyyi6b/1iSxHr1ffz5S94P7V57hL0/ce6c8de3e1bc31e99c66db3fa1fa3ee3/Schipholregulations_2025_V33.2.pdf

## 3. Three "Wait, What About…" Questions for the Strategist

**1. Wait, what about the prior study?**
MWAA spent $514K on an IAD Wayfinding and Signage Masterplan Study around 2019-2021. Before this report argues that MWAA needs to do the thing, someone should establish whether they already did the thing and just didn't implement it. If implementation stalled, that's the most important governance story in the brief — not the design methodology, but why good recommendations die between the study and the capital plan.

**2. Wait, what about the lease?**
The thesis frames governance as an organizational discipline question. But at US airports, the real enforcement mechanism — or the reason it doesn't exist — is in the lease agreements with airlines and concessionaires. TSA signage, airline gate cluster banners, and food court directionals are often not just aesthetic choices; they're contractual rights or federal requirements. The governance model the thesis recommends can only work if MWAA's lease and concession agreements are rewritten to give a named design authority actual veto power, not just advisory standing. Has anyone read the current MWAA Design Manual to see whether that authority exists on paper already?

**3. Wait, what about removing signs instead of adding them?**
Every conversation about wayfinding improvement defaults to adding or replacing signs. Hans Monderman's insight from road design — that removing guidance can improve performance by forcing active navigation — suggests there might be a sign-removal audit more valuable than a sign-addition plan. If IAD has 700 directional signs (as one procurement document suggests), the question isn't just "are they right?" but "which 200 are making the other 500 harder to read?"

---

## 4. What the Strategist Should Ignore

The Monderman tangent (#11) is the weakest — the shared-space road design analogy is intellectually interesting but the safety and liability context of airports makes wholesale sign removal a non-starter, and the Strategist should probably trim it to a single sentence rather than a developed argument.
