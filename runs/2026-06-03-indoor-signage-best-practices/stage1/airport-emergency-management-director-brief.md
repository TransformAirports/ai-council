# Airport Emergency Management Director Brief
## Run: Indoor Signage Best Practices
### Lens: Emergency Management — Activation Behavior, Egress Performance, and the Life-Safety Cost of Sign System Debt

---

## Key Findings

1. **Wayfinding is an emergency management infrastructure asset, and the industry has never classified it as one.** FAA AC 150/5200-31C, the governing advisory circular for Part 139 airport emergency plans, does not address wayfinding or signage systems as emergency capabilities. ACRP Report 52, the industry's comprehensive wayfinding guidance, treats emergency egress as a separate code-compliance matter (NFPA 101) rather than an integrated performance requirement. The result: two parallel systems — one designed for normal passenger flow, one designed to satisfy fire code — that have never been tested against each other under activation conditions. The seam between them is where the bodies pile up.

2. **Airport passengers are the worst possible population for emergency wayfinding, and every activation proves it.** The evacuation research literature rests on a foundational assumption of building familiarity. Airport passengers have none. Under stress, people route toward the entry point they remember, not the nearest exit. In terminal emergencies, that means the main security checkpoint, the ticketing hall, or the roadway curb — wherever they entered three hours ago — becomes a concentration point that no sign system currently accounts for. The FLL 2017 secondary panic, where approximately 10,000 passengers flooded onto the tarmac through airfield access doors, is not an outlier. It is what happens when a wayfinding system designed for ticketed passenger flow encounters a population trying to get out by any opening they can find. [Source: https://www.policinginstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/Ft.-Lauderdale-Hollywood-International-Airport-AAR.pdf]

3. **The FLL 2017 after-action report corrected everything except the sign system.** The Policing Institute's September 2017 AAR documented nine corrective actions: EOC relocation, Unified Command training, communications upgrades, mutual aid agreements, customer care protocols. Not one addressed the absence of any physical guidance directing 10,000 panicking passengers away from restricted airfield areas and toward safe assembly zones. That absence is not an oversight. It reflects an institutional assumption that wayfinding is someone else's lane — the design team's, the concessions group's, the marketing department's. What the EM record shows is that when the PA fails, when digital signs go dark, and when nobody knows where Unified Command is, the sign system on the wall is the last communications capability you have. At FLL, that system had nothing to say. [Source: https://www.policinginstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/Ft.-Lauderdale-Hollywood-International-Airport-AAR.pdf]

4. **ATL December 2017 is the definitive case study in digital sign dependency as a single point of failure.** When the underground Georgia Power electrical fire took down both primary and backup power simultaneously for 11 hours, every FIDS display, every directional digital sign, and every gate marker went dark. Thirty thousand passengers navigated the world's busiest terminal and its underground inter-concourse tunnel by cell phone flashlight. The corrective record focused entirely on backup generation capacity — a Georgia Power settlement saving roughly $100 million in capital costs, a microgrid feasibility study, an FAA order covering runway operations. Not one corrective action addressed photoluminescent egress path marking, static wayfinding panels showing terminal exits and assembly points, or the fundamental question of what information a passenger needs to orient themselves when every screen in the building is dead. The airport added power redundancy. The information void during total system failure was left untouched. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105203]

5. **Cognitive research on emergency wayfinding establishes the mechanism, not just the outcome.** Acute stress triggers measurable tunnel vision — a narrowing of the functional visual field that reduces sign detection rates independent of sign placement or design quality. A 2023 meta-analysis synthesizing the emergency wayfinding literature found that "excessive cognitive demands, fear, or mental stress narrows visual fields" and that exit signs selected by enhanced design features were chosen "over 18 times more frequently" than standard signs — but only when detected. Under high-stress tunnel vision conditions, detection rates fall. The design implication is specific and actionable: sign placement and path redundancy matter more than sign content under emergency conditions, because the attention window is narrow. A sign hierarchy that works at 2 p.m. on a Wednesday is partially non-functional during an active shooter or evacuation event. [Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S092575352300005X]

6. **A 2025 peer-reviewed study establishes the "halo effect" between routine wayfinding quality and emergency compliance — and it bears directly on MWAA's deferred standard problem.** Published in Accident Analysis and Prevention, the study found that passengers' perceived reliability of routine wayfinding signage spills over to emergency exit signage compliance willingness. The three attributes driving perceived reliability: visibility, consistency, and correctness. The operational implication is blunt: a terminal where passengers routinely encounter inconsistent, multi-generation signage has a measurably lower emergency compliance reserve. Every broken digital display left dark for two weeks, every sign pointing to a gate reconfigured six months ago, every contradiction between 1994-era and 2019-era sign conventions, is not just a passenger experience failure. It is a withdrawal from the trust account that EM needs intact on the worst day of the year. [Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0001457525002350]

7. **The AVOID/DENY/DEFEND doctrine and the existing airport sign system are architecturally incompatible.** The active shooter response framework explicitly divides the passenger population into two groups requiring contradictory directions simultaneously: evacuate via exits, or shelter and deny access. The standard airport wayfinding system — optimized for unidirectional ticketed-passenger flow — has no mechanism for this. Exit signs direct toward exits. There are no signs designating shelter-in-place locations. No US hub airport currently operates a dynamic signage system capable of displaying "SHELTER — Gates B22-40" while simultaneously displaying "EVACUATE — baggage level exits." The PA system becomes the only real-time directional tool, and airport PA systems in high-background-noise environments, tested under activation conditions, are frequently unintelligible. FLL's AAR cited PA communication failures as a compounding factor in the cascade. The sign system and the emergency doctrine are not aligned, and no corrective record acknowledges the gap. [Source: https://www.policinginstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/Ft.-Lauderdale-Hollywood-International-Airport-AAR.pdf; https://www.fema.gov/sites/default/files/documents/fema_shelter-in-place_guidance.pdf]

8. **First responders face the same unfamiliar-building problem as panicking passengers, with worse operational consequences.** ACRP Report 112 on airport terminal incident response explicitly flags that off-airport first responders "cannot readily interpret standard airport nomenclature" and documents specific instances of external responders driving through perimeter fences and crossing aircraft movement areas because they could not decode airport signage under field conditions. The grid map requirement — a separate navigation system parallel to the standard terminal sign vocabulary — exists precisely because the sign system optimized for ticketed passenger flow is not legible to a fire crew that visits twice a year. This is the airport industry's own documentation that the sign system fails its secondary user population during the events that matter most. [Source: https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/22333/airport-terminal-incident-response-planning]

## Evidence

### The Cognitive Foundation

Romedi Passini's foundational work established in 1984 and 1992 that wayfinding is active spatial problem-solving, not passive sign-reading. Passini stated explicitly that "signs do not fix the problems related to bad building design" — a 1992 observation that describes the architecture of every US hub terminal built or expanded between 1985 and 2015, where the sign system is being asked to compensate for a spatial layout never designed for legibility. [Source: https://books.google.com/books/about/Wayfinding_in_Architecture.html?id=khtUAAAAMAAJ]

More recent research on exit choice during emergencies (Safety Science, 2016) documents that people in emergencies do not use the nearest exit — they use the most familiar exit. Eye-tracking studies showed individuals with building familiarity were "less likely to pay attention to the exit door" because familiarity suppresses attention to alternatives. Airport passengers have familiarity with one exit: the entry they used. The bottleneck is predictable. [Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0925753516306038]

An Ottawa International Airport microsimulation study (2023) modeled low, medium, and high panic scenarios. At high panic, evacuation speed increased while density rose simultaneously — faster total egress, higher crowd-force injury risk. The counterintuitive finding has a direct EM implication: the goal of emergency wayfinding is not to maximize egress speed but to prevent secondary panic, which generates crowd force injuries independent of the initial threat. Clear, authoritative wayfinding during the first 90 seconds of an activation has a measurably different crowd-management effect than emergency-speed evacuation through undefined routes. [Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/00375497231185358]

### Life Safety Code: Compliance vs. Functional Performance

NFPA 101 Chapter 12 classifies airport terminal public areas as Class A assembly occupancies (occupant load greater than 1,000). The code requires exit signs visible from any egress direction, spaced no more than 100 feet apart, with 90-minute battery backup. These are minimums for code compliance. NFPA 101 does not address the scenario ATL experienced — total loss of both primary and emergency power for an extended period — because the code was not written for a total utility failure at a 100+ million square-foot facility serving 30,000 overnight passengers. Photoluminescent exit path marking under UL 924 would have remained functional at ATL with zero power dependency, at a fraction of the cost of the corrective generation infrastructure. No corrective record includes it. [Source: https://usmadesupply.com/resources/building-codes-standards/emergency-life-safety/nfpa-101; https://www.emergencylights.net/blogs/blog/photoluminescent-code-requirements-ul-924-nfpa-101]

FAA AC 150/5360-12F (Airport Signing and Graphics), the comprehensive FAA wayfinding guidance, was not written as an emergency management document and establishes no performance criteria for emergency conditions. FAA AC 150/5200-31C (Airport Emergency Plan), the governing emergency planning standard, does not cross-reference sign system requirements. These two advisory circulars have never been reconciled. The gap between them is a structural deficiency in the federal regulatory framework. [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrpwebresource13/faa-ac-150-5200-31c-airport-emergency-plan/; https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.information/documentID/1022248]

ACRP Report 52 (Wayfinding and Signing Guidelines, 2011) is the authoritative TRB research document for airport wayfinding. It addresses normal operational wayfinding, design strategy, technology, and visual standards. It does not address emergency egress signage as a distinct system requirement or establish performance criteria for stress conditions or power failure. The industry's comprehensive wayfinding document treats emergency signage as someone else's problem. [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrpwebresource2/acrp-report-52-wayfinding-and-signing-guidelines-for-airport-terminals-and-landside/]

### The Multi-Generation Sign System as Emergency Risk

Research on wayfinding in international airports (Interdisciplinary Journal of Signage and Wayfinding, 2017) documents that US terminals' accumulation of signage from multiple design eras produces a specific passenger response: "for passengers, any inconsistencies make all information elements suspect." LAX is specifically cited as a terminal where "overall wayfinding communication was found to be inconsistent" and lacked "an overarching logic." This trust calibration effect — documented in normal operations — is the mechanism underlying the 2025 halo effect finding: inconsistent sign systems train passengers to discount sign authority before an emergency occurs. [Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/317956118_Wayfinding_Signage_Considerations_in_International_Airports_Interdisciplinary_Journal_of_Signage_and_Wayfinding]

The egress map certification problem is a direct consequence of multi-generation sign accumulation. NFPA 101 requires egress plans to reflect actual terminal layout. In terminals carrying signage from three separate renovation eras under three different design standards, the fire marshal's accepted egress map may not match what a panicking passenger actually encounters. FAA AC 150/5370-2G governs construction safety signage for construction zones — it does not require a wayfinding-performance audit at the end of each construction phase to verify that the current sign system still supports the current egress map. No regulatory requirement closes this gap. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/documentLibrary/media/Advisory_Circular/150-5370-2G.pdf]

## The Preparedness Case

### For the Thesis

The EM record across FLL 2017, ATL 2017, LAX 2013, and LAX 2016 supports the thesis uniformly: wayfinding failure is present in every major US hub terminal activation, consequential in each, and invisible in every corrective action plan afterward. This is not coincidence. It is a category error in how the industry has classified the problem. The fire code owns emergency exit signs. The design team owns wayfinding. The EM director owns the activation. Nobody owns the interface between them, which is exactly where the cascade happens.

The CrowdStrike event (July 19, 2024) is the most recent operational proof-of-concept. When airline check-in and gate management systems failed across 8.5 million Windows devices globally, Hong Kong International activated emergency protocols and deployed staff with handwritten signs at check-in counters. Delta cancelled more than 7,000 flights over multiple days. The airports that maintained the most orderly passenger management during the outage were those where staff had independent spatial knowledge of the terminal — where they knew the floor plan without the digital system. That is institutional knowledge substituting for a resilient sign system. It should concern every EM director that their emergency management capability during a technology failure is partially a function of whether frontline staff can navigate from Terminal B to Terminal C without a screen. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_CrowdStrike-related_IT_outages]

The "cheapest operational lever" framing in the thesis is directly supported by the corrective cost analysis. The ATL corrective record focused on generation infrastructure that cost roughly $100 million in settlement and capital deferral. The photoluminescent egress path marking that would have provided functional wayfinding through the inter-concourse tunnel during those 11 hours costs on the order of signage installation. The FLL corrective that was never implemented — physical assembly point markers and shelter-in-place designation signs that do not depend on the PA system — costs on the order of fabrication and installation. These are not capital programs. They are deferred because no one owns the requirement and they generate no revenue.

### Against the Thesis

The honest counterargument from 25 years in this chair: no US hub airport has produced a documented case where wayfinding improvement as a distinct intervention — separate from ICS, mass notification, and mutual aid improvements — measurably changed an emergency outcome. The research evidence is simulation-based or correlational. The Daegu subway fire statistic (12% of survivors used nearest exit) is a transit occupancy with fundamentally different building geometry and occupant demography. Airport-specific egress outcome data is thin.

There is also a defensible argument that limited EM capital should go to ICS competency, mutual aid agreements, and tabletop exercise cycles before sign audits. The FLL AAR's prioritization of Unified Command, EOC relocation, and communications infrastructure is reasonable: those failures were more proximate to the cascade than absent assembly-point markers were. A terminal where ICS is functional and the EOC is staffed will always outperform a terminal with a perfect sign system but no command structure.

The strongest counterargument: most of what these activations attributed to wayfinding failure was partially mitigated by staff deployment. FLL responders eventually directed passengers off the tarmac. ATL gate agents eventually improvised. LAX screeners eventually directed passengers to buses. Every activation in this record was partially rescued by humans at decision points. If you have adequate trained staff, you can partially compensate for a mediocre sign system.

The EM director's position: staff at decision points and a functional sign system are complements, not substitutes. The argument for wayfinding investment is not that it replaces people or ICS. It is that staff using a coherent, legible sign system as the backdrop to their verbal directions are measurably more effective than staff improvising against a dark, contradictory, or actively misleading one. At ATL on December 17, 2017, there were plenty of airline employees. What there was not was anything to point at.

## Anchor Events and Program Details

**1. FLL January 6, 2017 — Tarmac incursion and secondary panic.** [Source: https://www.policinginstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/Ft.-Lauderdale-Hollywood-International-Airport-AAR.pdf]
5 killed, 6 wounded, 37 additional injuries in evacuation panic, 10,000+ passengers bused to Port Everglades, 20,000+ items of baggage abandoned. The nine corrective actions include nothing on wayfinding or physical assembly point designation. Use this as the activation where wayfinding failure was present, consequential, and systematically invisible to post-incident reviewers. The absence of a corrective action is the finding.

**2. ATL December 17, 2017 — Total power loss, 11 hours, 30,000 passengers.** [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105203]
Every digital display, FIDS board, and gate marker dark for 11 hours. Corrective record: generation infrastructure. Corrective gap: photoluminescent egress marking, static wayfinding backup panels, any information system that functions with zero power. This is the definitive case for why digital sign dependency is an emergency management risk, not just an operational resilience issue.

**3. LAX August 28, 2016 — Code-compliant exits as panic amplifiers.** [Source: https://abc7.com/lax-los-angeles-airport-shooting-active-shooter-false-alarm/1488874/]
Emergency exits activated as designed; heavy door mechanisms releasing generated sounds interpreted as additional gunfire; secondary panic cascaded to adjacent terminals. LAWA reviewed whether its own tactics contributed to the panic. This is the definitional case for the gap between code compliance and functional performance — the system worked to specification and produced an emergency management failure through an interaction effect the designers never modeled.

**4. CrowdStrike July 19, 2024 — IT-dependent wayfinding as a single point of failure.** [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_CrowdStrike-related_IT_outages; https://www.wtwco.com/en-us/insights/2024/09/how-did-the-crowdstrike-outage-affect-aviation-operations/]
Hong Kong International handwritten signs and manual check-in direction as emergency wayfinding substitute. Delta 7,000+ cancellations over multiple days. The airports with most orderly passenger management were those with staff who knew the floor plan without the digital system. The event that proved digital sign dependency is not a theoretical risk but a documented activation mode.

**5. ACRP Report 112, Airport Terminal Incident Response Planning (2014).** [Source: https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/22333/airport-terminal-incident-response-planning]
The only ACRP research document that directly addresses the sign system's failure to serve its emergency user population. Documents off-airport responders driving through perimeter fences and crossing aircraft movement areas because they could not decode airport sign conventions under field conditions. Requires grid maps as a separate navigation system parallel to the standard sign vocabulary. The airport industry's own research establishes that the standard sign system does not work as emergency navigation infrastructure for the people most responsible for emergency response.
