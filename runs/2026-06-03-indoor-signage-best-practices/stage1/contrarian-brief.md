# Contrarian Brief: Against the Wayfinding-as-Cheapest-Lever Thesis

**Agent:** Infrastructure Advocate / Capacity Planner
**Date:** 2026-06-03
**Thesis under attack:** Wayfinding is the cheapest operational lever in an airport and the one most consistently squandered by design committees. The airports that wayfind well (SIN, ZRH, MUC, HEL) treat signage as a system; the airports that wayfind poorly (most US hubs) do so because of governance dysfunction, not budget. The gap is discipline, not dollars.

---

## 1. The Strongest Case Against the Thesis

**Bullet 1: The smartphone has already displaced the static sign as the primary navigation tool for most passengers — and airports cannot reverse this.**

Only 7% of airport passengers use their departure airport's dedicated mobile app. That number sounds like a navigation vacuum — it is not. The vacuum is filled by Google Maps, Apple Maps, airline apps, and ambient familiarity. Apple Maps now provides indoor mapping for more than 30 major airports, including Dulles, Reagan National, JFK, LaGuardia, Newark, LAX, SFO, SEA-TAC, Denver, and Miami. Google's Live View indoor navigation — which uses the phone camera to overlay directional arrows in real time — is live at Zurich and Sydney, with expansion underway. The smartphone-as-navigator trend does not wait for airports to decide how to govern their sign hierarchy. It is happening now, with or without airport participation. A $2M sign rationalization program competes directly with a zero-incremental-cost navigation experience that most passengers already have in their pocket. The thesis does not adequately account for this substitution dynamic.

[Source: https://support.apple.com/guide/iphone/explore-airports-or-malls-iphd3705ff4e/ios]
[Source: https://www.macworld.com/article/342049/google-maps-can-now-give-you-directions-inside-a-mall-or-airport.html]

**Bullet 2: The international exemplars are not governance success stories — they are capital success stories embedded in single-authority physical environments that US airports cannot replicate.**

Helsinki Airport (HEL) consolidated all operations into a single terminal in June 2022 following a €1 billion ($1.19B) terminal expansion. Its wayfinding clarity is a direct product of having one roof, one entrance spine, one security checkpoint, and 50 gates arranged in a geometry designed from scratch around passenger flow. Zurich Airport's "Airport 2000" fifth construction phase cost CHF 2.4 billion and included a purpose-built Airside Centre connecting terminals with a unified hub. Munich's Terminal 2 is a joint venture between Flughafen München GmbH and Deutsche Lufthansa AG (60:40), which is described as "unique in Europe" — a single airline and one airport authority co-designing and co-operating a terminal from the ground up. Singapore's Changi Group is investing S$3 billion over six years to upgrade existing terminals and has committed to Terminal 5 at a reported S$5+ billion capital outlay. These are not examples of discipline over dollars. They are examples of billions of dollars creating conditions in which discipline becomes achievable. Attributing their wayfinding success to governance structure, while ignoring the capital base that made unified governance possible, is a category error.

**Bullet 3: The governance solution is not a fantasy — it is structurally illegal under most US airport-airline lease agreements.**

Airlines at US airports hold contractual approval rights over airport capital development projects under their use and lease agreements. These agreements — the financial bedrock of US airport operations — routinely give majority-in-interest airline coalitions the ability to approve or block capital expenditures above defined thresholds. Signage in airline-leased concourses is airline signage. TSA controls security checkpoint signage. CBP controls international arrivals signage. The FAA's Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F governs airfield signage but explicitly distinguishes between "systems typically installed by an airport operator" and "those typically installed by individual airlines." The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey — which spent years developing a comprehensive wayfinding system for 14 terminals and 17 AirTrain stations across JFK, LaGuardia, and Newark — produced an award-winning manual in 2021 that is still in prototype rollout as of 2023, years after inception, precisely because implementation requires threading through each terminal operator's authority. The reason US airports have sign chaos is not managerial incompetence. It is a legal and contractual structure that predates any individual airport director by decades.

**Bullet 4: Digital wayfinding — not better static sign standards — is the correct investment category, and it requires capital, not governance reform.**

The airports demonstrating measurable navigation improvements are deploying dynamic digital systems: interactive kiosks at Philadelphia International, multilingual digital assistance at Hamad International (20 languages), and AR wayfinding at Amsterdam Schiphol and Hong Kong. A single wayfinding kiosk costs $8,000–$15,000 to install and $1,500/year to maintain. A comprehensive airport-wide interactive kiosk network across a mid-sized US hub — say, 50 decision points — runs $400,000–$750,000 in hardware and installation alone, before software licensing, content management systems, integration with flight data feeds, and the staff to manage it. Static sign rationalization, by contrast, requires fabrication, permitting, installation during live operations (often at night, with associated overtime), repainting or re-mounting in airline-leased spaces (requiring airline consent), and then a permanent governance structure to prevent re-accumulation of unauthorized signs. Digital systems can be updated in real time from a content management system. Static sign corrections require a work order, a fabricator, and a night crew. The thesis is directionally right about the importance of wayfinding but wrong about the tool. The correct argument is for digital wayfinding capital investment, not sign committee discipline.

[Source: https://www.roveiq.com/buzz/7-real-life-examples-of-airport-digital-wayfinding-signage]
[Source: https://www.wavetec.com/blog/how-self-service-kiosks-help-with-wayfinding-at-airports/]

**Bullet 5: "Cheapest lever" is asserted, not demonstrated — and the competition is real.**

The thesis claims wayfinding is the cheapest operational lever in a US hub. The evidence does not support this claim against a rigorous comparison set. A comprehensive signage rationalization program at a major US hub involves: (a) a discovery phase cataloguing thousands of existing signs — the Port Authority documented over 10,000 signs across its three-airport system; (b) 8–12 months of planning and design; (c) fabrication of custom sign types in airport-rated materials; (d) installation during active operations with associated access restrictions; (e) a permanent enforcement mechanism. The Port Authority's wayfinding program, embedded inside a $30B airport transformation, has not yet completed full implementation across all terminals years after the manual was finalized. Meanwhile, PA system improvements, gate agent positioning, information desk placement, and flight status display refresh rates are interventions that cost tens of thousands of dollars and can be deployed in weeks without airline consent. Staff-based interventions — positioning multilingual ambassadors at known high-confusion decision points during peak hours — have immediate measurable impact on missed-connection rates and require no fabrication lead time. The thesis conflates "high impact" with "low cost" without pricing either claim.

[Source: https://www.jalbertgagnier.com/work/panynj]
[Source: https://guidestudio.com/realistic-budgets-and-timelines-for-signage-and-wayfinding-programs/]

**Bullet 6: The "discipline not dollars" framing lets capital decision-makers off the hook in ways that will produce bad outcomes.**

If the Council publishes a finding that wayfinding dysfunction is a governance problem rather than a resource problem, the institutional takeaway at most US airports will be: "form a committee." Airport directors will convene a wayfinding working group that will produce a design standards document that will sit in a drawer while the airlines continue to hang their own signs and TSA continues to put up its own stanchion-mounted notices. The governance-first framing is an excuse for under-investment in digital systems that would actually work. It gives boards a low-cost way to appear to act — approve the style guide — while avoiding the capital appropriation that would produce results. The politically responsible argument is that US airports need both governance standards AND capital appropriation for digital wayfinding infrastructure. Separating them, and calling governance "the real answer," produces inaction dressed as action.

**Bullet 7: The depreciation argument applies here — existing signage infrastructure is aging and requires capital renewal regardless of governance philosophy.**

US airports built or significantly expanded in the 1980s and 1990s are now operating signage systems that predate AirTrain, ridesharing, TSA security checkpoints as a discrete zone, and CBP automation. The Port Authority's own assessment found that its existing wayfinding standards were over 20 years old and did not account for any of these modern airport functions. Replacing aging sign infrastructure is not optional any more than replacing aging HVAC is optional. The question is not whether capital will be spent — it is whether it will be spent reactively (sign by sign, as failures occur) or strategically (system replacement tied to a coherent information hierarchy). The thesis frames this as a governance question. It is also, unavoidably, a capital budgeting question.

## 2. Evidence Section

### On smartphone displacement of static wayfinding

The travel app retention rate 30 days after installation is 2.94% globally. Airport-specific apps fare worse: only 7% of passengers use the departure airport's mobile app. Yet despite poor dedicated app performance, third-party navigation has filled the gap. Apple Maps provides indoor mapping at more than 30 US airports. Google Maps Live View indoor navigation is deployed at Zurich Airport — one of the thesis's four exemplars — and at Sydney Airport, using augmented reality overlays that project directions onto the live camera feed.

The critical implication: Zurich's celebrated wayfinding may owe part of its reputation to Google Maps indoor navigation, not to its sign hierarchy. A US airport deploying Google's indoor Live View program would immediately acquire Zurich-level digital navigation capability without touching a single physical sign.

[Source: https://www.macworld.com/article/342049/google-maps-can-now-give-you-directions-inside-a-mall-or-airport.html]

### On the capital base of the international exemplars

Helsinki's 2022 single-terminal consolidation followed a €1 billion terminal expansion. The unified wayfinding that results from having one building with one entrance is a consequence of capital investment in physical infrastructure, not an alternative to it. Changi's ongoing S$3 billion upgrade program, plus the S$5 billion Terminal 5 commitment, reflects a government-backed airport group with access to sovereign capital that no US hub airport can replicate. Zurich's CHF 2.4 billion "Airport 2000" program created the unified Airside Centre that gives ZRH its navigational coherence.

### On the legal structure of US airport-airline authority

Airline use and lease agreements at US airports frequently grant majority-in-interest (MII) airlines the right to approve or disapprove capital development projects. This is not informal custom — it is contractual. The FAA's advisory circular framework explicitly acknowledges a divided signage jurisdiction between airport operators and individual airlines. TSA controls security checkpoint environments. CBP controls international arrivals halls. A "single wayfinding authority with veto power over tenants, TSA, and airlines" is not a governance innovation that airports have failed to adopt out of managerial weakness. It is a legal construct that does not exist and cannot be created without renegotiating leases, federal agency MOUs, and security protocols simultaneously.

[Source: https://www.faa.gov/documentlibrary/media/advisory_circular/150_5360_12f.pdf]
[Source: https://scholar.smu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1715&context=jalc]

### On digital wayfinding results

Philadelphia International Airport deployed interactive kiosks providing real-time flight information, optimal route guidance, and remote assistance. Hamad International Airport deployed 20-language digital kiosks to manage passenger flow across its complex international transfer environment. Amsterdam Schiphol and Singapore Changi saw measurable reductions in help-desk queries after deploying multilingual digital signage. These outcomes are quantifiable and reproducible. The equivalent outcome from a static sign rationalization program — reduced confusion, reduced staff interruptions, reduced missed connections — has no published controlled study demonstrating causation at a US hub scale.

### On the IATA data on mobile adoption

IATA's 2025 Global Passenger Survey found that 78% of passengers want to use a single smartphone device combining digital wallet, digital ID, loyalty cards, and airport navigation. Airline app usage has risen to 23% of passengers, up 7% in five years. Biometric technology adoption reached 50% of passengers in 2025. The passenger is moving toward their phone as the primary airport interface across every touchpoint: booking, check-in, security, boarding, and navigation. A wayfinding strategy built around static sign hierarchy is optimizing for a passenger behavior model that is actively shrinking as a share of traffic.

## 3. Scenarios Where the Thesis IS Correct

The argument against the thesis is strongest in aggregate. But there are specific scenarios where the thesis holds, and a strategist who ignores them will lose credibility.

**Scenario 1: The new terminal build.** When an airport is building or rebuilding a terminal — JFK Terminal 6, new concourses at DFW, LaGuardia's recent rebuild — the thesis is exactly right. Design committees reliably produce sign proliferation because every stakeholder sees a new terminal as an opportunity to claim visual real estate. Establishing a wayfinding hierarchy in the design phase, before construction documents are issued, is essentially free compared to retrofitting after opening. The governance argument is valid and urgent at the design-development stage.

**Scenario 2: The "authorized sign" policy.** Even in fragmented US airports, the airport operator controls common-use areas: ticketing halls, security queue zones, baggage claim, and ground transportation curbs. In these areas — which represent the most critical decision points in the passenger journey — the airport has unambiguous authority. A sign moratorium and rationalization in these zones requires governance discipline, not capital. The thesis is correct that most US airports do not exercise this authority well.

**Scenario 3: The redundant sign problem.** The specific pathology the thesis identifies — every stakeholder adding their own sign until the visual environment is saturated — is real, well-documented, and does reduce the effectiveness of all signs through what environmental psychology researchers call "visual noise" or "sign clutter." Research on wayfinding in high-density environments confirms that visibility, consistency, and correctness of signage drive passenger compliance through a halo effect of perceived reliability. [Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0001457525002350] In this narrow sense, removing signs can improve navigation more than adding them — a genuinely counterintuitive finding that supports the thesis's core claim about discipline over proliferation.

**Scenario 4: Small and medium airports.** At airports under 10 million annual passengers, with limited multi-terminal complexity and no airline MII veto structure, the governance argument is more tractable. A regional airport with a single terminal, one dominant carrier, and direct airport authority over all concourse space can implement a wayfinding hierarchy by administrative decision. For this segment of the US airport system, the "discipline not budget" claim has real force.

## 4. Data Points a Strategist Must Address or Concede

**Data Point 1:** Only 7% of passengers use the departure airport's dedicated mobile app — but this does not mean passengers are navigating by static signs. It means they are navigating by third-party tools the airport does not control and cannot optimize. A sign rationalization program does not close this gap. [Source: https://tnmt.com/airport-apps-are-dying-out/]

**Data Point 2:** Helsinki's celebrated single-terminal wayfinding clarity was preceded by a €1 billion terminal expansion completed in 2021. The governance clarity came after — and because of — the physical consolidation. [Source: https://www.airport-technology.com/projects/helsinki-airport-terminal-expansion-and-infrastructure-developments-vantaa/] The causal arrow runs from capital to governance, not the reverse.

**Data Point 3:** The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey — one of the most sophisticated airport operators in the United States, with dedicated wayfinding expertise and an award-winning 2021 design manual — was still in prototype rollout at JFK in 2023, years after the manual was published. If governance discipline were sufficient, PANYNJ would be the proof case. Instead, it is the evidence that even disciplined operators face structural implementation barriers across fragmented multi-terminal systems. [Source: https://www.jalbertgagnier.com/work/panynj]

**Data Point 4:** Munich Airport Terminal 2 — one of the four thesis exemplars — exists as a coherent wayfinding environment because it is a joint venture between Flughafen München GmbH and Deutsche Lufthansa AG, with both parties having co-designed and co-financed the terminal from inception. This arrangement is described as "unique in Europe." [Source: https://www.munich-airport.com/terminal-2-gesellschaft-4047628] It is not a governance model. It is a co-ownership model. Terminal 1 at the same airport, operated on a conventional landlord-tenant basis, does not share Terminal 2's navigational clarity — it is subdivided into five modules (A through E), each functioning as a near-autonomous mini-terminal.

**Data Point 5:** Airline app usage reached 23% of passengers in 2025, up 7% in five years, while 78% of passengers want a single smartphone to handle their entire airport journey including navigation. [Source: https://www.iata.org/en/pressroom/2025-releases/2025-11-05-02/] The trajectory of passenger navigation behavior is unambiguously digital and phone-centric. Publishing a report in 2026 that frames static sign governance as the primary wayfinding lever is publishing a strategy optimized for 2010.

---

## Conclusion

The thesis is making a real argument about a real problem. Sign proliferation is a genuine pathology. The named exemplars do wayfind better than most US hubs. But the argument about cause is wrong. The exemplars perform well because they had the capital to build unified physical environments, not because they found a cheaper governance solution that US airports have been too undisciplined to copy.

The correct strategic argument for a Council report is this: US airports should invest in digital wayfinding infrastructure — interactive kiosks, indoor GPS partnerships with Google and Apple, real-time dynamic signage — because these tools are updateable, measurable, and able to operate within the fragmented authority structures that actually govern US airports. Static sign governance reform is a necessary complement to that investment, not a substitute for it. Presenting it as a substitute leads boards to form committees instead of appropriating capital, and produces no passenger-visible improvement.

The thesis needs to be reframed, not abandoned.
