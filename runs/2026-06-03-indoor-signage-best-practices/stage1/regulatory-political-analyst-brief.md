# Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief: Airport Indoor Wayfinding and Signage

**Run:** Indoor Signage Best Practices
**Stage:** 1 — Independent Research
**Agent:** Regulatory and Political Analyst
**Date:** 2026-06-03

---

## Key Findings

1. **FAA has almost no enforceable authority over indoor terminal wayfinding.** The two primary FAA instruments — Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F (Airport Signing and Graphics) and AC 150/5360-13A (Airport Terminal Planning) — are advisory, not regulatory. Compliance is not required for AIP eligibility. There is no federal regulation mandating a specific indoor wayfinding standard at commercial service airports. The constraint landscape is almost entirely contractual and political, not statutory.

2. **ADA creates a genuine regulatory floor, but compliance theater actively impairs wayfinding.** The 2010 ADA Standards for Accessible Design and 49 CFR Part 27 mandate specific physical sign characteristics (character height, tactile requirements, Grade 2 Braille, non-glare finish, color contrast) and apply to all airports receiving federal financial assistance. These requirements are enforceable and non-negotiable. The practical problem: minimum ADA compliance produces a proliferation of required signs — room-identification plaques, tactile directories, auditory announcement systems — that airports install as isolated compliance items rather than integrating into a coherent wayfinding hierarchy. The result is a secondary sign layer that visually competes with directional wayfinding.

3. **TSA and CBP each carve out mandatory signage zones inside the terminal.** TSA's Checkpoint Requirements and Planning Guide (CRPG, May 2025) mandates specific sign types, placements, and content within the checkpoint zone, including Mandatory Signs listing prohibited items and LAGs policy, and TSA Directional Signs controlling queue routing. CBP's Airport Technical Design Standards (2017) mandate signage in Federal Inspection Services halls, including dynamic signs for passenger flow routing between primary inspection lanes. Neither agency coordinates its signage standards with airport wayfinding programs by default. The result at international airports is two zones — checkpoint and FIS — where federal agencies have de facto design authority and the airport's wayfinding language often stops at the zone boundary.

4. **The contractual cage is the binding constraint at US hub airports.** Airline use and lease agreements — typically running 15 to 30 years — grant airlines the right to brand their gate areas, ticket counters, and jetways. Historically, these agreements have not included a mandatory obligation to conform to airport wayfinding standards. Airport authorities retain approval authority over tenant signage additions, but existing lease language at most large hubs either does not address wayfinding conformance or is ambiguous enough that airlines can resist standardization without breaching the agreement. Renegotiating this language requires airline consent, which is a political act with a multi-year timeline, not an administrative decision.

5. **Advertising concession agreements create a second contractual cage.** Long-term media concession contracts with operators like JCDecaux and Clear Channel Airports (typical terms: 10 to 15 years, revenue share around 50%) grant those operators the right to install advertising displays across terminal common areas. These agreements do not typically create hard caps on wayfinding sign counts, but they do create financial incentives — for both the concessionaire and the airport — to maximize advertising display density. The competitive pressure on sign placement between advertising and wayfinding is real, even where the airport formally retains authority over sign hierarchy.

6. **The governance vacuum is the root cause.** At a typical US large-hub airport, no single entity has unambiguous, contractually enforceable authority over the complete indoor wayfinding system. The airport authority controls common areas. Airlines control leased gate areas. TSA controls the checkpoint zone. CBP controls the FIS zone. Concessionaires control advertising positions. None of these parties is required by federal regulation to coordinate. The "committee design problem" — in which every stakeholder adds signs expressing their own priority — is not a cultural failing; it is the predictable output of a governance structure in which veto points are distributed and no one owns the whole system.

7. **AIP and PFC funding can technically reach interior terminal wayfinding, but the path is narrow and the competition is steep.** Under 49 USC § 47119 and AIP Handbook Order 5100.38D (Appendix N), terminal building improvements in public-use areas directly related to passenger and baggage movement are AIP-eligible. PFC-eligible projects must address safety, security, or capacity under 14 CFR Part 158. A wayfinding program structured around safety (egress clarity) and capacity (reducing dwell time at decision points) can be packaged for both funding streams. In practice, discretionary AIP dollars flow predominantly to airfield safety and security projects; terminal wayfinding competes poorly for discretionary funding and must rely on entitlement funds or PFCs — the latter still capped at $4.50 per enplanement since 2001.

8. **MWAA operates under specific structural conditions that compound the governance problem.** IAD's AeroTrain midfield concourse configuration means passengers transfer between two distinct wayfinding environments — Main Terminal and midfield A/B/C/D concourses — with a transit gap in between. The AeroTrain stations represent a third physical environment with their own design language. DCA's constrained footprint and phased renovations under Congressional budget oversight create perpetual "signage islands" — newly renovated sections with updated standards adjacent to unrenovated sections with 1990s-era signage. MWAA's Design Manual includes separate volumes for IAD and DCA with distinct tenant design standards, meaning the standards governing the two airports are already not unified, and the internal proliferation of volume-specific standards mirrors the broader fragmentation problem.

## Evidence Section

### FAA Authority

The FAA's primary instrument for indoor terminal wayfinding is **Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F, Airport Signing and Graphics** (September 26, 2013), which incorporates recommendations from ACRP Report 52 (2011). The AC provides guidance on four areas: roadways, parking, curbside and ground transportation, and terminal. The critical legal fact is that Advisory Circulars are guidance, not regulation. The AC itself states that "compliance with this AC is not mandatory" for AIP grant eligibility except where specific requirements are imposed by other statutory or regulatory authorities. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.information/documentID/1022248]

**Advisory Circular 150/5360-13A, Airport Terminal Planning** (current version) provides planning guidance for terminal facilities including a chapter on landside signage and wayfinding, but again is advisory. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/resources/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.current/documentNumber/150_5360-13]

**FAA Part 139 certification** (14 CFR Part 139) requires annual inspections of airport safety compliance, including airfield signs and markings. Part 139 inspection criteria do not extend to indoor terminal wayfinding. The FAA's certification and enforcement authority ends, functionally, at the terminal door with respect to airfield sign standards. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/airport_safety/part139_cert/what-is-part-139]

**AIP Handbook Order 5100.38D**, Appendix N, defines terminal building project eligibility: public-use areas directly related to passenger and baggage movement qualify under 49 USC § 47119(a)(1)(A). Interior wayfinding signage in common-use passenger areas is structurally eligible. Whether discretionary funds flow there is a different question. [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrpwebresource13/faa-order-5100-38d-airport-improvement-program-handbook/]

**ACRP Report 52: Wayfinding and Signing Guidelines for Airport Terminals and Landside** (2011, Transportation Research Board) is the most comprehensive industry document on airport wayfinding. It is not a federal standard. Its recommendations on governance — including the recommendation that airports establish a single responsible program manager for wayfinding — have no regulatory force. The report is 244 pages and is the basis for AC 150/5360-12F. [Source: https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/13640/wayfinding-and-signing-guidelines-for-airport-terminals-and-landside]

### ADA and Accessibility Requirements

The **2010 ADA Standards for Accessible Design** create the only federally enforceable signage requirements inside airport terminals. Relevant requirements include: tactile characters raised 1/32 inch minimum, character height 5/8 to 2 inches for tactile signage, Grade 2 Braille repeated below all tactile text, non-glare finish, and color contrast. Permanent spaces requiring room identification must have tactile and Braille signage mounted at specific heights on the latch side of the door. [Source: https://www.ada.gov/law-and-regs/design-standards/2010-stds/]

**49 CFR Part 27** (DOT Nondiscrimination on Basis of Disability) applies to all airports receiving federal financial assistance. Subpart B addresses accessibility requirements specifically for airport terminals, requiring that terminal facilities and services be "readily accessible to and usable by individuals with disabilities." The regulatory standard for signage under Part 27 refers back to the 2010 ADA Standards. [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-A/part-27/subpart-B]

**FAA Advisory Circular 150/5360-14A, Access to Airports by Individuals with Disabilities** reconciles ADA, Air Carrier Access Act (ACAA, 14 CFR Part 382), the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, and the Architectural Barriers Act of 1968. It addresses wayfinding specifically for persons with visual impairments, requiring auditory supplement to visual signage and Braille adjacency to doors and gates. It is an advisory circular and does not itself create enforceable signage requirements beyond those in the underlying regulations. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/documentLibrary/media/Advisory_Circular/150-5360-14A.pdf]

The practical governance implication: ADA compliance creates a mandatory minimum, but meeting that minimum requires no coordination with the overall wayfinding hierarchy. Airports routinely install ADA-mandated signage as a parallel system, procured separately, designed by different vendors, and managed by facilities staff rather than wayfinding program staff. The result is additive visual noise.

### TSA Checkpoint Signage Authority

TSA's **Checkpoint Requirements and Planning Guide (CRPG), May 2025** is the current operative document for checkpoint design. The CRPG replaced earlier iterations of the Checkpoint Design Guide (CDG), the last major version of which was CDG Revision 4.0 (August 29, 2012). [Source: https://www.tsa.gov/sites/default/files/checkpoint-requirements-and-planning-guide-read-me-first-crpg-may-2025.pdf]

TSA distinguishes between **Mandatory Signs** and **Directional Signs** at checkpoints. Mandatory Signs — displaying prohibited items, LAGs policy, and REAL ID requirements — must be visible from both sides of the queue, prominent, easy to read, and positioned along the passenger path without obstructing queue lanes. These are TSA-specified content and placement requirements that airport wayfinding programs must accommodate. TSA Directional Signs controlling flow through the checkpoint zone are also within TSA's design authority. The airport's wayfinding vocabulary stops at the checkpoint entry point and resumes after the checkpoint exit; the transition zone is frequently an uncoordinated handoff between two design standards. [Source: https://airportscouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Checkpoint_Design_Guide_CDG_Rev_4_0.pdf]

The CT scanner rollout beginning in 2019 required airports to reconfigure checkpoint lanes. New lane configurations trigger updated TSA signage requirements for passenger instruction at bin management areas. These updates are imposed by TSA on project timelines, regardless of where the airport's wayfinding program is in its own lifecycle.

### CBP and FIS Signage Requirements

**CBP's Airport Technical Design Standards (ATDS)**, published by the Office of Field Operations in 2017, establish the design framework for Federal Inspection Services facilities. The companion guide (PARAS 0002, May 2017) documents how CBP's standards interact with airport design. Key signage provisions: dynamic signs in FIS halls must route passengers between primary inspection processing modes (APC kiosks, Global Entry, standard lanes) based on operational conditions; signage must use CBP-approved terminology and not employ acronyms that confuse international passengers; Global Entry lanes must be clearly marked with CBP-approved branding. [Source: https://www.sskies.org/images/uploads/subpage/PARAS_0002.CBPATDSCompanionGuide.FinalReport.pdf]

At LAX, tenant signage standards explicitly state: "All signage in Federal Inspection Service Areas are mandated to strictly adhere to U.S. Customs and Border Protection signage guidelines for verbiage, colors and sign specifications." This is not airport preference — it is CBP's contractual design requirement as a condition of FIS operation.

CBP staffing constraints determine the operational configuration of FIS lanes, which in turn drives signage configuration. An FIS hall designed for 12 primary inspection booths but staffed for 6 creates real-time wayfinding problems that no static sign system anticipates. The signage is designed for a fully staffed scenario; the operational reality is variable. [Source: https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/29288/chapter/9]

### Building and Fire Codes

**NFPA 101, Life Safety Code** mandates exit signage in all occupancies, including airports. Requirements include: exit signs in all cases where the exit or path to an exit is not readily apparent; no point in an exit access corridor more than 100 feet from the nearest visible exit sign; emergency illumination backup power for at least 90 minutes. The International Building Code (IBC), Chapter 10, contains parallel egress requirements. [Source: https://usmadesupply.com/resources/building-codes-standards/emergency-life-safety/nfpa-101]

The **Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ)** question is complicated at airports. For life safety purposes, the AHJ is typically the local fire marshal or building official — not the FAA. At federally-owned airports (including IAD and DCA, which are operated by MWAA under lease from the federal government), the jurisdiction question has additional complexity. In practice, airports negotiate AHJ authority with local jurisdictions, but the fire marshal retains enforcement authority over egress signage, sprinkler systems, and emergency lighting. This creates a third independent signage authority layer — fire/life safety — that is genuinely non-negotiable and non-coordinated with wayfinding programs. [Source: https://public.airportal.maa.maryland.gov/PEGS/Volume_3_-_Life_Safety/Chapter_02_Fire_Prevention_and_Protection/2_8_Use_Occupancy_and_Construction_Types.htm]

### Airline Use and Lease Agreements

Airline-airport use and lease agreements are the primary private-law constraint on unified wayfinding. Exclusive gate agreements — historically running 15 to 30 years at US hub airports — grant airlines the right to display branding at ticket counters, gate areas, and jetways. Airlines own their branding in these spaces under the agreement. While most agreements require airport approval before airlines install additional signage, existing airline branding in leased spaces predates most current wayfinding programs and is grandfathered in. [Source: https://simpleflying.com/us-airport-gate-lease-agreements-guide/]

The FAA's guidance in AC 150/5360-12F acknowledges the distinction between "signing systems typically installed by airport operators and those typically installed by individual airlines," implicitly accepting that airline-controlled zones are outside the unified system. This acceptance is embedded in the primary federal guidance document. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.information/documentID/1022248]

The Chicago O'Hare lease agreement structure — with a 2016 Use and Lease Agreement that includes provisions for wayfinding and terminal access improvements — illustrates that modern lease renegotiations can include wayfinding conformance requirements. But renegotiating a legacy agreement over an airline's objection is not an administrative action; it is a negotiation in which the airline has substantial leverage, particularly at hub airports where one carrier holds majority gate positions. [Source: https://www.flychicago.com/docs/ord_use_lease.pdf]

### Advertising Concession Agreements

Major US airports operate under long-term advertising concession agreements with media operators. The Chicago agreement structure provides a representative example: JCDecaux and Clear Channel held competing agreements for O'Hare interior and exterior advertising, with revenue share at approximately 50% of sales. Agreement terms grant the concessionaire the right to place displays in designated locations; airports reserve the right to award additional advertising opportunities or reconfigure spaces, subject to agreement terms. [Source: https://moodiedavittreport.com/jcdecaux-clear-channel-set-for-chicago-advertising-concessions/]

Denver International Airport's advertising guidelines document the tension directly: airport signage serves "flight information, wayfinding, concession location, identifying amenities, airport marketing, and paid advertising." The guidelines attempt to manage this tension but do not establish a formal hierarchy with enforcement teeth. The fundamental conflict is economic: advertising display density drives revenue; wayfinding clarity reduces display density. The airport authority receives revenue from advertising contracts; it does not receive revenue from wayfinding signs. [Source: https://www.flydenver.com/app/uploads/2023/09/den_Advertising_Guidelines-1.pdf]

### AIP and PFC Funding Eligibility

Interior terminal wayfinding is AIP-eligible in principle under 49 USC § 47119 if it qualifies as a public-use area directly related to passenger and baggage movement. PFC eligibility under 14 CFR Part 158 requires the project to address safety, security, or capacity. A well-structured wayfinding program can credibly address all three: clearer egress routing addresses safety; clear checkpoint direction addresses security; reduced decision-point dwell time addresses capacity. The packaging problem is that wayfinding is not listed as a discrete eligible project category in AIP Handbook Order 5100.38D's Appendix N — it must be argued into eligibility as a component of a terminal improvement project, which requires FAA Airport District Office (ADO) and in some cases APP-500 headquarters concurrence. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/aip_handbook/chapter3]

PFC revenue, still capped at $4.50 per enplanement under 49 USC § 40117 (unchanged since 2001 despite repeated Congressional reform efforts), totals roughly $3.5 billion annually system-wide. At MWAA's traffic levels, PFC revenue is substantial but is committed years in advance to debt service on major capital programs. Wayfinding improvements — which generate no construction photographs, no ribbon-cutting event, and no bond covenant — compete poorly for PFC allocations against terminal expansion, baggage system replacement, and ADA compliance remediation.

### MWAA-Specific Context

MWAA's Design Manual (2020 edition) is organized into seven volumes: DCA Vol. 1 (Airport Standards and Signing Guidelines), DCA Vol. 2 (Tenant Design Standards), IAD Vol. 1 (Airport Design Standards and Signing Guidelines), IAD Vol. 2 (Main Terminal/Concourse Z Tenant Design Standards), IAD Vol. 3 (Concourse B Tenant Design Standards), and additional volumes. The existence of separate tenant design standards for IAD's Main Terminal and Concourse B — two parts of the same airport — is structural evidence of signage fragmentation: different volumes, presumably developed at different times by different consultant teams, govern adjacent spaces that passengers traverse as a single journey. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/business/airports-authority-design-manual]

IAD's AeroTrain system creates three distinct physical environments passengers navigate: the Main Terminal (built 1962, Eero Saarinen), the AeroTrain underground stations (opened January 26, 2010), and the midfield A/B and C/D concourses (built at different times). Each environment has its own design language and sign vocabulary. Passengers who arrive on an international flight, clear CBP's FIS hall, collect bags, and recheck for a domestic connection traverse at minimum five distinct wayfinding environments: FIS hall (CBP-governed), baggage claim (airport), recheckin (airport/airline), AeroTrain station (airport), and domestic concourse (airport/airline). The probability that each environment uses consistent terminology, iconography, font, and color coding is low given the governance history. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AeroTrain_(Washington_Dulles_International_Airport)]

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: Port Authority of New York and New Jersey — The Cost of Unified Standards

PANYNJ's system-wide wayfinding program, formally adopted in February 2020, is the most documented case of a major US airport authority attempting to impose a unified standard across multiple terminals and airports (JFK, LGA, EWR, TEB). The program was designed by Paul Mijksenaar — who previously redesigned Schiphol's wayfinding standard — and won the 2021 SEGD Honor Award.

The governance structure PANYNJ built is instructive. **Aviation Customer Experience in the Central Office** is designated as the enforcement authority for pedestrian wayfinding standards across all airports. A **Program Manager for Wayfinding and Connections Solutions** serves as the principal reviewer for all signage project submittals. Stakeholders and terminal operators are required through lease agreements to comply with the standards.

What the case reveals about difficulty: the program took the better part of a decade from initiation to adoption, required an extraordinary amount of political capital to push through terminal operators (including major airlines with exclusive terminal agreements at JFK), and required an "extraordinary effort" that the award citation notes "absolutely requires political and financial support" to be sustained. The standard is in place as of 2020; enforcement across all terminal operators at all three airports remains an ongoing project, not a completed one.

The PANYNJ case is the best available US counterexample to the committee-design problem — and it demonstrates that the solution requires institutional authority backed by lease enforcement, not just a good design. [Source: https://segd.org/projects/port-authority-wayfinding-manual/] [Source: https://www.panynj.gov/content/dam/airports/pdfs/pa-wayfinding-manual-executive-summary.pdf]

### Case Study 2: Denver International Airport — The Renovation Island Problem

Denver International Airport (DEN) began a comprehensive signage upgrade program through Gresham Smith that provides a documented example of the renovation-island problem. The program identified that roadway signage originally installed in 1993 and remodeled in 2000 was inconsistent with interior terminal standards and was generating passenger confusion. The upgrade replaced 23 roadside and 22 overhead signs along Peña Boulevard.

Simultaneously, major renovation of the Great Hall forced interim wayfinding gaps: new signage requirements conflicted with the existing installed signage base, creating navigation breaks during construction. DEN's response — implementing SignAgent, a sign asset management platform, to create a centralized, searchable database — addresses the symptom (lack of inventory knowledge) rather than the cause (no single authority over the full system with power to force tenant compliance). The SignAgent implementation is useful; it does not resolve the airline-lease governance question.

What DEN's case demonstrates: even airports with active wayfinding programs and real capital investment produce "signage islands" during renovation because terminal-by-terminal phased construction makes system-wide consistency structurally difficult. Capital programs create the problem they are ostensibly meant to solve. [Source: https://www.greshamsmith.com/projects/denver-international-airport-den-comprehensive-signage-upgrade/] [Source: https://signagent.com/denver-international-airport/]

### Case Study 3: Gatwick Airport — Signage Governance After Ownership Change

When Gatwick was divested from BAA in 2009, it lost contractual rights to use BAA's pictograms and sign font. The airport was required to commission entirely new signage standards — not because its existing system was functionally deficient, but because intellectual property rights embedded in the original standards belonged to the former parent company.

This case is analytically important for MWAA because it demonstrates that wayfinding standards contain embedded contractual dependencies — on typefaces, icon families, color systems, and software platforms — that may be owned by design firms, prior airport authorities, or technology vendors rather than by the airport. An airport that does not own its own wayfinding standard in contract law cannot enforce it or evolve it without the original rights holder's consent. Before MWAA invests in a new forward standard, the contractual ownership of existing standard elements at IAD and DCA requires legal audit. [Source: https://travelwayfinding.com/gatwick-airport-review/]

## Direct Quotes and Data Points for Strategist Use

**1.** "Restrictions need to be placed on airlines and concessionaires so as to not overwhelm passengers with promotional signage or display areas. Additionally, reduction of visual clutter within the terminal will require stronger tenant design guidelines by airport operators." — AC 150/5360-12F, Airport Signing and Graphics, FAA (2013). Use this: the FAA's own advisory document acknowledges that airline and concessionaire signage creates visual clutter and calls for stronger tenant guidelines — but provides no mechanism to enforce them.

**2.** "All signage in Federal Inspection Service Areas are mandated to strictly adhere to U.S. Customs and Border Protection signage guidelines for verbiage, colors and sign specifications." — LAX Tenant Signage Standards (LAWA, 2018). Use this: CBP's authority in FIS zones is total and displaces airport wayfinding standards. At IAD with its international traffic volume, a meaningful share of the passenger journey is in a zone the airport does not control.

**3.** "TSA Mandatory Signs display critical information and TSA policies to the passenger such as listing prohibited items or the liquids, aerosols, and gels (LAGs) policy. These signs need to be visible from both sides, prominent, easy to read, and located along the path of departing passengers without obstructing queue lanes." — TSA Checkpoint Design Guide. Use this: TSA mandates sign content and placement in the checkpoint zone. The airport's wayfinding system has no authority there.

**4.** 28% of passengers at SFO in a documented wayfinding study expressed dissatisfaction with the clarity of signage, with complaints specifically about unclear gate signs, misleading directions to public transportation, confusion regarding distance to gates or immigration, and difficulties during connecting flights. Use this: passenger dissatisfaction with wayfinding is measurable, documented, and directly correlated with overall airport satisfaction scores — creating a business case for the Strategist.

**5.** The PFC cap has been frozen at $4.50 per enplanement since 2001 under 49 USC § 40117. It generates approximately $3.5 billion annually system-wide. Every reform effort since 2001 has failed in Congress, where hub airlines — who pay the PFC and collect it — have consistently blocked increases. Use this: the primary self-financing mechanism for terminal capital projects is structurally constrained, making competition for PFC dollars more intense and making the case for low-capital, high-return wayfinding investments stronger in relative terms.

## Constraint Taxonomy

### Regulatory Floors (You Must Comply)
- 2010 ADA Standards for Accessible Design: tactile signage, Braille, character height, color contrast — enforceable, non-negotiable, binding on all airports receiving federal assistance
- 49 CFR Part 27: DOT disability nondiscrimination — same applicability, same enforcement mechanism
- NFPA 101 / IBC egress signage requirements: exit signs, emergency illumination, egress path marking — enforced by local AHJ (fire marshal), not FAA
- TSA Mandatory Signs and Directional Signs at checkpoints: TSA-specified content, placement, and visibility requirements within the checkpoint zone
- CBP ATDS signage requirements in FIS zones: CBP-approved terminology, dynamic lane routing, Global Entry marking

### Contractual Cages (Private-Law Constraints, Multi-Year Half-Lives)
- Airline use and lease agreements: branding rights in gate areas, ticket counters, jetways — renegotiation requires airline consent, typically at lease renewal (15-30 year terms)
- Advertising concession agreements: media operator display rights in common areas — renegotiation at contract renewal (10-15 year terms), with revenue implications for the airport
- Design IP ownership: typefaces, icon families, and standards documents may be contractually owned by design firms or predecessor authorities, not the airport

### Political Ceilings (Historically Persistent, Technically Changeable)
- PFC cap at $4.50: has been unchanged since 2001, every reform effort has failed, not changing on a 3-5 year project timeline
- FAA advisory circular framework: could be converted to mandatory rulemaking, but there is no political constituency for federal mandates on indoor wayfinding; not changing
- AIP eligibility for wayfinding projects: technically possible but requires ADO/APP-500 determination; political competition for discretionary funds is steep

### Governance Vacuums (Explains the Committee-Design Mess)
- No federal standard for indoor terminal wayfinding hierarchy, terminology, or sign placement
- No FAA enforcement mechanism for indoor wayfinding
- No required coordination between TSA checkpoint signage and airport wayfinding
- No required coordination between CBP FIS signage and airport wayfinding
- No required coordination between airline zone signage and airport common-area wayfinding
- No model lease clause requiring airline wayfinding conformance (no industry standard)
- No ACI or IATA instrument that creates binding obligations on US airports (IATA ADRM and IATA World Class Airports guidance are reference documents, not enforceable standards)

## Analyst Conclusion

The most important finding for any MWAA wayfinding program is this: **the federal regulatory apparatus does not explain the signage problem, and reforming it will not solve the signage problem.** The FAA has issued good advisory guidance — AC 150/5360-12F is a competent document. ACRP Report 52 is thorough. None of it is enforced, and none of it addresses the contractual and political forces that actually govern what gets placed inside a US airport terminal.

The MWAA wayfinding problem is a governance problem, not a standards problem. The standards exist. What does not exist is a single authority with contractual power over the complete passenger journey — from the airport entrance through check-in, security, the concourse, the FIS hall if international, and back out through baggage claim. Until lease agreements, CBP zone protocols, TSA checkpoint transitions, and advertising concession contracts are all subordinated to a single wayfinding authority with enforcement teeth, any new "forward standard" will produce the same outcome as the last one: islands of good design surrounded by the accumulated decisions of actors who were never required to comply.

The PANYNJ model is achievable. It requires institutional authority, lease enforcement, political will at the board level, and a program manager with genuine power — not a committee. It took PANYNJ nearly a decade. That is the realistic timeline for an airport authority that is serious about it.
