# Architectural-Historian Brief: Wayfinding as a Design Problem
## Council Run: Indoor Signage Best Practices
### Stage 1 Research Brief — Architectural History Lens

---

## Key Findings

1. **Wayfinding is an architectural problem that has been misclassified as a graphic-design problem since roughly 1974.** The US Department of Transportation's commission to AIGA that year — which produced the 50 DOT pictograms — marked the moment the discipline shifted from space-making to surface-dressing. Before that, the question of "how does a person know where to go?" was answered primarily by architects through spatial sequence, sightlines, and light. After that, it was answered by graphic designers through symbols, arrows, and color. Both are partial answers; the graphic-design answer has since consumed the field.

2. **Romedi Passini's foundational work (1984, 1992) established that wayfinding is a cognitive-spatial problem, not a sign problem.** His research showed that disorientation occurs primarily at decision points — intersections, level changes, ambiguous junctions — and that the quality of spatial legibility (whether a person can construct a coherent mental map of the environment) determines whether signs are even capable of doing their job. A spatially incoherent terminal will defeat any sign system. This finding has been repeatedly validated and is routinely ignored by airport operators treating signage as a separate budget line.

3. **The mid-century terminal generation was architecturally legible in ways the megaterminal generation was not, but for reasons unrelated to signage.** Saarinen's Dulles (1962) was designed as a single processional experience — arrive, orient, depart — with the mobile lounge providing the physical continuity between the terminal and the aircraft. The building's spatial clarity was built into the program, not applied to it afterward. When the mobile lounges were eventually replaced by the underground Aerotrain (the AeroTrain station opened in 1998, completed in phases through the 2000s-2010s), the coherent processional sequence was broken and the building became a wayfinding problem that no sign system can fully repair.

4. **Norman Foster's Stansted (1991) is the last canonical terminal where spatial legibility was treated as a primary design objective, not an afterthought.** The decision to invert the building — utilities below grade, passenger space above — was explicitly motivated by the desire to eliminate level changes and visual clutter from the passenger's environment. The result is the closest any post-WWII terminal has come to the Passini standard: a building where spatial comprehension precedes and supports the sign system. Heathrow's Terminal 5 (Richard Rogers, 2008) partially followed this logic but compromised it in the satellite buildings.

5. **The post-9/11 sterile-area boundary is an architectural condition, not merely a security procedure.** It divided every terminal into two spatially distinct environments — landside and airside — with no continuous spatial logic connecting them. The pre-security concourse had been designed to provide orientation before the decisive commitment to departure; the post-security environment is where a passenger must execute the rest of the journey without benefit of the architectural legibility that most buildings invest in their primary entry sequence. Passengers spend most of their time in the zone the architect spent least time designing. This is the central wayfinding crisis of the contemporary airport, and it is architectural in origin.

6. **The European airports that wayfind well (ZRH, MUC, HEL, SIN) share one governance characteristic absent from most US hubs: a single accountable entity with veto authority over what is allowed on a sign.** Per Mollerup's work for Copenhagen, Oslo, and Stockholm airports established a discipline he called "wayshowing" — the designer's job is to create the conditions for the user's own problem-solving, not to solve every problem with more information. Schiphol's 30-year partnership with Mijksenaar institutionalized this as a design management relationship, not a procurement exercise. The critical operational fact is that Schiphol maintains the authority to remove signs that do not meet system standards, including tenant and security-agency additions. Most US airports cannot demonstrate this authority.

7. **Dynamic signage earns its cost precisely where static signage is structurally inadequate: gate changes, baggage claim assignment, security wait-time variability.** The research literature is consistent that digital displays degrade wayfinding when deployed as concourse navigation aids — they compete with static orientation signage, create visual noise at decision points, and require attention management that static signs do not. Munich Airport's eye-tracking study (conducted by Tobii for the airport authority) found that passengers at high-information-density signs largely ignored non-journey-relevant information and that cluttered dynamic displays at decision points increased hesitation time measurably. The discipline of the best systems is not technological sophistication — it is restraint about where dynamic content belongs.

8. **The "contradiction debt" problem at US airports is an architectural-program failure, not an operations failure.** When a 1990s signage standard coexists with a 2010s standard coexists with a 2020s TSA-mandated addition, the result is not merely visual inconsistency — it is spatial incoherence. Each generation of signage reflects a different implicit model of how passengers move through the terminal. The 1990s model assumed ground-level linear circulation. The 2010s model added vertical circulation and inter-concourse connectivity. The post-security model layered security-compliance messaging over navigation messaging with no coordination between them. Passengers in MWAA's terminals are navigating on three simultaneous maps that do not agree.

## Evidence Section

### Foundational Research Literature

**Passini, Romedi and Arthur, Paul. *Wayfinding: People, Signs, and Architecture.* McGraw-Hill, 1992.**
The canonical two-part body of work: *Wayfinding in Architecture* (1984) established the cognitive-spatial model; the 1992 collaboration with Paul Arthur extended it to signage systems. Passini's central finding — that wayfinding is a decision-making process requiring spatial information, not just directional information — has never been seriously contested. The 1992 volume is the methodological foundation for ACRP Report 52.
[Source: https://books.google.com/books/about/Wayfinding.html?id=lmpQAAAAMAAJ]

**Mollerup, Per. *Wayshowing: A Guide to Environmental Signage Principles and Practices.* Lars Müller Publishers, 2005.**
Mollerup's distinction between "wayfinding" (what people do) and "wayshowing" (what designers do) is the most precise framing of the designer's actual responsibility in this domain. His airport commissions for Copenhagen (CPH), Oslo (OSL), and Stockholm (ARN) are the documented cases of this philosophy in practice. Critical quote: designing environmental signs is not about designing wayfinding but about designing wayshowing — what the designer does is to facilitate people's own problem-solving. This is the methodological opposite of the US practice of adding signs to solve orientation problems created by spatial incoherence.
[Source: https://www.lars-mueller-publishers.com/wayshowing]
[Source: https://www.designworkplan.com/read/wayshowing-wayfinding-book]

**ACRP Report 52: Wayfinding and Signing Guidelines for Airport Terminals and Landside. Transportation Research Board, 2011.**
The primary US practitioner standard. Commissioned in the aftermath of the post-9/11 redesign of airport security flows, it is the closest the US airport industry has come to a systematic decision-point methodology. Its subsequent companion reports — Report 161 (2016) on international customer service, where 1,000 passengers at the top eight US gateway airports ranked wayfinding as a top priority, and Report 177 (2017) on aging travelers and passengers with disabilities — document the persistent failure of US airports to implement what Report 52 recommended.
[Source: https://www.trb.org/Publications/Blurbs/165910.aspx]

**Mijksenaar, Paul. *Wayfinding for an Airport.* BIS Publishers, 2021.**
A design-practice document rather than academic research, but the primary source for Schiphol's signage philosophy. Mijksenaar's work at Schiphol, JFK, LaGuardia, and Newark gives it cross-system comparative value. The Schiphol case — a 30-year continuous relationship where a single design partner holds strategic authority over the entire sign environment — is the best-documented case of the governance model this thesis argues is the critical missing element at US airports.
[Source: https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/59701237-wayfinding-for-an-airport]
[Source: https://www.mijksenaar.com/project/amsterdam-schiphol-airport/]

### Architectural Cases and Precedents

**Foster + Partners, Stansted Airport, 1991.**
Primary documentation at Foster + Partners project archive. The Architectural Review described the design philosophy as: "There is a brand of logic within the Foster office ideally suited to the design of airports; identify the main human problem, invent a system to cope with it and pursue the result with ruthless rationality." The main human problem Foster identified was level changes and visual clutter. The solution — inverting the building to place all services below grade — is the most architecturally complete answer to the Passini problem in any large terminal built since 1970. Subsequent expansion by Pascall + Watson has progressively compromised the original spatial clarity.
[Source: https://www.fosterandpartners.com/projects/stansted-airport/]
[Source: https://byerussell.substack.com/p/fosters-stansted-pavilion]

**Munich Airport, Terminal 2 (Koch + Partner with Lufthansa, 2003).**
Munich's Terminal 2 is the best European case of systematic wayfinding governance in a complex multi-level terminal. The airport authority's eye-tracking study (Tobii, undated but widely cited in the industry) tested passenger engagement with signage along key routes and found: (a) passengers largely ignored non-journey-relevant information on navigation signs; (b) the effectiveness of dynamic digital displays was validated only for flight-status information, not for navigational orientation; (c) instructional floor signage was adjusted following the study results. The Munich case is important because the airport had the institutional structure to conduct a quantitative study, act on its results, and document the change — not because Munich's terminal is particularly elegant.
[Source: https://www.tobii.com/resource-center/customer-stories/munich-airport-wayfinding-study]

**DOT/AIGA Symbol Signs Project, 1974.**
The 50-symbol program commissioned by the US Department of Transportation from Cook + Shanosky Associates via AIGA represents the apex of the graphic-design approach to airport wayfinding. The designers surveyed existing pictograms from Tokyo International Airport, the 1972 Munich Olympics, and other sources, and applied legibility, international recognizability, and resistance to misinterpretation as evaluation criteria. The program's 80/10 rule — a symbol passes if 80% of test subjects correctly identify it with fewer than 10% interpreting the opposite meaning — established a functional standard that ISO 7001 subsequently adopted. These symbols now appear in 177 standardized forms. The intellectual problem with the 1974 program is not the quality of the symbols — several are genuinely excellent — but the institutional momentum it created, which substituted symbol standardization for spatial-design accountability. After 1974, the argument "we're using the standard symbols" effectively ended the design conversation.
[Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DOT_pictograms]
[Source: https://medium.com/@Follow_the_arrow/the-curious-case-of-the-failed-pictogram-4457de62fdfe]

## Three Architectural Cases That Frame This Thesis

### Case 1: Saarinen's Dulles (1962) — Architectural Legibility as Program, Not Afterthought

Dulles was designed on a clear spatial premise: passengers arrive at a single building, orient themselves in a single space, and depart via mobile lounges to aircraft. The entire wayfinding challenge was resolved architecturally, not graphically. The building's processional clarity — arrival hall, ticket counters, waiting room, mobile lounge — was a product of the program brief and the architect's execution of it. There is no evidence in the primary record that Saarinen spent significant design energy on signage because the building did not require a complex sign system. The spatial sequence was the sign system.

This is the correct model, but it no longer describes the building that exists at IAD. The replacement of mobile lounges with the underground Aerotrain system (designed by SOM, built in phases, fundamentally reshaping circulation from 1998 onward) converted Dulles from a processional terminal to a connector hub. The underground connector routes — the Aerotrain concourse, the baggage claim level, the inter-concourse transfers — are spatially illegible because they were not designed as an integrated spatial sequence. They were engineered as infrastructure appended to a monument. The wayfinding problems that passengers now encounter at IAD are not problems that better signs will fully solve. They are consequences of a decision to retrofit a hub-and-spoke operational model onto a building designed for a different circulation logic.

**The architectural lesson:** when a building's spatial program changes fundamentally, the wayfinding system that served the original program becomes actively misleading, not merely incomplete. The first investment at IAD is not new signs — it is a clear decision about what spatial diagram the building now actually serves.

### Case 2: Foster's Stansted (1991) — The Inversion Principle and Its Limits

Stansted's spatial clarity is the product of one design decision: place everything that is not passenger circulation below grade. The concourse level that results is a nearly column-free space under a structurally expressive roof, naturally lit, with clear sightlines to the aircraft stands. A passenger standing in the main concourse can, in principle, see where they need to go before they see a single sign.

The Architectural Review's characterization of the Foster office's method — identify the main human problem, invent a system to cope with it, pursue the result with ruthless rationality — describes something that is largely absent from US airport design practice. The main human problem at Stansted was spatial disorientation induced by level changes and overhead mechanical services competing with navigational information. The system invented to cope with it was structural: remove the problem from the passenger's spatial environment entirely.

The limits of this approach are visible in the building's subsequent expansion. Stansted was designed for 15 million passengers annually; it handles more than 28 million in peak years. The expansion has added gates, concourses, and dwell spaces that the original structure cannot accommodate within its spatial logic. The secondary spaces at Stansted are wayfinding disasters — they follow the standard multi-level, service-over-passenger model that Foster had specifically rejected. This illustrates a principle that the thesis should name: spatial clarity is difficult to expand and almost impossible to retrofit. It must be designed in from the beginning or it will not survive growth.

### Case 3: Schiphol's Signage System as a Governance Model

Schiphol is regularly cited as a wayfinding exemplar. The architectural-historical explanation for why is not primarily about design quality — it is about governance continuity. Mijksenaar has held strategic wayfinding authority at Schiphol for nearly 30 years. The "Total Airport Concept" is not a design document; it is a governance arrangement that gives a single design partner the authority and the contractual standing to reject sign additions that violate system standards.

The design principles themselves are well-documented: yellow as the system color, a strict hierarchy of destination types, the decision to extend the arrow field across multiple destinations rather than repeating separate arrows for each (which eliminates visual noise without reducing information), and the systematic reduction of sign content to journey-relevant information only. But these principles are valuable because they are consistently enforced, not because they are theoretically superior to alternatives.

The Schiphol model fails to transfer to US airports for one reason: no US airport operator has been willing to grant a single design partner veto authority over tenant, airline, and agency additions to the sign environment. The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey engaged Mijksenaar to redesign JFK, LaGuardia, and Newark in 2000. The design work was competent. The governance arrangement that had made Schiphol work was not replicated. The results speak for themselves.

## Direct Quotes and Documentary Citations

**1. Foster + Partners on Stansted's design intent (as recorded by *The Architectural Review*, widely cited in practice literature):**
> "There is a brand of logic within the Foster office ideally suited to the design of airports; identify the main human problem, invent a system to cope with it and pursue the result with ruthless rationality."

This is the design method that produces wayfindable buildings. It is not complicated. It requires identifying the human problem first and pursuing the spatial answer rather than the graphic answer.

[Source: https://www.fosterandpartners.com/projects/stansted-airport/]

**2. Per Mollerup, *Wayshowing* (2005), on the designer's actual task:**
> "Designing environmental signs that guide people as they try to find their way is not about designing wayfinding, but rather about designing wayshowing. Wayshowing relates to wayfinding as cooking relates to eating, or writing to reading — what the designer does is to facilitate people's own problem-solving."

This framing collapses the standard US practice of treating wayfinding as a problem to be solved with more information. It places the obligation back on the designer to create conditions for the user's own cognition — which means the spatial conditions must be adequate to begin with.

[Source: https://www.designworkplan.com/read/wayshowing-wayfinding-book]

**3. Schiphol / Mijksenaar on information discipline:**
> "The right information in the right way at the right time to passengers and visitors."

The operative word is "right" used three times, not "complete." Every stakeholder at a US airport who has ever added a sign to a post has done so because they believed more information was better. The Schiphol standard rejects this. The design argument for discipline over completeness is not aesthetic; it is cognitive. The research on visual fixation times (typically under 300 milliseconds for navigational signs in transit environments) establishes that information not processed in the first glance is not processed at all.

[Source: https://www.mijksenaar.com/project/amsterdam-schiphol-airport/]

**4. Munich Airport Eye-Tracking Study (Tobii), on the failure of information density:**
> "The study revealed that passengers largely ignored information on navigation signage that wasn't relevant to their journey."

This is the empirical validation of what the cognitive literature has said since Passini: passengers at decision points are not reading signs — they are scanning for the specific piece of information they need at that moment. Information overload does not provide more navigation assistance; it actively degrades it by requiring the passenger to filter rather than confirm.

[Source: https://www.tobii.com/resource-center/customer-stories/munich-airport-wayfinding-study]

**5. ACRP Report 52 (2011) on the scope of the wayfinding failure at US airports:**
A follow-on survey of 1,000 passengers at the top eight US gateway airports (ACRP Report 161, 2016) found that finding their way ranked as a top passenger priority, placing it above amenity satisfaction. This is a remarkable finding: wayfinding is not a background satisfaction driver at these airports — it is a leading concern. The implication for capital investment sequencing is that fixing wayfinding yields satisfaction returns that expanding retail and amenity programs cannot.

[Source: https://www.trb.org/Main/Blurbs/173760.aspx]

## Architectural-Historical Framing: The Displacement Problem

The central argument this brief advances is that the professional conversation about airport wayfinding has been displaced from its correct domain — architectural design — into two less adequate domains: graphic design and operations management.

The graphic-design displacement happened in 1974 with the AIGA/DOT symbol program. The operational displacement happened continuously through the 1980s and 1990s as corporate planning firms (HOK, SOM, TAMS, and their successors) replaced auteur architects as the primary authors of large terminal programs. These firms are excellent at engineering and operations; they are not primarily spatial designers, and their terminal plans reflect this. The typical hub-and-spoke megaterminal of 1985–2005 is an operations diagram expressed in steel and glass, not a spatial sequence designed for human orientation.

The research literature — Passini, Mollerup, Mijksenaar, ACRP Report 52, the Munich and Heathrow eye-tracking studies — converges on a finding that airport operations rarely internalizes: the quality of the sign system is downstream of the quality of the spatial design. A building that provides good sightlines, clear spatial sequence, consistent materiality and lighting as orientation cues, and a rational decision-point structure needs fewer signs and can use those signs more effectively. A building that provides none of these things will defeat any sign system, regardless of symbol quality, color consistency, or typeface.

MWAA's accumulated signage contradiction at IAD and DCA is not primarily a graphic-design problem or an operations-management problem. It is evidence of three decades of capital decisions that treated the terminal as infrastructure to be expanded rather than space to be designed. The forward standard the thesis proposes is necessary and correct. But it should be grounded in the architectural-historical record: the airports that wayfind well got the spatial program right first, and the sign system second. Trying to repair spatial incoherence with signage discipline will produce a better-signed incoherence.

The practical implication is this: any wayfinding standard that MWAA adopts should include a spatial-legibility audit as its first step — not a sign inventory. The sign inventory describes the symptom. The spatial-legibility audit identifies whether the disease is curable with signage alone.
