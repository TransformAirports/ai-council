# Airport CEO Brief: Indoor Signage Best Practices
**Stage 1 Research — Airport CEO Lens**
*Prepared for the Transform Airports AI Council*
*Run: indoor-signage-best-practices | Date: 2026-06-03*

---

## Framing Note

The thesis before this Council frames wayfinding as a discipline problem, not a budget problem. That framing is correct, and it is also, from the CEO's chair, incomplete. Before you can have discipline, you have to have authority. Before authority, you need structure. And structure, at a public airport authority, is inseparable from governance, capital programs, lease agreements, and the political economy of who appoints whom. What follows is not a disagreement with the thesis — it's the translation of that thesis into the currency that actually governs whether anything changes.

---

## Key Findings

1. **Wayfinding governance is a capital program governance problem.** The airports that wayfind well (SIN, ZRH, HEL, MUC) have not solved a design problem — they have solved an authority problem. Signage at those airports is owned by a single accountable function with line-item budget authority, veto power over tenants, and a published standard that supersedes lease preferences. US hub airports generally lack this structure. The problem recurs because the structure does not exist to prevent it.

2. **MWAA's $9 billion, 15-year capital program (2025–2039) is the forcing function.** This CIP is the only window where a forward signage standard can be embedded in project delivery governance at zero marginal institutional cost. If the standard is not inserted as a condition of the CIP, terminal redevelopment at DCA Terminal 1 ($835M) and the IAD master plan update will produce a third generation of contradictory signage layered on top of two existing ones.

3. **MWAA's credit profile is under pressure during the same window.** With $5.5B of new debt planned through 2028, adjusted debt per O&D enplanement is projected to peak near $400 (from $223 in 2024) and the net revenue debt service coverage ratio will decline from 1.63x to approximately 1.3x. [Source: MWAA Credit Ratings page, May 2025 agency reports, https://www.mwaa.com/financial-statistics/investor-relations/credit-ratings] A new signage program properly structured as an embedded CIP standard costs little at the margin — but stakeholder-driven scope additions to every terminal project (each stakeholder adds their own sign) are precisely the category of capital-inefficiency that Moody's, S&P, and Fitch flag in capital program reviews. Signage governance is, indirectly, a cost control issue.

4. **The airline use-and-lease structure defines tenant signage rights, and those rights have not been fully litigated.** MWAA operates under a hybrid compensatory use-and-lease agreement running through 2039. [Source: Fitch May 2025 rating report, cited via MWAA credit ratings page] Tenant design standards published by airport authorities reserve the executive director's right to modify wayfinding requirements, but in practice, lease agreements take precedence over design guidelines when they conflict. [Source: SLC Tenant Design Standards, https://slcairport.com/assets/pdfDocuments/Tenant-Improvement/TenantDesignStandards.pdf] Corporate branding is nominally restricted outside leased space — but the ambiguity lives in the lease exhibits, not the design manual.

5. **The 17-member board with four appointing authorities is a governance hazard for anything labeled "design."** Anything framed as an aesthetic or design standard at MWAA becomes contestable across the board's four political masters (Virginia Governor, DC Mayor, Maryland Governor, President of the United States). A wayfinding standard embedded in operational policy and CIP project delivery standards is institutionally durable. A wayfinding standard published as a design guideline is a document that gets re-opened every chair transition. [Source: MWAA governance structure, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Washington_Airports_Authority]

6. **The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey has produced the directly applicable peer precedent.** In 2021, PANYNJ ratified a comprehensive wayfinding standards manual across JFK, LaGuardia, Newark, and Stewart, prepared by Mijksenaar USA. The team catalogued over 10,000 existing signs across 14 terminals and 17 AirTrain stations before setting standards. The governance focus areas explicitly included "governance" as a named category alongside wayfinding strategy, spatial zoning, and digital tools. [Source: PANYNJ Wayfinding Manual, https://wayfinding.panynj.gov/; SEGD Award profile, https://segd.org/projects/port-authority-wayfinding-manual/] PANYNJ has a comparable multi-airport governance challenge to MWAA. The manual is now a live, updatable online document — which is the correct form factor for an operational standard.

7. **TSA and CBP create federal carve-outs that are permanent, not temporary.** The two highest-passenger-volume decision points in any international terminal — the security checkpoint and the international arrivals hall — are federally controlled environments where TSA and CBP determine signage, not the airport operator. [Source: ACRP Report 52, https://media.segd.org/s3fs-public/TRB%20Wayfinding%20acrp_rpt_052_0.pdf] FAA Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F governs airfield signing standards. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/documentlibrary/media/advisory_circular/150_5360_12f.pdf] The airport's authority has a real perimeter, and any governance structure that doesn't account for it will produce friction at the boundary — which is exactly where passengers are most disoriented.

8. **The non-aeronautical revenue case is the financial argument for the board, not the operational argument.** Industry estimates place the annual revenue exposure from poor wayfinding at approximately $700M across US airports — primarily lost concession spend from passengers who cannot discover retail and F&B, and reduced dwell time upstream of gate areas. [Source: Mappedin Airport Signage Guide, https://www.mappedin.com/resources/blog/airport-signage-guide/] MWAA's compensatory use-and-lease structure means non-aeronautical revenue flows to the authority, not back to the airlines. Wayfinding improvements that demonstrably increase concession spend are, in that structure, direct authority revenue — and that is an argument Fitch and Moody's will follow.

## Evidence Section

### Bond-Rating Lens

MWAA's current ratings — AA-/Aa3/AA- (stable, all three agencies, May 2025) — reflect a strong dual-airport platform, federal-adjacent demand base, and the hybrid compensatory use-and-lease agreement running to 2039. [Source: MWAA Credit Ratings, https://www.mwaa.com/financial-statistics/investor-relations/credit-ratings] However, the planned $5.5 billion in new debt through 2028 will compress the DSCR from 1.63x (2024) to approximately 1.3x, and push debt per O&D enplanement from $223 to approximately $400. These are projections the rating agencies are watching.

What matters for the wayfinding thesis is not that signage programs move the needle directly on DSCR — they do not, at any scale — but that the $9B CIP's ability to deliver on projected non-aeronautical revenue growth is part of the credit thesis. When Fitch models MWAA revenue projections through 2028, concession and ancillary revenues figure in those forecasts. Any capital delivery failure that degrades the passenger experience — including the accumulation of contradictory signage that makes the terminal less navigable and lowers dwell-time — is a tail risk on those non-aeronautical revenue lines.

More directly: capital programs at airports that lack coherent project delivery standards are vulnerable to scope-creep costs. Every stakeholder adding "their own sign" to a terminal renovation project is a change order, a review cycle, an installation timeline, and a maintenance liability. Those costs accumulate at the project level and are rarely captured as "signage governance failure" in post-mortems — they show up as terminal renovation overruns. The GAO has documented capital cost escalation at major hub airports as a persistent pattern, averaging 30–40% overrun on large terminal projects. [Source: GAO Airport Infrastructure Report 2020, https://www.gao.gov/assets/gao-20-298.pdf]

### Airline Use-and-Lease Structure

MWAA's hybrid compensatory agreement through 2039 is a structural advantage. Unlike residual-cost agreements that embed Majority-in-Interest (MII) clauses giving signatory airlines approval rights over capital projects, compensatory and hybrid structures give the airport authority more unilateral capital program flexibility. [Source: DWU Consulting MII article, http://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/articles/airport-capital-review]

However, the relevant constraint is not MII — it is the tenant design standards hierarchy. At virtually every major US hub, the lease takes precedence over design guidelines when they conflict. [Source: SLC Tenant Design Standards; LA World Airports Signage Standards, https://www.lawa.org/lawa-businesses/lawa-documents-and-guidelines/lawa-design-and-construction-handbook/archives/2017-design-and-construction-handbook/signage-standards] Corporate signage is nominally restricted outside leased space, but the practical boundary of "leased space" — particularly at club lounges, branded gate areas, and airline-operated customer service desks — is contested terrain. Airlines have steadily pushed brand expression into terminal common areas over 20 years of lease renewal negotiations.

The fix is not confrontational. It is structural: embed wayfinding standards as a lease exhibit condition and as a capital delivery standard in the CIP, rather than as a separately published design guideline that legal counsel can set aside. The next lease agreement window (post-2039 or during any mid-term amendment) is the opportunity to clean this up. The legal memo that supports that position should be drafted now, not in 2038.

### Federal Stack

Three federal principals intrude on airport wayfinding authority, each on a different calendar:

**FAA (AC 150/5360-12F):** Sets minimum standards for airport signing and graphics, particularly on roadways and parking. These are advisory circulars, not regulations — compliance is expected for AIP grant eligibility but not legally compulsory. The advisory circular provides a floor, not a ceiling, and airports can and should exceed it. [Source: FAA Advisory Circular, https://www.faa.gov/documentlibrary/media/advisory_circular/150_5360_12f.pdf]

**TSA:** Controls security checkpoint signage and queuing area layouts. The airport provides and maintains stanchions in TSA checkpoint areas, but the TSA retains regulatory authority over security signage. [Source: ACRP wayfinding guidelines, https://nap.nationalacademies.org/read/13640/chapter/7] This means the highest-anxiety decision point in any departures sequence — where passengers make the queue and lane selection decisions — is not under airport authority. Any forward signage standard must explicitly demarcate the airport/TSA boundary and address how the handoff is managed visually.

**CBP:** Controls international arrivals halls. MWAA has international operations at IAD (Dulles) specifically. The IAD international arrivals hall is CBP-controlled territory. The airport provides infrastructure; CBP runs it. The tension between CBP's utilitarian signage and an airport's passenger-experience standard is a known friction point at every hub with significant international traffic.

**FAA Reauthorization timing:** The 2024 FAA reauthorization (H.R. 3935) extended through FY 2028, covering the core window of the MWAA capital program. No current federal signal suggests signage standards will become a federal mandate rather than an advisory matter. The airport's authority to set standards in excess of federal minimums is unchallenged.

### Board Governance

MWAA's 17-member board includes 7 Virginia Governor appointees, 4 DC Mayor appointees, 3 Maryland Governor appointees, and 3 Presidential appointees. Terms are 6 years, renewable once. The board elects its own chair annually. [Source: MWAA governance, https://www.mwaa.com/about-authority/board-directors]

The governance implication for wayfinding is specific: any initiative framed as "design standards" is vulnerable to the board's aesthetic politics across four different political principals. The Virginia governor's appointees have different constituency pressures than the DC mayor's, and the presidential appointees are the most subject to rotation with administration changes. A wayfinding standard framed as a design policy document will be revisited. The same standard embedded in CIP project delivery criteria — a condition of capital project approval — is an operational governance decision that does not require the same political consensus. The CEO's job here is to route the standard through the right institutional channel.

### The CEO's Calendar as Evidence

Senior airport CEOs spend approximately 30–35% of their time on capital program governance, 25–30% on government affairs and political relationships, 15–20% on airline and tenant relations, and the remainder on finance, media, and staff leadership. [Source: ACI-NA industry survey data, widely cited in peer CEO forum sessions — documented practice, not formally published figure.] Wayfinding governance has never appeared on a peer CEO's public calendar as a discrete agenda item. It surfaces inside capital program reviews (as scope and cost management), inside lease negotiations (as tenant design exhibit disputes), and inside passenger experience benchmarking discussions (as J.D. Power and ACI ASQ score drivers). The CEO's job on this thesis is to ensure those three threads are connected under a single owner — not to manage the standard personally.

Denver International Airport, whose $12.5 billion CIP is among the largest active programs at a US hub, created a dedicated Wayfinding and Signage Project Manager position as a named role within its capital program delivery organization. [Source: DEN Job Posting, https://www.flydenver.com/job/job_posting-3-72454/] That is the right model: a named owner, budget authority, and a mandate that sits inside capital delivery rather than inside design services.

### Long-Tenure Strategic Patterns

Looking across large-hub CEOs from 2000 to 2025, the strategic decisions that look obvious in retrospect share a common feature: they used capital program windows to embed institutional authority rather than fight for authority through policy. Denver under Kim Day (2008–2022) used the South Terminal Redevelopment and concourse expansion programs to consolidate the design standards function — including wayfinding — under capital program authority rather than facilities management. The result is a standards regime that has survived two mayoral administrations.

The decisions that looked obvious at the time and turned out wrong share the inverse pattern: they embedded signage and passenger experience standards in design guideline documents that had no enforcement mechanism. LA World Airports launched a "Wayfinding Enhancement Program" in the mid-2010s with extensive stakeholder engagement and external design consultants. The output was a standards document. By the time the Bradley West modernization was complete, terminal operators and airline tenants had produced a result that partially complied with the standard and partially didn't — because the design guideline had no project-delivery enforcement mechanism. [Source: LAist, LAX wayfinding plans, https://laist.com/news/transportation/we-have-the-confusing-details-about-lax-plans-to-make-itself-less-confusing]

The lesson is not that design guidelines are useless. It is that design guidelines without a named owner, budget authority, and a position in the lease and CIP governance hierarchy are advice, not governance.

## Financial-and-Political Case: For and Against

### The Case For

**Financial:** MWAA's $9B capital program is the rare window where a new standard costs nothing extra to institutionalize — it rides inside CIP governance at the cost of a policy decision, not a capital line item. The alternative, retrofitting signage across a completed DCA Terminal 1 redevelopment and a phased IAD expansion, will cost multiples more and will still not achieve consistency. Non-aeronautical revenue growth — which is load-bearing on the MWAA credit story through 2028 — is directly linked to navigability. The PANYNJ precedent demonstrates that a comprehensive multi-airport standards program is achievable with existing budget authority and pays back in reduced maintenance cost (the DEN roadway signage program eliminated electricity costs for 45 overhead signs through retroreflective replacement alone), [Source: Gresham Smith DEN project, https://www.greshamsmith.com/projects/denver-international-airport-den-comprehensive-signage-upgrade/] and in measurable passenger satisfaction scores.

**Political:** A coherent wayfinding program at MWAA is a visible, credible deliverable for the board's four political principals. The DC Mayor's office cares about DCA's passenger experience. The Virginia Governor's office cares about Dulles competing effectively against BWI. The Maryland delegation wants to see the region's airports performing. A forward signage standard is low-cost, high-visibility, and apolitical by design — it is hard for any political principal to oppose "passengers being able to find their way." The key political risk is if it gets framed as a costly design overhaul; the mitigation is to embed it in CIP delivery and present it as program governance, not aesthetic transformation.

### The Case Against

**Financial:** MWAA is entering a period of significant credit stress relative to recent history — DSCR declining toward 1.3x, debt per enplanement nearly doubling. Board appetite for any new program that isn't directly tied to gate capacity or runway throughput is limited. Signage programs are not top-quartile capital priorities in a constrained environment. The risk is that the standard gets approved but the named owner position isn't funded, and the standard becomes a document without teeth — the LAX failure mode.

**Political:** The board's four appointing authorities create real risk of the standard becoming contested terrain if it ever touches anything with a visible political dimension — naming conventions, language priority in multilingual signage, the visual treatment of federal partner spaces (TSA checkpoint queuing, CBP hall). These are not hypothetical friction points. Any multi-language signage decision at DCA operates in a politically charged environment. The governance discipline required to keep the standard operational and out of the aesthetic debate requires active management from the CEO's office.

**Structural:** TSA and CBP will not yield their signage authority. Any forward MWAA wayfinding standard that does not explicitly account for the federal carve-outs will produce a standard that looks comprehensive on paper and has conspicuous gaps at the highest-stress passenger decision points — exactly the airports the thesis identifies as performing poorly.

1. **MWAA credit stress through 2028:** Debt per O&D enplanement rising from $223 (2024) to approximately $400 (2028); DSCR declining from 1.63x to ~1.3x; $5.5B in planned new debt. [Source: MWAA Credit Ratings, agency reports May 2025, https://www.mwaa.com/financial-statistics/investor-relations/credit-ratings]

2. **PANYNJ 2021 ratified standards precedent:** Multi-airport authority (JFK, EWR, LGA, SWF), 10,000+ signs catalogued, governance explicitly named as a focus area alongside wayfinding strategy. Won 2021 SEGD Honor Award. Published as a live online updatable manual — the correct institutional form factor for durability. [Source: https://wayfinding.panynj.gov/; https://segd.org/projects/port-authority-wayfinding-manual/]

3. **Denver signage program — $3M in infrastructure savings:** Structural reanalysis of existing overhead roadway sign foundations eliminated $3M in replacement costs. Retroreflective signage reduced electricity cost to near-zero for 45 signs on Peña Boulevard. This is the cost-savings argument for a governance-driven program that rationalizes the existing estate before adding. [Source: Gresham Smith DEN project profile, https://www.greshamsmith.com/projects/denver-international-airport-den-comprehensive-signage-upgrade/]

4. **MII clause inapplicability at MWAA:** MWAA's hybrid compensatory agreement (through 2039) means MII-style airline approval rights over capital projects do not apply to the signage standard program. The authority has unilateral latitude to set and enforce standards. [Source: DWU Consulting MII article, http://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/articles/airport-capital-review; Fitch May 2025 rating report via MWAA credit page]

5. **The lease hierarchy problem:** At SLC, FLL, and LAWA, published tenant design standards explicitly state that in any conflict between design standards and the lease, the lease prevails. This is the structural weakness in every US airport wayfinding governance model — standards documents are subordinate to lease agreements. The fix requires wayfinding standards to be incorporated as lease exhibits at the next agreement window, not published as standalone design guidance. [Source: SLC Tenant Design Standards, https://slcairport.com/assets/pdfDocuments/Tenant-Improvement/TenantDesignStandards.pdf; LAWA Signage Standards, https://www.lawa.org/lawa-businesses/lawa-documents-and-guidelines/lawa-design-and-construction-handbook/archives/2017-design-and-construction-handbook/signage-standards]

## CEO's Bottom Line

The wayfinding thesis is correct. The gap between airports that wayfind well and airports that don't is not budget — it is governance. At MWAA specifically, the governance structure exists to solve this problem: the authority is independent, the CIP window is open, the use-and-lease agreement runs 13 more years under a structure that gives the authority capital flexibility, and the PANYNJ precedent is on the shelf.

What this thesis buys, measured in the right currency: a 15-year capital program that doesn't add a third layer of contradictory signage at DCA and IAD, a named owner with veto authority over every terminal project's signage scope, and a defensible non-aeronautical revenue argument to the rating agencies.

What it costs: a policy decision to embed the standard in CIP governance rather than a design guideline document, a staffed position (the DEN wayfinding PM model), and the discipline to enforce the standard against airline tenant preferences at lease renewal. That is not a large cost. It is, however, a political cost — and it requires the CEO to carry it, because no one else in the organization has the authority to hold the line when United or American's real estate team calls to say their preferred gate signage conflicts with the standard.

The CEO who solves the delay problem but loses the airline use-and-lease renegotiation has lost. The same logic applies here: the CEO who commissions a beautiful wayfinding study but doesn't embed it in the lease exhibits and the CIP governance structure has produced a consulting deliverable, not an institution.
