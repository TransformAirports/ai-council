# Airline Commercial Strategist Brief
## Run: Indoor Signage Best Practices
**Date:** June 3, 2026
**Author perspective:** Senior airline commercial strategist, 20 years carrier-side, 10 years airport-side

---

## Key Findings

1. **Signage authority at US airports is contractually divided, not administratively unified.** In exclusive-use gate holdrooms and club spaces, airlines hold signage rights under their use agreements — subject to airport approval that is structurally broad in theory and rarely exercised in practice. In common-area corridors, checkpoint approaches, baggage claim halls, and ground transportation interfaces, the airport operator has clear authority. The passenger experience lives at the seam between these two zones, and that seam is where wayfinding breaks. Fixing it requires renegotiating contractual expectations, not selecting a better typeface.

2. **Carrier concentration at MWAA creates a structural veto over any airport-wide standard.** United Airlines carried 49.9% of IAD mainline enplanements in 2025 (66% including United Express operators Mesa, Republic, GoJet, SkyWest). American Airlines controls 53% of DCA flights. At these concentration levels, a wayfinding overhaul that touches airline-controlled space — or that goes into the rate base and raises cost per enplanement — will require the dominant carrier's acquiescence. There is no design solution to a governance problem.

3. **Minimum connection time is the commercial transmission mechanism linking wayfinding quality to airline route economics.** Delta's 35-minute domestic MCT at ATL is not achievable without navigable terminals. The Plane Train matters, but so does the ability of a connecting passenger to identify gate, board transport, and locate the destination gate in under five minutes of decision time. Airports with poor wayfinding structurally require longer MCTs, which reduce the pool of sellable connections in the booking system, which reduces hub traffic volume. This is the mechanism by which wayfinding failure becomes a route economics problem — not through direct cost, but through compressed yield.

4. **36 of the 66 largest US airports have majority-in-interest (MII) provisions that give signatory airlines review authority over capital programs.** Under MWAA's 2025–2039 Airport Use and Lease Agreement (hybrid residual model), airlines collectively cover costs in Airline Supported Areas. A terminal-wide signage overhaul classified as a capital improvement will enter the rate base and face MII scrutiny proportional to its cost impact. At IAD, United's ~56% of landed weight (mainline only) means it has practical control over any affirmative MII threshold.

5. **The airports that actually wayfind well achieve coherence through carrier dominance, not design discipline alone.** Delta's ~75% share at ATL means signage decisions in Delta-controlled space are enforced by Delta's commercial interest in fast connections. American's control of Terminals A through D at DFW produces consistent wayfinding in those terminals; Terminal E, where non-American carriers cluster, is noticeably different. This is not an argument against design standards — it is an argument that design standards without governance enforcement are wishful thinking. The high-performing international airports (SIN, ZRH, MUC) achieve coherence through a different mechanism: single-operator authority over the full terminal, with airline tenant signage explicitly subordinated by contract.

6. **MWAA is currently operating in at least three simultaneous sign-standard generations.** DCA converted from a three-terminal to two-terminal structure and added concourse letter prefixes to over 1,000 gate signs in June 2022 as part of the $1 billion Project Journey capital program. American's new concourse construction (Concourses D and E, now part of Terminal 2) layered a fourth generation on top of this. At IAD, Mobile Lounge-era signs (pre-2010), AeroTrain-era signs (post-2010), and recent capital project additions coexist across concourses that use two fundamentally different ground transportation modes for the same hub connection use case.

7. **The post-2016 four-major oligopoly shifted negotiating leverage from airports to carriers at hub airports.** Post-consolidation, the four majors (American, Delta, United, Southwest) control approximately 80% of domestic seat capacity. At concentrated hubs, the dominant carrier can credibly reduce flying or divert growth to alternative focus cities. This leverage applies to wayfinding: carriers can resist standards that conflict with their branded environments by framing the objection as a CPE concern — any capital upgrade entering the rate base is a target. MWAA's beyond-perimeter provision (DCA domestic CPE triggering a $1 million payment to IAD if it exceeds IAD's by $2 or more) demonstrates that the authority itself has internalized the CPE-comparison dynamic between its two airports.

## Evidence Section

### The Use Agreement Architecture and Signage Authority

The foundational document governing airline-airport relationships is the use and lease agreement. Standard provisions in US airport use agreements draw a consistent line: within its leased holdroom space, the airline may display logo and other similar signage, as well as crowd control and decorative signage, "subject to the reasonable approval of the Airport Director." No signage may be positioned outside the leasehold. [Source: ACI-NA/Airports Council International model agreement, https://airportscouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/rsw_airport__airline_use_agreement.pdf]

The practical effect of "reasonable approval" is weaker than it sounds. Airport operators reviewing airline tenant signage proposals rarely invoke their approval authority to impose directional hierarchy on airline-branded content. The airline views its gate holdroom as its product environment — the last-mile brand touchpoint before boarding — and objects strenuously to subordinating its signage to airport standards. The airport, dependent on the airline's gate fees and traffic for its rate base, typically acquiesces. The result is that airline gate signage optimizes for brand recall rather than directional clarity, at precisely the point in the passenger journey where directional clarity is most critical: the gate holdroom, where the passenger must confirm they are at the right gate, identify adjacent connections, and locate boarding information.

The FAA's Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F on Airport Signing and Graphics draws an explicit line between "systems typically installed by an airport operator" and "those typically installed by individual airlines," confirming the divided-authority structure. [Source: FAA AC 150/5360-12F, https://www.faa.gov/airports/resources/advisory_circulars/index.cfm/go/document.current/documentNumber/150_5360-12] The AC provides minimum standards for wayfinding continuity but does not compel airports to override airline tenant signage decisions within leased areas.

### Majority-in-Interest Clauses and Capital Programs

MII clauses give signatory carriers the right to review and veto capital projects that could increase the rates they pay for airport facilities. Two structures exist: affirmative MII (the airport must receive approval from the majority to proceed — silence equals disapproval) and negative MII (the airport can proceed unless it receives disapproval from the majority — silence equals approval). At hub airports with concentrated carrier profiles, these are not symmetric: affirmative MII at a United-dominated IAD means United's silence blocks the project.

The FAA has documented that 36 of the nation's 66 largest airports have MII provisions, and that "officials at eight airports told us that MIIs greatly impede projects and thus have the effect of discouraging expansion and reducing competitive access." The GAO has characterized MII clauses as "potentially anti-competitive tools of the incumbent airlines" that can delay or kill capital projects. [Source: FAA airport business practices study, https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/airports/aip/airport-business-practices-and-their-impact-on-airline-competition.pdf]

MWAA's 2025–2039 Airport Use and Lease Agreement operates under a hybrid residual rate-setting methodology. Airlines collectively cover costs allocable to Airline Supported Areas at each airport, with a 1.40x debt service coverage target built into rate formulas and a 1.25x bond covenant floor. Any capital improvement that enters the terminal cost base flows directly into the rates airlines pay. A comprehensive signage overhaul — even one scoped at $20–50 million across both airports — will appear in the CPE calculation and trigger formal airline review under the use agreement. [Source: DWU Consulting Dulles RFI analysis, https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

### Carrier Concentration at MWAA

**IAD (Washington Dulles):** United Airlines mainline carried 49.9% of Dulles passenger enplanements in 2025 (35.7% domestic and 14.2% transborder/international). Including United Express affiliated carriers (Mesa, Republic, GoJet, SkyWest), United-affiliated traffic reaches approximately two-thirds of all Dulles enplanements. United's mainline landed weight is 46.0%; including identified United Express carriers, this rises to approximately 56%, and to 59.6% including CommutAir. This concentration makes United "by a wide margin the dominant carrier" at IAD, with practical gatekeeping influence over the 65% airline-approval threshold required for certain revenue structures. [Source: DWU Consulting, https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/dulles-rfi-analysis]

**DCA (Reagan National):** American Airlines holds approximately 53% of DCA flights. The airport serves eight airlines across 97 destinations. The 2022 terminal renaming exercise — converting from three terminals to two, with over 1,000 signs changed — required coordinated airline compliance on boarding passes and FIDS within a five-day window. American's new Concourses D and E (Terminal 2 expansion, part of a $1 billion capital program) are being built to standards American negotiated, including its own Admirals Club configuration, jetbridge specifications, and holdroom environment — all of which establish a de facto signage standard in American's newly controlled space. [Source: MWAA announcement, https://www.mwaa.com/news/reagan-national-airport-enhance-gate-and-terminal-signage; DCist, https://dcist.com/story/22/05/31/dca-national-airport-renaming-terminals-gates/]

### Connection Economics and Wayfinding Quality

MCT is the commercial mechanism through which wayfinding quality translates to route economics. The relationship is specific and measurable:

- **ATL (Delta):** Published domestic-domestic MCT is 35 minutes, among the shortest at a US major hub. Delta's Plane Train runs every 1-2 minutes connecting all concourses within the secure area. The linear layout of ATL's concourses (A, B, C, D, E, F, T in a single spine with the Plane Train beneath) means that the maximum within-hub connection distance is bounded and predictable. This enables the 35-minute MCT because wayfinding execution time is genuinely short and consistent. [Source: Mighty Travels, https://www.mightytravels.com/2024/07/navigating-atlantas-hartsfield-jackson-is-a-40-minute-layover-feasible/]

- **IAD (United):** United's published domestic-domestic MCT is also 35 minutes for mainline-to-mainline connections at IAD. But this figure is misleading when the inbound or outbound gate is in Concourse D, which the AeroTrain does not reach. Concourse D connections require Mobile Lounge service — with vehicles running every five minutes and direct trips taking up to 15 minutes — plus time to locate and board the lounge. A connecting passenger who misreads IAD's transport signage and arrives at the AeroTrain for a D-gate connection has added 10-15 minutes of recovery time to a 35-minute window. The signage challenge here is structural: two fundamentally different transport modes (AeroTrain, Mobile Lounge) serve the same hub connection use case, and passengers must correctly identify which to use before committing to either. [Source: Transport Politic, https://www.thetransportpolitic.com/2010/01/26/dulles-airport-replaces-distinctive-mobile-lounge-system-with-aerotrain/; Airliners.net connection forum data]

The difference between these two MCT environments is not primarily budget — MWAA has spent far more than ATL on its physical infrastructure. The difference is that ATL's layout, transport system, and wayfinding all point in the same direction. IAD's do not.

- **Comparison airports:** Large hub MCTs at Chicago O'Hare (ORD, United) run 45 minutes domestic-domestic and longer for terminal-change connections. O'Hare's multi-terminal structure, where Terminals 1 (United), 2 (American), 3 (other carriers), and 5 (international) require airside-to-landside movement for cross-terminal connections, structurally inflates MCT regardless of signage quality. The wayfinding challenge at ORD is partly a geography problem; the wayfinding challenge at IAD is partly a transport-mode problem. Both reduce the sellable connection inventory in the booking system.

### Hub Carrier Influence on Terminal Design and Wayfinding Standards

The airports that achieve coherent wayfinding share a common structural feature: a dominant carrier that controls enough terminal space to enforce consistency through its own commercial interests.

**Dallas-Fort Worth (DFW):** American Airlines controls Terminals A, B, C, and D — the entire operational core of DFW. Terminal E handles all other carriers and international arrivals. Within American's four terminals, wayfinding is consistent because American specifies the environment as part of its capital investment. Between American's terminals and Terminal E, the wayfinding standard changes visibly. American did not achieve this by collaborating with other tenants on a shared sign system; it achieved it by controlling the space. [Source: Dallas Fort Worth International Airport Wikipedia entry]

**Atlanta (ATL):** Delta controls approximately 75% of ATL operations and all terminal space except the North Terminal (Southwest and others). Delta's commercial interest in fast connections at its primary hub translates directly into investment in navigable terminal environments. The 35-minute MCT is a marketing claim in Delta's hub competitiveness pitch; maintaining it requires the terminal to actually work. [Source: Mighty Travels MCT analysis]

**The MWAA contrast:** Neither IAD nor DCA has a carrier at Delta-ATL or American-DFW levels of terminal control. At IAD, United controls Concourses C and D (by gate count) but does not control the Main Terminal or Concourses A and B. At DCA, American controls Concourses C, D, and E in Terminal 2 but shares the terminal complex with Southwest, Alaska, Delta, United, and others. The governance condition that enables coherent wayfinding at ATL and DFW does not exist at either MWAA airport. This is not a solvable design problem — it is a structural feature of the airports' multi-carrier configuration.

### The Contradiction-Debt Problem at MWAA

MWAA's signage environment is a stratigraphy of past capital decisions, each of which made sense at the time and now conflicts with what came after:

**IAD layers:**
- Pre-2010: Mobile Lounge-era signage designed for a fully mobile-lounge-served airport. Signs directed passengers to lounge boarding positions at the Main Terminal perimeter.
- Post-2010: AeroTrain signage added for Concourses A, B, and C connections. The AeroTrain station for Concourse C is 500 feet from the concourse building, accessible via underground moving walkway — a non-obvious routing that requires additional wayfinding investment to explain.
- Ongoing: Concourse D remains Mobile Lounge-served. Signage must distinguish between AeroTrain (A/B/C) and Mobile Lounge (D/international arrivals) destinations, at decision points where passengers cannot verify their ground transportation requirement without knowing their outbound gate concourse.

**DCA layers:**
- Pre-2022: Three-terminal structure (A, B/C) with gate numbers only.
- June 2022: Terminal renaming to two terminals, 1,000+ signs converted, alphanumeric gates added (C23 becomes Gate C23).
- 2021–ongoing: New Concourse E (Gates E46-E59) opened in 2021; Concourse D expansion active. American's new holdrooms establish a branded environment standard different from the pre-Project Journey terminal.

The cost of this stratigraphy is not primarily in sign replacement — it is in the cognitive load on passengers who must hold multiple mental models simultaneously, and in the staff time expended redirecting passengers who followed a sign that was accurate when it was installed but is now misleading. MWAA has recognized this: its enhanced signage announcement at DCA cited the goal of simplifying passenger travel "from curb to gate." That it took a $1 billion capital program to prompt a fundamental sign-system rethinking tells you something about how sign governance has worked historically.

## Case Studies: Carrier Responses to Airport Decisions

### Case Study 1: DCA Terminal Renaming, June 2022

**What happened:** MWAA converted DCA from a three-terminal system to two terminals, added concourse letter prefixes to gate designations, and converted over 1,000 signs in a five-day window tied to the Project Journey expansion opening. Airlines were required to update boarding passes, FIDS displays, and printed materials on the same timeline.

**What this reveals about carrier-airport leverage:** The compressed timeline was necessary because MWAA needed airline boarding pass systems to reflect the new gate codes simultaneously with the physical sign changes. American, as the dominant carrier with 53% of flights, could have requested a longer implementation window. That it did not — that the change happened in five days — reflects both MWAA's determination to move and American's calculation that the new Concourses D and E (part of the same Project Journey) would serve its own interests.

**The carrier response question:** American's cooperation on the 2022 renaming does not signal openness to subsequent wayfinding overhauls that might constrain its design choices in the new concourses. The new concourse holdrooms, jetbridges, and club spaces are being built to American's specifications. A future MWAA wayfinding standard that attempts to regulate signage in those spaces will encounter an American that has just spent $1 billion installing its own standard.

**The lesson:** Carrier cooperation on sign changes is easier to obtain when the carrier is also getting capital investment it wants. It is harder to obtain when the airport is asking the carrier to subordinate its branded environment to an airport standard without a corresponding capital benefit.

### Case Study 2: IAD AeroTrain Navigation — The Transport-Mode Wayfinding Failure

**What happened:** In 2010, MWAA replaced Mobile Lounge service to Concourses A, B, and C with the AeroTrain underground people mover. Concourse D retained Mobile Lounge service due to funding limitations. The result is a hub where connecting passengers face two different ground transportation modes for an identical-seeming use case (inbound flight, need to reach outbound gate).

**What this reveals about carrier-airport leverage:** United, as the hub carrier, was aware of this split at the time of the AeroTrain opening. United's published MCT of 35 minutes for domestic-domestic connections at IAD applies to any domestic connection, including those involving Concourse D Mobile Lounge service. The MCT figure is constructed for the best-case connection (C-to-C or A-to-C via AeroTrain); the D-gate connection case is operationally handled through buffers and rebooking, not through MCT adjustment.

**The wayfinding implication:** The signage system at IAD must do something no sign system was designed to do well: tell passengers which of two transport modes to use before they know which mode they need. The decision point is the AeroTrain station / Mobile Lounge boarding area interface, where a passenger must choose — and that choice requires knowing the outbound gate's concourse, which requires reading a FIDS, which requires finding a FIDS, which requires wayfinding to get to a FIDS location. This is a decision chain, not a single decision point. Each link is a wayfinding problem.

**The carrier response question:** United has commercial interest in resolving D-gate confusion (misconnects damage United's brand, require rebooking, and create passenger service costs). But United's preferred solution is completing the AeroTrain loop to Concourse D — a capital program estimated in the hundreds of millions. A signage-only solution to a transport-mode problem is second-best at best, and United knows it. Any MWAA wayfinding standard that does not address the AeroTrain completion question will be treated by United as necessary but insufficient.

### Case Study 3: ATL — What Good Looks Like, and Why

**What happened:** Delta's transformation of Hartsfield-Jackson from a high-MCT 1970s hub to a 35-minute MCT industry benchmark is the clearest demonstration of what carrier-driven wayfinding coherence produces. The Plane Train carries approximately 200,000 passengers per day, departing every 1-2 minutes, connecting all concourses within the secure area in a linear sequence.

**What made it work:** Delta controls approximately 75% of ATL traffic. Its commercial interest in maintaining the 35-minute MCT as a hub competitiveness claim (it is cited in Delta investor materials as a structural advantage over multi-terminal competitors) means Delta has active commercial incentive to maintain navigable terminals. The wayfinding at ATL is not primarily an airport achievement — it is a carrier achievement that the airport enables through its linear terminal design.

**The transferable lesson for MWAA:** The lesson is not "MWAA should build a Plane Train." The lesson is that wayfinding coherence follows from unified operational control — one entity that owns the passenger experience from inbound gate to outbound gate and has commercial incentive to make the connection work. MWAA does not have this at either airport. United does not control the full IAD passenger journey; American does not control the full DCA journey. A forward signage standard must account for this by establishing clear airport-operator authority over the common areas and transition zones where the wayfinding breaks, while acknowledging that airline-controlled space will remain a governed negotiation rather than a commanded standard.

## The Carrier-Response Test Applied to the Wayfinding Thesis

The run prompt asks for a forward signage standard at MWAA that treats wayfinding as a system with explicit performance criteria. This is correct as a design philosophy. It will fail as a governance strategy unless it answers the carrier-response question at each step.

**Question 1: Will United support an IAD wayfinding standard that covers United-controlled concourse space?**
Answer: United will support standards that improve connection performance in common areas and transition zones. It will resist standards that constrain its investment in Polaris lounge environments, gate holdroom branding, or proprietary gate display technology. The negotiating leverage runs toward United: a 66% affiliated-carrier share means United can credibly argue that its brand environment in its gate areas is not the airport's business, and the use agreement supports that position.

**Question 2: Will American support a DCA wayfinding standard that covers the new concourse?**
Answer: No, not as a blanket standard. American has just invested $1 billion in new gates built to its own specifications. Asking American to subordinate that environment to an airport wayfinding standard is asking it to retroactively undermine a capital decision it has already made. The airport's leverage point is the lease negotiation for the new concourse space — that window has passed.

**Question 3: Does the MII clause create a formal veto over a wayfinding capital program?**
Answer: Depends on project scope and rate-base treatment. A capital program under the threshold for MII review (typically $5-10 million, varying by agreement) avoids the formal process. A comprehensive, terminal-wide overhaul will cross that threshold. MWAA should structure a wayfinding standard as an operating standard (operating expense, not capital expenditure) wherever possible, and reserve capital classification for infrastructure-grade elements (structural sign supports, digital display backbone infrastructure). This is not just accounting — it is a governance strategy.

**Question 4: What does carrier behavior imply about the root cause of wayfinding failure?**
Answer: The airports where wayfinding works (ATL, DFW within American's space, SIN, ZRH) have one feature in common: a single entity controls the full passenger journey in the terminal and has commercial interest in making it navigable. MWAA's wayfinding problem is not primarily that MWAA has made bad design choices — it is that MWAA has ceded design authority in the most commercially important terminal zones to carriers who have no incentive to make their space navigable for passengers connecting to other carriers. The forward standard needs to establish airport authority in the transition zones and common areas as a legal and contractual matter before it can be meaningful as a design matter.

**The veto in plain terms:** A wayfinding strategy that does not address the use agreement boundary between airport common space and airline leased space is incomplete. The design work matters, but it is downstream of the governance question. The governance question is: what authority does MWAA hold over signage in airline-controlled space, and is MWAA willing to exercise it in the 2025–2039 use agreement framework? Until that question is answered, the best sign system money can buy will stop at the holdroom door.
