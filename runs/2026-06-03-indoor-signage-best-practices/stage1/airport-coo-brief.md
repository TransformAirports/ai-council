# COO Research Brief: Indoor Airport Wayfinding and Signage
**Prepared for Council Run | June 2026**
**Role: Chief Operating Officer, Major US Hub Airport**

---

## Key Findings

1. **The signage problem is a governance problem, not a design problem.** Every major US hub can point to a design standard. What they cannot point to is a single authority who can reject a sign proposed by TSA, American Airlines, or a concessionaire without the request dying in a committee. The airports that wayfind well — Schiphol since 1967, Changi through its Terminal 1 redesign, Munich through its eye-tracking-validated adjustments — have each resolved this governance question before they touched a sign panel. US hubs have not.

2. **Decision-point discipline is the operative concept, and it is almost entirely absent from US hub practice.** ACRP Report 52 codifies it: map where passengers must choose, confirm that the sign at that location answers the question the passenger is actually asking, verify the sign is visible before the choice-point, not after. The failure mode at IAD, LAX, ORD, and DFW is not bad typography — it is signs placed where designers had wall space, not where passengers had questions. Arora Engineers catalogued 26,096 signs across DFW's facilities and roadways before recommending a comprehensive rebuild; the existence of that number alone tells you what the governance problem produces over time.

3. **IROPS converts mediocre wayfinding into a passenger flow crisis.** On a normal operating day, bad wayfinding costs you information-desk staff time — one study found that airport staff spend approximately 80 percent of their time answering directional questions that a functional sign system would have eliminated. When a ground stop hits and 40 gate changes execute in 90 minutes, the static sign system becomes actively wrong. Passengers who successfully used the system in the morning cannot use it in the afternoon because the physical plant now points to the wrong place. This is the failure mode that ends careers: it is visible, it generates complaints, and it happens exactly when every other operational system is also under stress.

4. **The three-era problem is structural and MWAA carries a textbook case.** DCA's 1941 structure was overlaid with post-9/11 sterile-zone reconfigurations that severed intuitive flows. Project Journey's Concourse E, opened in phases, produced the specific symptom: signs in the new concourse had the letter "E" blacked out before gate numbers until the comprehensive 2022 renaming standardized the alphanumeric system. IAD runs a worse version of the same problem: AeroTrain-era signage (2010 forward), International Arrivals Building signage from the CBP Model Ports Initiative, and the Concourse D gap where no AeroTrain serves and the mobile lounge boarding location requires passengers to locate a mid-concourse transfer node near Gates D5–D8. Three standards, three vintages, one building the passenger is told to navigate.

5. **Dynamic signage earns its capital cost at specific decision points and degrades wayfinding at others.** FIDS and BIDS earn their keep: real-time gate assignments, baggage carousel designation, and IROPS messaging are genuinely dynamic and the static alternative fails operationally. DFW's Passenger360 deployment — 37 screens, multilingual dynamic content selected by incoming flight origin — demonstrates what the system does when properly scoped. Where digital signage degrades the system is in the wayfinding layer: LED brightness competing with overhead directional signage, mode failures producing blank screens at critical junctions, and content management systems that allow operators to populate directional sign real estate with promotional content. A blank screen at the sterile-corridor split is worse than no screen.

6. **The operator loses the signage fight to TSA, airlines, and concessionaires routinely and repeatedly.** TSA's Checkpoint Design Guide establishes four mandatory sign categories — Mandatory, Instructional, Directional, and Local — with the local Federal Security Director as the authority for exceptions. That standard does not coordinate with FAA AC 150/5360-12F or the airport's own wayfinding system. Airlines at common-use gates deploy their own gate-display hardware with their own brand typography. Concessionaires execute sign changes within their lease footprint on their own schedule. When a concessionaire relocates, printed wayfinding maps become wrong overnight. The airport operator sees all of this happen and has contractual levers that are slower than the problem.

7. **Contradiction-debt compounds with every capital project that does not carry wayfinding as a line item.** The IAH Terminal A Signage and Wayfinding project brief documented exactly what accumulates: "multiple families of signs in some locations," "visual clutter with no benefit to the customer," "confusing, lacked consistency, and was outdated." That description matches the pre-renovation state of most post-2000 US hub terminals. Every major capital project that opened a new gate cluster or renovated a concourse without retiring the adjacent legacy signage added to that debt. LAX's wayfinding assessment found "overarching logic" absent despite visual consistency — the signs looked the same but answered different questions by different rules.

8. **The cheap-lever thesis is mostly true and partly wrong in a specific way.** Wayfinding is cheaper than a runway, a baggage system, or a people mover. But treating it as cheap is how you end up with DFW deploying 37 dynamic screens and 26,096 catalogued static signs — the capital cost of the digital layer and the remediation cost of the static inventory are not trivial. The thesis is correct that the gap between high performers and low performers is discipline, not budget. It is wrong if it implies the solution costs nothing to execute well.

## Evidence Section

**Decision-point methodology and ACRP framework:**
ACRP Report 52, *Wayfinding and Signing Guidelines for Airport Terminals and Landside* (2011), establishes the four coverage areas — roadways, parking, curbside, and terminal — and the principle that signs must answer the question being asked at the decision point. The report was developed by Gresham Smith with the Texas Transportation Institute and SEGD. [Source: https://www.greshamsmith.com/projects/acrp-report-52-wayfinding-and-signing-guidelines-for-airport-terminals-and-landside/]

ACRP Report 161, *Guidelines for Improving Airport Services for International Customers* (2016), documents that approximately 34 percent of foreign-language passengers could read only a little or no English, establishing the operational necessity of pictographic and multilingual systems. The research involved interviews with more than 1,000 passengers at eight US gateway airports. [Source: https://www.greshamsmith.com/projects/acrp-report-161-guidelines-for-improving-airport-services-for-international-customers/]

ACRP Research Report 177, *Enhancing Airport Wayfinding for Aging Travelers and Persons with Disabilities* (2018), introduced the Wayfinding Accessibility Audit Checklist and documented that cognitive, sensory, and mobility challenges require wayfinding systems that go beyond ADA minimum compliance. [Source: https://www.greshamsmith.com/projects/acrp-report-177-enhancing-airport-wayfinding-for-aging-travelers-and-persons-with-disabilities/]

**Foundational research:**
Arthur and Passini (1992), *Wayfinding: People, Signs, and Architecture*, defined wayfinding as a problem-solving process with two stages: formulating the route plan and executing the plan. Passini's verbal-report protocol studies, beginning in 1984, established that passengers fail at decision points where signage answers a different question than the one being asked. This is the academic foundation that ACRP Report 52 built upon. [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7677306/]

Per Mollerup's *Wayshowing: A Guide to Environmental Signage* (2005) and the updated *Wayshowing > Wayfinding* (2013) distinguish between wayfinding (what the passenger does cognitively) and wayshowing (what the airport operator provides). Mollerup designed the wayfinding systems for Copenhagen Airport (1989), Oslo Airport (1996), and Stockholm Arlanda (1998). His operational principle — simplicity versus redundancy — directly addresses the accumulation problem US hubs have produced. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Per_Mollerup]

**Schiphol as the reference standard:**
Amsterdam Schiphol's system, established in 1967 and maintained through a 35-year partnership with Bureau Mijksenaar from 1990 forward, uses a four-color hierarchy: yellow for arrivals/departures, blue for commercial facilities, anthracite for waiting areas, and green reserved exclusively for escape routes. The decision to lock green out of commercial use is a governance decision, not a design decision. Any stakeholder who wants to use green for anything other than emergency egress is told no. [Source: https://www.mijksenaar.com/project/amsterdam-schiphol-airport/]

**Munich eye-tracking validation:**
Munich Airport used Tobii eye-tracking glasses to study passenger engagement along key routes, measuring visibility, attention, and comprehension. The study found passengers ignored signage not relevant to their journey. The airport adjusted floor signage and validated the adjustment with follow-up measurement. This is decision-point methodology applied operationally, not as a planning exercise. [Source: https://www.tobii.com/resource-center/customer-stories/munich-airport-wayfinding-study]

**DFW signage inventory and digital deployment:**
Arora Engineers' signage planning assessment at DFW documented 26,096 signs across facilities and roadways, identifying outdated Skylink vehicle and station signage, misalignment between terminal construction modifications and curbside signage, and the absence of a centralized management database. The recommended remediation included ArcGIS integration and a comprehensive maintenance protocol. [Source: https://www.aroraengineers.com/project/dfw-professional-services-signage-planning-assessment-design/]

DFW's Passenger360 multilingual deployment drives 37 screens with content selected dynamically based on incoming flight origin, displaying up to four languages simultaneously with animated graphics. DFW received the 2022 Airport Service Quality award for best airport serving over 40 million passengers in North America. The program addressed the specific failure of static, legacy digital signage in customs and baggage reclaim areas. [Source: https://www.aviationpros.com/airports/signage/article/53061278/dfw-uses-passenger360-for-multilingual-wayfinding]

**IAH Terminal A documented contradiction-debt:**
The Houston Airport System's Terminal A Signage and Wayfinding project brief directly documented the legacy problem as justification: the existing system was "confusing, lacked consistency, and was outdated with multiple families of signs in some locations" producing "visual clutter with no benefit to the customer." This is a direct quote from a public procurement document, not a consultant characterization. [Source: https://fly2houston.com/airport-business/business-partnerships/contracting/solicitations/details/terminal-ab-garage-signage-wayfinding-hwc-abway-2021-008/proposers/]

**DCA 2022 renaming — the transition as case study:**
More than 1,000 signs were swapped during the June 2022 DCA terminal and gate renaming. The immediate trigger was Project Journey's Concourse E opening with the letter "E" blacked out on gate signs — an interim state that created active passenger confusion. The renaming replaced Terminals A and B/C with Terminals 1 and 2, added concourse letters A through E before gate numbers, and renamed parking garages to correspond. The transition required airlines to update boarding passes and flight information screens on a coordinated schedule. MWAA's own reporting described it as aligning with "navigation standards found at many other airports worldwide." [Source: https://dcist.com/story/22/05/31/dca-national-airport-renaming-terminals-gates/] [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/reagan-national-airport-enhance-gate-and-terminal-signage]

**IAD Concourse D access:**
Concourse D sits outside the AeroTrain network. Passengers reach it via mobile lounge from the Main Terminal or by walking from Concourse C — approximately 1,600 feet. The mobile lounge boarding location is near Gates D5–D8 at a mid-concourse transfer node. Current airport guidance acknowledges that "the route choice between mobile lounge versus AeroTrain plus walking remains the core map skill" for passengers touching Concourse D. The airport advises passengers not to hesitate to ask an employee for routing help — which is an acknowledgment that the static sign system does not reliably answer the question. [Source: https://airportmaphq.com/dulles-airport-map-terminal.html]

**LAX wayfinding assessment:**
Gresham Smith's assessment of LAX identified the core failure: visual consistency without operational logic. Signs maintained the same graphic standard while lacking "overarching logic" in the wayfinding hierarchy. The assessment found non-intuitive international-to-domestic connection paths and inconsistent terminal and gate numbering. The field methodology used was "Look, Listen and Learn" — observing passenger behavior at decision points rather than evaluating sign panels in isolation. [Source: https://www.greshamsmith.com/projects/los-angeles-international-airport-lax-wayfinding-assessment-of-future-projects/]

**Staff query rate as operational proxy:**
One study found that airport information-desk staff spend approximately 80 percent of their time answering directional questions. A 2023 SITA study found that digital signs reduced service counter questions by nearly two-thirds in deployments. Amsterdam Schiphol and Changi both reported measurable drops in help-desk query volume following multilingual digital signage implementation. These are the operational metrics that translate wayfinding performance into staffing cost. [Source: https://www.mappedin.com/resources/blog/airport-signage-guide/]

**TSA checkpoint signage authority:**
TSA's Checkpoint Design Guide (Revision 4.0) establishes four sign categories — Mandatory, Instructional, Directional, and Local — with Local signs requiring coordination with the local Federal Security Director. The FAA AC 150/5360-12F and TSA CDG operate as parallel frameworks without a formal conflict-resolution hierarchy. The practical result is that checkpoint-adjacent wayfinding is often the worst-performing segment of the terminal sign system precisely because two authorities share the space and neither has explicit precedence in the wayfinding hierarchy. [Source: https://airportscouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Checkpoint_Design_Guide_CDG_Rev_4_0.pdf]

**MWAA Design Manual:**
MWAA's 2020 Design Manual includes separate Airport Standards and Signing Guidelines volumes for DCA (6.36 MB) and IAD (11.7 MB). IAD's document uses the 'Saarinen' typeface designed for the airport. The existence of two separate sign guidelines documents for two airports under one authority is itself a governance data point — it means any future standardization effort must reconcile two existing systems rather than build from one. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/business/airports-authority-design-manual]

## Operational Case For the Thesis

The thesis holds strongest where it meets operational reality: **wayfinding governance failures are cheap to create and expensive to undo.**

The DCA renaming required swapping more than 1,000 signs across a single mid-sized airport to correct a naming inconsistency that had accumulated over decades of incremental decisions. IAH Terminal A required a full procurement action to document and remediate what multiple project closeouts had deposited. DFW required a 26,096-sign inventory before it could write a remediation plan. None of these were expensive per-sign; the expense was in the accumulated scope and the operational disruption of executing change in an active building.

The discipline argument is strongest in IROPS. When gates change en masse during a ground stop, the operator's only real-time communication tools are FIDS and gate agents. The static sign system cannot update. If the FIDS system has been allowed to carry promotional content in directional real estate, or if mode failures produce blank screens, the operator has no fallback. The airports that perform well during IROPS are the ones that treat FIDS as a safety system, not a revenue channel — which is a governance decision made years before the event.

The multilingual case is also strong from the operator's chair. ACRP Report 161 documented 34 percent non-English literacy among foreign passengers. US hubs handle significant international volumes. DFW's Passenger360 deployment addressed the direct operational failure — international passengers unable to self-navigate customs and baggage claim — with a system that costs a fraction of the staff augmentation alternative.

The highest-value intervention the thesis points to is earlier in the project cycle than the design phase: **at the point when a capital project scope is written**, the operator must specify that wayfinding integration is a deliverable, not a punch-list item. The LAX pattern — visual consistency without wayfinding logic — happens when architects control the sign package without operator-defined performance criteria against which the system will be tested. Munich's eye-tracking validation is not an innovation; it is what operational acceptance testing looks like for a sign system, and US airports almost universally skip it.

## Operational Case Against the Thesis

**The honest steelman is that wayfinding discipline requires sustained organizational will that airports structurally struggle to maintain.**

Schiphol's 35-year partnership with Mijksenaar works because Schiphol is a single-site authority with a consolidated commercial and operational leadership structure. It does not negotiate wayfinding decisions with a tenant airline council, a federal security director, a concessionaire with a 30-year lease, and a host jurisdiction with overlay authority. US hub airports operate in exactly that environment. The governance recommendation — one authority, one standard, veto power over stakeholder additions — runs directly into the legal and contractual architecture of how US airports are built and tenanted.

TSA is not a negotiating partner on checkpoint sign placement; it is a federal authority with independent legal standing in the terminal. Airlines at exclusive-use gates have lease agreements that pre-date any new wayfinding standard. Concessionaires have design rights within their footprints. The airport operator can write the best sign standard in North America and still be unable to enforce it in the 40 percent of the terminal footprint controlled by someone else.

The digital wayfinding layer introduces its own operational risk. A FIDS screen showing the wrong gate is worse than no screen — it actively misdirects. A blank screen at a critical decision point during IROPS is a failure that no static backup exists to cover. The capital cost of digital wayfinding at scale (hardware, CMS licenses, content management staffing, integration maintenance) is not trivial; one honest look at the lifecycle cost of a major digital signage deployment compared with a well-maintained static system will not always favor digital.

The thesis also understates how much of the wayfinding problem comes from architecture, not signs. IAD's Concourse D gap is a people-mover design decision made in 2003; no sign resolves the fact that AeroTrain does not go there. DCA's layout complexity is a 1941 building augmented in ways that create physical wayfinding constraints no sign system can fully overcome. Wayfinding is not the cheapest lever when the constraint is structural.

Finally, the comparison airports the thesis uses — SIN, ZRH, MUC, HEL — are greenfield or near-greenfield single-terminal designs built in compressed timeframes under unified authorities. They did not inherit 80 years of accumulated sign decisions made by 15 different stakeholders under three different regulatory frameworks. The discipline gap is real; the conditions under which that discipline was achieved are not transferable wholesale to a legacy US hub.

## Five Specific Examples

### 1. DCA Gate E Blackout — The Signature of Phased Openings Without Sign Governance

When DCA's Concourse E opened as part of Project Journey, new gate signs had the letter "E" physically blocked out before the gate number. The concourse was open; the sign system had not been updated. Passengers at Gate E10 were at a gate where the sign said "10" — the same format as gates in Terminal 1 that also began with numerals. This was not a cosmetic problem: it was an active wayfinding ambiguity in a terminal where Terminal 1 and Terminal 2 share a security processing area.

The fix required the 2022 renaming exercise — more than 1,000 signs, coordinated airline boarding pass and FIDS updates, phased roadway and transit signage changes over a week. The root cause was a capital closeout process that handed operations a new concourse with a sign system that contradicted the existing one. Operations did not fail the test; the project did not include the test.

**Operator lesson:** Sign system integration is a commissioning criterion, not a punch-list item. The question "does this new facility's sign system answer the same questions in the same format as the adjacent existing facility?" must be answered before ribbon-cutting, not six months after.

### 2. IAD Concourse D — The Structural Wayfinding Gap That Signs Cannot Fix

Concourse D has no AeroTrain station. The people-mover system that opened in 2010 connects the Main Terminal to Concourses A, B, and C. Concourse D is served by mobile lounge from the Main Terminal, with boarding at a mid-concourse transfer node near Gates D5–D8, or by a roughly 1,600-foot walk from Concourse C.

The current signage guidance acknowledges the problem by advising passengers to ask an airport employee if confused. That is a formal acknowledgment that the physical plant creates a wayfinding problem the sign system cannot resolve — the question "how do I get to Gate D3?" has three valid answers depending on the passenger's starting point, time available, and physical mobility, and the sign system cannot present conditional routing.

During IROPS, when flights move from A/B concourses to D concourse or vice versa, this becomes a category-one passenger movement problem. Passengers who used the AeroTrain successfully for their inbound connection cannot use it for the IROPS-driven gate reassignment.

**Operator lesson:** Any capital investment in IAD wayfinding must address the Concourse D modal split as an explicit design constraint, not a note in the appendix. The sign system that works for a normal day operating pattern fails predictably on any day with significant gate reassignments touching Concourse D.

### 3. DFW's 26,096 Signs — What Happens When No One Owns the Inventory

Arora Engineers' signage planning assessment found 26,096 signs across DFW's facilities and roadways. The count itself is the finding. A sign system with 26,096 elements that cannot be managed without a GIS-assisted mobile device inventory is not a system — it is an accumulation. The assessment also found that the Skylink vehicle and station signage was "outdated and does not integrate current technology advancements in digital and dynamic wayfinding," curbside signage was misaligned with terminal construction modifications, and there was no centralized database.

The remediation recommendation was ArcGIS integration and a comprehensive maintenance protocol — in other words, the first step is knowing what you have. DFW is one of the best-resourced airports in North America. If its sign inventory reached this state, the thesis that governance is the failure mode rather than budget is correct.

**Operator lesson:** A sign inventory database is not a technology project; it is a governance project. The database only exists if someone owns the standard and has authority to enforce it. The database is the instrument of that authority.

### 4. Munich Airport Eye-Tracking Study — What Operational Validation Actually Looks Like

Munich Airport used Tobii eye-tracking glasses to study how passengers engaged with signage along key routes. The study measured three things: whether the sign was positioned where it could be seen (visibility), whether passengers looked at it (attention), and whether they correctly interpreted it (comprehension). The finding that passengers ignored signage not relevant to their journey led to specific adjustments in floor signage placement.

This is not a research project. This is operational acceptance testing for a sign system. The equivalent at a US hub almost never happens. Signs are evaluated by the design team for compliance with the standard, not by operations for functional performance at decision points with real passengers navigating in real time.

The Munich methodology is directly applicable to a MWAA standard refresh: instrument two or three key decision points at IAD and DCA, run a 60-minute passenger observation session, and document where passengers stop, look around, backtrack, or ask an employee. The results will locate the specific failures more precisely than any design review can.

**Operator lesson:** Performance criteria for a sign system must include observational validation, not just design compliance. The deliverable for a wayfinding project is not a sign package that meets the standard — it is passengers successfully navigating at decision points.

### 5. The Checkpoint-Adjacent Sign Vacuum — Where TSA Authority and Airport Authority Diverge

The security checkpoint is the single highest-density decision point in any terminal. Passengers exiting the checkpoint must orient to concourse direction, gate number range, restrooms, FIDS, and — in terminals with split sterile-zone configurations like DCA — which concourse they are in. This is the moment of highest cognitive demand and lowest environmental orientation cue, because the checkpoint area is designed around security throughput, not wayfinding.

TSA's Checkpoint Design Guide classifies signs into four categories with Local signs requiring FSD coordination. The FAA AC 150/5360-12F addresses terminal wayfinding separately. There is no formal hierarchy establishing which authority prevails when TSA instructional signage and airport directional signage conflict for wall and ceiling real estate adjacent to the checkpoint exit. In practice, TSA gets what TSA needs and the airport wayfinding system works around what is left.

At DCA post-Project Journey, passengers exit new security halls into National Hall with sight lines to expanded concession space. The wayfinding logic — which of the five concourses am I in, and which direction are my gates — competes with both the physical orientation of the security exit and the visual noise of the expanded retail footprint. This is not a sign problem that can be solved with better typography; it requires a governance conversation between MWAA, TSA, and the concession operator about what information the checkpoint-exit environment is responsible for delivering, in what priority order.

**Operator lesson:** The checkpoint exit is the most important wayfinding moment in the terminal and the one where the operator has the least unilateral authority. Any serious wayfinding standard must address it explicitly and must include a protocol for coordinating with TSA at the design phase, not in post-occupancy remediation.

## Bottom Line for the Council

The thesis is operationally correct on the governance argument. The airports that wayfind well made a single decision — one authority, defined performance criteria, veto power over stakeholder additions — and held it over time. The airports that wayfind poorly made no single decision; they accumulated the preferences of everyone who had a stakeholder interest and called the result a system.

The thesis is operationally incomplete on the cost argument. Wayfinding is cheaper than a runway. It is not cheap when the accumulated contradiction-debt requires 1,000-sign remediation exercises, 26,000-sign inventory projects, and FIDS deployments with seven-figure lifecycle costs. The correct framing is not "wayfinding is cheap" — it is "wayfinding governance is cheap, and wayfinding remediation is expensive." The difference between those two states is the governance decision made or not made at the beginning.

For MWAA specifically: IAD and DCA each have their own design manual volumes. There is no unified standard. The 2022 DCA renaming corrected a symptom. The Concourse D gap at IAD is structural. The capital program now underway at both airports is the best available opportunity to establish a single forward standard with explicit performance criteria before another generation of capital projects deposits another layer of contradiction-debt on top of what is already there.

The Monday 5:45 AM push does not care about the design standard on the shelf. It cares about whether the sign at the bottom of the escalator answers the question the passenger is asking before they reach the bottom, in the language they read, with enough visibility lead time to avoid a midstream direction change that backs up the escalator. That is the test. Most of what is on the shelf does not pass it.
