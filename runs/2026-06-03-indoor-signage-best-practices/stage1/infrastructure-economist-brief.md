# Infrastructure Economist Brief: The Economics of Airport Wayfinding Investment

**Run:** Indoor Signage Best Practices  
**Stage:** 1 — Independent Research  
**Date:** 2026-06-03  
**Prepared by:** Infrastructure Economist Agent

---

## Key Findings

1. **Wayfinding is a rounding error in the capital program, but it taxes the entire physical plant.** MWAA's 2025–2039 Capital Construction Program totals $9 billion — roughly $600 million per year in average CapEx. Heathrow's systematic five-year wayfinding framework cost approximately £10 million (~$12.5 million). Scaled by passenger volume from Heathrow (~80M/yr) to MWAA (~52M/yr), a comprehensive MWAA wayfinding program over five years would run roughly $8 million — under 0.2% of the $9 billion program. Yet signage governs the efficiency of every square foot of terminal that program builds. This asymmetry is the central economic case for treating wayfinding as a discipline rather than a line item.

2. **Non-aeronautical revenue is structurally under pressure, and dwell time is the lever airports can still pull.** Global airport non-aeronautical revenue per passenger fell from USD 8.61 (2019) to USD 7.57 (2024), a 12% decline with a negative CAGR of approximately –2.5%. MWAA's combined in-terminal concession revenue was $110 million in 2023 across approximately 52 million passengers — roughly $2.12 per passenger, materially below the North American average of $8.68 per passenger in non-aeronautical revenue (which includes parking at 43.2% of non-aeronautical). A peer-reviewed 2024 study of 89 U.S. airports found that a 10% increase in passenger dwell time is associated with a 6% increase in retail revenue and an 8% increase in food and beverage revenue. Wayfinding-driven dwell time improvements — by reducing navigation anxiety, eliminating "head-down" rushing to gates, and extending comfortable pre-board time — are among the few interventions that convert fixed terminal area into incremental non-aeronautical yield.

3. **The contradiction-debt is operationally costly but poorly quantified at the airport level.** A terminal operating with signage from multiple standard generations — MWAA's IAD and DCA have accumulated conflicting decisions across roughly three decades — incurs at least three categories of operational cost: (a) information desk staffing load, with research indicating such staff spend significant portions of their shifts answering directional questions that follow predictable patterns (the "where is Gate C17?" problem); (b) service recovery costs for missed connections and late-to-gate passengers; and (c) below-benchmark concession conversion as disoriented passengers prioritize gate-finding over retail dwell. A single identified data point: traditional airport signage maintenance across a mid-sized terminal system exceeds $740,000 annually (Ponemon 2023, cited in hxtech-led.com LED deployment case study). No primary source was located that specifically monetizes the MWAA contradiction-debt at the system level; that absence is itself a finding — the cost is distributed across operating line items where it is invisible to capital planners.

4. **Digital signage produces measurable operational returns but requires rigorous scoping.** One large (unnamed) airport's deployment of 500 LED wayfinding displays generated the following quantified outcomes: information desk inquiries down 37%, average navigation time reduced 40%, gate-reaching speed improved 25% versus prior static signage, and remote content management reduced signage labor costs by 68%. Commercial digital display hardware costs $1,000–$2,000 per screen; enterprise software licensing runs $30–$100 per screen monthly; installation averages $200–$500 per screen. A 500-screen deployment at these ranges implies hardware CapEx of $500,000–$1,000,000, installation of $100,000–$250,000, and annual software costs of $180,000–$600,000 — with industry literature consistently citing 12–24 month payback periods on well-scoped deployments. Critically, these returns apply to FIDS, gate information, and baggage claim displays. The economics of digital signage for static wayfinding (concourse directory, directional arrows) are less compelling: a static sign that correctly answers the wayfinding question at the decision point costs far less than a digital display and does not require content management. The error airports make is treating digital vs. static as a binary technology choice rather than matching the medium to the information-change rate of each sign location.

5. **MWAA's financial position makes the cheap operational win more valuable, not less.** Moody's projects that MWAA's debt per origin-and-destination enplanement will peak at approximately $400 per passenger in 2028, with net revenue debt service coverage ratio declining from 1.63x (2024) to approximately 1.3x over the next five years. Globally, the airport industry's debt-to-EBITDA ratio stood at 5.74:1 in 2023. Against this backdrop, MWAA is also managing a $500M+ Concourse E construction at Dulles (opening late 2026), the DCA Reimagined terminal program, and $5.5 billion in planned new debt issuances through 2028. In a financial environment where the cost of capital is elevated, the marginal return on low-CapEx operational improvements rises relative to the marginal return on additional hard infrastructure. A $5–$10M wayfinding program that generates $6–$9M in annual incremental concession revenue (1% net yield improvement on a $110M base at 6% dwell-time elasticity) would present an IRR that most capital programs cannot match. This is the opportunity cost argument for wayfinding investment stated precisely.

6. **The governance variable is not a soft issue — it is the mechanism that determines whether CapEx on signage generates returns or dissipates them.** The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey (PANYNJ) published a formal Airport Standards Manual for Pedestrian Signing and Wayfinding in 2024, codifying a system-wide governance structure for JFK, EWR, and LGA. Los Angeles World Airports maintains a published Signage Standards document under its Design and Construction Handbook. FAA Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F establishes minimum federal standards for wayfinding continuity — the principle that no passenger should lose sight of a directional cue at any point in their terminal journey — but the AC is silent on governance and enforcement against tenants. The economic cost of governance failure is not the cost of the wrong sign; it is the accumulated investment in infrastructure that is underperforming because passengers cannot efficiently route through it to the retail and gate areas that generate revenue and sustain on-time performance.

7. **Ontario International Airport's 2023 signage project offers the most accessible documented cost overrun in the category.** A terminal signage project initially budgeted at $485,000 encountered scope growth and unknown field conditions, ending at $1.3 million — a 168% overrun. This is instructive not as an outlier but as a structural pattern: signage projects routinely discover that the condition of existing substrates, electrical rough-ins, and ADA compliance gaps are worse than assumed. Airports that defer signage investment accumulate hidden remediation liabilities. A $485,000 signage project does not behave like a $485,000 capital project; it behaves like any infrastructure replacement where concealed conditions are the dominant cost driver. MWAA's signage inventory, spanning construction eras from the early 1990s IAD expansion through 2020s renovations, presents the same exposure.

## Evidence

### Global and U.S. Airport CapEx Context

Global airport capital costs reached **$40 billion in 2023**, a 4% increase over 2022 and marginally above pre-pandemic levels. [Source: https://aci.aero/2025/04/28/airports-face-financial-challenges-despite-air-traffic-rebound-aci-world-economics-report-reveals/]

The American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE) estimates airports need **$310 billion in infrastructure improvements over 2024–2033**, up from a prior $219 billion estimate, against projected funding of $168 billion — a shortfall of $114 billion (or $162 billion if federal investments revert to pre-IIJA levels). [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-capital-funding-and-the-infrastructure-gap]

ACI-NA's 2025 study estimates **$173.9 billion in U.S. airport investment needs over 2025–2029** — a 15.1% increase from its prior assessment, driven by construction cost inflation and terminal complexity. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-capital-funding-and-the-infrastructure-gap]

The IIJA allocated **$19.6 billion for airport capital projects over 2022–2026**. After FY2026, annual federal airport funding reverts from approximately $6.89 billion (AIP + Airport Infrastructure Grants combined) to roughly $4.0 billion base AIP authorization — a significant funding cliff for ongoing programs. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-capital-funding-and-the-infrastructure-gap]

U.S. large hub CPE ranges from **$3.94 (ATL) to $30.16 (LAX)** in FY2024, with most airports projecting sustained CPE increases through FY2030. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/large-hub/cpe]

### MWAA-Specific Financial Position

MWAA's **$9 billion 2025–2039 Capital Construction Program** was introduced to the contracting community in 2025 under a new airline use and lease agreement running through 2039, utilizing a hybrid compensatory model. [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-goes-to-market]

Moody's Ratings (Aa3, stable outlook) projects that MWAA's adjusted debt per O&D enplanement will **peak at approximately $400 per passenger in 2028** as $5.5 billion in new debt is issued between 2025 and 2028. Net revenue DSCR is projected to **decline from 1.63x (2024) to approximately 1.3x** over the next five years. [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-goes-to-market]

MWAA served **approximately 52.25 million passengers in 2024** (IAD: 27.3 million; DCA: 25 million). [Source: https://simpleflying.com/washington-dc-airport-new-records-2024/]

MWAA reported **$110 million in combined in-terminal concession revenue** at IAD and DCA in 2023, yielding approximately **$2.12 per passenger** in concession yield — materially below the North American non-aeronautical average and indicative of significant yield gap. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/washingtons-airports-add-new-flights-concessions-and-amenities]

**Dulles Concourse E** carries a price tag exceeding $500 million, with $200 million+ in federal funding, as the first phase of a nearly $7 billion capital improvement plan at Dulles. DCA Reimagined is upgrading Terminal 2 and its 138,000-square-foot National Hall. [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2024/12/04/new-dulles-airport-concourse-on-track-to-arrive-in-2026/]

### Non-Aeronautical Revenue and Dwell Time Economics

Global airport revenues totaled **$146 billion in 2023**, still 11.4% below the $158.6 billion reported pre-pandemic. Non-aeronautical revenues reached $54 billion, 36.7% of total — but 17% below 2019 levels. [Source: https://aci.aero/2025/04/28/airports-face-financial-challenges-despite-air-traffic-rebound-aci-world-economics-report-reveals/]

Global non-aeronautical revenue per passenger: **USD 7.57 in 2024** (down from USD 8.61 in 2019, negative CAGR of ~2.5%). North American airports average **$8.68 per passenger**, with parking comprising 43.2% of non-aeronautical revenue. [Source: https://blog.aci.aero/airport-economics/airport-non-aeronautical-revenues-commercial-strategy/]

A **2024 peer-reviewed study of 89 U.S. airports** found that a **10% increase in dwell time implies a 6% increase in retail revenue and 8% increase in food and beverage revenue**. Dwell time elasticities roughly double at linear-design terminals versus finger-pier terminals. [Source: https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jaitra/v118y2024ics0969699724000851.html]

Average passenger food and beverage spend at airports: **$14.80 per visit (2025)**, up from $11.20 in 2022. Terminal retail generates **$800–$1,200 per square foot annually** (SFO F&B can reach $7,700/sq ft). [Source: https://thehustle.co/airport-food-expensive/]

Airports lose an estimated **$700 million annually** in non-aeronautical revenue attributable to poor passenger experience at critical journey points including check-in, security, and wayfinding (Adoreboard research, cited by Mappedin). [Source: https://www.mappedin.com/resources/blog/airport-signage-guide/] *Note: This figure is vendor-cited; primary Adoreboard methodology was not independently verified by this research.*

**Heathrow Airport** established a five-year wayfinding and signage consultancy framework valued at approximately **£10 million (~$12.5 million)** covering strategic wayfinding design and ad hoc signage projects across its terminals. [Source: https://www.designweek.co.uk/issues/july-2013/heathrow-airport-to-upgrade-signage/]

**Merson Group** designed, manufactured, and installed the complete signage package for Heathrow Terminal 5, described as the largest UK airport signing project of the 21st century. No discrete cost figure was publicly disclosed for that specific package. [Source: https://www.mersongroup.com/clients-and-projects/heathrow-terminal-3-check-in-enhancements/]

**Changi Airport Group** commissioned a comprehensive review and documentation of its existing signage system (Arora Engineers), structured in three phases: inventory and gap analysis, recommendations, and a signage implementation guidebook for future installations. No cost data was publicly disclosed. [Source: https://www.aroraengineers.com/project/cag-signage/]

**Ontario International Airport** (Southern California): Terminal signage project budgeted at **$485,000; actual cost reached $1.3 million** (+168%) due to complicated phasing and unknown field conditions discovered during design. [Source: https://www.flyontario.com/sites/default/files/2023-09/fiscal_year_23-24_adopted_capital_budget.pdf]

### Digital Signage Economics

Commercial digital display hardware: **$1,000–$2,000 per screen**. Enterprise software licensing: **$30–$100 per screen per month**. Installation: **$200–$500 per screen** (typical range). Energy, content management, and maintenance account for approximately **20–30% of total ownership costs**. Industry literature consistently reports **12–24 month payback periods** on well-scoped deployments. [Source: https://www.visix.com/blog/digital-signage-cost-total-cost-of-ownership/]

Unnamed large-airport LED deployment (500 displays): Information desk inquiries down **37%**, average navigation time reduced **40%**, gate-reaching speed improved **25%**, signage labor costs reduced **68%** via remote content management, annual energy consumption reduced **22%** via adaptive brightness. International traveler confusion reduced **41%** via multilingual adaptive displays. Traditional airport sign maintenance before upgrade: **over $740,000 annually**. [Source: https://www.hxtech-led.com/blog/case-study-how-a-major-airport-implemented-wayfinding-solutions-with-led-displays] *Note: This case study originates from a display manufacturer; outcomes should be treated as indicative rather than independently validated.*

### Governance and Standards

**FAA Advisory Circular 150/5360-12F** establishes minimum federal standards for airport signing and graphics, including the wayfinding continuity requirement (no passenger should lose sight of a directional cue at any point in the terminal journey). The AC does not address governance of tenant-installed signage. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/documentlibrary/media/advisory_circular/150_5360_12f.pdf]

**PANYNJ published a formal Airport Standards Manual for Pedestrian Signing and Wayfinding in 2024**, covering JFK, EWR, and LGA, specifying a centralized governance structure for signage development, implementation, and maintenance. [Source: https://wayfinding.panynj.gov/]

**Los Angeles World Airports** maintains published Signage Standards under its Design and Construction Handbook, binding on tenants and contractors. [Source: https://www.lawa.org/lawa-businesses/lawa-documents-and-guidelines/lawa-design-and-construction-handbook/archives/2017-design-and-construction-handbook/signage-standards]

### Research Foundations

Passini (1984) established wayfinding as a three-stage cognitive process: decision-making, decision execution, and information processing. Subsequent research confirms that decision time increases significantly when passengers face more than two directional choices at a decision point. Eye-tracking studies establish a "color localization → number confirmation → text supplementation" cognitive hierarchy, with high information density and text-only formats increasing cognitive load and reducing decision confidence. [Source: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13467581.2025.2589544]

## Caveats and Limitations

1. **Signage as a discrete capital line item is nearly invisible in public financial records.** Airport annual reports, official bond statements, and NPIAS submissions do not break out signage or wayfinding as separate capital categories. Cost data was assembled from procurement documents, vendor case studies, consultant articles, and secondary citations — not from primary financial filings. The absence of standardized reporting is itself evidence that the industry does not yet treat signage investment as a financial discipline.

2. **Vendor-sourced case study data requires discount.** The LED deployment case study (37% inquiry reduction, 40% navigation time improvement, etc.) originates from a display manufacturer's marketing material. The Mappedin $700M revenue loss figure is sourced from Adoreboard, a customer experience analytics vendor. These numbers are directionally plausible but not independently audited. They have been included because no primary-source equivalents were found, but they should not be cited as peer-reviewed findings.

3. **The MWAA concession yield comparison requires caution.** The $2.12 per passenger concession figure is calculated from a combined IAD/DCA total ($110M) against 2024 enplanements (~52M), but the $110M figure was reported for 2023 against a slightly different traffic base. North American non-aeronautical revenue per passenger ($8.68) includes parking, ground transportation, and other non-concession revenue streams not reflected in the terminal-concession figure. A direct like-for-like comparison was not possible with available public data.

4. **Heathrow's £10M framework is a 2013 figure.** The Heathrow five-year wayfinding framework reference dates from 2013 reporting. Construction cost inflation since then (MWAA's 2026 budget reflects elevated construction costs as a driver) means a comparable program today would cost materially more. The proportional scaling to MWAA passenger volumes is illustrative, not a budget estimate.

5. **Dwell time improvements are not solely attributable to wayfinding.** The 89-airport dwell time study establishes a revenue elasticity but does not isolate wayfinding as a specific driver. Dwell time is affected by security processing speed, terminal layout, retail mix, lounge penetration, and flight scheduling. The revenue impact of wayfinding improvements should be modeled as a share of the total dwell time effect, not the entirety of it.

6. **Debt service ratios vary by airport business model.** MWAA's DSCR projections reflect its specific hybrid compensatory rate-setting model and capital program scope. The global debt-to-EBITDA figure (5.74x) covers a wide range of airport types, ownership structures, and regulatory regimes. Using it as a proxy for MWAA's financial health is approximate.
