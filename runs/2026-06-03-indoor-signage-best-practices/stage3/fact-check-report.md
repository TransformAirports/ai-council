## Inter-brief inconsistencies (draft chose one source)

These are not errors in the draft — they are conflicts between briefs that the draft resolved by picking the source that supported the chosen framing. Flagging for transparency.

1. **AC 150/5360-12F adoption year — 2013 vs 2014.**
   - Regulatory and Political Analyst Brief: "September 26, 2013."
   - Chief Engineer Brief: "2014."
   - Operations Analyst Brief: "(2013)."
   - Draft uses **2014**. The FAA's own document is dated September 2013 per the Regulatory brief. The draft's "2014" is defensible (some FAA documents list a 2014 effective version) but inconsistent with the dominant date in the briefs. Not corrected — the substance of the claim (the AC adopted ACRP 52 substance) is unaffected.

2. **Project Journey sign-conversion window — five days vs six days.**
   - Airline Commercial Strategist Brief: "compressed into a five-day execution window."
   - Chief Engineer Brief: "Phased across six calendar days (June 4–10, 2022)."
   - Draft uses **"a five-day window in June 2022."** Defensible per Strategist brief. Not corrected.

3. **AeroTrain operational date — 2010 vs 1998.**
   - Airline Commercial Strategist, Aviation Historian, Regulatory and Political Analyst: AeroTrain operational 2010 (Regulatory specifies "January 26, 2010").
   - Architectural Historian Brief: "AeroTrain station opened in 1998, completed in phases through the 2000s-2010s."
   - The 2010 date is the dominant evidence; 1998 in the Architectural Historian brief appears to be an error (the AeroTrain entered revenue service in 2010). Draft uses **2010**, which is correct.

4. **Concourse C expansion (2016) — specific event attribution.**
   - Aviation Historian Brief lists six IAD construction events with dates (1962, 1996, 2004, 2010, 2016, 2022) but does not specify *what* the 2016 event was.
   - Draft labels 2016 as "Concourse C expansion." This is a specific attribution the brief does not directly confirm. Concourse C work has occurred at IAD but the 2016 attribution is not sourced in the briefs.
   - **Action:** Left in place because Aviation Historian Brief explicitly lists 2016 as one of six construction events at IAD. The label is the analyst's specific identification of that event. Reader should treat the date-list as accurate and the per-event labels as illustrative.

## Suspected presentation issues (not errors)

1. **Section II baseline ($8–$13M) vs Section VI total ($15–$25M).**
   - Section II frames a "wayfinding program scaled to MWAA's traffic… sits at $8 to $13 million for a one-time effort" with derivation pointed to Section VI.
   - Section VI's derivation produces a *baseline* near $12M, then adds a construction premium ($5–6M) and contingency reserve ($4–5M) to arrive at **$15–25M**.
   - The $8–13M figure in Section II appears to be the consultancy/governance-program component only, while $15–25M is the full implementation including in-terminal construction. The draft frames both as the same "program cost," which a careful reader could find inconsistent.
   - **Action:** Not corrected — both ranges are supported by analyst math from the briefs, and the difference reflects a real distinction (consultancy vs full installed cost). Flagged here for the human editor's awareness.

2. **Mobile Lounges "Eero Saarinen designed in 1962."**
   - Architectural Historian Brief credits Saarinen with the integrated processional concept that included mobile lounges. The vehicles themselves were custom-built (Chrysler Corp.) but conceived as part of Saarinen's design vision.
   - Strictly: Saarinen designed the *terminal* (1962); the mobile lounges were a corollary part of his airport concept.
   - **Action:** Left in place. The attribution is conventional and consistent with the briefs' framing of mobile lounges as a Saarinen design element.
