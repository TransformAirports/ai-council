# Chief Engineer Brief — The MWAA Contracting Manual as a Capital-Delivery Instrument

**Lens:** 25 years of airport program delivery — constructability, lifecycle cost, delivery-method selection, change-order and claims machinery, interface risk, and the megaproject failure modes I have watched repeat. I read the Contracting Manual (Revised Sixth Edition 6.0, effective 5/20/2026) the way I read a phasing plan: not "is it legal," but "will it let us build the thing on a live airfield without the schedule eating the contingency."

**Where my default stance conflicts with the run thesis — stated up front.** My reflex is to defend approval gates. The first schedule is always wrong, every project is harder than the feasibility study assumed, and most controls in this manual were written in blood — Silver Line Phase 2, the Big Dig, Denver's Great Hall. So I am the wrong agent to argue "cut the red tape." My finding is narrower and, I think, more defensible: MWAA's manual already contains a modern delivery toolkit. The problem is not the menu of controls; it is that the controls are **timed to procurement events, not to the velocity of an active construction site**, and that the single highest-leverage engineering decision — which delivery method to use — is left to ad hoc judgment with no framework. That is a capability gap, not a compliance gap.

---

## 1. Key Findings

1. **The delivery toolkit is genuinely current. Do not let the report claim otherwise.** Chapter 6 already authorizes CMAR, bridging design-build, progressive design-build, two-phase design-build, and Multiple Award Construction Contracts (MACCs) [Source: Contracting Manual v6.0, §6.2.1]. A 2026 manual that names progressive design-build by name is ahead of most public owners. The reform target is *how* these tools get selected and governed — not whether they exist.

2. **The manual never tells anyone how to choose a delivery method — the costliest decision in any capital program.** CMAR and design-build may be used "when the Contracting Officer and COTR determine that such a delivery method is in the Authority's best interests" subject to VP/OSCM approval [Source: §6.2.1]. That is the entire selection logic. There is no constructability test, no risk-allocation matrix, no schedule-criticality screen. Delivery-method choice is the variable most correlated with cost and schedule growth; leaving it to undocumented judgment is the gap a chief engineer flags first.

3. **The manual's collaborative-delivery provisions sit on top of an adversarial claims posture, and the two fight each other.** Progressive design-build and CMAR work because the owner and builder open the books early and negotiate a guaranteed maximum price as design matures. But the disputes chapter is built for arm's-length, fixed-price, claims-defensive contracting — total-cost and anticipatory-profit claims are flatly prohibited, jury trials waived, litigation venue fixed in Virginia [Source: §7.2–7.4]. Those are good DBB controls. Bolted under a PDB job, they signal a default of distrust where the method needs trust.

4. **The $3,000,000 Board reservation puts the Board in the critical path of routine capital execution.** The Board reserves approval of any contract at or above $3M [Source: §1.4.1]. For a multi-billion-dollar capital authority, $3M is a rounding error — nearly every meaningful construction package and a large share of material change orders will clear it. On a live job, a change order that waits for a Board cycle is a change order that idles a crew. I cannot verify the statutory or historical basis for the $3M figure and will not assume it is stale, but relative to program scale it is low, and the manual provides **no expedited lane** for time-critical construction modifications.

5. **There is no field-directive / construction-change-directive mechanism — the manual stops work to price changes.** Section 5.4 correctly applies the cardinal-change test ("a change in work that falls outside the general scope of the Contract shall be treated as a new procurement") [Source: §5.4]. But it offers no instrument to *direct* a contractor to proceed on an in-scope change while price is negotiated later. Differing site conditions, utility conflicts, and design-error changes are inevitable; on an operating airfield, the cost of stopping to negotiate dwarfs the disputed amount. Best practice is a unilateral change directive plus later equitable adjustment. The manual has the cardinal-change gate but not the keep-building tool.

6. **Lifecycle cost is essentially absent from the award architecture.** This is a transaction-and-award document. Value engineering appears only where the FTA compels it [Source: §10.5.7.3]. "Best Value with Trade-Off" exists [Source: §3.6.2.2] but the manual never directs total-cost-of-ownership or 30-year O&M as a scored trade-off factor. For an infrastructure owner, buying the low-first-cost baggage system, HVAC plant, or apron lighting and underfunding renewal is exactly how the deferred-maintenance tail accumulates. Award criteria are the right place to require whole-life costing on capital assets, and the manual is silent.

7. **MACC authority exists but the state-of-good-repair opportunity (Job Order Contracting) is under-built.** Section 6.2.1.3 authorizes MACCs for "recurring need for construction, repair, or renovation." Condition-driven capital — pavement at a falling PCI, end-of-life mechanical, apron lighting — is recurring and predictable, and Job Order Contracting against a published unit-price book cuts delivery time by roughly 25–50% in comparable public programs. The manual treats MACC as a one-paragraph sidebar with no unit-price/catalog mechanism. That is a quick win hiding in plain sight.

8. **The innovation channels already in the manual are hobbled by their own incentives.** Unsolicited proposals are accepted, then must be fully re-advertised, which destroys the originator's reason to bring the idea [Source: §3.8.7.1]. Pilots are capped near one year [Source: §3.8.8]; stipends are discretionary [Source: §4.1.7]. Good bones, weak incentives. For technology and P3 partnerships, the re-competition requirement is the value-killer.

## 2. Evidence

**The delivery menu is modern — quote it before critiquing it.** The manual: "The Airports Authority may use construction management at-risk (CMAR) and design-build delivery methods when the Contracting Officer and COTR determine that such a delivery method is in the Authority's best interests" [Source: §6.2.1]. It then defines three distinct design-build paths — bridging, progressive, and two-phase — and gives progressive design-build a clean description: the Authority issues "a brief programmatic description of the project," prices design/management up front, and develops construction cost "subsequent to award and after the design has progressed to either the schematic or design development phase" [Source: §6.2.1.2]. That is textbook PDB. The deficiency is the absence of selection criteria: the manual lists *what* the tools are and *who* approves their use, never *when* each is the right call.

**Delivery method drives outcomes — this is where the framework gap costs money.** Industry delivery-performance data (from DBIA, the design-build trade association, so read with that interest in mind) finds design-build projects average 3.8% less cost growth than design-bid-build and 2.4% less than CMAR, run 1.7%–3.9% less schedule growth, and complete the construction phase 13% faster than CMAR and 36% faster than design-bid-build [Source: https://dbia.org/new-research-shows-design-build-continues-to-deliver-project-most-efficiently/]. Progressive design-build now accounts for roughly one-third of design-build procurements, concentrating on projects with technical complexity, cost volatility, and evolving scope [Source: https://dbia.org/blog/taking-a-closer-look-progressive-design-build-and-best-value-design-build/] — precisely the profile of airside and terminal work. The point for the report: a manual that authorizes these methods but provides no decision rule for using them leaves the largest single source of cost/schedule variance to chance.

**The change-order and contingency reality — from MWAA's own program.** On Dulles Metrorail Silver Line Phase 2, charges against contingency reached roughly $329 million against a $527.7 million project contingency — about 62% consumed — and Package B change orders alone exceeded $41.9 million, as of November 2019 [Source: https://www.transit.dot.gov/sites/fta.dot.gov/files/docs/foia/148376/201911-pmoc-monthly-report-mwaa-dulles-phase-2.pdf]. Substantial completion, originally scheduled for January 31, 2019, slipped roughly four years, and federal auditors had warned a decade and a half earlier — citing Boston's Big Dig — that an airports authority departing from its core business to deliver a multibillion-dollar program risked "long delays and ballooning costs" [Source: https://www.washingtonpost.com/transportation/2022/11/12/silver-line-extension-dulles-challenges/]. This is the empirical case for an expedited modification lane: when change orders consume contingency at this rate, every one that waits on a $3M Board gate converts cost risk into schedule risk.

**Heavy-handed owner governance is its own failure mode — Denver's Great Hall.** Denver International terminated its $1.8 billion design-build-finance-operate-maintain Great Hall concession after 14 months. At termination the concessionaire sought roughly $288 million in change orders and termination costs, of which about $166.7 million covered some 24 change orders for items as mundane as restrooms, door hardware, and wall-panel substitutions [Source: https://www.constructiondive.com/news/denver-great-hall-project-new-report-what-went-wrong/629795/]. The after-action critique cut both ways: immature scope at financial close *and* an owner that "wanted to micromanage the project but lacked the authority to make decisions" [Source: https://www.denver7.com/news/local-news/new-report-faults-dia-for-heavy-handed-oversight-that-contributed-to-breakdown-of-great-hall-project]. This is the counterweight to my own instinct: more approvals do not equal more control. Control comes from mature scope and decision authority placed where the work is, not from a longer signature chain.

**The state-of-good-repair vehicle MWAA underuses.** Job Order Contracting / IDIQ against a unit-price catalog "allowed [the Army] to begin projects in about half the time it took to do a standalone contract," and reduces project delivery "by as much as 25%" with fewer staff, per NIGP/Gordian benchmarking [Source: https://federalnewsnetwork.com/federal-insights/2020/04/job-order-contracting-the-armys-own-idiq-procurement-solution/; Source: https://www.gordian.com/products/job-order-contracting/]. JOC programs explicitly cover "airport runways and taxiways" and incidental structures. MWAA's MACC authority [Source: §6.2.1.3] is the legal hook; what is missing is a standing unit-price-book program for condition-driven renewal.

**The claims posture is well-built — and that is the tension.** The manual: "Monetary claims based on anticipatory profits are prohibited. Monetary claim requests based on a total cost approach are prohibited. Time extension requests or claims on a total time approach are prohibited" [Source: §7.3], with mandatory exhaustion of administrative disputes before litigation and a jury-trial waiver [Source: §7.2, §7.3]. As a chief engineer I want these clauses on a hard-bid DBB job; they kill the lazy total-cost claim. But the manual's only named ADR is "non-binding evaluative mediation," offered as something the CO "should consider" [Source: §7.3.1.2]. There is no Dispute Review Board — the standing, real-time panel that keeps megaproject disputes off the critical path and is standard on large infrastructure. For a program the size of Silver Line, the absence of a DRB mechanism is a genuine gap.

**A/E procurement is correct — no action needed.** The manual uses qualifications-based selection then price negotiation with the most qualified firm, proceeding down the ranked list if a fair and reasonable price cannot be reached [Source: §6.1.3]. That is Brooks Act-style QBS and it is right. The report should not "reform" it.

## 3. Cost and Schedule Examples a Strategist Can Use

- **MWAA, Silver Line Phase 2 (the home example):** ~62% of a $527.7M contingency consumed and ~4-year schedule slip from a January 2019 substantial-completion date; federal auditors invoked the Big Dig as the cautionary parallel [Source: https://www.transit.dot.gov/sites/fta.dot.gov/files/docs/foia/148376/201911-pmoc-monthly-report-mwaa-dulles-phase-2.pdf; https://www.washingtonpost.com/transportation/2022/11/12/silver-line-extension-dulles-challenges/]. Use this to argue for an expedited change-order lane and a DRB — MWAA does not need a hypothetical, it has its own scar.
- **Denver Great Hall:** $1.8B P3 terminated at 14 months; ~$288M in change-order/termination claims, much of it routine scope; after-action faults *both* immature scope and an owner micromanaging without decision authority [Source: https://www.constructiondive.com/news/denver-great-hall-project-new-report-what-went-wrong/629795/; https://www.denver7.com/news/local-news/new-report-faults-dia-for-heavy-handed-oversight-that-contributed-to-breakdown-of-great-hall-project]. Use this to defeat the "add another review" reflex.
- **Delivery-method performance:** design-build runs ~3.8% less cost growth than DBB and completes construction ~36% faster; PDB is ~one-third of all design-build and rising on complex, scope-volatile work [Source: https://dbia.org/new-research-shows-design-build-continues-to-deliver-project-most-efficiently/]. Use this to justify a written delivery-method selection framework — but cite DBIA's trade-association interest honestly.
- **Job Order Contracting:** ~25–50% faster project starts/delivery on recurring repair-and-renewal work, with airport airfield work explicitly eligible [Source: https://federalnewsnetwork.com/federal-insights/2020/04/job-order-contracting-the-armys-own-idiq-procurement-solution/]. Use this to convert MACC authority into a standing state-of-good-repair program.

The most expensive decision on any capital program is left to undocumented judgment."* — grounded in §6.2.1 and DBIA delivery-performance data.

## 5. What I Would Tell the Strategist Not to Overclaim

- **Do not call this manual obsolete.** Its delivery menu, its QBS A/E process, and its claims defenses are current and, in the claims chapter, hard-won. Argue *timing and selection*, not *replacement*.
- **Do not argue "fewer controls" as a generic good.** Great Hall is the proof that an under-scoped, fast contract fails as badly as an over-controlled one. The honest reform is controls *re-timed* to the construction site: an expedited in-scope modification lane, a construction-change-directive instrument, a Dispute Review Board, and a written delivery-method selection framework.
- **Name the uncertainties.** I could not verify the statutory basis or vintage of the $3M Board threshold, and the published manual does not expose MWAA's internal CEO-to-staff delegation dollar-ladder — which is the variable that actually governs day-to-day cycle time. If the report makes a cycle-time claim, it should flag that the delegation schedule below Board level is not in the public manual excerpt and should be requested before any number is asserted.
- **Discount the DBIA numbers appropriately.** The design-build performance data comes from the design-build trade association. The direction is right and corroborated by program experience; the precise percentages should be presented as indicative, not gospel.
