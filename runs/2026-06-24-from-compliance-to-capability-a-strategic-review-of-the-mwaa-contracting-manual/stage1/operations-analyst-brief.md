# Operations-Analyst Brief — MWAA Contracting Manual (Edition 6.0)

**Lens:** Procurement as a throughput system. Where is the actual bottleneck — the *structural rules* in the manual, or the *operational coordination* (approval choreography, evaluation sequencing, meeting cadence, intake mechanics) layered on top of them?

**One-line finding:** MWAA's *rules* are largely modern; its *flow* is not measured. The manual specifies dozens of controls and not a single cycle-time standard. The bottleneck is coordination, not policy — and most of the latent capacity is recoverable without touching the competition or governance provisions that actually earn their delay.

---

## 1. Engagement with the source material

I read Edition 6.0 (effective 5/20/2026) as a process-control document, the way I'd read a runway-occupancy or turnaround-time SOP: every clause is either a station the work passes through or a clock it must respect. Three structural facts stand out before any benchmarking.

**First, the manual's thresholds are current — this is not a stale document.** Micro-purchase is pegged to "the then-current threshold established by the FAR (in Subpart 2.1 – Definitions)" rather than a hard-coded number [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §3.2], so it auto-tracks the FAR micro-purchase level (raised to **$15,000** effective Oct 1, 2025) [Source: https://www.acquisition.gov/threshold-changes]. Simplified Acquisition Procedures run "up to… an estimated value at $350,000" [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §3.3], matching the new FAR Simplified Acquisition Threshold of **$350,000** (also Oct 1, 2025) [Source: https://smallgovcon.com/statutes-and-regulations/finalized-increases-to-micro-purchase-simplified-acquisition-and-other-thresholds/]. On thresholds, MWAA is at the federal frontier, not behind it.

**Second, the modern delivery tools are all authorized.** The manual permits CMAR and design-build [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §6.2.1], two-step IFB [§3.4.3], Requests for Qualifications and shortlisting [§3.5], pilot programs of up to a year [§3.8.8], unsolicited proposals [§3.8.7], and cooperative/joint procurement off other governments' competitively-awarded contracts [§3.7]. The *capability* to move fast exists in the text.

**Third — and this is the operations finding — the manual contains no throughput standard anywhere.** Every time clock in it is either a *minimum dwell* the Authority must observe (an IFB "shall be advertised for at least 30 Days" [§3.4.1]; a sole-source notice posts "no less than fifteen (15) Days prior to Contract award" and stays up 30 Days [§3.8.5.2]) or a *deadline imposed on outside parties* (protest on evaluation "no later than seven (7) Days" [§9.2.2]; VP-OSCM "will attempt to respond to a protest within seven (7) business days" [§9.4.1]). There is no target for how long an award should take, no service-level commitment to requesting offices, and no requirement to measure cycle time. The document optimizes *inputs* — "the action has been coordinated… all required approvals have been obtained" [§5.3.3] — and is silent on *output velocity*. You cannot manage a queue you never time.

That asymmetry is the whole brief. Where my default evidence base (cycle-time benchmarking, lean-procurement studies) would lead with a delay number, the manual gives me none, because MWAA does not appear to publish one. The conflict isn't that the manual says the wrong thing about speed — it's that it says *nothing*.

---

## 2. Key findings

1. **MWAA defines controls precisely and throughput not at all.** The manual prescribes minimum advertising/notice dwells and external deadlines, but sets no internal cycle-time target, no SLA to requesting offices, and no requirement to measure time-to-award [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §§3.4.1, 3.8.5.2, 5.3.3]. The public benchmark for a public-sector RFP is **57 days posting-to-award** (median of 6,000+ RFPs); MWAA cannot say whether it beats or lags that, because it doesn't track it [Source: https://eunasolutions.com/resources/your-guide-to-rfp-cycle-times-in-public-procurement/].

2. **The Board reserve sits below where comparable authorities draw the line, and it injects meeting-cadence latency.** The Board must approve any goods/services contract "at a cost of $3,000,000 or more (including… all option years)" and *any* less-than-full-and-open award "at a value of $200,000 or more" [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §1.4.1]. Because boards meet roughly monthly, every contract that crosses these lines inherits a multi-week queue independent of its complexity — a fixed latency tax unrelated to risk.

3. **The evaluation-committee process is rigorously serial.** Section 3.6.7 enumerates a 12-step technical evaluation — independent scoring, organized discussion, reference checks, optional competitive range, oral interviews, discussions/BAFO, signed individual *and* summary reports — with price "withheld… until the technical evaluations are completed" [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §3.6.7.3]. Each handoff is a station with queue time. eSourcing platforms cut the purely clerical evaluation load sharply — evaluator scoring from ~16 hours to ~2, score aggregation from ~12 hours to ~0 — and vendors report this "cutting RFP cycle times in half" [Source: https://eunasolutions.com/resources/your-guide-to-rfp-cycle-times-in-public-procurement/]. The manual neither requires nor forbids such tooling; it simply doesn't mention it.

4. **Sole-source and other-than-full-open work carry stacked approvals *plus* a public clock.** A sole source at or above $200,000 needs the requestor's manager, the Vice President, *and* the VP-OSCM [§3.8.5.2]; at or above $50,000 it also triggers a 15-day pre-award public posting that stays up 30 days [§3.8.5.2]; and at or above $200,000 it additionally requires Board approval as "other than full and open competition" [§3.8]. A single $250,000 sole source can therefore touch four approval levels and a two-week public notice window before award. Some of this is worth it (Finding 8); the question is whether all of it is.

5. **Approval depth scales with dollars, but several controls scale with *process type* regardless of value.** Three written quotations are required for everything above the micro-purchase threshold up to $350,000 — i.e., on a routine $25,000 buy [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §3.3.1]. CMAR and design-build require VP-OSCM sign-off and OGC coordination *before the solicitation issues* [§6.2.1]. Prepayment requires three separate VP approvals [§5.6.1]. These are reasonable individually; collectively they are the kind of "multiple layers of sign-off" that lean-procurement studies identify as the dominant cause of delay [Source: https://spendmatters.com/2026/03/24/approvals-and-controls-scaling-governance-without-slowing-execution/].

6. **The protest channel is analog and slow by design.** Protests must arrive by certified mail or hand delivery — "Protests sent electronically will not be accepted" [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §9.1] — and the appeal ladder runs VP-OSCM → CEO → Board, each step gated by a 7-day filing window and discretionary extensions [§§9.4.1, 9.5]. For context, GAO received **1,803** protests in FY2024 with a **16%** sustain rate and a **52%** effectiveness rate [Source: https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/2025-gao-bid-protest-annual-report-where-have-all-the-protests-gone]; protests are a real, frequent operational event, and a mail-only intake adds days of pure dwell to a process that GAO runs electronically.

7. **The cheap, high-yield levers are coordination levers, not policy levers.** The cycle-time literature is consistent: process redesign (collapsing serial approvals, parallel-pathing) delivers **30–50%** cycle-time reduction; layering automation on top adds another **20–30%** on targeted bottlenecks [Source: https://www.sirion.ai/library/contract-management/how-to-improve-procurement-efficiency/]. The Harvard Kennedy School Government Performance Lab helped Tulsa, OK "cut cycle times by nearly a month" using a standing cross-departmental body to find and remove pain points — no new statute, no new staff [Source: https://govlab.hks.harvard.edu/strategic-capability/results-driven-procurement-contract-management/]. MWAA already authorizes cooperative purchasing [§3.7], which independently compresses cycle "from months to weeks or even days" [Source: https://www.sledai.com/blog/cooperative-purchasing-vendor-guide/].

8. **Not every control is fat — some genuinely earn their delay.** The sole-source public posting [§3.8.5.2] is a competition-injection mechanism, not bureaucracy: it converts an opaque single-source decision into a public market test. The Board reserve on less-than-full-open awards [§1.4.1] and the EC conflict-of-interest signatures [§3.6.7.1] are the controls that *prevent* the 52%-effectiveness-rate protest from landing. The diagnosis is not "fewer controls." It is that MWAA has not separated the controls that buy down real risk from the ones that are sequencing inertia.

## 3. Evidence

**The throughput baseline MWAA isn't measuring against.** Euna's analysis of 6,000+ public-sector RFPs puts the average cycle at **57 days from posting to award**, with **78%** finishing in 1–2 months, **6%** exceeding four months, and **3%** exceeding six [Source: https://eunasolutions.com/resources/your-guide-to-rfp-cycle-times-in-public-procurement/]. This excludes the *pre-posting* phase — requirements definition, market research, specification drafting — which the manual loads onto the requesting office [§4.1.4] and which is, in most agencies, where the largest and most invisible delays live. MITRE's analysis of federal practice notes that getting from "need identification to solicitation" commonly takes "three to six months" — and that this is *before* the clock the 57-day figure measures even starts [Source: https://aida.mitre.org/demystifying-dod/timelines/]. MWAA's manual instruments none of this. The procurement forecast [§1.5.1] is a demand-planning tool, not a cycle-time tool; it tells you what's coming, not how long it takes once it arrives.

**Where the manual creates fixed latency.** Two minimum dwells are mandated regardless of urgency: the 30-day IFB advertising floor [§3.4.1] and the 15-day sole-source pre-award notice [§3.8.5.2]. Both are defensible competition protections. The *avoidable* latency is the Board-cadence tax: contracts ≥$3M and all less-than-full-open awards ≥$200K wait for a Board meeting [§1.4.1]. Notably, "All competitively offered construction Contracts are fully delegated to the CEO" [§1.4.2] — so MWAA has already proven it trusts delegation for its largest, most schedule-sensitive category. The asymmetry (full delegation for competitive construction, $3M Board reserve for goods/services) is a policy choice worth re-examining for time cost.

**Where the manual creates serial-process latency.** The 12-step EC sequence [§3.6.7.3] and the price-after-technical rule [§3.6.7.3(11)] are textbook serial design. They protect evaluation integrity — but they also mean a proposal sits through independent scoring, then a consensus meeting, then reference checks, then possibly a competitive-range determination, then orals, then discussions/BAFO, then two rounds of signatures, each a queue. The lean-procurement evidence is that the gains here come from *parallelizing and digitizing*, not deleting steps: one public-sector organization cut purchase-order cycle time by **50%** "using automated approvals alone," and most organizations see **30–40%** improvement within 90 days of an eSourcing go-live [Source: https://www.sirion.ai/library/contract-management/benefits-of-procurement-automation/]. MWAA's manual is silent on whether evaluation runs on paper or platform — and §3.6.7.3(12) ("Proposals provided to the EC members in hard copy format should be returned to the Contracting Officer") strongly implies paper is still in the workflow.

**Where modern delivery is authorized but under-instrumented.** CMAR's value proposition is explicitly schedule: the manual itself states a CMAR contract "can expedite overall project delivery timelines as it allows early phases of the construction work to proceed while other portions of the design are being finalized" [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §6.2.1.1]. The industry record backs this — San Diego delivered a 526,000-sq-ft, 10-gate terminal expansion "early and under budget" via CMAR [Source: https://www.mastt.com/blogs/alternative-delivery-methods]. But MWAA gates every CMAR/design-build solicitation behind VP-OSCM approval and OGC coordination *before issuance* [§6.2.1], with no standing pre-approval or template pathway. The tool that exists to *save* schedule has a setup step that *costs* schedule.

**Where the latent capacity is.** Three reservoirs, in descending order of recoverability:
- *Measurement.* Adding a single cycle-time KPI (days from requisition to award, by method and dollar band) and a quarterly variance review costs nothing structural. The HKS Government Performance Lab's entire results-driven model rests on this; Tulsa recovered "nearly a month" from instrumentation plus a standing review body [Source: https://govlab.hks.harvard.edu/strategic-capability/results-driven-procurement-contract-management/].
- *Parallelization.* Converting serial approvals to concurrent ones is the 30–50% lever [Source: https://www.sirion.ai/library/contract-management/how-to-improve-procurement-efficiency/]. The manual's pre-award review [§5.3.3] reads as a checklist that *can* be run in parallel but isn't required to be.
- *Channel digitization.* Mail-only protest intake [§9.1] and hard-copy evaluation [§3.6.7.3(12)] are pure dwell; eSourcing removes the clerical hours wholesale [Source: https://eunasolutions.com/resources/your-guide-to-rfp-cycle-times-in-public-procurement/].

## 4. Counterexamples — controls where the delay is worth it

Intellectual honesty requires naming where "streamline it" is the wrong answer. These are the procurement equivalents of the cases where operations *can't* solve the problem and the structural control is correct.

1. **The sole-source public posting [§3.8.5.2] should stay — and arguably expand.** The 15/30-day notice is not friction; it is a competition test that surfaces sources the buyer didn't know existed. Removing it to save two weeks would trade a small, predictable delay for a large, unbounded risk of uncompeted spend. The delay earns its keep.

2. **The Board reserve on less-than-full-open awards ≥$200K [§§1.4.1, 3.8] is the right altitude for that decision.** Non-competitive awards are exactly where capture and favoritism risk concentrate. A bi-state compact authority answerable to Virginia, the District, and federal appointees needs a visible governance brake here. GAO's data — a **52%** protest effectiveness rate, meaning more than half of protests yield the protester *some* relief — shows competition disputes are real and frequent [Source: https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/2025-gao-bid-protest-annual-report-where-have-all-the-protests-gone]. The control that prevents the dispute is cheaper than the dispute.

3. **The three-quote minimum above micro-purchase [§3.3.1] is cheap insurance, not bureaucracy.** Three quotes on a $25,000 buy costs hours, not weeks, and the manual already provides a relief valve when only one quote materializes [§3.3.4]. The control's delay is de minimis relative to its pricing-discipline value.

4. **EC independence and conflict-of-interest signatures [§3.6.7.1] are the integrity backbone.** These signatures are what survive a protest. Deleting them to save a signing cycle would be optimizing throughput at the direct expense of defensibility — the exact failure mode the run brief warns against.

The synthesis: MWAA's problem is *not* that it over-controls. It is that it has never sorted its controls into "buys down real risk" versus "is just the order we've always done it in" — because it has never timed the line.

## 5. Five data points a strategist can use verbatim

1. **"The MWAA Contracting Manual specifies a 30-day minimum advertising dwell, a 15-day sole-source notice, and a 7-day protest deadline — but nowhere sets a target for how long an award should take. It manages every clock except its own."** [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §§3.4.1, 3.8.5.2, 9.2.2]

2. **"The public-sector benchmark is 57 days from RFP posting to award across 6,000+ procurements, with 78% finishing inside two months. MWAA cannot say where it falls on that distribution, because the manual requires no cycle-time measurement."** [Source: https://eunasolutions.com/resources/your-guide-to-rfp-cycle-times-in-public-procurement/]

3. **"Process redesign alone — collapsing serial approvals into parallel ones — yields 30–50% cycle-time reduction in the procurement literature; one public-sector body cut purchase-order cycle time by 50% through automated approvals with no other change."** [Source: https://www.sirion.ai/library/contract-management/benefits-of-procurement-automation/]

4. **"MWAA already fully delegates every competitively-offered construction contract to the CEO, yet routes a routine $3 million goods-or-services buy to a monthly Board meeting. The Authority has proven it trusts delegation where schedule matters most; the question is why it stops at the airfield gate."** [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §§1.4.1–1.4.2]

5. **"The manual still bars electronic protest filing — 'Protests sent electronically will not be accepted' — while GAO, which handled 1,803 protests last year, runs its entire docket online."** [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §9.1; https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/2025-gao-bid-protest-annual-report-where-have-all-the-protests-gone]

### Methodological note
All MWAA citations are to the operator-supplied Edition 6.0 extract (effective 5/20/2026); section numbers are as printed in that document. External benchmarks are public-sector medians, not airport-authority-specific, and I flag two as vendor-reported (Euna, Sirion) — directionally reliable, not audited. The central claim of this brief — that MWAA does not publish a procurement cycle-time standard — is inferred from the *absence* of any such provision in the manual text; it should be confirmed against MWAA's internal OSCM metrics before the Strategist treats it as established fact.
