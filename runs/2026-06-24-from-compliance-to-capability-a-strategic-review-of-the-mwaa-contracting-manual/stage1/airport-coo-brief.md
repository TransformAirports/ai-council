# Stage 1 Brief — The Operator's Chair

**Agent:** airport-coo
**Run:** From Compliance to Capability — A Strategic Review of the MWAA Contracting Manual
**Lens:** Chief Operating Officer of a top-tier US hub. The OPSCOMM is in my pocket. I have to make this airport work tomorrow morning regardless of which procurement strategy is fashionable.

---

## Orientation: what I read in the source before I formed an opinion

I read the manual the way I read a new jet-bridge spec — looking for the place it will fail at 5:45 AM, not the place it reads well in a board binder. The headline finding surprised me: **the toolkit is already modern.** Edition 6.0 carries CMAR, three flavors of design-build (bridging, progressive, two-phase), Multiple Award Construction Contracts, task-order IDIQs, blanket purchase agreements, pilot programs, unsolicited proposals, stipends to keep losing bidders at the table, RFIs and draft solicitations, and a best-value-with-tradeoff selection method [Source: Contracting_Manual-v6_0-05-20-2026-2.pdf, §6.2.1, §3.6.2.2, Appendix B]. The Board reserves approval only for contracts at or above **$3,000,000**, sole-source actions at or above **$200,000**, and concessions yielding **$3M+** in annual revenue [Source: Contracting_Manual, §1.4.1]. All competitively offered construction is fully delegated to the CEO [Source: Contracting_Manual, §1.4.2]. That is not a timid document.

So I want to name a conflict with the run thesis up front. The framing "from compliance to capability" implies the manual is the binding constraint on capability. **From the operator's chair, it largely is not.** The manual is the toolbox. Capability is won or lost in three places the manual barely touches: who is in the source-selection room when an asset I will operate for 25 years gets bought, whether anyone priced the year-7 failure, and how fast I can buy a part at 4 AM during a runway closure. A modernization roadmap that adds more tools to an already-stocked box, without fixing those three things, will read beautifully and fail in execution.

---

## 1. Key findings (framed as an experienced operator would recognize them)

- **The manual buys assets; operations inherits them — and the handoff is ungoverned.** Nothing in the contract-administration chapter makes operational acceptance, commissioning, a spares package, or a warranty term a condition of final payment or closeout [Source: Contracting_Manual, §5.13]. This is the single recurring source of capital handed back broken: the jet bridge that was bought on first cost and starts throwing faults in year 7, the baggage line that ramp writes up every Monday in July. The manual lets me inherit the operating cost without ever pricing it.

- **Lifecycle cost is optional exactly where it should be mandatory.** Best-value-with-tradeoff exists, but the manual never requires total cost of ownership as an evaluation factor for the asset classes that hurt — BHS, jet bridges, escalators/elevators, HVAC, security technology [Source: Contracting_Manual, §3.6.3]. "Value engineering" appears only in the federal (FTA) chapter [Source: Contracting_Manual, §10.5.7.3], not as a general operating discipline. ACRP's own research tells airports to do the opposite: price the asset across its life, not at the bid table [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrp0715/wp-content/themes/acrp-child/documents/200/original/acrp_r186.pdf; https://onlinepubs.trb.org/onlinepubs/acrp/acrp_rpt_252Appendices.pdf].

- **Operations is not a required voice in source selection.** Evaluation committees and source-selection plans are governed in detail [Source: Contracting_Manual, §3.6.7], but the manual does not require a maintenance or operations representative on the committee that selects the firm whose building I will run. The COTR — my person — shows up *after* award to manage day-to-day [Source: Contracting_Manual, §5.2.2]. By then the operating penalty is already designed in.

- **The IROPS lever exists but is throttled in exactly the wrong band.** Urgent/critical procurement (§3.8.6) lets a Contracting Officer act during business hours *with VP-OSCM approval*, and lets the COO/CEO/CRO authorize after hours — but only above the micro-purchase threshold, with a Board after-action report inside five business days for anything over $200,000 [Source: Contracting_Manual, §3.8.6]. In the first 90 minutes of a runway closure or a system outage, I am not reading §3.8.6. I am calling a vendor already on a MACC or IDIQ. Capability in IROPS is whether the standing vehicle exists *before* the event — the manual enables it but does not require ops to pre-position it.

- **A large slice of what I coordinate daily is outside the manual entirely.** It does not apply to Airline Use and Lease Agreements, in-flight caterers, fuel providers, or tenant businesses [Source: Contracting_Manual, Foreword], and airline improvements to airport facilities ride a separate, less-competitive track [Source: Contracting_Manual, §3.8.4]. So common-use deployment, gate assignments, de-ice and fuel coordination — the partnered, transactional grind of airline relations — sit in a seam. Tenant-procured projects bypass the manual; I inherit the integration risk on the back end.

- **The real drag is cycle time in the mid-size operational band, not the Board threshold.** The $3M Board line is generous; below it I have room. The pain is the source-selection committee calendar for the $350K–$3M fixes — the carousel motor replacement, the gate-area re-power, the curb re-stripe — that I need finished *before next summer's peak*, not after it. Every added review in that band is a peak day I did not buy back.

- **Pilot authority is real but capped at the size of a science fair.** Pilots are capped at one year and require VP-OSCM sign-off [Source: Contracting_Manual, §3.8.8]. Good for trying a queue sensor. Useless for de-risking a nine-figure baggage or common-use program, which is precisely where the operator most needs a governed way to learn before committing.

## 2. Evidence

**The manual's own provisions.** The delegated-authority structure is operator-friendly at the top: Board approval reserved at $3M, full delegation of competitive construction to the CEO, CEO power to extend concessions up to 12 months before an extension becomes a new sole-source action requiring the Board [Source: Contracting_Manual, §1.4.1–§1.4.2, §6.3.3]. The procurement-method ladder is conventional and workable: micro-purchase; three written quotes up to $50K and again from $50K–$350K; sealed bid, RFQ, and competitive proposal above that [Source: Contracting_Manual, §3.3]. Alternative delivery is permitted but gated: CMAR and design-build each require VP-OSCM approval and OGC coordination before the solicitation issues [Source: Contracting_Manual, §6.2.1]. None of this is broken. It is the *silence* on lifecycle, acceptance, and operational participation that costs me.

**Lifecycle cost — the research says price the whole life.** ACRP Research Report 172, *Guidebook for Considering Life-Cycle Costs in Airport Asset Procurement*, and ACRP Research Report 252, *Airport Baggage Handling System Decision-Making Based on Total Cost of Ownership*, both exist because airports systematically buy on first cost and pay on operating cost [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrp0715/wp-content/themes/acrp-child/documents/200/original/acrp_r186.pdf; https://onlinepubs.trb.org/onlinepubs/acrp/acrp_rpt_252Appendices.pdf]. ACRP Report 139 on retrocommissioning documents the same pattern in building systems — O&M penalties baked in at design that someone then pays to unwind [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrpwebresource2/acrp-report-139-optimizing-airport-building-operations-and-maintenance-through-retrocommissioning-a-whole-systems-approach/]. A manual that does not require TCO scoring is a manual that bets against its own operators.

**Delivery-method selection — the discipline already exists outside the manual.** ACRP Research Report 21 and the 2024 ACRP Research Report 267, *Selecting, Procuring, and Implementing Airport Capital Project Delivery Methods*, give a structured framework for matching delivery method to project, weighting operations and maintenance among the selection drivers [Source: https://www.nationalacademies.org/publications/27951; https://crp.trb.org/acrpwebresource13/acrp-research-report-21-a-guidebook-for-selecting-airport-capital-project-delivery-methods/]. MWAA's manual authorizes the methods but offers no selection framework and no requirement that O&M weigh in the choice. That is a roadmap item that costs nothing and changes outcomes.

**IROPS — the events that prove the point.** When a Georgia Power switchgear fire took out Hartsfield-Jackson's primary *and* redundant substations in December 2017, more than 1,000 flights cancelled and the field went dark for roughly 11 hours [Source: https://www.cnn.com/2017/12/17/us/atlanta-airport-power-outage/index.html]. The 2022 Southwest meltdown — 15,000-plus cancellations and a record $140M DOT penalty — turned on an outdated crew-scheduling system, not on a runway [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Southwest_Airlines_scheduling_crisis; https://www.npr.org/2022/12/27/1145616523/southwest-airlines-flight-cancellations-2022]. In both, the deciding variable was whether a contract or system was already in place when the event hit. Procurement either pre-positioned the capability or it did not. The first 90 minutes do not wait for an evaluation committee.

**Common use — the seam the manual leaves open.** ACRP Report 30, the reference guide on common use, documents how shared gate and systems infrastructure shifts the operator from landlord to system integrator [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrp0715/wp-content/themes/acrp-child/documents/033/original/ACRP_30_Reference_Guide_on_Understanding_Common_Use_at_Airports.pdf]. The manual's exclusion of airline-tenant arrangements means the most operationally consequential integration — common-use platform standards, gate flexibility — is partly negotiated outside the procurement discipline the roadmap is trying to modernize.

## 3. The operational case — for and against the thesis

**Where the thesis is right, and gets stronger by saying so.** The move from compliance to capability is correct in spirit, and it gets *stronger* the moment it admits that the binding constraints are operational, not procedural. A roadmap that says "we already own the tools; the gap is that operations is never in the room and the lifecycle is never priced" is far more credible to a skeptical board than one promising a new toolkit. Three reforms carry the whole thesis from the operator's side, and none requires legislation:

1. **Make operations a required source-selection participant** for any asset class operations will own (BHS, vertical transportation, jet bridges, HVAC, security tech). This costs a seat on a committee and saves a decade of write-ups.
2. **Mandate total-cost-of-ownership scoring** in best-value tradeoff for those same asset classes — ACRP 172 and 252 are the off-the-shelf method [Source: https://onlinepubs.trb.org/onlinepubs/acrp/acrp_rpt_252Appendices.pdf].
3. **Make operational acceptance a payment gate.** Commissioning, a spares package, training, and a defined warranty become conditions of closeout under §5.13, not afterthoughts.

Add a fourth that is pure IROPS hygiene: **require standing emergency vehicles** (MACCs, IDIQs, BPAs) for the systems that fail — power, snow, IT, BHS — so the urgent/critical clause is the exception, not the plan.

**Where the thesis runs into operational reality, and could fail.** The danger is that "modernization" becomes a synonym for more tools and more speed without governance of the handoff. Speed that buys the cheapest jet bridge faster is not capability; it is a faster path to deferred maintenance. Compress source selection on a complex baggage program and you get an integrator who underbid, a guaranteed-maximum-price that balloons in change orders (the manual caps a single added-work item at $500,000 [Source: Contracting_Manual, p.66]), and a ramp crew living with it. The other failure mode is treating the manual as the whole field of play. The biggest operational risks — airline-tenant projects, common-use integration, the AULA — sit *outside* it. A roadmap that polishes the manual and ignores the seams will modernize the part of the system that was already working.

The honest counter to my own case: every gate I am defending — the TCO score, the ops seat, the acceptance gate — is a control, and the run thesis is rightly suspicious of controls that add time without value. My answer is the test the run prompt itself sets: a control earns its place only when the value exceeds the delay. The ops seat and the TCO score add days at the front and save years at the back. That is the trade the operator will defend in front of the board, because the operator is the one who eats the back end.

## 4. Specific examples that frame the thesis concretely

**a) The jet bridge bought on first cost.** A sealed-bid or lowest-price-technically-acceptable procurement [Source: Contracting_Manual, §3.6.2.1] selects the cheapest compliant bridge. No TCO score, no ops representative on the committee, no spares package mandated at closeout. Year 7, the bridge throws faults on a Monday push; the inbound captain learns the gate is down after pushback clearance. The operator knew this was coming the day the bid was opened. The roadmap fix is a TCO factor and an ops seat — both free.

**b) The baggage system handoff.** ACRP built an entire TCO decision framework for BHS because airports keep buying on capital cost and paying on jam rate and mean-time-between-failure [Source: https://onlinepubs.trb.org/onlinepubs/acrp/acrp_rpt_252Appendices.pdf]. MWAA's manual authorizes CMAR and progressive design-build — the right vehicles for a complex BHS [Source: Contracting_Manual, §6.2.1] — but does not require lifecycle scoring or an operational-acceptance gate. The vehicle is modern; the outcome still lands on the carousel ramp writes up every July.

**c) The 4 AM runway closure.** A pavement failure closes a runway at 4 AM. Under §3.8.6 the COO can authorize emergency repair after hours above the micro-purchase threshold, with a Board after-action inside five days [Source: Contracting_Manual, §3.8.6]. That works *only* if a pavement contractor is already on a standing MACC. If not, the clause authorizes a scramble. Atlanta 2017 is the cautionary tale: when the primary and redundant systems both failed, the question was what was already contracted, not what could be competed [Source: https://www.cnn.com/2017/12/17/us/atlanta-airport-power-outage/index.html].

**d) The common-use seam.** A carrier funds a gate-area refit as an airline improvement to airport facilities [Source: Contracting_Manual, §3.8.4], procured on a track outside the manual. The common-use platform standard, the gate-management integration, the fallback during the next IT outage — all inherited by operations with no procurement lever to have shaped them. ACRP 30 is explicit that common use turns the operator into the integrator [Source: https://crp.trb.org/acrp0715/wp-content/themes/acrp-child/documents/033/original/ACRP_30_Reference_Guide_on_Understanding_Common_Use_at_Airports.pdf]. The roadmap should name this seam, not pretend the manual covers it.

**e) The mid-size fix that misses the peak.** A $1.2M curb re-power needs to be done before summer. It clears the $350K threshold into full competitive proposal with an evaluation committee and source-selection plan [Source: Contracting_Manual, §3.6.7], but sits well under the $3M Board line. The pain is not approval authority — the CEO has it. The pain is the committee calendar. Miss it by six weeks and the operator holds the peak with the old equipment. This is the band where cycle-time reform, not new tools, returns the most operational value.

---

## Bottom line for the Strategist

The thesis is sound but aimed slightly off-center. The manual is not a compliance cage holding capability back — it is a modern toolbox that goes quiet at the exact moments capability is decided: source selection, lifecycle pricing, and the handoff to the people who run the building. Tell the board the truth an operator recognizes: most capital plans fail in execution not because procurement was too slow, but because nobody who would have to use the building was in the room when it was bought, and nobody priced the Monday in July. Fix that — ops in the room, lifecycle on the scorecard, acceptance as a payment gate, standing vehicles for IROPS — and "from compliance to capability" stops being a slogan and becomes something the duty desk can feel.
