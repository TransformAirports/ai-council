# Procurement Chair Brief: Reading the MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0 as a Capability Instrument

*Stage 1 research brief — airport-procurement-expert lens. Independent of other Stage 1 agents.*

The thesis asks whether the Contracting Manual moves MWAA "from compliance to capability." I read the document the way I read any rulebook before I have to defend it in front of an OIG: not for whether it is correct, but for what it actually lets the Authority *do*, on what schedule, at what cost in optionality. Three findings frame everything below.

First, this Manual is not a neutral procedural artifact. It is a **post-scandal compliance instrument**, and you cannot evaluate it without naming the scandal. In 2012 the DOT Inspector General found that MWAA had used "categorical exceptions" to limit competition on roughly **two-thirds of its contracts over $200,000** — 117 of 190 contracts between 2009 and 2011, about **$225 million** — awarded sole-source contracts without adequate justification, started work before award, fed favored bidders nonpublic information, lost track of who held delegated contracting authority, and spent an estimated **$83.6 million (14% of contracts)** without the Board approval its own rules required [Source: https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-112hhrg76706/html/CHRG-112hhrg76706.htm]. Edition 6.0's spine — the $200,000 / $3,000,000 Board reservation thresholds, the centralized delegation registry, the public quarterly report, the 22-item competition-exception list — is the institutional scar tissue from that audit. Any "capability" recommendation that loosens a control has to answer the question: *does this reopen a 2012 finding?* That is the steelman the Strategist must clear, not wave away.

Second, and pulling the other direction: MWAA enjoys **more design freedom over its own procurement rules than almost any peer.** Section 1.7 states it plainly — the Authority "is not subject to the procurement laws or regulations" of Virginia or the District, "nor is it subject to the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) or the various statutes that generally govern federal procurement" [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §1.7]. It is a creature of a Virginia–D.C. compact ratified by Congress. It writes its own book. The Vice President, OSCM can make "non-material changes" administratively; the Board amends the rest by resolution (§1.8). So the most common excuse in public procurement reform — *the state code won't let us* — is largely unavailable here. The binding constraints are only two: the federal strings attached to the funding stack, and the political memory of the scandal. That is a rare and underexploited reform surface, and the thesis should say so.

Third, the Manual's federal-grant architecture is **built for the wrong grant.** Chapter 10 — the single longest chapter, with its own clause library in Appendix C — is devoted entirely to the **Federal Transit Administration** and the Dulles Corridor Metrorail (Silver Line) project [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §10.0, Ch.10 intro]. There is no equivalent structured chapter for **FAA Airport Improvement Program (AIP)** or **Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (BIL)** airport grants, even though the airports are about to spend a **$9 billion 2025–2039 capital program, $6.99 billion of it at Dulles** including a new 14-gate concourse and design of a fifth runway [Source: https://www.wusa9.com/article/news/local/virginia/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-approves-9-billion-for-projects-at-dulles-reagan/65-f19e2e29-d5c4-4df8-a384-71ede2139a86], with a Dulles master-plan rebuild reportedly scoped at up to **$22 billion through 2034** [Source: https://www.ffxnow.com/2026/05/12/report-new-plan-to-overhaul-dulles-airport-would-eliminate-mobile-lounges/]. The Manual handles FAA money in passing — DBE references in Chapter 2, a generic "Grant Funding" clause hook in §5.3.2.2 — while reserving its full procedural rigor for a transit project that is nearly complete. That asymmetry is the single largest structural gap a modernization roadmap should close.

## 1. Key Findings

- **The Manual is a 2012-OIG compliance instrument wearing a 2026 date.** Its $200K/$3M Board thresholds and 22-item competition-exception list exist because the DOT IG found MWAA limiting competition on two-thirds of its over-$200K contracts and spending $83.6M without Board approval; reform that loosens controls must show it does not reopen those findings [Source: https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-112hhrg76706/html/CHRG-112hhrg76706.htm].
- **MWAA can rewrite its own procurement rules without asking a legislature** — §1.7 exempts it from the FAR, Virginia, and D.C. procurement law — so the binding constraint on capability is the federal funding stack and political memory, not statute [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §1.7].
- **The Manual's grant chapter covers the wrong program.** All of Chapter 10 and Appendix C serve FTA/Silver Line; there is no parallel FAA AIP/BIL chapter, even as a $6.99B AIP- and BIL-eligible Dulles program comes online [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, Ch.10; https://www.wusa9.com/article/news/local/virginia/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-approves-9-billion-for-projects-at-dulles-reagan/65-f19e2e29-d5c4-4df8-a384-71ede2139a86].
- **Construction still defaults to sealed bid; modern delivery is permissioned, not presumed.** CMAR and design-build (bridging, progressive, two-phase) are authorized in §6.2.1 but require case-by-case VP/OSCM approval, while 50–75% of major airport programs now run CMAR or progressive design-build as their default [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §6.2.1; https://www.weitz.com/progressive-design-build-in-aviation-construction-building-smarter-for-the-future/].
- **The protest system is fast and authority-favorable — there is no automatic stay.** A protest does not halt award; the CEO may proceed "in the Authority's best interests" (§9.6), windows are 7–14 days, and MACC task orders are protest-immune except on procedure (§6.2.1.3). This is a genuine speed asset, but the rule that "protests sent electronically will not be accepted" (§9.1) is an anachronism that signals the document's age [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, §§9.1, 9.6, 6.2.1.3].
- **Every federal dollar in the $9B stack flows down a restriction the master plan rarely prices:** Buy America/BABA (55% domestic content on manufactured products), Davis-Bacon, qualifications-based A/E selection (49 U.S.C. §47107(a)(17)), DBE goals (49 CFR 26), and FAA *prior approval* for any noncompetitive award (2 CFR §200.320(f)) [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/buy_american; https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/procurement/general].
- **The Manual under-uses the speed vehicles and over-uses the slow ones.** Cooperative purchasing (§3.7), MACCs (§6.2.1.3), and task-order/IDIQ contracts (Appendix B.8) all exist, but the document gives them a paragraph each while building full RFP machinery for routine buys; job order contracting cuts delivery time on recurring work by as much as 25% [Source: https://www.gordian.com/resources/what-is-job-order-contracting/].
- **Realistic board-to-NTP on a major Dulles capital action is 24–48 months, not the 12–18 a master plan implies** — once NEPA, Section 106, FAA grant concurrence, and the Manual's own evaluation-and-protest windows are added in series.

## 2. Evidence by Sub-Question

### 2.1 The funding stack and what it binds

MWAA's $9B program is not Authority money in any clean sense; it is a blend of PFCs, Authority bonds, AIP entitlement and discretionary grants, and BIL programs (the Airport Terminal Program and Airport Infrastructure Grants). Each federal layer flows a different restriction into the contract language, and the Manual currently absorbs these unevenly.

**Buy America / BABA.** Under 49 U.S.C. §50101, all steel and manufactured goods in AIP-funded work must be U.S.-produced; BABA tightened the manufactured-products test so that U.S. components must exceed **55% of cost**, and FAA waivers (public-interest, non-availability, 25%-cost-differential) run a maximum of five years [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/buy_american; https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/BABA-Implementation-Fact-Sheet.pdf]. This binds *design*, not just procurement: a jet-bridge, a baggage-handling controls package, or a curtain-wall system specified before a domestic-content check can force a re-spec or a waiver application that adds months. The Manual's Appendix C carries a Buy America clause (§C.2.10) — but for FTA. There is no airport-side BABA compliance workflow.

**A/E selection is locked to qualifications-based selection by statute.** AIP sponsors must select architect-engineers on qualifications alone, negotiating price only after selection, under 49 U.S.C. §47107(a)(17), which incorporates the Brooks Act regime [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/procurement/professional_services]. The Manual's §6.1 already does this correctly — SF 330, descending-order criteria, "price will not serve as a basis for the evaluation," negotiate-then-fall-to-next-firm. This is a place the Manual is fully compliant and modern; the thesis should not "reform" it.

**Noncompetitive awards require FAA's prior blessing on grant work.** 2 CFR §200.320(f) permits sole-source only for single-source items, public exigency, inadequate competition, or "when the Federal awarding agency expressly authorizes" it — and FAA's AIP guidance requires sponsors to "seek prior approval from FAA" before using noncompetitive methods [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/procurement/general; https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-2/subtitle-A/chapter-II/part-200/subpart-D/subject-group-ECFR45ddd4419ad436d/section-200.320]. The Manual's own sole-source machinery (§3.8.5 — website posting 15 days prior, 30 days total, for buys ≥$50K) is robust, but it is the *Authority's* gate, not FAA's. On AIP/BIL dollars there is a second federal gate the Manual does not narrate.

**Thresholds now track the Uniform Guidance.** The Manual pegs micro-purchase to the FAR definition (currently $15,000 after the 2024 increase) and sets simplified acquisition at $350,000 (§§3.2, 3.3), which matches 2 CFR Part 200 as revised effective October 1, 2024 [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-2/subtitle-A/chapter-II/part-200; https://www.feldesman.com/procurement-rules-just-got-easier-for-grantees/]. Good. By pegging to the FAR rather than hard-coding a number, the Manual auto-updates — a quiet piece of good drafting.

### 2.2 Delivery method as a strategic decision

The Manual authorizes CMAR and design-build "when the Contracting Officer and COTR determine that such a delivery method is in the Authority's best interests," but only after VP/OSCM approval and OGC sign-off "prior to issuing any such Solicitation" (§6.2.1). It usefully distinguishes three design-build flavors — **bridging** (preliminary design issued, fixed/GMP to finish), **progressive** (programmatic description only, price-to-manage, construction cost developed post-award after schematic/DD), and **two-phase** (firm price before design, max five phase-two offerors) (§6.2.1.2). It also added **Multiple Award Construction Contracts** with protest-immune task orders (§6.2.1.3). This is genuinely current drafting.

The strategic gap is *default and presumption*. Chapter 3 still treats sealed bid as the construction baseline; alternative delivery is the exception requiring justification. The market has moved the other way: roughly **50–75% of major airport projects now run some form of CMAR**, with progressive design-build "becoming the preferred model" for scopes that will evolve during design [Source: https://www.weitz.com/progressive-design-build-in-aviation-construction-building-smarter-for-the-future/]. Peer evidence is concrete and recent — San Antonio's $1.7B Terminal C (CMAR, ground-broken December 2024), St. Louis Lambert's $3B consolidated terminal (CMAR, AECOM Hunt–Clayco) [Source: https://www.enr.com/articles/61637-st-louis-lambert-airport-selects-cmar-for-proposed-3b-terminal-overhaul], Sacramento's SMForward Concourse B (CMAR), and Seattle-Tacoma's North Main Terminal (progressive design-build). TRB/ACRP research now documents progressive design-build as a maturing standard in airport capital programs [Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/03611981241249743].

The Dulles program — a 14-gate concourse and a fifth runway whose scope is still moving — is a textbook progressive-design-build candidate: define the program, lock the team on qualifications, develop the GMP as design matures, preserve the owner's ability to steer. CMAR's preconstruction value beats design-build's schedule compression precisely when the operator needs to keep changing the scope, which is Dulles. The reform move is to make alternative delivery *presumptive for major capital* and sealed bid the exception — inverting §6.2.1's burden of proof.

### 2.3 Schedule arithmetic the master plan omits

Master plans quote construction durations. Procurement quotes the gauntlet from Board direction to notice-to-proceed, and on federally-funded new ground it runs in series:

- Scope/program definition: 60–120 days
- Procurement plan + (for AIP/BIL scopes) FAA concurrence: 30–90 days
- Advertising window: the Manual sets a 30-day floor for IFBs and RFPs (§§3.4.1, 3.6) — 30–60 days
- Evaluation, shortlist, interviews, BAFO, selection: 45–120 days (longer for two-phase design-build with five finalists)
- Protest window + decision: solicitation-terms protests due in 14 days, evaluation/award protests in 7 days, VP/OSCM response in ~7 business days, with appeal ladders to CEO and Board (§§9.2–9.5) — 15–45 days if contested
- Negotiation and execution: 30–90 days
- Board approval for anything ≥$3M (§1.4.1) — adds a board-cycle wait

That is **9–17 months of pure procurement** before a shovel, *before* environmental review. New ground disturbance — a fifth runway, a new concourse footprint — triggers **NEPA (6–24 months)** and, given Dulles's Saarinen-era main terminal and the airport's eligibility for historic consideration, potentially non-trivial **Section 106 consultation (add 12–24 months)**. The honest board-to-NTP figure for a major Dulles capital action is **24–48 months.** A master-plan deck that pencils first-shovel 12–18 months after a Board vote is off by a year or more — and the Manual itself is not the bottleneck; the federal review stack stacked on top of it is.

### 2.4 DBE / SLBE reality

The Manual runs two programs in parallel: the federal **DBE program** (49 CFR Parts 23 and 26, including ACDBE for concessions) for FAA/FTA/FHWA-funded work, and the Authority-funded **Small Local Business Enterprise** program — explicitly "race and gender neutral," 100-mile radius of the D.C. zero-milestone, SBA size standards (§§2.3–2.4). Goals go to the federal agency for concurrence (§2.3.1); good-faith-effort waivers require documented, non-"pro-forma" outreach (§2.5.3).

Two live realities the thesis must price. First, **49 CFR Part 26 was amended April 9, 2024 (89 FR 24966)**, refining goal-setting and counting [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-A/part-26]; the Manual's program description should track the current rule. Second, the legal ground under race-conscious measures is shifting — peer agencies are now setting goals met **100% race/gender-neutral** to survive post-*SFFA* litigation exposure. MWAA's SLBE program, being neutral by design, is a built-in hedge — a quietly smart piece of architecture worth foregrounding, not reforming. The operational caution the Strategist needs: DBE goals are neither decorative nor infinitely elastic. On specialty airport scopes — custom baggage-handling integrators, jet-bridge OEMs, specialized airfield electrical — local DBE capacity is thin, and a goal set without market reality produces either waivers or a non-compliance finding. The DOT IG's recurring complaint across airport audits is "DBE goal-setting that does not reflect the actual market." Goal-setting tied to a real availability study is the defense.

### 2.5 Protest risk and the chilling effect

MWAA's protest forum is its own — not GAO, not the Court of Federal Claims, because it is not a federal agency. Protests go to the VP/OSCM, then CEO, then (for Board-approval contracts) the Board, whose decision is final (§9.5). The defining feature: **no automatic stay.** "If a Contract has not been awarded at the time a protest is timely filed, the Contract may not be awarded while the protest is pending, *unless the CEO determines* that the award... is in the Authority's best interests" (§9.6). Compare GAO's CICA stay, which automatically freezes award. MWAA can push through. That is a real schedule asset and a reason the Authority's protest exposure is lower than a FAR buyer's.

The under-discussed cost is the same one that afflicts every protest regime: **defensive specification.** When evaluation criteria must survive challenge, scope language drifts generic to avoid attack, then gets value-engineered downward in construction because the spec never said what the operator actually wanted. The Manual's tight 7-day evaluation-protest window and protest-bond option (§9.3.1) suppress *volume*, but they do not cure the defensive-drafting incentive. Two anachronisms also date the document: protests "sent electronically will not be accepted" (§9.1) in 2026, and the mail/hand-delivery-only rule. These are small, but they are exactly the kind of "the rulebook is old" signal the thesis is hunting.

### 2.6 Sole source, brand-name, and OEM lock-in

The Manual permits sole source only for "unique or innovative concept" or "patents or restricted data rights" (§3.8.5.1), with escalating approvals and public posting (§3.8.5.2). It is disciplined drafting. But it is silent on the *de facto* sole sources that dominate airport technology: airfield lighting controls, baggage-handling system controls, security and access-control platforms, and CUPPS/CUTE common-use systems all carry real interoperability lock-in to incumbent OEMs. The DOT IG's 2016 sole-source audit found that even at the FAA, controls failed in practice — for 24 of 34 sole-source contracts, *no one* in the process filed the required conflict-of-interest agreement, and the agency could not produce adequate independent cost estimates for many awards [Source: https://www.oig.dot.gov/sites/default/files/FAA%20Sole-Source%20Contracts_Final%20Report%5E5-9-16.pdf]. The lesson for MWAA: the controls on paper are good; the audit failures happen in documentation discipline. The thesis should flag which of the Dulles program's technology assumptions are *already* sole-source procurements (BHS controls integration, common-use platform continuity) before they reach an RFP dressed as competitive.

### 2.7 The cooperative-purchasing and IDIQ toolkit

The Manual permits riding other governments' competitively-awarded contracts (GSA, MWCOG, "other federal, state, regional, county, or local" entities) on a written best-interest determination (§3.7), and Appendix B authorizes single- and multiple-award task-order (IDIQ) contracts (§B.8) plus MACCs for recurring construction (§6.2.1.3). The tools exist. They are under-deployed in the document's emphasis. Job order contracting and on-call IDIQ vehicles cut delivery time on recurring small-to-medium work by **as much as 25%** [Source: https://www.gordian.com/resources/what-is-job-order-contracting/]. The pattern across airport programs is the opposite of optimal: full RFPs for routine buys that could be task orders, and not enough pre-positioned IDIQ capacity for the work that actually needs speed (terminal refresh, deferred maintenance, tenant fit-out support). One caution the thesis must keep: cooperative contracts can violate 2 CFR Part 200 competition rules if the underlying contract was not competed to federal standards — so the speed advantage is real on Authority-funded work and *conditional* on grant-funded work.

### 2.8 Audit posture

Here the Manual earns its keep. The public quarterly report listing every award, the basis for any non-competition, the extent of competition, and the awarding official (§1.5.2); the centralized delegation registry (§1.4.4); the mandatory procurement-integrity training (§1.6.3) — these are direct answers to the 2012 findings. A modernization roadmap that trades any of these away for speed will produce audit headlines, not capability. The reform discipline is to find speed *inside* a clean audit posture, not by spending it down.

### 2.9 The authority's procurement model

MWAA is *sui generis* — an independent interstate-compact authority with its own Inspector General, not a municipal department (O'Hare, LAWA), not a port authority (PANYNJ), not a state DOT. That independence is why §1.7 can exempt it from FAR and state code, and why the LaGuardia-style P3 / unsolicited-proposal model used by the Port Authority on its $4B Central Terminal B [Source: https://www.trccompanies.com/projects/laguardia-airport-redevelopment-public-private-partnership-p3/] is available to MWAA only to the extent its compact and Board allow. The Manual already accepts unsolicited proposals (§3.8.7) and authorizes pilot programs (§3.8.8) — two latent innovation channels the thesis can amplify without legislation. Anchor every recommendation to *this* rulebook and *this* governance, not the generic federal frame.

## 3. The Schedule-Honest Verdict

For the thesis's main capital action — the new Dulles concourse and the early-mobilization elements of the $6.99B program — the procurement math supports a **notice-to-proceed roughly 24–36 months after Board direction** for vertical work on largely-disturbed ground, and **36–48 months** for any element on new ground requiring NEPA and Section 106 (the fifth runway, new footprints). A master plan assuming first-shovel inside 18 months is assuming away the federal-review stack and the Authority's own 30-day advertising floors, multi-step evaluations, and Board-approval cycles. The Manual itself is not the long pole; it is reasonably efficient. The long pole is everything the federal funding stack bolts on top of it — which is precisely why the missing FAA/BIL chapter matters.

## 4. The Trade Map

Each major funding source, and the obligation it imports — stated as the trade the Board makes implicitly when it accepts the dollar:

- **AIP entitlement/discretionary (FAA):** accept the dollar, accept **qualifications-based A/E selection (no price competition for design)**, FAA prior approval for any noncompetitive award, and grant-assurance flowdowns — design freedom narrows to FAA-eligible scope [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/procurement/general].
- **BIL Airport Terminal Program / Airport Infrastructure Grants:** accept the dollar, accept **full BABA domestic-content rules (55% on manufactured products)** that constrain equipment and material specification *before* design freezes [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/buy_american].
- **Any AIP/BIL construction labor:** accept **Davis-Bacon prevailing wages**, which set the labor-cost floor and remove "cheaper local labor" as a value lever.
- **Any FAA/FTA/FHWA dollar:** accept the **49 CFR Part 26 DBE goal** and its good-faith-effort/availability-study burden; on thin specialty scopes this means waivers or a compliance finding, not optional participation [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-A/part-26].
- **PFC (49 U.S.C. §40117):** accept FAA approval of the project scope and the airline-consultation requirement — flexible on procurement method but binding on *what* can be built.
- **FTA (Silver Line legacy):** the one stack the Manual fully equips (Chapter 10), now largely behind the Authority — the rigor is pointed at a closing project while the opening $9B airport program runs under thinner federal scaffolding.

If the thesis quietly assumes the full federal stack to make the $9B pencil, the price of that stack is paid in design freedom: QBS removes price competition on design, BABA constrains the equipment palette, Davis-Bacon sets labor cost, DBE shapes the subcontracting tree, and NEPA/Section 106 set the calendar. None of these are decorative, and none are infinitely flexible.
