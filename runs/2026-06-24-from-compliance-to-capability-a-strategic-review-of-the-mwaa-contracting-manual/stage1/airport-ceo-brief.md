# Airport CEO Brief — From Compliance to Capability: The MWAA Contracting Manual

*Stage 1 independent research brief. Lens: the chief executive's chair at a large hub. The currency is bond covenants, board votes, and 30-year amortization schedules — not procurement cycle time for its own sake.*

---

## The frame, stated plainly

A procurement manual is back-office plumbing. The load-bearing walls of this institution are two instruments the manual **explicitly does not govern**: the Airline Use and Lease Agreement and the federal lease that lets MWAA hold the airports at all. The thesis asks whether the Contracting Manual lets MWAA "operate as a high-performing, innovation-oriented" authority. The honest CEO answer is: the manual is necessary but not where the institution is won or lost. A modernization roadmap that speeds the plumbing while ignoring how it interacts with the $5.5 billion of debt MWAA is about to issue, the 15-year airline agreement that just took effect, and the rating agencies who price both — is a roadmap that optimizes the wrong variable.

My job here is to tell the Strategist what this thesis costs and what it buys in the only currencies that survive a chair transition.

---

## 1. Key findings — for a board chair reading once

- **The manual excludes the contract that matters most.** Page 1 states the Manual "is not applicable to … Airline Use and Lease Agreements." MWAA's most consequential negotiated instrument — the 15-year agreement effective January 1, 2025, carrying a ~$9 billion capital program — sits outside the document this run is reviewing. Any "procurement modernization" thesis that doesn't name this boundary is selling the board a remodel of the guest bathroom while the foundation is being poured next door.

- **MWAA is about to lever up hard, and the rating agencies have already said so.** Coverage was a healthy 3.16x in 2024, but Moody's expects the net-revenue debt-service coverage ratio to fall toward **~1.3x** as MWAA issues roughly **$5.5 billion** of new debt between 2025 and 2028. That is the window in which procurement *execution risk* converts directly into basis points on the next issuance. Slow, defensible contracting that delivers projects on budget is a credit asset. Fast contracting that produces a Denver-style overrun is a credit event.

- **The Board's own approval threshold has not kept pace with the capital program.** The Board reserves approval for any contract ≥ **$3,000,000**, any concession generating ≥ $3M annual revenue, and any less-than-full-and-open award ≥ $200,000. On a $9B program, $3M is a rounding error — which means an enormous share of capital actions land on a board agenda. This is the single most defensible, board-absorbable cycle-time reform in the entire document, and it is a governance question the board can answer about itself.

- **Governance here is a four-principal bistate compact, not a city department — and that is the whole game.** The 17-member board answers to four appointing powers (7 Virginia, 4 D.C., 3 Maryland, 3 the U.S. President), with six-year terms renewable once. No single principal controls a majority. A reform that needs sustained board will must survive a chair rotation across appointing authorities who do not share a political master.

- **The federal partners run on their own clock, and one of them is MWAA's landlord.** MWAA does not own its airports; it leases them from the United States through 2067. PFC is frozen at $4.50 (unchanged in the 2024 FAA reauthorization), AIP is authorized at only ~$4B/year nationally through FY2028, and the BIL supplemental airport grants expire September 30, 2026. The capital program the manual must deliver is funded by a stack with a hard federal ceiling and a closing window.

- **Carrier concentration is a rating input, and MWAA carries it on both sides.** United anchors Dulles as a fortress hub; American dominates the slot-constrained, perimeter-ruled Reagan National. Rating agencies bucket single-carrier exposure at ~30% / 30–60% / >60%. The new agreement deliberately shifts up to $300M from Reagan to subsidize Dulles — a cross-subsidy the agencies and the dominant carriers both watch closely.

- **Innovation tools already exist in the manual; the constraint is appetite, not authority.** Unsolicited proposals, pilot programs, stipends, RFIs, two-step IFBs, and best-value tradeoff are all present. The gap is not a missing clause — it's the institutional willingness to use them when a slow, fully-competed path is the career-safe default. Reform should target the *default*, not add tools.

## 2. Evidence

### What the manual actually says (the primary source)

The Foreword scopes the document: it governs procurement of goods, services, concessions, and construction for Reagan National, Dulles, the Dulles Toll Road, and the Silver Line, "and … is not applicable to … Airline Use and Lease Agreements or other similar arrangements with the airline carriers." [Source: outputs/sources/.../Contracting_Manual-v6_0-05-20-2026-2.pdf.extracted.md, Page 1] **This is the conflict I am obligated to name openly:** my default evidence base treats the airline use-and-lease agreement as the most strategically important contract a hub CEO touches. The Manual deliberately walls it off. The Strategist should not let a reader infer that fixing the Manual fixes MWAA's commercial position with its carriers — it cannot, by the document's own terms.

The delegation structure: the Board "has reserved the right to approve: (i) Contracts … at a cost of $3,000,000 or more (including … all option years); (ii) concessions Contracts or leases … of $3,000,000 or more [annual revenue]; and (iii) any Contract … through less than full and open competition at a value of $200,000 or more." [Source: ...extracted.md, Page 21, §1.4.1] The CEO holds broad delegated authority, including *all* competitively offered construction contracts and the power to grant 12-month concession extensions with notice to the Business Administration Committee. [Source: ...extracted.md, Page 21, §1.4.2]

The authority is statutory and bistate: the Manual is adopted under "Va. Code §5.1-156" and "D.C. Code §9-905." [Source: ...extracted.md, Page 21, §1.3] The innovation tools exist: unsolicited proposals must be "innovative and unique" but still trigger advertising and competition before award [Source: ...extracted.md, Page 44, §3.8.7]; pilot programs are capped at "typically no longer than one year" and require VP-OSCM approval [Source: ...extracted.md, Page 45, §3.8.8]. Chapter 10 carries a parallel FTA-assisted regime for the federally funded Silver Line — meaning MWAA already runs two procurement rulebooks in one house.

### The credit lens

MWAA's Airport System Revenue Bonds are rated **Aa3 (Moody's) / AA- (S&P) / AA- (Fitch), stable**, with the most recent reports in May 2025. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/financial-statistics/investor-relations/credit-ratings] Debt-service coverage was **3.16x in 2024** (up from 2.77x in 2023), and the Aviation Liquidity Fund held roughly **1,006 days cash on hand** at year-end 2024 — exceptional liquidity. But Moody's flagged that ~**$5.5 billion** of new debt across 2025–2028 will drive net-revenue coverage down toward **~1.3x**, from 1.63x in 2024. [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-coming-to-market-with-829-million-municipal-bond-deal]

Fitch evaluates five dimensions — Revenue Risk-Volume, Revenue Risk-Price, **Infrastructure Development and Renewal**, Debt Structure, and Financial Profile — with explicit attention to single-carrier concentration (bucketed at ~30% / 30–60% / >60%) and the rate-setting framework. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airport-bond-ratings] "Infrastructure Development and Renewal" is the dimension procurement directly touches: the agencies are asking whether this authority can deliver a multibillion-dollar program without blowing the budget. That is a procurement-governance question wearing a credit-rating costume.

### The airline agreement and carrier leverage

The agreement effective January 1, 2025 is a 15-year pact requiring sign-off by carriers representing a majority of flights, bundling a ~$9 billion capital program (including ~$6.99B at Dulles — a 14-gate concourse and fifth-runway design) and permitting up to **$300 million** to be shifted from Reagan to offset Dulles operating costs over a decade. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/airports-authority-board-approves-use-and-lease-agreement-airlines-addresses-reagan-dulles] United runs Dulles as a hub with 200+ daily departures [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/airports-authority-and-united-airlines-execute-long-term-lease-dulles-international]; Dulles CPE peaked at $26.47 in 2013 [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/financial-and-statistics]. The "majority of flights" sign-off requirement is the functional equivalent of a Majority-in-Interest clause: the dominant carriers hold a structural veto over the capital program the procurement system is being asked to deliver faster.

### The federal stack and its calendars

The airports are leased from the United States under the Metropolitan Washington Airports Act of 1986; the original 50-year lease has been extended to 80 years, **expiring June 2067**. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Washington_Airports_Authority] The 2024 FAA Reauthorization Act left the **PFC frozen at $4.50**, authorized AIP at ~$4B/year through FY2028, and did not extend the BIL supplemental airport grants, which **expire September 30, 2026**. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/passenger-facility-charges] [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/bil-airport-infrastructure-grants] Reagan National additionally lives under a federal slot-and-perimeter statute that Congress periodically reopens — a political variable no manual can route around. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/assets/720/710987.pdf]

### Governance composition

17 members: 7 appointed by the Governor of Virginia (confirmed by the General Assembly), 4 by the D.C. Mayor (confirmed by Council), 3 by the Maryland Governor, and 3 by the President (Senate advice and consent). Six-year terms, renewable once; members may not hold other political office and serve uncompensated. [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/about-authority/board-directors/board-members] [Source: https://law.lis.virginia.gov/authorities/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority/]

### The precedent that should frighten the board

Denver International terminated its $650M+ Great Hall P3 in August 2019 after the contract was signed at only **30% design**, with the partner projecting **$288 million** in overruns and 2+ years of delay; DEN reverted to an airport-led CM/GC model and an After-Action Report. Auditors later faulted DEN for *both* heavy-handed interference *and* insufficient oversight. [Source: https://www.cpr.org/2019/08/13/denver-international-airport-will-terminate-the-great-hall-renovation-contract/] [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/denver-great-hall-p3-failure] The lesson is not "avoid innovative delivery." It is that procurement *speed* without procurement *discipline* at the design-maturity gate produces the single most expensive failure mode a hub CEO can author.

## 3. The financial-and-political case, as the CEO sees it

### For the thesis (why I'd spend capital on it)

- **The timing aligns with the largest capital push in MWAA history.** A procurement system that shaves months off the front end of a $9B program — through earlier industry engagement, progressive design-build, and a raised board threshold — converts directly into faster revenue-generating asset delivery and stronger coverage during the very window Moody's says coverage is thinnest.
- **Cycle time is a competitiveness argument with the carriers.** Every quarter a concourse slips is a quarter of CPE pressure the dominant carriers will use against me in the next MII fight. Faster, disciplined delivery is leverage I can spend at the negotiating table.
- **Most of the tools are already in the manual.** I am not asking the board to authorize something novel and risky; I am asking it to make underused, already-approved mechanisms the default. That is a low-political-cost reform.

### Against the thesis (where it can cost me votes, basis points, or the room)

- **Speed is not free in credit terms.** The agencies reward predictability of delivery, not velocity. A reform marketed as "faster procurement" that a rating analyst reads as "weakened controls during a $5.5B issuance program" can widen spreads. The framing must be *disciplined throughput*, never *deregulation*.
- **The four-principal board punishes anything that smells like reduced oversight.** A bistate compact with three of seventeen seats appointed by the President operates under permanent OIG/GAO scrutiny (MWAA has a documented governance history that invites it). A roadmap that reads as "fewer approvals" hands organized opposition — a skeptical appointee, a delegation member, a watchdog — a free shot.
- **The most important contracts are out of scope.** Even a flawless manual leaves the airline agreement and the federal lease untouched. Selling this internally as transformational over-promises; the institution's strategic position turns on instruments this document explicitly excludes.

The CEO's reconciliation: **back the thesis, but reframe it.** The deliverable the board can vote for is "raise the obsolete $3M threshold, default to progressive/best-value delivery on major capital, and codify a design-maturity gate so we never sign a Great Hall at 30% design." That is a value-creation story the rating agencies and the airlines both reward — and it survives a chair transition because it strengthens controls where they matter while removing them where they only add delay.

## 4. Data points and precedents a strategist can use

1. **The $3M board threshold against a $9B program.** The Board reserves approval at $3,000,000 (incl. options) [Source: ...extracted.md, §1.4.1] while overseeing a ~$9B capital program. Re-indexing the threshold to the program's scale is the highest-impact, lowest-effort, most board-absorbable reform in the document — and it is the board governing *itself*, not staff asking for slack.

2. **Coverage falling from 3.16x to ~1.3x on $5.5B of new debt (2025–2028).** [Source: https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-coming-to-market-with-829-million-municipal-bond-deal] Use this to anchor every procurement-reform recommendation to its credit consequence: the window where execution risk is most expensive is *now*.

3. **The Denver Great Hall failure: signed at 30% design, $288M projected overrun, contract terminated in 14 months.** [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/denver-great-hall-p3-failure] The precedent that lets a strategist argue for a *design-maturity gate* as a pro-speed, pro-credit control — not a bureaucratic one.

4. **The $300M Reagan-to-Dulles revenue shift and "majority of flights" sign-off.** [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/airports-authority-board-approves-use-and-lease-agreement-airlines-addresses-reagan-dulles] Concrete evidence that the dominant carriers hold an MII-equivalent veto over the capital program — and that procurement modernization lives downstream of an airline agreement the manual cannot touch.

5. **BIL airport grants expire September 30, 2026; PFC frozen at $4.50; AIP capped at ~$4B/yr through FY2028.** [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/bil-airport-infrastructure-grants] The federal funding ceiling and closing window that make procurement *cycle time* a real-dollar question: dollars not obligated before the window closes are dollars the bondholders backfill.
