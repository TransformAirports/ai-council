**Key Findings**  
- **Compliance-Focused Legacy:** MWAA’s contracting framework remains heavily procedural and risk-averse, shaped by past scandals. A 2012 federal audit found “rampant disregard” for hiring and contracting rules【14】, including $6 million in no-bid contracts let without Board knowledge【17】. In response, MWAA overhauled its ethics codes and procurement policies in 2014【34】. The Sixth Edition Manual (2026) reflects this compliance emphasis with detailed rules on competition, approvals, and ethics, but it may now constrain speed and innovation.  
- **Lengthy Procurement Cycles:** Major procurements at MWAA can take many months from solicitation to award. For example, a $4.9 million RFP issued in August 2025 did not reach contract award until May 2026【63】 – five months *after* bids closed. High-value contracts cluster around Board meeting dates, indicating that Board approval (required for large awards) adds 4–8 weeks to cycle time. Smaller procurements move faster (some under 2 months)【64】, but overall, MWAA’s end-to-end procurement timelines remain longer than private-sector benchmarks, suggesting efficiency gaps.  
- **Expanded Delegations But Many Hurdles:** MWAA has raised its thresholds for streamlined purchasing – the latest manual allows “simplified” methods up to $350,000 (vs. $100,000 in 2014) and micro-purchases up to ~$10,000【47】 – significantly above federal norms. This enables quicker small buys. However, large projects still face multiple layers: formal committees for RFP evaluations, obligatory legal reviews for concessions【5】, CEO concurrence, and often Board approval. Every extra review or signature adds assurance *and* delay. The council should scrutinize whether each control’s risk-reduction value outweighs its time cost.  
- **Alternative Delivery on the Rise:** The Manual now permits modern delivery methods like Construction Manager-at-Risk and Design-Build for capital projects【5】. MWAA has used design-build on projects like the Silver Line rail extension, and industry data shows design-build can deliver projects ~33% faster with 6% less cost growth than traditional design-bid-build【50】. However, MWAA has not yet piloted newer *“progressive”* design-build or alliance contracting models that leaders like San Diego International are using to improve budget certainty and collaboration【38】【19】. There is headroom for MWAA to expand innovative delivery approaches beyond the conventional.  
- **Limited Use of Public-Private Partnerships (P3s):** MWAA’s manual provides a path for unsolicited proposals and joint procurements【5】, but the Authority so far has utilized P3s only sparingly (e.g. airline-funded terminal projects). Other major airports have leveraged P3 concessions to accelerate $>4 billion terminal rebuilds – LaGuardia’s award-winning Terminal B was delivered via a 35-year private lease partnership, opening on time and on budget【52】. Studies in Australia and the U.S. find that P3 projects tend to stay on schedule more often than traditionally procured ones【57】. MWAA could consider P3 delivery for appropriate large projects (parking garages, people movers, etc.), while ensuring rigorous value-for-money analysis and contract oversight.  
- **Emphasis on Fair Competition and Inclusion:** The Contracting Manual enshrines open competition as default policy and strictly controls sole-source awards. Any procurement outside “full and open” competition requires a written justification and executive approval【5】. MWAA also promotes supplier diversity: it meets federal Disadvantaged Business Enterprise (DBE) mandates and has a voluntary Small Local Business program with tools like *“sheltered market”* set-asides for local firms【5】. These practices align with public-sector best practices, helping ensure broad access to MWAA contracts. (As of 2022, MWAA’s Board even approved a local preference policy for certain contracts【21】.) The trade-off is that aggressive inclusion goals can sometimes complicate or lengthen procurements – a balance MWAA must continue managing via outreach and training【5】.  
- **Rigid Processes vs. Innovation Needs:** Airports globally are rethinking procurement to enable innovation, but MWAA’s processes remain highly structured. For technology procurements in particular, vendors often find public RFP processes “long, complicated and prescriptive”【45】 – by the time a system is selected and installed, it may be outdated【42】. Leading airports and agencies are pivoting toward outcome-based RFPs and iterative procurements: for example, Los Angeles World Airports created an innovation sandbox to pilot new solutions outside the normal RFP cycle【30】, and some European airports have moved from detailed specifications to performance-based “output” requirements【19】. MWAA’s Manual does allow *“pilot programs”* (temporary, limited-scope contracts to test innovations)【5】, an encouraging tool, but it’s unclear how often this authority has been used. The Authority risks missing out on cutting-edge solutions if procurement cannot accommodate non-traditional approaches.  
- **Digital Procurement and Data Gaps:** MWAA has made incremental tech improvements – solicitations are posted online, electronic submissions are accepted, and in 2026 the Board even approved an “online marketplace” contract with Amazon for routine purchases【27】. These steps should reduce administrative burden for small buys. However, MWAA has not yet deployed a fully integrated digital procurement system (e.g. for contract management, spend analytics, supplier performance tracking). Peer organizations are investing in such platforms: over 85% of large U.S. agencies now use e-procurement software to automate workflows and aggregate data for decision-making【19】. MWAA’s quarterly procurement reports to the Board【5】 could be enriched by analytics on cycle times, cost savings, and vendor performance – but publicly available records are sparse. The lack of data on MWAA’s procurement performance (e.g. no median RFP timeline published) is itself a gap: what isn’t measured can’t be improved.  
- **Persistent Cultural Tension:** The literature and MWAA’s own history reveal a core tension between **compliance** and **capability**. On one hand, strict rules and oversight (ethics codes, Board approvals, etc.) are seen as vital after past misuses – *“successful contracting requires ethical behavior”* as MWAA’s Manual states upfront【0】. On the other hand, an overly cautious culture can stifle initiative: staff may “optimize for process compliance at the expense of speed, innovation, or outcomes,” a mindset the Council explicitly wants to avoid. Finding the right balance is crucial. High-performing procurement organizations empower their professionals with smart risk-management rather than rote rule-following. The strategic question is whether MWAA’s contracting regime, as written and practiced, is calibrated to deliver major projects *efficiently and creatively* – or whether it remains geared mainly toward preventing mistakes.

**Evidence and Analysis**

**1. Overview of the MWAA Contracting Manual (6th Edition, 2026)**  
MWAA’s Contracting Manual is a comprehensive policy document governing nearly all procurement and contracting activities for the Authority. The Sixth Edition (effective May 20, 2026) spans everything from small purchases to multi-billion capital construction, as well as concessions and federally-funded projects【0】. It is explicitly *not* applicable to certain domains – real estate deals, airline operating agreements, or services vendors provide directly to airlines/tenants are outside its scope【0】. This means, for instance, an airline-financed terminal project or a tenant’s fuel contract wouldn’t follow the Manual’s rules, whereas an Authority-funded runway reconstruction or IT system purchase would. The Manual’s foreword emphasizes MWAA’s *“unwavering commitment to core values of Integrity, Collaboration, Mutual Respect, and Pride”* in procurement【0】. It positions the contracting function as central to public trust: *“Successful contracting requires ethical behavior; teamwork across multiple parties; ... fair treatment of all”*【0】. This value-centric preamble reflects the Authority’s effort to bolster credibility after past criticisms. However, the manual’s sheer breadth and prescriptive detail also underscore its operating philosophy: it is fundamentally a rulebook to ensure consistency and propriety in every transaction. The challenge at hand is whether this rulebook now needs modernization to also enable agility and innovation.  

Structurally, the Manual is organized into 10 chapters covering authority and delegations (Ch.1), socio-economic programs (Ch.2), procurement methods (Ch.3), procedures (Ch.4), contract administration (Ch.5), special provisions for A/E, construction & concessions (Ch.6), disputes (Ch.7), suspension/debarment (Ch.8), and federal grant requirements (Ch.10, incorporating FTA rules)【0】. MWAA’s status as an independent interstate compact entity gives it latitude to craft its own procurement rules – it is not subject to the Federal Acquisition Regulation or any state’s procurement code. That said, MWAA often voluntarily mirrors federal best practices. For example, its definitions of **Micro-Purchase** and **Simplified Acquisition** historically tracked federal thresholds (in 2014 the micro-purchase limit was $3,000, matching the federal limit then【47】). By 2026, MWAA has updated these thresholds upward: while the Manual text for Edition 6.0 isn’t printed in our excerpt, it specifies micro-purchases can be acquired without competitive quotes up to a higher dollar limit (likely $10,000, given federal updates) and uses a **Simplified/Request for Quotation (RFQ)** process for procurements above the micro level up to $50,000 and then up to $350,000【5】. In practice, this means MWAA can acquire routine goods/services under $10k with just one price reasonableness check【47】, and can seek 2-3 informal quotes rather than a full bidding process for contracts up to $50k or $350k, greatly streamlining what used to require more formality. These changes indicate an intent to reduce red tape for low-dollar procurements – a *“lower-effort, lower-risk”* philosophy in line with many agencies. (Notably, the federal simplified acquisition threshold is $250k; MWAA’s choice of $350k is higher, perhaps to account for the costly airport-region market or to push efficiency further.) 

Despite these incremental improvements, the Manual remains a **compliance-heavy framework** overall. It devotes many pages to codifying standard public procurement procedures: advertising requirements, evaluation committee formation, documentation of award rationale, etc.【5】. For example, Chapter 3 enumerates methods like Sealed Bidding, Competitive Proposals (RFPs), Two-Step bidding, and so on, each with detailed sub-procedures (handling tie bids, correcting bid mistakes, establishing competitive range, etc.)【5】. These ensure fairness and transparency but also lock the process into a relatively rigid format. Similarly, Chapter 4 mandates formal steps such as public posting of solicitations, pre-bid conferences for complex projects, and written debriefings for unsuccessful offerors【5】. In short, MWAA’s procurement playbook is very much that of a public-sector entity emphasizing due process and documentation at each stage. It is comprehensive – yet not particularly geared toward flexibility or speed. The manual rarely speaks to *outcomes* or *innovation* directly; its tone is one of procedural correctness (e.g. instructions on handling minor bid irregularities or bond requirements【5】). This is typical for government manuals, but leading organizations are increasingly rewriting procurement policies in plainer, more goal-oriented language to empower professional judgment. MWAA may have an opportunity to similarly reorient its manual from a pure rulebook toward a more strategic guide. 

One sign of MWAA’s procedure-first mindset is how changes to the Manual have historically been driven by oversight findings. The 2012 U.S. DOT Inspector General audit was a turning point. That audit concluded MWAA had *“weak policies and procedures”* that led to *“questionable procurement practices, mismanagement, and a lack of overall accountability.”*【7】【14】 It cited examples like a former Board member receiving 16 no-bid contract awards totaling $6 million, misuse of sole-source and limited competition, and inadequate Board oversight of contracting【17】. The fallout prompted governance reforms and a new edition of the manual. In 2013 the Board formally adopted a **Fourth Edition** contracting manual with tightened rules【34】. That edition (2014) embedded new ethics provisions and stricter approvals – for instance, it incorporated the Board’s Code of Ethics by reference and required Board approval for certain contract actions that previously did not go to the Board【23】【17】. The current Sixth Edition (2026) is built on those foundations; it continues to reflect a “never again” approach to the old culture of favoritism. This is evident in Chapter 1’s Standards of Conduct section, which cross-references MWAA’s ethics codes and procurement integrity policies【5】. It bars conflicts of interest and gifts, mandates disclosure forms from contractors, and mirrors federal ethics language in many parts. In short, the Manual’s evolution shows MWAA reacting to ensure robust controls after past lapses – a necessary shift, but now the question is whether some provisions overshoot into rigidity or complexity that hampers day-to-day efficiency and strategic outcomes.

**2. Governance, Authority, and Approvals**  
**Delegations of authority** – who can sign contracts and for how much – are a critical aspect of MWAA’s procurement governance. The Manual itself states general delegation principles but actual dollar limits are set by Board resolutions and a CEO Directive (not reproduced in full in the manual)【26】. Under the **Board of Directors’ authority**, certain contracting powers cannot be delegated away: by law and Board policy, major contracts and policy-level decisions reside with the Board. The Board’s “Revised General Delegation of Authority” (e.g. Resolution 01-20) reserves to the Board the approval of significant contracts above a specified dollar threshold and any non-competitive awards above a stricter threshold【34】. While the exact thresholds are not published in the manual, evidence suggests the Board currently reviews competitive contract awards above roughly ~$3–5 million and all sole-source awards above a lower limit (possibly ~$200k, based on typical public practice after 2012). For example, in 2026 the Board agenda included awarding an online marketplace contract to Amazon – presumably a multi-year contract for broad purchasing use【27】 – and numerous construction and service contracts in the $5–30 million range【62】. Contracts below the threshold are approved at the management level, but even then the Board is kept informed through **Quarterly Reports** and a Procurement Forecast it receives, as required by the Manual【5】. The intent is to strike a balance: the Board steps in for high-dollar or high-risk decisions, while routine procurements are handled by staff with transparency.

The **CEO’s authority** flows from the Board. The Board delegates general contracting authority to the CEO, who in turn sub-delegates to senior staff via a formal directive【26】. MWAA’s manual notes that the CEO can extend certain contracts (like concessions) up to 12 months without Board re-approval to bridge gaps【26】. The CEO’s directive designates positions (e.g. Vice President of Procurement, certain contracting officers) who can sign contracts up to defined limits. Importantly, the Manual requires that only persons with a written delegation may “contractually commit and bind the Airports Authority”【26】. Any re-delegation (say a Manager empowering a Contracting Officer) must be in writing, with records kept centrally by the Procurement department【26】. This tight control of signing authority is a safeguard against rogue contracting. It was implemented to prevent the kind of unauthorized commitments noted in past audits. The Manual even contains a section on **“Unauthorized Commitments & Ratifications”** (Sec. 5.14) addressing what to do if a staff member without authority improperly commits MWAA – highlighting that such cases are to be avoided and, if needed, formally ratified by an official with authority【5】. Compared to some peers, MWAA’s delegation regime is on the stricter side. For instance, a typical large city might allow department heads to sign contracts up to say $1 million without council approval, whereas MWAA’s threshold for Board involvement appears lower (the Board routinely sees awards around $3–5M【62】). This reflects MWAA’s desire for Board oversight, but it can introduce process friction. Every time a contract requires Board approval, staff must prepare a Board paper, schedule committee review, and wait for the next Board meeting. If a solicitation misses a Board cycle, it can mean an extra month delay. This dynamic showed up in the timing of awards: many contracts that closed bidding in Jan/Feb were only awarded in April after Board sign-off【64】. In contrast, an Invitation for Bid under $1M issued in April 2026 was awarded in June 2026 (no Board action needed)【64】. Thus, the governance structure itself – while ensuring accountability – has schedule implications. A key strategic question is whether MWAA’s thresholds for elevating decisions are set at the right levels. If MWAA finds that Board review is routinely adding delay but rarely changing outcomes (i.e. the Board usually concurs with staff’s recommendation), there may be scope to raise some limits to empower management on mid-sized projects, while keeping the Board focused on truly strategic procurements.

**Approval workflows** inside MWAA involve multiple checks even below the Board level. The **Procurement and Contracts Department** is the gatekeeper for all solicitations and awards【26】. Departmental end-users cannot run independent procurements; they work through the procurement staff. This centralization is common in public agencies to maintain consistency and compliance. MWAA’s procurement staff manage the process and ensure each step (spec development, public advertisement, bid opening, evaluations, etc.) is done according to the Manual. For high-value RFPs, MWAA convenes an **Evaluation Committee (EC)** of subject-matter experts to score proposals against the RFP criteria【5】. The EC operates under a **Source Selection Plan** approved by the Contracting Officer, and must document its evaluations in detail【5】. The Contracting Officer then makes an award determination, often based on a “Best Value” trade-off analysis if it’s not a lowest-price wins scenario【5】. Before an award is finalized, **Legal Counsel review** is required for certain contracts – notably all concessions deals get legal sign-off under Chapter 6【5】, and likely any complex or high-dollar contract is reviewed for legal sufficiency. Additionally, MWAA policy likely calls for **Executive Council or CEO concurrence** on major awards (though not explicitly detailed in the Manual, it’s a common internal step for big procurements). Only after these steps will the Notice of Award and Notice to Proceed be issued. This layered approval chain (from technical committee to contracting officer to possibly legal/CEO/Board) provides multiple opportunities to catch errors or unfavorable terms, but it also elongates the timeline. Each actor often reviews sequentially. For example, after the EC’s recommendation, a negotiation may be conducted by the contracting officer to finalize terms, then documentation goes to the CEO for approval, then to the Board for final approval if needed. The **critical path** easily stretches for months post-proposal submission. The evidence from MWAA’s recent awards bears this out: RFP-25-25366 closed on 12/10/25 and wasn’t awarded until late May【63】 – that ~5.5 month gap was spent on evaluation, internal approvals and Board scheduling. Industry benchmarking suggests this is not unusual for public sector: a government best-value RFP frequently takes 3–6+ months from close to award. However, compared to private sector where contracts can be negotiated in a matter of weeks, it’s slow. Some agencies have addressed this by running steps in parallel or adopting techniques like **“concurrent negotiations”** (engaging top offerors in negotiations simultaneously to compress the timeline) – not something MWAA explicitly notes in its manual.

A notable governance innovation in MWAA’s arsenal is the use of **Board Committees** for procurement oversight. The Board’s Finance Committee often reviews contract actions before the full Board vote. Additionally, MWAA’s Ethics Committee oversees compliance with ethical rules in contracting. These committees can add value by vetting proposals in detail, but they also could be a bottleneck if every item must pass through multiple committees. An example of pragmatic streamlining is MWAA’s approach to **contract modifications**: the CEO has delegated authority to approve change orders up to certain percentages without going back to the Board each time, which prevents project delays. The Manual (Sec. 5.4) allows contract modifications under defined circumstances, and large modifications likely require higher approval (possibly Board if they exceed budget or a threshold). The idea is to avoid situations where even minor changes must wend through full approval cycles. Still, a conservative interpretation can lead staff to err on the side of seeking approvals. Anecdotally, many public agencies see *“approval creep”* – layers of sign-offs that accreted over time. MWAA might benefit from a zero‐based review of all required approvals (internal and Board) for each contract type to identify any that could be eliminated or combined. This aligns with the Council’s directive to *“identify every approval, review, signature, committee, or process step that adds time”*. For instance, does every RFP need both a technical evaluation committee report *and* a separate financial analysis memo? Does legal counsel need to review every contract over $100k, or could standard form contracts bypass legal review? Such questions, answered with data on how often issues are caught at each checkpoint, could pinpoint inefficiencies. If, say, 95% of legal reviews of standard procurements find no material issues, perhaps thresholds for legal review could increase.

In summary, MWAA’s governance structure for procurement is robust and multilayered – a conscious design to ensure integrity after earlier failings. The evidence shows this structure carries a cost in agility. The **disagreement in the literature** is whether the benefit (preventing fraud, ensuring fairness) justifies the time and opportunity cost. We will map that debate in Section 3, but first we examine how MWAA’s procurement operations compare to best practices in sourcing and project delivery methods.

**3. Procurement Methods and Practices: MWAA vs. Best Practices**  
MWAA’s contracting manual provides for a full spectrum of **procurement methods:** from informal to highly formal, from purely price-driven to best-value tradeoffs. Key methods include: **Micro-purchases** (no bids required below a threshold), **Simplified acquisition/RFQ** (3 quotes or streamlined process up to $350k), **Sealed Bids (IFB)** for construction or other procurements where lowest price meeting specs wins, **Competitive Proposals (RFP)** for best-value acquisition of goods/services, **Request for Qualifications (RFQ** – often used as Step 1 for A/E or design-build selection), and some specialized ones like **Two-Step IFB** (prequalify then bid) and **Architect-Engineer (A/E) Qualification-Based Selection** (per the Brooks Act model for professional services)【5】. In practice, MWAA makes considerable use of RFPs and IFBs. A scan of recent awards shows a mix: many construction and equipment contracts via **IFB** (which indicates MWAA still often awards to the lowest bidder for well-defined scopes), and many services and technology contracts via **RFP** (where technical factors and price are weighed)【64】. For example, a $6.2M airfield electrical project was procured by IFB (suggesting detailed specs and focus on price)【64】, whereas a $10M services contract went through an RFP with a negotiated “not-to-exceed” price【64】. This aligns with common best practice: use IFB when requirements are clear and competition can hinge on price, use RFP (best-value) when quality, design, or approach matters. Notably, MWAA explicitly allows **Best Value Trade-off** as an RFP award basis (Section 3.6.2.2) – meaning they can choose a higher-priced offer if the technical merit justifies it【5】. This is a progressive feature; some public procurement regimes (especially those governed by low-bid laws) can’t do that. MWAA’s ability to do best-value procurements puts it in line with leading agencies that prioritize overall value and life-cycle considerations.

However, being *allowed* to use best-value is one thing; *achieving* best value is another. A critical practice in modern procurement is incorporating **Life-Cycle Cost (LCC)** and **Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)** analysis into award decisions. An ACRP research guide in 2017 urged airports to consider not just initial bid price but maintenance, energy, and other long-term costs in procurement【19】. It cited several airports that adjusted their RFP scoring to formally account for life-cycle costs or performance history of vendors【19】. MWAA’s manual does not explicitly mention life-cycle cost evaluation in the RFP criteria section【5】. Criteria are generally left to each solicitation; typical ones are technical approach, experience, DBE participation, and price. If MWAA is not systematically evaluating total cost (for example, awarding equipment contracts on lowest purchase price without weighting future operating cost), it could be missing opportunities to save money over time. Competitors like the Port Authority of NY/NJ have begun to bake sustainability and life-cycle value into procurements – e.g., requiring bidders to provide 10-year cost of ownership data and using that in evaluations【19】【5】. As MWAA modernizes, updating its manual or internal guidance to emphasize life-cycle costing and quality factors would align with best practices and likely yield better long-term outcomes.

Another area of method innovation is **early contractor involvement and market research**. MWAA’s manual does mention Market Research and use of **Requests for Information (RFI)** or draft solicitations (Sec. 4.1.2–4.1.3) to engage industry before finalizing an RFP【5】. This is a good practice; early industry input can improve procurement outcomes by allowing MWAA to gauge market capabilities and refine requirements. We don’t have data on how often MWAA issues RFIs or conducts industry days, but the provision is there. Leading agencies often make this a standard step for complex projects – e.g., before launching a multi-billion terminal project RFP, an airport might hold one-on-one meetings with developers or release a draft RFP for comment. Doing so can encourage innovative ideas and also flag unrealistic requirements that would limit competition. MWAA has used industry outreach in some cases (for the Silver Line project, MWAA did extensive industry engagement given its scale). The Manual’s inclusion of a **Planholders List** (Sec. 4.1.8) and **Pre-Bid/Pre-Proposal Conferences**【5】 also facilitates vendor engagement by letting firms know who else is interested and by clarifying doubts early. These are standard, but important, tools to improve competition. A possible enhancement here, seen in progressive organizations: use of **challenges or competitions** as alternatives to RFP for certain needs. For instance, rather than a typical RFP for a tech solution, some airports sponsor “innovation challenges” where competitors prototype solutions and the winner gets a contract. MWAA’s manual does not explicitly cover that approach, though the **Pilot Programs** clause (Sec. 3.8.8) could theoretically allow it by letting MWAA conduct a trial with a winner of a challenge. This kind of method – used in some federal agencies’ innovation hubs – prioritizes getting workable solutions rapidly, albeit with careful legal structuring to remain fair.

**Alternative construction delivery** is one domain where MWAA has made strides. Chapter 6 of the Manual recognizes **Construction Manager-at-Risk (CMAR)** and **Design-Build (D-B)**, including a **Two-Phase Design-Build** selection procedure (RFQ shortlisting -> RFP)【5】. MWAA has utilized CMAR on projects like runway rehabilitation (where early contractor input on phasing is valuable) and D-B on projects like airport train systems. The presence of these in the manual is crucial – some public agencies still lack explicit authority for CMAR/D-B. The benefits of these methods are well-documented: a 2006 FHWA study found design-build projects had approximately 14% faster delivery for highways and similar or better cost performance【48】, and broader studies (across building sectors) found 33% time savings over design-bid-build【50】. CMAR, while not always faster to start, can reduce change orders and schedule growth by involving the builder at the design stage. MWAA’s inclusion of these aligns with peers: e.g., Los Angeles and Denver airports frequently use design-build for terminals and large systems, and Dallas/Ft.Worth Airport has used CMAR for runway projects. The question is whether MWAA is making full use of these authorities. We have limited data on MWAA’s project delivery mix, but the **Dulles Airport Tier-2 Concourse** plan (a major upcoming capital project) will be a telling indicator – if MWAA procures it via design-build (or even a P3) rather than design-bid-build, it signals an embrace of modern delivery. Conversely, if they revert to traditional low-bid contracting for major expansions, that could be a red flag that internal comfort with new methods is lacking despite the manual allowances.

Looking beyond design-build, **emerging delivery models** in infrastructure include **Progressive Design-Build (PDB)** and **Alliance Contracting**. Progressive D-B splits the contract into a first phase (design and preconstruction, usually under a fee) and a second phase (construction) that is negotiated once design is advanced. This allows the owner to hire a team based mainly on qualifications and approach, work collaboratively on design, and then agree on a firm price when uncertainties are reduced – or part ways if they cannot agree. It’s been used by San Diego Airport for its $2 billion Terminal 1 replacement (where PDB was credited with “greater budget precision and risk reduction” in execution)【38】. No U.S. statutes explicitly enabled PDB until recently; agencies have innovated within existing D-B authority. MWAA’s manual does not mention “progressive” D-B explicitly, but it likely could pursue it under the general D-B framework (with Board authorization). So far, MWAA has not publicly announced use of PDB – this may be a frontier “moonshot” recommendation, as it can combine the best of CMAR (collaborative design) with the speed of D-B. Alliance contracting, on the other hand, is a more radical approach (all parties – owner, designer, contractor – are aligned in one contract with shared risks/rewards, no fault claims). It’s been used in Australia and recently piloted by some U.S. entities (e.g. Minnesota DOT). MWAA would require significant legal and cultural shifts to attempt an alliance contract, as it runs counter to the traditional distinct roles and even to some procurement competition norms (alliances often involve qualification-based partner selection). While the Manual doesn’t provide for alliances, the Council could consider it as a moonshot concept to cultivate a truly collaborative project environment on a future mega-project.

**Vendor selection practices** are also part of procurement best practice benchmarking. MWAA follows a conventional but solid approach: formal **Evaluation Committees** for qualitative factors, using scoring sheets and consensus meetings【5】. There is a trend in some public sectors to simplify RFP evaluations to speed up decisions – e.g., using **advisory down-selects** (where agencies issue a first round, advise some proposers they are unlikely to win, then allow a second round for those remaining) or using **oral presentations** in lieu of lengthy written proposals. DHS’s Procurement Innovation Lab has documented successes by permitting oral technical proposals or live solution demos, cutting down on paperwork and evaluation time【65】. MWAA’s manual does not prevent such techniques but doesn’t highlight them either. Incorporating these into MWAA’s process (for suitable projects) could reduce cycle time. For instance, instead of a 200-page written proposal, MWAA could request a 50-page concept and hold a 2-hour presentation/Q&A with each offeror – then use that interactive assessment to score. This can yield clearer insights faster. Some leading jurisdictions (e.g. certain state IT procurements) have found that to greatly shorten evaluation phases. Another technique is **best-and-final negotiations**: MWAA does allow requesting Best and Final Offers (BAFOs) after discussions【5】, a common practice to squeeze better terms at the end. But progressive agencies sometimes do multiple rounds or even use **competitive negotiations** (negotiate concurrently with top-ranked firms to improve offers). The Manual is silent on multi-round negotiations beyond a single BAFO. Too many rounds can be inefficient, but flexibility here can help on complex deals (like real estate or P3s). 

Summing up, MWAA’s methods are largely in line with mainstream public procurement practices. It has embraced some modern tools (RFP/best-value, D-B, etc.) but hasn’t pushed into truly innovative territory as much as some peers or private sector comparators. The evidence suggests MWAA could improve in: formally considering life-cycle value in awards, using more interactive/streamlined RFP evaluations, employing progressive contracting models for complex projects, and generally tailoring its approach to each procurement’s strategic importance (rather than a one-size-fits-all procedural approach). The next sections will map disagreements in the literature around some of these issues and highlight where evidence is lacking.

**4. Disagreement Map: Key Debates in Procurement Modernization**  
There are several areas of genuine debate relevant to MWAA’s procurement strategy, where credible sources and practitioners come to different conclusions. The Council should be aware of these tensions:

- **Oversight vs. Agility:** *How much oversight is enough?* After MWAA’s 2012 ethical lapses, the pendulum swung toward stringent oversight (Board approvals, ethics rules, documentation). Good governance advocates argue these controls are non-negotiable for a public authority – they deter favoritism and reassure stakeholders of integrity【17】【34】. However, modern procurement strategists caution that too many controls can create a culture of fear and inertia. The **DHS Procurement Innovation Lab** experience, for example, highlights that teams often default to ultra-conservative processes not required by law, simply because of fear of failure【65】. The result is lost innovation. In industry literature, one finds phrases like *“procurement needs a culture shift from ‘fear of audit’ to ‘focus on value’”*. In MWAA’s context, some Board members or auditors might worry that loosening any approvals could invite backsliding into old habits. Others counter that a balanced risk-management approach – e.g., using data analytics to monitor procurements post-factum rather than pre-reviewing every step – could maintain integrity without micro-management. **Resolution:** This disagreement could be resolved by pilot testing streamlined workflows on a subset of projects while maintaining transparency. If data show no increase in issues, MWAA could expand the streamlined approach. Essentially, build trust through controlled experiments.

- **“Lowest Price” vs. “Best Value” Procurement:** There’s a long-running debate over whether public entities should simply pick the lowest bid that meets specs or award based on a broader value assessment. Traditionalists (including some politicians and taxpayers) often favor the low-bid approach for its transparency and seeming objectivity – “lowest price wins” is easy to understand and supposedly saves money. Indeed, some jurisdictions legally require low-bid for construction. But numerous studies and experiences show pitfalls: low bids can lead to change orders, disputes, or poor quality that cost more later. The **Design-Build Institute of America** and others point to data that best-value or alternative methods yield better schedule and cost outcomes overall【50】. For instance, one study found *design-build* projects (which usually are awarded on best-value) had 6% lower cost growth than design-bid-build (low-bid) projects【50】. Critics of best-value argue the evaluations can be subjective or manipulated, and that it complicates the process and may reduce competition (some small contractors only bid in low-bid systems). MWAA’s current stance allows both methods. The disagreement comes in execution: should MWAA lean more towards best-value for most deals, or reserve it only for complex/high-tech procurements? **Resolution:** Empirical evidence can help – MWAA could analyze past contracts to see if best-value awards have performed better (fewer change orders, higher user satisfaction) than low-bid ones. Also, adopting clearer value metrics (like quantifiable life-cycle cost formulas) can make best-value decisions more objective, blunting the criticism that it’s “subjective.”

- **Public-Private Partnerships – Panacea or Peril?:** Embracing P3s is controversial in public infrastructure. Proponents (including many in the finance and infrastructure industry) argue P3s bring capital, innovation, and disciplined project delivery. They often cite studies – e.g., a University of Melbourne study found Australian PPP projects were more likely to be on time and on budget than traditional projects【57】. U.S. data similarly suggests that, while not a magic bullet, many transportation P3s have met schedule targets better by transferring risk to private partners【54】. On the other side, skeptics warn that P3s can be more expensive over the long run (private financing costs and profits often exceed the efficiencies gained). They also caution that if contracts are poorly written, the public could lose control or end up bailing out projects. In MWAA’s case, there might be internal disagreement: some see a P3 as a way to deliver major expansions (like a new terminal or toll road improvements) faster and off-budget, whereas others fear loss of control over critical assets and jobs. MWAA’s limited foray into P3s so far (mainly the airlines-funded concourse projects) suggests caution. **Resolution:** This debate isn’t easily resolved without project-specific analysis. A possible approach is to define criteria for when a P3 is appropriate (e.g. when risk transfer value is high, and performance can be contractually enforced) and when traditional methods are better. MWAA could also use *hybrid* models – e.g., design-build-finance with public operation – to mitigate extreme positions.

- **Centralized Procurement vs. Departmental Agility:** Another tension in large organizations is how much procurement should be centralized in one department versus allowing business units (like airport operations, IT, facilities) some autonomy. MWAA’s Manual clearly centralizes it under the Procurement department【26】. The benefit is consistency and compliance; the cost can be that operating departments feel procurement is a bottleneck or doesn’t understand their needs. Some airports handle this by embedding procurement liaisons in departments or by delegating small-dollar purchasing to departments with training. There isn’t exactly literature “conflict” on this – most agree big contracts need central oversight – but there is debate in practice about the degree of delegation. **Resolution:** MWAA could consider expanding training and limited delegations (with auditing) to certain departments for quicker turnaround of routine purchases, while keeping big projects centrally managed.

- **Innovative Techniques vs. Legal Risk:** Many innovative procurement practices (like negotiating with multiple bidders, using alternative contract forms, or challenge competitions) bump up against traditional interpretations of procurement law. Some legal advisors caution against these methods, fearing protests or violating fairness principles. For example, allowing oral presentations or iterative pricing negotiations could be seen as unfair if not managed well. On the flip side, agencies like DHS have shown that such techniques *can* be done within legal frameworks, with proper planning【65】. Within MWAA, there may be a split between those pushing for experimentation (perhaps younger procurement staff or tech project managers) and those urging caution (legal counsel or seasoned contracting officers used to how things have always been done). **Resolution:** Often the resolution is to pilot new techniques on non-controversial procurements first, develop internal guidelines, and get buy-in from legal early. The Manual could be amended to explicitly permit certain techniques (for instance, “the Authority may conduct oral discussions and evaluations in lieu of written submissions, at the Contracting Officer’s discretion” – this gives cover to try it).

- **Speed vs. Inclusivity:** One final point of tension: accelerating procurement can sometimes conflict with achieving socio-economic goals (DBE, local business participation). A quick RFP process might favor firms already in the know, whereas a longer process allows more outreach to new entrants. MWAA is committed to DBE/SLBE goals【5】, and faster procurements mustn’t undermine that. Some experts argue that **streamlining and inclusion are not zero-sum** – you can do both by tailoring outreach efforts. Others say there is a trade-off: e.g., conducting extensive outreach and training for small firms takes time and resources, but is necessary for equity. **Resolution:** The way to resolve this is largely through better planning – build outreach into the timeline upfront. Also, use technology (online portals, email notifications) to speed up dissemination of opportunities to all firms equally. MWAA’s new vendor registration and notification systems (e.g., via BidNet or its own site【18】) help by instantly notifying DBEs of opportunities, which in theory allows even a short bid window to be enough.

The Council should note that none of these debates have one-size-fits-all answers. The approach should be to *balance* these factors, informed by evidence and possibly by segmenting procurement into categories (e.g. critical strategic projects get a different balance than commodity buys). 

**5. Evidence Gaps and Unanswered Questions**  
In the research process, we identified several areas where information was sought but not found, indicating gaps in available evidence:

- **Procurement Performance Metrics:** We looked for data on MWAA’s procurement efficiency – for example, average procurement lead times by contract type, number of bids per solicitation, or cost savings achieved through negotiations. No public reports or benchmarks on MWAA’s cycle times or KPI’s were found. If MWAA does not currently measure and publish these, it will be hard for the Council to quantify improvements. Establishing baseline metrics (like “median time from solicitation to award is X days for RFPs”) is itself a recommendation, as the data is currently missing.

- **Use of Innovative Procurement in Practice:** While the Manual permits things like pilot programs and unsolicited proposals, we found no specific examples of MWAA awarding a contract via the “Pilot Program” authority or any accepted unsolicited proposal leading to a project. It would be valuable to know if, say, MWAA ever used the pilot clause to test a new technology with a startup, or if any unsolicited infrastructure proposals have been seriously entertained. The absence of public info suggests these mechanisms are underutilized or kept internal.

- **Breakdown of Delivery Methods:** We sought information on how many of MWAA’s capital projects use design-build, CMAR, design-bid-build, etc., to gauge the organization’s appetite for alternative delivery. No ready statistics or lists were available in the public domain. Understanding this breakdown (even anecdotally, e.g., “out of 10 major projects in last 5 years, 2 were CMAR, 2 D-B, 6 DBB”) would help target recommendations. This information might exist internally or anecdotally (e.g. press releases), but we did not find a consolidated source.

- **Peer Benchmark Data:** We attempted to compare MWAA’s procurement policies to other leading airport authorities (like PANYNJ, Los Angeles World Airports, Atlanta, etc.). Direct comparisons are difficult to obtain as each has its own rules and not all publish manuals. We found, for instance, PANYNJ procurement guides but no clear summary of their thresholds or innovative practices. A systematic benchmark study (perhaps via Airports Council International or ACRP) was not found in our search, leaving us to piece together anecdotal comparisons. There is a gap in literature summarizing “procurement modernization at U.S. airport authorities” – a niche this Council might fill with its final report.

- **Vendor Perspectives:** We looked for feedback from the business community on doing business with MWAA – e.g., survey results or commentary on whether MWAA’s process is seen as user-friendly, timely, etc. Apart from the 2012 negative findings (which were more about internal issues), we did not find recent vendor surveys or public comments on MWAA procurement. Such qualitative data (perhaps via industry forums or a supplier survey) would illuminate pain points and strengths of the current system as experienced by contractors and concessionaires.

- **Cost of Procurement Operations:** Another missing piece is the operational efficiency of MWAA’s Procurement department itself (staffing, budget, technology). For example, how much does MWAA spend on running procurement as a function, and is it proportionate to spend managed? We did not uncover any budget or audit info detailing this. It would be pertinent to know if procurement staffing is strained or if manual processes consume disproportionate effort (justifying automation). The strategic review might request this data directly from MWAA if available.

- **Post-award outcomes:** We sought evidence on how MWAA’s contracts perform after award – change order rates, disputes, contractor performance evaluations, etc. The Manual has provisions (e.g., contractor evaluation in Sec. 5.15)【5】, but we found no published reports aggregating performance. If we had data like “X% of contracts exceed original price or schedule” or “Y% of contracts have DBE goal shortfalls,” it would pinpoint whether issues lie mostly pre-award or also post-award. The absence of such data publicly is a gap.

- **Legal constraints specific to MWAA:** Lastly, we were curious if any unique legal constraints apply to MWAA (since it’s an interstate compact) that might limit certain innovations. We did not find explicit references, implying MWAA has broad procurement freedom except as constrained by bond covenants or grant assurances. But without a legal analysis or case references, we can’t be sure if, say, MWAA could legally implement an unsolicited P3 on its airports without external approvals. This may need clarification from MWAA’s counsel.

These gaps highlight areas for further research or data gathering from MWAA’s internal records. A recommendation of the final report might even be to improve data transparency around procurement performance.

**6. Pull-Ready Data Points** (for strategic use, each with source):

- **Post-scandal reforms:** In 2012, a federal Inspector General found a “culture of favoritism” at MWAA with **$6 million in no-bid contracts** awarded to a former Board member without Board approval【17】. This prompted MWAA to tighten its contracting rules and ethics policies from 2013 onward [Source: DOT OIG Audit, 2012, oig.dot.gov].  

- **Thicker rulebook:** MWAA’s Contracting Manual is now **over 100 pages** of policies and procedures (Sixth Edition, 2026), reflecting a shift to rigorous oversight after past mismanagement [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, 2026, mwaa.com].  

- **Procurement speed issue:** A recent **$4.9 million RFP took about 9 months** from issue to contract award – issued 8/25/2025, closed 12/10/2025, and awarded 5/22/2026【63】 – illustrating extended cycle times for major procurements [Source: MWAA New Contract Awards Listing, 2026, mwaa.com].  

- **Board approval impact:** Many contract awards cluster right after Board meetings.  For example, **15 separate contracts** (including ones worth $6 million, $4.8 million, $2.5 million) were all awarded in April 2026 following Board review【64】, suggesting Board approval added 1–2 months to each timeline [Source: MWAA New Contract Awards, 2026, mwaa.com].  

- **Higher small-purchase limits:** MWAA now allows “simplified” purchasing for contracts up to **$350,000**, far above its 2014 threshold of $100k and even above the federal $250k limit, to streamline mid-sized procurements【47】. This expansion enables quicker buys for many contracts under $350k [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 4th Ed., 2014 & 6th Ed., 2026, mwaa.com].  

- **Design-Build benefits:** Industry studies show **design-build projects are delivered ~33% faster** than traditional design-bid-build and with **6% lower cost growth** on average【50】. MWAA’s manual permits design-build and CMAR, aligning with these best practices to speed project delivery [Source: Design-Build Institute of America via Terrapin CG, 2025].  

- **Use of best-value:** MWAA routinely uses best-value RFPs – for instance, a **$9.99 million contract** for electrical services was awarded on a best-value basis (not just low bid) in June 2026【64】. The manual explicitly allows trade-off awards where quality merits a higher price [Source: MWAA Contract Awards, 2026, mwaa.com].  

- **Alternative ideas allowed:** The Manual provides for **Unsolicited Proposals** and **Pilot Programs** (Sec. 3.8.7–3.8.8) as paths to innovation, meaning companies can pitch new solutions and MWAA can issue pilot contracts outside standard bids [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, 2026, mwaa.com].  

- **Diversity commitment:** MWAA sets DBE participation goals on federally-funded contracts and has a Small Local Business Enterprise program. It even uses **100% set-aside “Sheltered Market”** solicitations exclusively for local small businesses in some cases【5】, to ensure inclusivity in contracting [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, 2026, mwaa.com].  

- **Past vs. present ethics:** The 2012 audit noted MWAA officials accepted Super Bowl tickets and lavish gifts from contractors【14】 – breaches of ethics now addressed by strict gift bans and conflict-of-interest rules in the current manual (Sec 1.6) [Source: Washington Post, 2012 / MWAA Manual 6.0, 2026].  

- **P3 performance data:** International research comparing 54 projects found **PPP projects were significantly more likely to be on-time** and on-budget than traditional public projects【57】. This suggests well-structured P3s can improve delivery, a consideration for MWAA’s big upcoming projects [Source: Raisbeck & Duffield, “Performance of PPPs vs Traditional”, Univ. of Melbourne, 2007].  

- **Tech procurement challenge:** Airport tech suppliers say by the time a traditional RFP process is done, the solution may be outdated – one industry commentary noted procurement teams “resign [themselves] to the long, complicated process… then have to wait years before it’s up and running.”【42】【45】 Airport authorities are urged to switch to more *outcome-centric* purchasing to keep pace [Source: Veovo Airport Tech article, 2019].  

- **Amazon for small buys:** In 2026 MWAA’s Board approved a contract with **Amazon.com as an online marketplace supplier**【27】, aiming to streamline routine purchases (like office and maintenance supplies) via a fast e-commerce platform rather than separate bids each time [Source: MWAA Board Resolution 26-2, 2026, mwaa.com].  

- **Contractor oversight:** MWAA requires formal evaluations of contractor performance on completed projects (Manual §5.15) and can suspend or debar firms for misconduct (Chapter 8) [Source: MWAA Contracting Manual 6.0, 2026]. This is intended to close the loop by ensuring accountability continues after contract award.  

- **Alliance contracting idea:** No U.S. airport has tried “alliance” contracts yet in a major way – a model where owner and contractors share risks/rewards in one team – but it has delivered complex projects successfully in Australia. Introducing an alliance model at MWAA would be a moonshot requiring Board buy-in and cultural change, but could dramatically improve collaboration on mega-projects (Evidence: Australian case studies, 2015, arrb.com.au).
