# Virtual Chris — Stage 1 Brief
### From Compliance to Capability: the connections the Contracting Manual is sitting on

*The lens here is deliberate. Every other chair on this council will inventory what the manual gets wrong. My job is to map what it already makes possible — the adjacencies, the latent alliances, the openings — so the Strategist can hand the reader an argument that is not just defensible but actionable. Optimism about possibilities, rigor about facts.*

---

## The reframe that organizes everything

Read the manual (Edition 6.0, effective May 20, 2026) once and you see a careful, FAR-flavored control regime: micro-purchase pegged to "the then-current threshold established by the FAR" (§3.2 — now $15,000 after the October 1, 2025 inflation adjustment), simplified acquisition to $350,000 (§3.3, mirroring the federal SAT increase from $250K the same day), a full Federal Transit Administration chapter (Chapter 10), and a Board that reserves approval for any contract at or above $3,000,000, and any less-than-full-and-open award at or above $200,000 (§1.4.1).

Read it twice, against the external record, and a different fact jumps out. The Department of Transportation's own Inspector General has stated that **MWAA "is not subject to Federal or State laws that govern procurement, ethics, civil service, and transparency"** — it is bound only by the Airports Act, the DOT lease, and the interstate compact. MWAA adopts FAR thresholds, FAR-style methods, and FTA discipline *by choice*, not by command, for the large share of its work that isn't federally grant-funded.

That is the connection the whole report should turn on. **MWAA's structural position is a procurement arbitrage that almost no peer enjoys** — it can borrow the best of the federal regime *and* the best of the commercial/progressive regime because it is captive to neither. The manual currently spends that freedom mimicking federal conservatism. The 2012 DOT OIG report ("MWAA's Weak Policies and Procedures Have Led to Questionable Procurement Practices…") and the reform pressure from Rep. Eleanor Holmes Norton explain *why* the dial is set to caution. But a scar from 2012 should not permanently set the throttle for a $22 billion capital decade.

**The conflict with my evidence base, named plainly:** the manual presents federal procurement norms as a default ceiling. The OIG record shows they are, for non-grant work, an optional floor MWAA chose. The opportunity is to keep the integrity the scar bought and stop paying the conservatism tax the scar also installed.

## 1. The lattice — eight connections the thesis activates

**1. Capital surge ↔ Multiple Award Construction Contracts (§6.2.1.3) ↔ Office of Engineering / COO.** The Dulles revitalization shared with airlines in May 2026 runs to roughly **$22 billion through 2034**, on top of the **~$190 million FAA-funded Concourse E** build already underway. The manual *already authorizes* multiple-award construction vehicles, CMAR, and design-build (§6.2.1). LAWA's March 2025 **$5 billion Multiple Award Task Order Contract** — a pre-qualified pool of 31 prime design-builders, 13 of them first-time primes — is the working proof. *Why it matters: this turns a sequential, project-by-project procurement bottleneck into a standing pool, the single highest-leverage speed move available without changing a word of policy.*

**2. DBE/SLBE program (Chapter 2) ↔ first-time-prime pipeline ↔ the Board's regional constituency.** The sheltered-market 100% set-aside (§2.4.4) and SLBE machinery are usually filed under compliance. Bolted to the capital surge, they become a regional economic-development story the Board's Virginia, D.C., and federal appointees can all claim. *Why it matters: LAWA converted a $5B pool into 13 new prime contractors — MWAA can make the $22B build the largest small-business on-ramp in the region's history, and that is a political asset, not a cost.*

**3. FTA Chapter 10 (Silver Line) ↔ FAA/BIL grant compliance ↔ future federal money.** The foreword notes Chapter 10 governs FTA-assisted contracts "until completed" — the Metrorail Silver Line is winding down. The instinct will be to treat Chapter 10 as dead weight. *Why it matters: don't retire the discipline — repurpose it. The Concourse E FAA grant and any BIL/AIP dollars need exactly this clause-matrix rigor (Appendix C). Chapter 10 is a reusable compliance template, already built and audit-tested.*

**4. Unsolicited proposals (§3.8.7) + Pilot programs (§3.8.8) ↔ technology/innovation initiatives ↔ Virginia's PPEA/PPTA model.** These are the two thinnest sections in the manual — the pilot-program provision is a single paragraph requiring only VP OSCM approval. They are also the latent private-capital and innovation front door. Virginia's VAP3 office runs formal public-private intake for the Department of Aviation under the PPTA/PPEA; MWAA isn't bound by those statutes but can copy the doorway. *Why it matters: MWAA already holds the authority to accept innovation and private capital — it simply hasn't built the intake ramp that turns a good unsolicited idea into a contract.*

**5. Cooperative purchasing (§3.7.1, §10.4.8.6 Federal Supply Schedules) ↔ GSA OASIS+ / Sourcewell / OMNIA ↔ OSCM cycle time.** The manual permits buying off contracts competitively procured by other governmental entities and off federal schedules. GSA's **OASIS+ Phase II** opened rolling awards in May 2026; Sourcewell and OMNIA aggregate competitively-awarded contracts for 90,000+ public agencies. *Why it matters: the competition is already done by the lead agency, so piggybacking collapses procurement lead time on commoditized IT and professional services without sacrificing the competition principle the manual prizes (§3.1).*

**6. Quarterly public reports + procurement forecast (§1.5) ↔ DOT OIG / Norton transparency legacy ↔ supplier market intelligence.** The forecast and the public quarterly report (vendor, dollar value, competition basis, awarding official) were built to answer the post-2012 transparency demand. *Why it matters: the same published forecast is a market-making instrument — it lets suppliers, including SLBEs, prepare and team in advance, which raises competition. A compliance artifact does double duty as a supplier-engagement engine.*

**7. Delegation thresholds ($3M Board / $200K less-than-full-open / $350K simplified) ↔ CEO and VP OSCM ↔ schedule risk on the $22B program.** A $3M Board-approval line calibrated for a steady-state authority becomes a throttle when the program is $22 billion. *Why it matters: at current thresholds the Dulles build implies hundreds of discrete Board actions; a portfolio-level delegation keeps Board oversight at the program tier while freeing transaction speed — control reallocated, not removed.*

**8. Standards of conduct / procurement integrity (§1.6) ↔ the 2012 reputational scar ↔ protest and schedule risk.** Integrity provisions, mandatory debriefings (§4.7), and ADR (§7.3.1.2) are MWAA's reputational moat. *Why it matters: better debriefs and ADR reduce protests, and on a $22B program a single sustained protest is measured in schedule months — integrity and speed are allies here, not opponents.*

## 2. Three pattern matches

**A. Sarbanes-Oxley, and the art of tiering a crisis fix (regulatory history).**
After Enron and WorldCom, SOX Section 404 imposed internal-control testing on every public filer. It restored trust — and became a permanent deadweight cost for clean, routine firms, until regulators *scaled* it (exempting smaller filers, risk-tiering the testing) rather than repealing it. **Mapping:** the 2012 OIG findings were MWAA's Enron moment; the manual's layered controls were its 404. The lesson is precise and load-bearing: the reform that worked elsewhere was not repeal but tiering controls to risk. MWAA's move is identical — keep heavy scrutiny on novel, high-dollar, sole-source actions; thin it on routine, commoditized, cooperative-eligible buys. The run's own success criterion — "determine whether the value of that control exceeds the delay it creates" — is the SOX-scaling test restated.

**B. Slime mold and the Tokyo rail map (network science).**
In a 2010 experiment, *Physarum polycephalum* — a brainless slime mold — was placed on a map with food at the locations of Tokyo's rail stations. It reinforced the nutrient paths that carried the most flow and pruned the rest, reproducing a network nearly identical to the engineered rail system. **Mapping:** a procurement system is a resource-allocation network. Nature allocates the thickness of a channel in proportion to the flow through it. The manual applies near-uniform control thickness — the same approval architecture whether the buy is novel or commoditized. The biological rule says: reinforce control where value and risk flow (the $22B program, sole-source, OCIs), prune it where they don't. The slime mold's cost function *is* MWAA's modernization mandate.

**C. Moneyball and the structural arbitrage nobody is using (culture / sport).**
The Oakland A's beat richer franchises not by outspending them but by exploiting a market inefficiency — on-base percentage — that everyone else ignored. **Mapping:** MWAA's on-base percentage is its legal freedom. State DOTs are captive to the Virginia Public Procurement Act; federal agencies are captive to the FAR; MWAA, per the DOT OIG, is captive to neither for non-grant work. It can cherry-pick the most flexible mechanism from each regime. Right now it is playing like a big-market team copying FAR's conservative defaults instead of exploiting the arbitrage that is its actual competitive edge. The modernization thesis, at its sharpest, is: *stop imitating the leagues you don't have to play in.*

## 3. The opportunity register (ranked by 24-month actionability)

**1. Stand up a cooperative-purchasing playbook.** *(Pure activation — authority already in §3.7.1 and §10.4.8.6.)* Highest actionability: nothing to amend. **First step:** VP OSCM publishes an approved cooperative-vehicle list (OASIS+, GSA Schedules, Sourcewell, OMNIA) and a one-page piggyback decision tree for IT and professional services, with a documented price-reasonableness check to satisfy §3.3.5.

**2. Activate a Multiple Award Construction Contract pool for the capital program.** *(Authority in §6.2.1.3; LAWA's $5B MATOC is the template.)* **First step:** OSCM and the Office of Engineering jointly scope a pre-qualified design-build pool RFQ sized to the Dulles program, with explicit SLBE/first-time-prime lanes.

**3. Convert the quarterly forecast into an annual industry-and-teaming day.** *(Authority in §1.5; near-zero cost.)* **First step:** schedule a forecast-anchored industry day before the next capital RFP wave; invite SLBEs and incumbents into the same room to form teams.

**4. Risk-tier controls via a programmatic delegation to the capital surge.** *(Needs Board action — medium effort, high impact.)* **First step:** present the Business Administration Committee a portfolio-level not-to-exceed delegation for task orders inside an approved program budget, paired with a quarterly portfolio dashboard and red-flag triggers.

**5. Build the unsolicited-proposal / pilot front door into a real innovation intake.** *(Authority exists; the manual even says pilot guidance "must comply with guidance issued by the OSCM" — that guidance is the deliverable.)* **First step:** VP OSCM issues the pilot-program guidance and a published unsolicited-proposal portal modeled on Virginia's PPEA intake.

**6. Repurpose Chapter 10 as the FAA/BIL grant-compliance template.** *(Medium-term; preserves a tested asset.)* **First step:** map the Concourse E FAA grant clauses onto the existing Appendix C matrix and brand it the Authority's federal-grant compliance kit.

## 4. Collaboration paths for the three hardest oppositions

**Opposition 1 — the control guardians (Office of Audit, Legal, and behind them the DOT OIG / Norton transparency legacy).** They will hear "modernization" as "fewer controls" and reach for 2012. **What they need:** assurance that the audit trail gets *stronger*, not thinner. **Path from against to with:** reframe the reform as control *reallocation* — every approval removed from a routine buy is matched by analytics-based monitoring and tighter scrutiny on the genuinely risky transactions. Net control *quality* rises. **Convener:** CEO with VP OSCM. **First meeting:** present the slime-mold value-vs-delay framework and a side-by-side showing where controls thicken (sole-source, OCI, novel) as they thin elsewhere.

**Opposition 2 — the Board (Business Administration Committee).** The $3M and $200K approval reservations (§1.4.1) are the Board's accountability lever; they will not surrender it blindly. **What they need:** continued visibility and political cover. **Path:** trade transaction-level approval for program-level oversight — a portfolio dashboard, exception reporting, and pre-set red-flag triggers that pull an item back up for individual review. **Convener:** CEO. **First meeting:** quantify the friction — show that the $22B program at current thresholds means hundreds of separate Board actions and a real schedule drag — then offer the programmatic delegation as *better* governance, not less.

**Opposition 3 — incumbent large primes.** A wider pre-qualified pool and cooperative purchasing can read as dilution of their position. **What they need:** a continued pipeline and a defined role. **Path:** pair pool expansion with mentor-protégé and SLBE teaming incentives so incumbents become mentors — capturing revenue and goodwill — rather than displaced. **Convener:** OSCM supplier engagement with the DBE/Equity office. **First meeting:** the industry day, framed explicitly as market expansion with teaming credit, not replacement.
