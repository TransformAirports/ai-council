# Regulatory & Political Analyst — Stage 1 Brief
## From Compliance to Capability: The Regulatory and Political Forces That Bind (and Don't Bind) the MWAA Contracting Manual

**Bottom line up front:** Almost everything the Strategist will be tempted to call a "legal constraint" in this manual is a *policy choice* MWAA made for itself — and most of those choices are scar tissue from a 2012 federal corruption finding, not requirements of law. MWAA is one of the least externally constrained large procurement entities in the United States. That is the opportunity and the trap. The freedom is real; the political durability of the self-imposed conservatism is also real, and it does not move on a project timeline. This brief tells the Strategist which constraints are regulation (someone outside MWAA requires it), which are politics (MWAA requires it of itself and could change it tomorrow but won't), and what each attaches to on the authority's actual decision calendar.

---

## 1. Key Findings

1. **MWAA writes its own procurement law.** It is statutorily exempt from the Federal Acquisition Regulation, from the Virginia Public Procurement Act, and from the D.C. procurement code. The manual is adopted by Board resolution under Va. Code §5.1-156 / D.C. Code §9-905 (Manual §1.3). **Consequence: roughly 90% of any modernization roadmap needs a Board vote, not an act of any legislature.** The "public-sector procurement constraints prevent innovation" excuse the run-prompt warns against does not apply here on the law — only on the politics.

2. **The $3,000,000 Board-approval threshold and the $200,000 non-competitive Board trigger (Manual §1.4.1) are policy, not law — and they are far more conservative than the federal baseline.** The federal government does not require its own senior-official sign-off on a sole-source award until $900,000 (FAR, as of Oct. 1, 2025). MWAA pulls the Board in at $200,000. That is a ~4.5x more conservative trigger, set by choice, raisable by resolution.

3. **The binding political constraint is dated November 1, 2012.** The DOT Inspector General report "MWAA's Weak Policies and Procedures Have Led to Questionable Procurement Practices, Mismanagement, and a Lack of Overall Accountability" is why this manual looks the way it does. Most of the heavy controls — public quarterly reporting, layered ethics certifications, written sole-source justification, Board reservation of low-dollar non-competitive awards — are direct descendants of that finding. **Reforms framed as "reduce oversight" reopen a wound; reforms framed as "same accountability, less cycle time" can pass.**

4. **The genuinely non-negotiable constraints are narrow and grant-triggered.** They are: AIP grant assurances (49 U.S.C. §47107) on federally funded airport development; DBE/ACDBE programs (49 CFR Parts 26 and 23) once FAA funds exceed $250,000 in a fiscal year; FTA third-party rules and 2 CFR Part 200 on the Silver Line; and Davis-Bacon prevailing wages above $2,000 on construction. These live in Chapters 2 and 10 and should be treated as fixed walls, not reform targets.

5. **The manual cites a superseded regulation.** Chapter 10 is built on **FTA Circular 4220.1F**, but FTA replaced it with **Circular 4220.1G effective January 2025** — more than a year before this May 2026 edition. This is a concrete currency gap and a clean quick win (see §3, conflict note).

6. **MWAA reforms in a permanent fishbowl.** The DOT IG holds standing jurisdiction; the federal lessor (DOT) sits above the Board; and a 17-member Board split among Virginia (7), D.C. (4), Maryland (3), and the President (3) means no single political principal can shepherd reform — but several can veto it.

7. **The 2024 lease extension to 2100 lengthens MWAA's planning horizon and strengthens the case for durable procurement vehicles** (progressive design-build, long-term IDIQ/MACCs, P3s) over the transaction-by-transaction control the current thresholds encode.

## 2. Evidence

### The legal status: why this is mostly politics, not regulation

MWAA is an independent public body created by interstate compact between Virginia and the District of Columbia, with congressional consent, and operating the two airports under a federal lease from DOT [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/49/49106]. The manual states its own authority plainly: it "has been adopted by the Board pursuant to the Board's authority… under the relevant provisions of the Airports Authority's organizing statutes (See Va. Code §5.1-156; D.C. Code §9-905)" (Manual §1.3).

This matters because of what MWAA is *not* subject to. The Virginia Public Procurement Act defines a covered "public body" to include independent agencies and bodies that operate "exclusively within the Commonwealth of Virginia" — language a bistate compact entity falls outside of [Source: https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title2.2/chapter43/section2.2-4301/]. MWAA has consistently taken the position that state disclosure and procurement regimes do not bind it [Source: https://www.opengovva.org/mwaa-disclosure-laws-dont-apply]. The FAR binds federal executive agencies; MWAA is not one. The result: **the manual is a self-authored code.** Its conservatism cannot be blamed on Richmond, on the D.C. Council, or on the FAR.

The manual confirms its own discretionary breadth. It carves out 22 categories of goods and services as "not commercially practicable to engage in competition" — from credit-rating agencies to legal services to intergovernmental agreements — and lets the OSCM Vice President amend that list with Board approval (Manual §1.1.1). That is a body setting the boundaries of its own competition requirement. No external regulator hands MWAA that list.

### The thresholds are choices, and they run conservative

The Board has reserved approval of (i) contracts of **$3,000,000 or more**, (ii) concessions/leases yielding **$3,000,000 or more** in annual revenue, and (iii) **any contract through less than full and open competition at $200,000 or more** (Manual §1.4.1). All competitively offered construction contracts are fully delegated to the CEO (Manual §1.4.2).

Benchmark those against the federal government, which has no inherent authority over MWAA but is the natural yardstick. As of October 1, 2025, the FAR micro-purchase threshold is **$15,000**, the simplified acquisition threshold is **$350,000**, and the approval threshold for a justification for *other than full and open competition* is **$900,000** [Source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/08/27/2025-16412/federal-acquisition-regulation-inflation-adjustment-of-acquisition-related-thresholds]. MWAA's simplified-acquisition ceiling of $350,000 (Manual §3.3.2) now tracks the FAR exactly — evidence MWAA *does* update toward modern thresholds when it chooses to. But its **$200,000 Board trigger for non-competitive awards is 4.5x more conservative than the FAR's $900,000 sign-off point.** That gap is the single clearest illustration that MWAA's controls are a political posture, not a legal floor.

### The 2012 finding is the load-bearing politics

The DOT IG's November 1, 2012 audit found "a culture" permitting "questionable contracting practices including initiating work before contract award, awarding sole source and limited competition contracts without proper justification, and providing nonpublic information that gives potential contractors an unfair advantage" — practices the IG judged inconsistent with the Metropolitan Washington Airports Act of 1986 and MWAA's own manual [Source: https://www.oig.dot.gov/library-item/28647]. A documented example: a $100,000 sole-source legal-opinion contract awarded to Jenner & Block without competition [Source: https://www.oig.dot.gov/sites/default/files/MWAA%20Final%20Report%2010-31-2012_FINAL_signed_508_rev%2012-3-12.pdf]. The audit was requested by Representatives Frank Wolf and Tom Latham, and a member of Congress publicly called the body "a poster child for corruption" [Source: https://www.govexec.com/oversight/2012/11/body-overseeing-washington-airports-called-poster-child-corruption/59633/].

Read the current manual against that history and the lineage is unmistakable. The publicly posted quarterly procurement report listing every award, every non-competitive justification, the extent of competition, and the awarding official (Manual §1.5.2); the reservation of low-dollar non-competitive awards to the Board (§1.4.1(iii)); the written sole-source justification requirement (§3.8.1, §1.2(7)); annual procurement-integrity training (§1.6.3) — these are the institutional answers to the 2012 charges. **They are not arbitrary bureaucracy. They are a settlement with the IG and Congress, and they are politically priced accordingly.**

### The hard external walls (do not touch)

These are the constraints that genuinely originate outside MWAA and bind whenever federal money is in the deal:

- **AIP grant assurances.** When MWAA accepts FAA airport-development grants it signs assurances under 49 U.S.C. §47107 covering competitive procurement, Buy American, and nondiscrimination [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/aip/grant_assurances]. These ride with the money, project by project.
- **DBE/ACDBE.** Any FAA recipient awarding prime contracts exceeding $250,000 in FAA funds in a fiscal year must maintain a DBE program under 49 CFR Part 26 (and Part 23 for concessions) [Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-A/part-26]. This is the federal source of Manual Chapter 2's DBE structure. The SLBE (Small Local Business Enterprise) program layered on top, by contrast, is MWAA's own creation — a politics layer, not a regulation layer.
- **FTA / Silver Line.** Chapter 10 imports the full federal third-party apparatus for the FTA-funded Metrorail Silver Line. As that project completes (the foreword says the manual governs the Silver Line "until completed"), this chapter's day-to-day relevance is sunsetting — but while live, 2 CFR Part 200 controls [Source: https://www.transit.dot.gov/funding/procurement/third-party-procurement/applicability-fta-circular-42201f].
- **Davis-Bacon** prevailing wages on construction over $2,000, flowing through federal funding (Manual §10.5.7.5; multiple §10 references to the $2,000 trigger).

The pattern: **federal money is the carrier of federal control.** MWAA's baseline freedom contracts precisely to the share of any given project that is federally funded.

### Federal oversight is structural and permanent

The 2011 reform that grew the Board from 13 to 17 members (Pub. L. 112-55, §191(a)(1)) redistributed appointments — Virginia 7, D.C. 4, Maryland 3, President 3 — and was part of the same reform wave that produced heightened DOT scrutiny [Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/49/49106]. The DOT IG maintains a standing MWAA portfolio and has returned repeatedly (e.g., a 2015 review of MWAA's own audit function) [Source: https://www.oig.dot.gov/agency/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority]. The federal lease — extended in 2024 by 33 years, now running to roughly 2100 [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Washington_Airports_Authority] — keeps DOT as residual lessor above the Board indefinitely.

**Net:** MWAA cannot reform its way out of being watched. It can, however, choose whether its controls are *theater that satisfies the watchers slowly* or *instrumentation that satisfies them in real time.* That distinction is the whole modernization play.

## 3. Conflict with the source material (named per the run-prompt)

The run-prompt instructs me to name where the source conflicts with my evidence base. One clear conflict:

**The manual is built on a superseded FTA circular.** Manual §10.2.1 and dozens of Chapter 10 citations rely on **FTA Circular 4220.1F**. FTA published **Circular 4220.1G**, which supersedes 4220.1F and incorporates the IIJA and the updated 2 CFR Part 200 (effective Oct. 1, 2024), with the new circular effective January 2025 [Source: https://www.transit.dot.gov/regulations-and-guidance/fta-circulars/third-party-contracting-guidance]. A May 2026 edition citing the prior circular is a currency defect. It is low-effort to fix and it is exactly the kind of finding that erodes credibility if a federal reviewer catches it first. (Caveat: as the Silver Line completes, the practical exposure shrinks — but the manual still presents Chapter 10 as live.)

A second, softer tension: the manual's foreword frames its purpose as "fair and ethical procurement practices for the cost-effective acquisition of goods and services." The 2012 record shows that *cost-effectiveness and speed lost to control* once the scandal hit. The manual's own balance still tilts toward control. That is defensible given the history — but the run-prompt's thesis (compliance → capability) is precisely a request to rebalance, and the rebalance must be argued *into* the post-2012 political settlement, not around it.

## 4. Case Studies: where a political/regulatory force determined the outcome

**Case 1 — The 2012 scandal wrote the current control architecture.** The causal chain is direct: IG finding (Nov. 2012) → public characterization as "poster child for corruption" → board reform (Pub. L. 112-55) and ethics/contracting overhaul → today's quarterly public reporting, $200K non-competitive Board trigger, and mandatory written sole-source justification. *Lesson for the Strategist:* the controls you most want to streamline are the ones most expensively earned. Propose substitution (faster + auditable), never subtraction.

**Case 2 — Federal money importing federal process (the Silver Line).** An otherwise FAR-exempt, state-law-exempt authority took on the *entire* FTA third-party procurement regime (Manual Chapter 10) because it accepted FTA capital dollars. *Lesson:* the modernization roadmap should explicitly segregate "MWAA-funded" procurement (maximum design freedom) from "federally funded" procurement (locked to grant terms). Treating them as one regime forces the free 80% to wear the constraints of the bound 20%.

**Case 3 — The DBE vs. SLBE split.** DBE (Chapter 2, §2.3) exists because 49 CFR Part 26 requires it when federal funds flow. SLBE (§2.4), including the 100% sheltered-market set-aside (§2.4.4), is MWAA's own policy. *Lesson:* this is a clean worked example of the regulation/politics line running *through a single chapter.* The Strategist can modernize SLBE mechanics freely; touching DBE mechanics risks a grant-compliance finding.

## 6. What can and cannot be moved — and on what timeframe

| Lever | Regulation or Politics? | Reform vehicle | Realistic timeframe |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dollar thresholds ($3M Board / $200K non-comp / $350K SAT) | **Politics** (self-set) | Board resolution amending Manual | One Board cycle (months) — *if* framed as accountability-preserving |
| Sole-source / non-competitive documentation | **Politics** (post-2012 scar tissue) | Board resolution; substitute speed for paperwork, keep audit trail | Slow; needs IG-credible substitute before the Board will loosen |
| Quarterly public reporting | **Politics** (scandal settlement) | Don't remove; *automate* it | Immediate as a quick win (digitize, don't delete) |
| Approval-layer / delegation depth | **Politics** (self-set, §1.4) | CEO/Board delegation revisions | Months; the freest large lever available |
| DBE / ACDBE program | **Regulation** (49 CFR 26/23) | Federal — not MWAA's to change | Fixed while taking FAA funds |
| AIP grant assurances, Davis-Bacon, Buy American | **Regulation** (federal grant terms) | Federal | Fixed, project by project |
| FTA Chapter 10 / 2 CFR 200 | **Regulation** (Silver Line funds) | Federal; sunsets as project completes | Fixed while live; update F→G now |
| Citing current FTA circular (4220.1G) | **Regulation currency** | Editorial correction | Immediate quick win |

**The one thing the Strategist must not assume:** that the post-2012 political settlement is renegotiable on the schedule of a manual revision. It is not. The controls are durable because they were bought with a scandal, and the watchers (DOT IG, House appropriators, the four-principal Board) are permanent. The winning frame is *not* "MWAA is over-controlled and should deregulate itself." It is **"MWAA is free to choose its controls, and it should choose instrumentation that satisfies its overseers in real time instead of theater that satisfies them slowly."** Same accountability. Less cycle time. That argument survives the fishbowl. "Less oversight" does not.
