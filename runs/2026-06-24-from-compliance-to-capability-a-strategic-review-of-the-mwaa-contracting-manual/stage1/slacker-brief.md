# Slacker Brief — From Compliance to Capability: The MWAA Contracting Manual

*Independent gut-check brief. I read the run thesis and the attached Contracting Manual v6.0 (eff. 5/20/2026), then froze a cold take, then spent a strict eleven-search sprint trying to break it. The delta between Part 1 and Part 3 is the only thing in here worth the Strategist's time.*

---

## 1. Gut thesis (frozen — written before any web research)

The title *From Compliance to Capability* is a tell. It presumes the manual is trapped in compliance mode and needs liberating toward innovation. On its face, fair: public procurement manuals ossify, controls accrete like coral, and a 2026 manual still organized around FAR-style methods (sealed bid, RFQ, competitive proposal) reads like a compliance artifact.

Here's what's suspicious. I actually read the thing. This manual already contains the exact tools a modernization roadmap would "recommend": Best Value with Trade-Off (§3.6.2.2), **Pilot Programs** (§3.8.8), **Unsolicited Proposals** (§3.8.7), stipends (§4.1.7), two-step IFB (§3.4.3), RFI/draft solicitations (§4.1.3), Request for Qualifications/QBS (§3.5), and cooperative/piggyback purchasing off other governments' contracts (§3.7). Those *are* the innovation toolkit. They're already in the book. So the gap is probably not the text — it's usage.

And the delegation is generous, not stingy. The Board reserves approval only for contracts **$3,000,000 or more** (incl. option years), concessions of $3M+ annual revenue, and **any less-than-full-and-open award at $200K+** (§1.4.1). Everything else — including *all competitively offered construction* — is delegated to the CEO. That is not a hamstrung authority. That's a fairly empowered one on paper.

The joke: this is a manual pleading to be set free that already has the keys in its own pocket.

So my cold call: the binding constraint at MWAA is not the manual's permissions. It's cycle time and the risk culture of buyers who won't *use* the permissive tools, because nobody ever got fired for running a sealed bid. The comparable that pops to mind is federal **category management** and the UK procurement reforms — the lever that actually moved money was buying as an enterprise, not inventing new methods.

Two cold instincts I'll test: (1) the $3M threshold is high vs. peers (~$100K–$1M); (2) loosening controls *at MWAA specifically* may be dangerous — I half-remember a Dulles-era board/procurement scandal. A "capability" slogan that ignores buyer capacity is a slogan.

## 2. The sprint log (11 searches, then I stopped)

1. **Peer airport board approval thresholds** → Tampa/Hillsborough requires *Board approval at $100K*; state law raised it from $30K to $100K. **CONFIRMED, hard** — MWAA's $3M delegation is wildly more generous than at least one peer.
2. **Progressive design-build adoption in aviation** → PDB growing in transit but *still limited in aviation* due to legislative/regulatory/funding constraints. **COMPLICATED** my implied "alt-delivery is freely available" assumption.
3. **Public-sector RFP cycle time** → median *57 days* posting-to-award; large/complex *1–3 years*. **CONFIRMED** cycle time is the real battlefield, and the manual text isn't the main driver of the 1–3 year cases.
4. **Unsolicited proposals / P3 frameworks** → USPs carry real value-for-money risk if negotiated directly; World Bank/Miami-Dade build *competitive Swiss-challenge* structures around them. **COMPLICATED** — MWAA's §3.8.7 USP provision is good, but the frontier is the competitive tendering wrapper, which I should check it has.
5. **PANYNJ board threshold** → couldn't pin the dollar figure. **KILLED** my hope of a clean multi-airport threshold dataset. (See §5.)
6. **MWAA OIG procurement audit history** → DOT OIG: *"MWAA's Weak Policies and Procedures Have Led to Questionable Procurement Practices, Mismanagement, and a Lack of Overall Accountability."* **CONFIRMED + escalated** my half-memory of a scandal.
7. **Federal category management outcomes** → OMB reports *$27.3B saved over 3 years*; $16.9B cost avoidance in 2024 alone. **CONFIRMED** enterprise-buying as the highest-evidence reform lever.
8. **Why public procurement is slow (rules vs. people)** → research: risk-averse buyer culture + "Silver Tsunami" workforce retirements + expertise gaps, *not* simply restrictive rules. **CONFIRMED** my core thesis.
9. **MWAA 2012 scandal specifics** → OIG found *five sole-source contracts over $200K awarded without Board approval*, abuse of the "urgent" exception, contracts to *former Board members*, work started pre-award, "continually creating loopholes to sidestep regulations on bids over $200,000." **KILLED any naive "just loosen controls" recommendation.** The $200K and sole-source thresholds *are the scar tissue from this.*
10. **Airport e-procurement portals** → Sanford (SFB) deployed OpenGov supplier portal; cloud e-procurement is the sector quick win. **CONFIRMED** an unglamorous, low-effort cycle-time lever.
11. **GenAI in public procurement** → 80% of CPOs plan GenAI in 3 yrs; near-term use = *spend analytics + contract management + RFP drafting*; only ~36% have meaningful deployments. **NEW** — a real 2025–26 frontier with adoption data; candidate moonshot.

## 3. Revised position (resubmitted)

**What survived.** Three of my cold calls held. First, the manual already contains the modern toolkit — the deficit is dormancy, not absence. Second, the binding constraint is cycle time and buyer risk-culture, now backed by the 57-day median and the workforce-capacity research. Third, my threshold instinct was *understated*: I guessed peers sat at $100K–$1M and MWAA's $3M was "high." Tampa's Board approves at **$100K**. MWAA delegates *thirty times* more before the Board gets involved. The "compliance-strangled authority" framing the run title invites is, on the delegation evidence, partly backwards.

**What I'm revising.** The scandal history is far more central than I treated it. The 2012 DOT OIG report and congressional hearings found *exactly* the abuses the current thresholds police: sole-source awards over $200K with no Board approval, "urgent" loophole abuse, contracts to former Board members. So "from compliance to capability" cannot be argued as "shed the controls." At MWAA the compliance apparatus is **earned scar tissue**, and the $200K less-than-full-and-open Board trigger and the $200K sole-source Board trigger (§3.8.5, §1.4.1) are the specific provisions that exist *because someone abused exactly those paths*. The honest reform framing is "faster *and* cleaner," not "looser." Any recommendation that reads as deregulation hands the Board a reason to say no and reopens a scandal narrative.

**What I got flat wrong.** I implied alternative delivery (PDB) is freely available to MWAA — aviation PDB adoption is still legislatively constrained, so that's a "long-term aspirational" item, not a quick win. And I had no idea category management had *banked $27.3B in three years*; that makes enterprise/strategic-sourcing the single best evidence-backed medium-term reform, stronger than any new procurement *method*.

**What I hadn't considered (D).** Two things. (a) GenAI for spend analytics and RFP/solicitation drafting is a genuine frontier with real public-sector adoption data — a defensible *moonshot* that attacks cycle time and buyer-capacity *simultaneously*, which is the actual constraint. (b) The unglamorous quick win is a cloud e-procurement/supplier portal (Sanford/SFB did it on OpenGov): it attacks the 57-day clock and the §1.5.2 quarterly-reporting transparency burden at once, with near-zero policy risk.

So my revised one-liner for the Strategist: **MWAA's problem is not a permissions problem — it's a velocity-and-usage problem dressed as a manual problem. The reform that earns its keep makes the already-permitted modern tools the default path and shortens the calendar, while explicitly preserving the post-2012 controls as the thing that makes speed politically survivable.**

## 4. Three "wait, what about…" questions for the Strategist

1. **Is the friction the threshold, or the calendar?** A $3M contract clears the *rule* easily but may still wait 6–8 weeks for the next Board meeting. Has anyone measured the **time-to-Board** drag separately from the dollar threshold? If the Board meets monthly/quarterly, raising the threshold does nothing and a consent-agenda reform does everything.

2. **Which permissive tools are actually used?** How many **Pilot Programs (§3.8.8)**, **Unsolicited Proposals (§3.8.7)**, Best-Value-with-Trade-Off awards, or stipend-backed solicitations has MWAA run in the last 24 months? If the answer is near zero, the roadmap's center of gravity is *adoption and training*, not new policy — and the structured agents will over-recommend policy.

3. **What's the political cost of being seen to loosen?** Given the 2012 OIG report and congressional hearings, can capability reforms be sold to the Board as *control-preserving* (faster + more auditable via digital trails), or does any visible deregulation reopen the scandal story? The reform's framing may matter more than its content.

---

## 5. One sentence on what to ignore

My peer-threshold benchmarking is anchored on a single data point — **Tampa at $100K** — because I couldn't pull PANYNJ's or LAWA's actual Board dollar figures in the sprint; treat "MWAA delegates far more than peers" as a strong directional claim, not a clean multi-airport dataset, and have the procurement-expert agent verify it before it goes in the matrix.
