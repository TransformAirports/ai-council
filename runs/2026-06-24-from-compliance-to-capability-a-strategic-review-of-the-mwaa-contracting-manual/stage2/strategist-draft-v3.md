## I. The clock the manual forgot to keep

On August 25, 2025, the Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority issued a request for proposals for work worth about $4.9 million. Bids closed on December 10. The contract was awarded on May 22, 2026 — roughly nine months after the solicitation hit the street, and more than five months after the last proposal landed [Source: deep-research-brief]. This was a services buy. No environmental review, no land disturbance, no federal grant concurrence stood between the requirement and the award. The only thing between them was the Authority's own process — the Contracting Manual, Revised Sixth Edition 6.0, effective May 20, 2026, more than 130 pages of it. The manual does not contain a single sentence that says how long an award is supposed to take.

That one procurement is not, by itself, an indictment. But it is not an outlier either. In April 2026 alone, fifteen separate contracts cleared the Board and were awarded in a single post-meeting cluster — three of them worth $6 million, $4.8 million, and $2.5 million — while smaller buys that needed no Board action moved in under two months [Source: deep-research-brief]. The pattern is the tell: high-dollar work waits for a monthly calendar, and the manual's own process implies the rest of the wait is spent in series — a proposal moving through an evaluation committee, a consensus meeting, reference checks, a negotiation, an internal approval chain, and then the queue for the next Board meeting [Source: deep-research-brief; operations-analyst-brief, §3.6.7]. None of that is scandalous. Most of it is defensible. All of it is invisible, because the manual is meticulous about time everywhere except its own.

It mandates that an Invitation for Bid "shall be advertised for at least 30 Days" [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §3.4.1]. It requires a sole-source notice to post "no less than fifteen (15) Days prior to Contract award" and remain up for 30 [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §3.8.5.2]. It gives a protester exactly seven days to file [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §9.2.2]. It manages every clock that constrains an outside party and every dwell that protects competition. It sets no target, no service-level commitment, and no measurement for the one interval the requesting office actually feels: how long it takes to buy the thing.

That asymmetry is the argument — and it has to be scoped honestly, because there are two procurement regimes inside this manual and they behave nothing alike. On a major Dulles capital action on new ground, the longest pole is not in this document at all; it is the federal review stack — NEPA, Section 106 consultation, FAA grant concurrence — that can push board-to-notice-to-proceed to 24 to 48 months, and most of that is outside MWAA's unilateral control [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. On *that* class of work, the manual governs a minority of total elapsed time. But the $4.9 million services award above, the April clustering, and the broad mid-size operational band where most of MWAA's transactions actually live carry no environmental pole at all. On that class — the carousel motor, the curb re-strip, the $1.2 million gate re-power — the procurement gauntlet *is* essentially the whole clock, and the manual owns nearly all of the recoverable time.

So the precise claim is this: **on the non-capital, non-grant band that makes up most of MWAA's transaction volume, the manual governs almost the entire clock and times none of it; on major federally funded capital, the manual governs a minority interval, but it is also the only instrument through which MWAA can shape the slice of the federal stack — grant concurrence, domestic-content workflows, sole-source prior approval — that is actually its to shape.** The thesis this review was asked to test, that the manual must move "from compliance to capability," assumes the document is a cage. It is not. It is a modern toolbox whose tools are rarely the default and whose clock is never timed. MWAA's binding constraint is not what the manual forbids, because it forbids remarkably little: the Authority writes its own procurement law. The constraint is what the manual fails to measure, to presume, and to instrument — and the reform that matters is not less control. It is the same accountability, delivered faster and proven cleaner.

## II. Executive summary

1. **The toolkit is already modern; the deficit is dormancy, not absence.** Edition 6.0 authorizes construction manager-at-risk, all three flavors of design-build including progressive design-build, Multiple Award Construction Contracts, two-step IFB, qualifications-based selection, best-value tradeoff, pilot programs, unsolicited proposals, stipends, and cooperative purchasing off other governments' competed contracts [Source: chief-engineer-brief, §6.2.1; operations-analyst-brief, §§3.4.3, 3.5, 3.7, 3.8.7, 3.8.8]. A 2026 manual that names progressive design-build by name is ahead of most public owners [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. The reform target is how these tools get selected and defaulted, not whether they exist.

2. **The manual measures every clock except its own.** It sets minimum advertising dwells and external deadlines but no cycle-time target, no SLA to requesting offices, and no requirement to measure time-to-award [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §§3.4.1, 3.8.5.2, 5.3.3]. The public-sector benchmark is 57 days from posting to award across more than 6,000 procurements, with 78 percent finishing inside two months; the manual sets no equivalent standard, and no public evidence shows MWAA measures one internally [Source: operations-analyst-brief].

3. **MWAA writes its own procurement law, which means roughly 90 percent of any reform is a Board vote, not a legislative act.** Section 1.7 exempts the Authority from the Federal Acquisition Regulation and from Virginia and D.C. procurement codes [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief, §1.7; regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. The most common excuse in public procurement — *the state code won't let us* — is unavailable here. The binding constraints are only two: the federal strings on grant money, and political memory.

4. **The controls are scar tissue from a 2012 IG corruption finding, and that is the durable constraint.** The $200,000 and $3,000,000 Board thresholds, the public quarterly report, and the written sole-source justification are direct descendants of a finding detailed in full in Section IV [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief; regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. Any reform framed as "reduce oversight" reopens that wound; a reform framed as "same accountability, less cycle time" can pass.

5. **The Board threshold has not kept pace with the capital program, and re-indexing it is among the highest-impact, most Board-absorbable reforms in the document.** The Board reserves approval of any contract at or above $3 million and any less-than-full-and-open award at or above $200,000 [Source: airport-ceo-brief, §1.4.1]. Against a $9 billion capital program — $6.99 billion of it at Dulles, including a 14-gate concourse and a fifth-runway design — and a Dulles master plan reportedly scoped at up to $22 billion through 2034, $3 million is a rounding error that puts the Board in the critical path of routine execution [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief; virtual-chris-brief]. MWAA already fully delegates *all* competitively offered construction to the CEO [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §1.4.2] — proof it trusts delegation where schedule matters most.

6. **The largest operational losses are designed in at award, where the manual goes silent.** Nothing requires total-cost-of-ownership scoring, an operations representative on the source-selection committee, or operational acceptance as a condition of final payment for the asset classes operations will run for 25 years [Source: airport-coo-brief, §§3.6.7, 5.13]. The jet bridge bought on first cost throws faults in year seven; the operator knew it the day the bid was opened. This bundle adds days at the front and saves a decade of write-ups — which is why it ranks at the top of the agenda, tied with the governance moves the Board can take about itself.

7. **The grant chapter is built for the wrong grant.** Chapter 10, the longest in the manual, equips the Federal Transit Administration regime for the nearly complete Silver Line; there is no parallel chapter for FAA Airport Improvement Program or Bipartisan Infrastructure Law money, even as the $6.99 billion Dulles program comes online [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief, Ch. 10]. The dominant external delays on major capital — NEPA and Section 106 — sit outside the manual and outside MWAA's unilateral control. But a meaningful slice of the federal stack *is* the manual's to shape — FAA grant concurrence, domestic-content workflows, sole-source prior approval — and on exactly that slice the document is unbuilt. The manual also cites FTA Circular 4220.1F, superseded by 4220.1G effective January 2025 — more than a year before this edition issued [Source: regulatory-political-analyst-brief].

8. **The technology gap is Tier 1, not artificial intelligence.** The manual defines its "Contract management system" as one that "consists of procurement policies and processes" — a governance binder, not software [Source: technology-scout-brief, §1.2(10)]. Virginia's eVA, operating in MWAA's own region, reports cutting order-processing time up to 70 percent; the most trustworthy airport-specific evidence is more modest — digital procurement halved turnaround from about eight days to four [Source: technology-scout-brief]. The unglamorous foundation, not the autonomous-AI pitch, is where the evidence lives.

**The agenda that follows from these findings** is ranked in Section VI on Impact, Effort, and Innovation. Its center of gravity is not deregulation. At the top of the matrix sit a cluster of reforms tied on score: the lifecycle-cost / operations-seat / acceptance-gate bundle, and four governance moves the Board can largely take about itself — re-index the $3 million threshold and move routine awards to a consent agenda; invert the burden of proof so alternative delivery is presumptive for major capital; install a design-maturity gate so MWAA never signs a contract at immature design; and stand up the risk-tiered control architecture that thickens scrutiny where fraud actually hides and thins it everywhere else. The five quick wins — a cycle-time KPI, a cooperative-purchasing playbook, an industry-and-teaming day, digitized protest and evaluation channels, and a one-line circular correction — cost almost nothing and can start now. The bottom line for the Board: the integrity the 2012 scandal bought stays untouched, because integrity is not what slows MWAA down. Sequence is.

## III. The argument

### The document is not behind. It is asleep.

Read the manual the way an operator reads a new jet-bridge spec — looking for the place it fails at 5:45 a.m., not the place it reads well in a board binder — and the first surprise is how little is missing. Edition 6.0 carries CMAR, bridging, progressive, and two-phase design-build, Multiple Award Construction Contracts, task-order IDIQs, blanket purchase agreements, pilot programs, unsolicited proposals, stipends to keep losing bidders at the table, RFIs, draft solicitations, qualifications-based selection, and best-value-with-tradeoff [Source: airport-coo-brief, §§6.2.1, 3.6.2.2]. Its thresholds are current: micro-purchase is pegged to "the then-current threshold established by the FAR" — now $15,000 after the October 1, 2025 inflation adjustment — and simplified acquisition runs to $350,000, matching the federal Simplified Acquisition Threshold to the dollar [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §§3.2, 3.3]. On thresholds, MWAA sits at the federal frontier, not behind it.

So the framing that the manual is "trapped in compliance" and needs liberating is, on the text, partly backwards. The Authority delegates more before its Board gets involved than at least one peer delegates by an order of magnitude: Tampa's airport authority requires Board approval at $100,000; MWAA's line is $3 million, thirty times higher [Source: slacker-brief]. (That comparison rests on a single clean peer data point and should be read as directional, not as a multi-airport dataset — the slacker brief flags it honestly, and so should this report.) This is not a hamstrung authority. It is a fairly empowered one that has not built the habit of using its own permissions.

The gap, then, is not in the menu. It is in three things the menu cannot supply on its own: a clock, a default, and an instrument.

### The clock

A queue you never time is a queue you cannot manage. The manual instruments none of the intervals that actually accumulate into a nine-month award. The procurement forecast in §1.5.1 is a demand-planning tool — it tells you what is coming, not how long it takes once it arrives [Source: operations-analyst-brief]. The pre-posting phase, where requirements get defined and specifications drafted, is loaded onto the requesting office and is, in most agencies, where the largest and most invisible delays live. Federal practice — a non-federal proxy, used here only for directional scale, since MWAA is not a FAR agency — routinely takes three to six months just to get from need to solicitation, before the posting-to-award clock even starts [Source: operations-analyst-brief].

The cost of not timing the line is not abstract. The cycle-time literature is consistent and unglamorous: collapsing serial approvals into parallel ones delivers a 30 to 50 percent reduction; one public-sector body cut purchase-order cycle time by 50 percent through automated approvals alone, with no other change [Source: operations-analyst-brief]. Harvard's Kennedy School helped Tulsa recover "nearly a month" of cycle time using a standing cross-departmental body to find and remove pain points — no new statute, no new staff [Source: operations-analyst-brief]. The instrument that produces those gains is a single KPI — days from requisition to award, broken out by method and dollar band — plus a quarterly variance review. It costs nothing structural. The manual requires neither.

This matters most exactly where the lead anecdote and the April clustering live: the mid-size operational band, below the federal-review threshold, where the manual owns almost the entire clock. A $3 million contract clears the *rule* easily but still waits weeks for the next Board meeting [Source: slacker-brief]. That is a fixed latency tax unrelated to a contract's complexity or risk — a cadence drag that raising or lowering a dollar threshold does nothing to fix, and that a consent-agenda or programmatic-delegation reform fixes entirely. The slacker brief poses the question the structured analysis keeps missing: *is the friction the threshold, or the calendar?* For the carousel motor, the gate-area re-power, the $1.2 million curb re-strip that has to be done before summer peak, the pain is not approval authority, which the CEO already holds. The pain is the evaluation-committee calendar in the $350,000-to-$3-million band [Source: airport-coo-brief]. Miss it by six weeks and the operator holds the peak with the old equipment.

### The default

The single most expensive decision on any capital program is which delivery method to use, and the manual leaves it to undocumented judgment. CMAR and design-build may be used "when the Contracting Officer and COTR determine that such a delivery method is in the Authority's best interests," subject to VP/OSCM approval [Source: chief-engineer-brief, §6.2.1]. That is the entire selection logic. There is no constructability test, no risk-allocation matrix, no schedule-criticality screen. The tool that exists to *save* schedule sits behind a setup step — VP/OSCM sign-off and OGC coordination before the solicitation issues — that *costs* schedule, with no standing pre-approval or template pathway [Source: chief-engineer-brief].

Delivery method is also the variable most correlated with cost and schedule growth. Design-build projects average 3.8 percent less cost growth than design-bid-build and complete construction about 36 percent faster; progressive design-build now accounts for roughly a third of design-build procurements, concentrated on exactly the technically complex, scope-volatile work that describes airside and terminal projects [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. (Those figures come from the design-build trade association and should be read as indicative, not gospel — the chief-engineer brief says so plainly.) The market has voted: 50 to 75 percent of major airport programs now run CMAR or progressive design-build as their baseline, with peers like San Antonio's $1.7 billion Terminal C, St. Louis Lambert's $3 billion consolidated terminal, and Seattle-Tacoma's North Main Terminal already on these vehicles [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. The transit and state-DOT world got there first — progressive design-build matured in transportation-agency capital programs before aviation adopted it, which is part of why a 2026 airport manual that already names the method is ahead of its own sector [Source: slacker-brief; chief-engineer-brief]. Yet Chapter 3 still treats sealed bid as the construction default and alternative delivery as the exception that must be justified.

The reform is not to add a method. It is to invert the burden of proof — make alternative delivery presumptive for major capital and sealed bid the exception — and to write the one thing the manual lacks: a delivery-method selection framework that weights operations and maintenance among the drivers. The discipline already exists off the shelf in ACRP Research Reports 21 and 267 [Source: airport-coo-brief]. The Dulles concourse, with a scope still moving, is a textbook progressive-design-build candidate: define the program, select the team on qualifications, develop the guaranteed maximum price as design matures, keep the owner's hand on the wheel [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief].

But there is a trap inside this reform that the chief engineer flags and that a careless roadmap would walk straight into. Progressive design-build and CMAR work *only* because the owner and builder open the books early and negotiate a target price as design matures — they run on trust. The manual's disputes chapter is built for the opposite posture: it flatly prohibits total-cost claims and anticipatory-profit claims, waives jury trials, and fixes litigation venue in Virginia [Source: chief-engineer-brief, §7.2–7.4]. Those are excellent controls on a hard-bid, arm's-length job. Bolted underneath a progressive design-build contract, they signal a default of distrust exactly where the method needs trust. MWAA has, in the chief engineer's words, "bolted a collaborative front end onto an adversarial back end" [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. You cannot make alternative delivery the presumption and leave the adversarial claims posture untouched; the two will fight each other on the first hard change order. Re-tooling the disputes layer — at minimum standing up a Dispute Review Board so disagreements stay off the critical path — is a precondition for the default to function, not a separate nicety.

### The instrument

The manual's most revealing line is its definition of its own infrastructure. Section 1.2(10) describes a "Contract management system, for which the OSCM is responsible. This system, which consists of procurement policies and processes…" [Source: technology-scout-brief]. Everywhere else in public procurement, "contract management system" means a digital tool — a searchable repository, clause libraries, obligation tracking, spend analytics. MWAA uses the same words to mean a binder of rules. The few digital touchpoints are thin: solicitations over $50,000 post to "the Airports Authority's website or its managed portal" with no required electronic submission [Source: technology-scout-brief, §4.1.5]; vendors "may register to be included in a list" — an opt-in Planholders List, not a managed supplier database [Source: technology-scout-brief, §4.1.8]; past-performance assessment is two sentences with no central scored repository [Source: technology-scout-brief, §5.15]. And in 2026, protests "sent electronically will not be accepted" — certified mail or hand delivery only — while the Government Accountability Office runs its entire 1,803-protest docket online [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §9.1].

The instinct is to reach for artificial intelligence. The evidence says reach lower. The proven, low-risk wins are Tier 1 and Tier 2 — electronic sourcing, structured supplier registration, spend analytics, procure-to-pay — which carry a longer and more independently verified track record than anything with "AI" in the name [Source: technology-scout-brief]. Virginia runs the most relevant benchmark in MWAA's own backyard: eVA reports more than $280 million in cumulative savings since 2001 and order-processing time cut up to 70 percent, on an Ivalua source-to-pay platform [Source: technology-scout-brief] — figures that are operator- and integrator-reported, not audited, and should be read as direction rather than business-case input. The most trustworthy number is the most modest: a peer-reviewed study of digital procurement at an airport company found cycle time fell from about eight days to four [Source: technology-scout-brief]. Halving cycle time is the realistic prize. The vendors advertising 40 to 60 percent cuts and 300 to 500 percent ROI in 18 months are selling marketing, and the same literature concedes that ERP-class implementations run over budget and over schedule 80 percent of the time [Source: technology-scout-brief]. MWAA does not have an AI problem. It has a Tier-1 problem, and skipping the foundation to chase the roof is how procurement-technology budgets die.

There is a specific blind spot the instrument has to be built to see. The manual's sole-source rules are disciplined on paper — "unique or innovative concept" or "patents or restricted data rights," escalating approvals, public posting [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief, §3.8.5]. But they are silent on the *de facto* sole sources that dominate airport technology: baggage-handling controls, CUPPS/CUTE common-use platforms, airfield lighting controls, access control. These carry real interoperability lock-in to incumbent OEMs, and on a $6.99 billion Dulles program several of them are already sole-source procurements that will arrive at the contracting office "dressed as competitive" [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief, §2.6]. The DOT IG's 2016 audit is the warning: the controls did not fail on paper, they failed on documentation discipline — for 24 of 34 sole-source contracts, no one filed the required conflict-of-interest agreement [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. The instrument MWAA buys should make those lock-ins visible *before* an RFP issues, not after a protest.

### The freedom MWAA declines to use

Stand all of this against the legal record and a sharper fact emerges. MWAA is, by the DOT Inspector General's own characterization, "not subject to Federal or State laws that govern procurement" — it is a creature of a Virginia–D.C. compact ratified by Congress, bound only by the Airports Act, the federal lease, and grant strings [Source: virtual-chris-brief; regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. It adopts FAR thresholds, FAR-style methods, and FTA discipline *by choice*. The federal government does not require senior sign-off on a sole-source award until $900,000; MWAA pulls in its full 17-member Board at $200,000 — a control 4.5 times more conservative than the FAR, set by resolution and raisable by a single vote [Source: regulatory-political-analyst-brief].

This is the arbitrage almost no peer enjoys. State DOTs are captive to their procurement acts; federal agencies are captive to the FAR; MWAA is captive to neither for non-grant work and can cherry-pick the most flexible mechanism from each regime [Source: virtual-chris-brief]. Right now it spends that freedom imitating the conservative defaults of leagues it does not have to play in. The modernization thesis, at its sharpest, is not "deregulate." It is: stop paying a conservatism tax you were never charged.

## IV. The counter-case, honestly presented

There is a serious argument that this entire diagnosis is naive, and it deserves to be stated at full strength before it is answered.

**Start with the scandal, because everything starts there.** In 2012 the DOT Inspector General did not find a paperwork problem. It found a culture — "questionable contracting practices including initiating work before contract award, awarding sole source and limited competition contracts without proper justification, and providing nonpublic information that gives potential contractors an unfair advantage" [Source: regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. It found competition limited on roughly two-thirds of contracts over $200,000 — 117 of 190 contracts, about $225 million — an estimated $83.6 million spent without required Board approval, and contracts steered to former Board members [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief; slacker-brief]. Congress held hearings. A member called the Authority a poster child for corruption [Source: regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. The controls this report wants to streamline — the $200,000 non-competitive Board trigger, the written sole-source justification, the public quarterly report naming every award and the official who made it — are not bureaucratic accretion. They are the specific provisions that exist *because someone abused exactly those paths* [Source: slacker-brief]. They are a settlement with the IG and Congress, and they are priced politically, not operationally. A reform that loosens them does not save cycle time; it reopens a finding.

**Then add the money, because the timing could not be worse.** MWAA is about to lever up harder than at any point in its history. Coverage was a healthy 3.16x in 2024, but Moody's expects the net-revenue debt-service coverage ratio to fall toward roughly 1.3x as the Authority issues about $5.5 billion of new debt between 2025 and 2028 [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. Rating agencies do not reward velocity. They reward predictability of delivery — the "Infrastructure Development and Renewal" dimension Fitch scores explicitly [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. A reform marketed as "faster procurement" that a rating analyst reads as "weakened controls during a $5.5 billion issuance program" can widen spreads. Slow, defensible contracting that delivers on budget is a credit asset. Fast contracting that produces an overrun is a credit event.

**And the overrun has a name.** Denver International signed a design-build-finance-operate-maintain concession for its Great Hall — a roughly $650 million construction scope inside a deal whose full lifecycle concession value ran to about $1.8 billion — at roughly 30 percent design, and terminated it 14 months later, with the partner seeking about $288 million in change orders and termination costs, much of it for items as mundane as restrooms, door hardware, and wall panels [Source: chief-engineer-brief; airport-ceo-brief]. (The two figures most likely measure different things — the larger the DBFOM concession over its life, the smaller the construction scope — though no brief states the reconciliation outright; read it as analyst inference, not a sourced fact.) The after-action verdict cut both ways: immature scope at financial close *and* an owner that "wanted to micromanage the project but lacked the authority to make decisions" [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. The lesson the counter-case draws is blunt: procurement *speed* without procurement *discipline* at the design-maturity gate produces the single most expensive failure a hub can author. More approvals did not cause Great Hall — but neither would fewer have saved it.

**Add the technology cold shower.** The dream that software fixes process has a body count. GAO found that at one agency nearly 40 percent of reverse-auction dollars drew a single bidder, that 28 of 30 contracting officials could not explain the fees they paid, and that there was "a lack of sufficient data available to verify actual cost savings"; GSA shut its own reverse-auction platform in 2018 [Source: technology-scout-brief]. The federal past-performance system, CPARS — far more structured than MWAA's two sentences — is independently criticized as late, inaccurate, and biased toward "satisfactory" because it is too cumbersome to justify anything else [Source: technology-scout-brief]. A tool that produces one bidder is worse than the three-quote rule it would replace. A vendor-scoring database without enforced data discipline reproduces CPARS's failure.

**Finish with governance physics.** Even a flawless manual leaves untouched the two instruments that actually decide MWAA's fate: the Airline Use and Lease Agreement — a 15-year pact effective January 1, 2025, carrying the roughly $9 billion capital program and permitting up to $300 million to be shifted from Reagan to subsidize Dulles — and the federal lease that lets MWAA hold the airports at all [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. The manual states on page one that it "is not applicable to… Airline Use and Lease Agreements" [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. The dominant carriers hold a "majority of flights" sign-off — the functional equivalent of a Majority-in-Interest veto — over the very program the procurement system is being asked to deliver faster [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. And the reform itself must survive a four-principal bistate Board — 7 Virginia, 4 D.C., 3 Maryland, 3 presidential appointees — where no single principal controls a majority but several can veto, all under permanent DOT IG jurisdiction [Source: regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. The honest version of the counter-case is not "don't reform." It is: the manual is the back-office plumbing, the controls were bought with a scandal, the credit window makes execution risk maximally expensive right now, and the contracts that matter most are out of scope. Polish the plumbing if you like. Just don't sell it as transformation.

## V. Why the counter-case is insufficient

Concede the parts that must be conceded, by name, without hedging.

The 2012 scandal is real, and its controls are earned. The $200,000 non-competitive Board trigger, the sole-source public posting, the written justification, the quarterly public report, the evaluation-committee conflict-of-interest signatures — these are not the fat. They are the integrity backbone, and several of them are the controls that *prevent* a protest from landing rather than the ones that merely sequence the work [Source: operations-analyst-brief]. The credit risk is real: a $5.5 billion issuance program makes execution risk maximally expensive, and an overrun now is dearer than an overrun ever was [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. The Great Hall lesson is real: speed at the expense of design maturity is the most expensive failure mode available [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. The reverse-auction and CPARS records are real: technology amplifies a process; it does not fix one [Source: technology-scout-brief]. And the manual genuinely cannot reach the airline agreement or the federal lease — anyone who sells manual reform as a fix for MWAA's commercial position is overpromising [Source: airport-ceo-brief].

None of that rescues the status quo. It only disciplines the reform.

**First, the counter-case quietly conflates two different things — control and pre-approval — and the 2012 record does not support the conflation.** What the IG punished was *uncompeted spend* and *unaccountable spend*: sole-source awards without justification, work begun before award, spend that never reached the Board it should have [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. What the manual now imposes in response includes a great deal of *sequential pre-review* that has nothing to do with either failure — paper evaluations returned in hard copy [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §3.6.7.3(12)], serial approval chains that could run in parallel, a mail-only protest channel, a monthly Board cadence applied to a $3 million goods buy. None of those prevented the 2012 abuses, because none of them addresses competition or accountability. They address *order of operations*. The honest test the run prompt itself sets — does the value of a control exceed the delay it creates — separates the two cleanly. The sole-source public posting earns its 15 days; it converts an opaque single-source decision into a public market test [Source: operations-analyst-brief]. Hard-copy evaluation and certified-mail-only protests earn nothing; they are dwell. Tiering the controls to risk is not deregulation. It is what the Sarbanes-Oxley regime did after Enron — it scaled and risk-tiered Section 404 testing rather than repealing it, exempting clean routine filers and concentrating scrutiny where fraud actually hides [Source: virtual-chris-brief]. The reform that restored trust elsewhere was tiering, not subtraction. MWAA's move is identical: thicken control on novel, high-dollar, sole-source, conflict-prone actions; thin it on routine, commoditized, cooperative-eligible buys. Net control *quality* rises.

**Second, disciplined throughput is both a credit asset and a competitiveness asset — the two strongest objections collapse into the same answer.** Take the credit argument first. Rating agencies reward predictability of delivery, and the reforms that most threaten predictability are the ones the status quo already tolerates. A $3 million Board threshold that puts the Board in the critical path of routine contract actions, including the large modifications that cross that line with no expedited lane, does not protect delivery; it endangers it [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. On Silver Line Phase 2, MWAA burned about 62 percent of a $527.7 million contingency, with one package's change orders alone exceeding $41.9 million, and substantial completion slipped roughly four years [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. When change orders consume contingency at that rate, every one that waits on a Board cycle converts cost risk into schedule risk and idles a crew on a live airfield. The credit-protective reform is not slower procurement. It is a design-maturity gate that guarantees MWAA never signs a Great Hall at 30 percent design, plus an expedited in-scope change-order lane and a Dispute Review Board so disputes stay off the critical path [Source: airport-ceo-brief; chief-engineer-brief]. Those are *controls* — they strengthen the agencies' "Infrastructure Development and Renewal" score — and they happen to be faster. Now the out-of-scope objection, which turns out to be the same point wearing different clothes. Yes, the manual cannot touch the airline agreement or the lease. But every quarter a concourse slips is a quarter of cost-per-enplanement pressure the dominant carriers will use in the next majority-in-interest fight [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. Faster, disciplined delivery is negotiating power MWAA can spend at the table the manual cannot reach. The boundary the CEO is right to insist on does not shrink the reform; it tells you what the reform is *for*.

**Third, the "technology doesn't fix process" warning is an argument for sequencing, not for paralysis.** The reverse-auction failure was a failure of a *Tier-1 tool bolted onto a thin process*; CPARS fails on *data discipline*, not on the idea of structured past performance. Both cautions point to the same conclusion the technology scout reaches independently: buy the proven foundation — electronic sourcing, structured supplier data, spend analytics — with the data-quality requirement written into the manual, and discount the autonomous-AI pitch entirely [Source: technology-scout-brief]. The benchmark to underwrite is the airport that halved its turnaround, not the vendor promising to cut it 60 percent [Source: technology-scout-brief]. That is not techno-optimism. It is the opposite: it is refusing to buy the roof before the foundation.

**Fourth, the federal review stack is the long pole on major capital — and that relocates the thesis, it does not defeat it.** The honest board-to-notice-to-proceed figure for a major Dulles capital action is 24 to 48 months once NEPA, Section 106 consultation on a Saarinen-era field, FAA grant concurrence, and the manual's own advertising and protest windows run in series [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. NEPA and Section 106 dominate that figure, and they are largely outside the manual and outside MWAA's unilateral control; conceding that is what keeps the cycle-time case honest rather than overblown. But a slice of that federal stack *is* the manual's to shape — FAA grant concurrence, domestic-content workflows, sole-source prior approval — and on exactly that slice the document is unbuilt. The manual reserves its full procedural rigor — all of Chapter 10, a complete clause library in Appendix C — for the FTA-funded Silver Line, which is nearly finished, while the $6.99 billion AIP- and BIL-eligible Dulles program comes online under a generic grant-clause hook and no dedicated FAA chapter [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. Every federal dollar in that stack imports a restriction the master plan rarely prices: Buy America's 55 percent domestic-content test that constrains equipment specification before design freezes, Davis-Bacon, qualifications-based A/E selection, DBE goals tied to a real availability study, and FAA prior approval for any noncompetitive award [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. So the long pole does not demolish the thesis. It tells you where the manual's leverage actually is: re-instrumenting the internal clock recovers the non-capital band MWAA owns outright, and building the missing FAA chapter shortens the one part of the major-capital pole the manual can touch. A reform that polishes the dormant FTA chapter and ignores the live FAA exposure modernizes the part of the system that was already finished.

So the thesis holds, under three conditions the counter-case actually supplies. Reform must *substitute, never subtract* — every approval pulled from a routine buy matched by analytics-based monitoring and tighter scrutiny on the genuinely risky transaction. It must be *framed as accountability preserved, not oversight reduced* — the only frame that survives the four-principal Board and the permanent IG. And it must *strengthen control where value and risk concentrate while thinning it where they do not* — the slime-mold rule, where a resource network thickens the channels that carry the most flow and prunes the rest [Source: virtual-chris-brief]. Same accountability. Less cycle time. That argument survives the fishbowl. "Less oversight" does not.

## VI. Implications for MWAA: a ranked agenda

The run asked for a prioritized roadmap with each item scored on Potential Impact, Implementation Effort (lower is easier), and Strategic Innovation, on a 1–10 scale. The scores below are analyst judgment, derived from the briefs' evidence and benchmarks; they are a way to sort the agenda, not a measurement. Priority is computed transparently as Impact + Innovation + (10 − Effort), so high-impact, low-effort, high-innovation items rise. Every recommendation is anchored to authority MWAA already holds and to the named facts above.

Two notes on how to read the tables. First, the three tiers — quick win, medium-term, moonshot — sort by *implementation effort and political-risk class*, not by priority score; priority cuts across the tiers. A moonshot can outscore a quick win (M1, the risk-tiered control architecture, scores 21 — above every quick win), and the highest-scoring reforms are not the easiest ones. Each table is sorted by descending priority within its tier. Second, there is no single runaway #1. A cluster of four reforms ties at the top of the matrix at 20 — the lifecycle-cost bundle and three governance moves the Board can take about itself — and that flat top is the honest result. The agenda's center of gravity is execution discipline and Board self-governance, not any one heroic reform.

Four observations specific to MWAA's position frame this agenda. First, the cluster at the top of the matrix is not deregulation: it pairs rec 6 — the operations seat, the lifecycle score, the acceptance gate — with the governance moves the Board can take about *itself*. Rec 6 is the one a skeptical board finds most credible, because it admits the binding constraint is execution, not paperwork [Source: airport-coo-brief]. Second, the governance cluster — re-indexing the threshold, the consent agenda, the design-maturity gate — is the cleanest answer to a $9 billion program implying hundreds of discrete Board actions, and these are *controls* that protect credit during the $5.5 billion issuance window, not their absence [Source: airport-ceo-brief; virtual-chris-brief]. Third, the structural gap to close before the next major Dulles RFP is the missing FAA chapter (rec 14); the manual's rigor is pointed at a closing project while the opening program runs under thinner scaffolding, and that chapter is the one place manual reform reaches the slice of the federal pole MWAA can shape [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. Fourth, the benchmark set MWAA should measure itself against is not only other airports (San Antonio, St. Louis, Sea-Tac, LAWA, LaGuardia, Denver) but the transportation agencies and state governments that ran ahead of aviation — transit's earlier adoption of progressive design-build, Virginia's own eVA platform next door, federal category management's banked savings, and Tulsa's standing review body [Source: slacker-brief; technology-scout-brief; operations-analyst-brief].

### A concluding vision

What would a leading airport-authority procurement system look like at MWAA? Not a thicker manual. A thinner, sharper one that does three things the current document does not. It would *time itself* — publishing cycle-time targets and variance the way it already publishes awards — so that velocity is a managed metric, not an accident. It would *default to capability* — alternative delivery presumed for major capital, lifecycle cost scored, the operator in the room, the claims posture aligned to the delivery model, the controls tiered to risk rather than applied in a uniform sheet — so that the modern tools already in the book become the path of least resistance instead of the brave exception. And it would *spend its freedom on purpose* — using the rare structural latitude of a compact authority to borrow the best of every regime instead of mimicking the conservatism of the one regime it is not even subject to. The integrity the 2012 scandal bought would be untouched, because integrity is not what slows MWAA down. Sequence is.

The federal lease keeps DOT above this Board for decades — to 2067 under the 80-year term, and on one reading to roughly 2100 under the 2024 extension; the planning horizon is measured in lifetimes regardless of which date you take [Source: airport-ceo-brief; regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. The manual that emerged from the 2012 finding is a document built to make sure nothing bad ever happens again. The manual MWAA needs for the multi-billion-dollar decade ahead is one built to make sure the right thing happens *fast enough to matter* — and provably, auditable to the last signature, so that the watchers in the permanent fishbowl can see for themselves that speed and integrity were never the enemies the old document assumed they were.
