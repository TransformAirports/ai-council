# From Compliance to Capability: A Strategic Review of the MWAA Contracting Manual

*Transform Airports AI Council — Stage 2 Strategist draft (v1)*

---

## I. The clock the manual forgot to keep

On August 25, 2025, the Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority issued a request for proposals worth about $4.9 million. Bids closed on December 10. The contract was awarded on May 22, 2026 — roughly nine months after the solicitation hit the street, and more than five months after the last proposal landed [Source: deep-research-brief]. Somewhere in that interval a proposal sat through an evaluation committee, then a consensus meeting, then reference checks, then a negotiation, then an internal approval chain, then the wait for a Board meeting that happens about once a month. None of that is scandalous. Most of it is defensible. All of it is invisible, because nowhere in the 130-page document that governed that procurement — the Contracting Manual, Revised Sixth Edition 6.0, effective May 20, 2026 — is there a single sentence that says how long an award is supposed to take.

The manual is meticulous about time everywhere except its own. It mandates that an Invitation for Bid "shall be advertised for at least 30 Days" [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §3.4.1]. It requires a sole-source notice to post "no less than fifteen (15) Days prior to Contract award" and remain up for 30 [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §3.8.5.2]. It gives a protester exactly seven days to file [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §9.2.2]. It manages every clock that constrains an outside party and every dwell that protects competition. It sets no target, no service-level commitment, and no measurement for the one interval the requesting office actually feels: how long it takes to buy the thing.

That asymmetry is the whole argument. The thesis this review was asked to test — that the manual must move "from compliance to capability" — assumes the document is a cage. It is not. It is a modern toolbox whose tools are rarely the default and whose clock is never timed. MWAA's binding constraint is not what the manual forbids, because it forbids remarkably little: the Authority writes its own procurement law. The constraint is what the manual fails to measure, to presume, and to instrument — and the reform that matters is not less control. It is the same accountability, delivered faster and proven cleaner.

## II. Executive summary

1. **The toolkit is already modern; the deficit is dormancy, not absence.** Edition 6.0 authorizes construction manager-at-risk, all three flavors of design-build including progressive design-build, Multiple Award Construction Contracts, two-step IFB, qualifications-based selection, best-value tradeoff, pilot programs, unsolicited proposals, stipends, and cooperative purchasing off other governments' competed contracts [Source: chief-engineer-brief, §6.2.1; operations-analyst-brief, §§3.4.3, 3.5, 3.7, 3.8.7, 3.8.8]. A 2026 manual that names progressive design-build by name is ahead of most public owners [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. The reform target is how these tools get selected and defaulted, not whether they exist.

2. **The manual measures every clock except its own.** It sets minimum advertising dwells and external deadlines but no cycle-time target, no SLA to requesting offices, and no requirement to measure time-to-award [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §§3.4.1, 3.8.5.2, 5.3.3]. The public-sector benchmark is 57 days from posting to award across more than 6,000 procurements, with 78 percent finishing inside two months; MWAA cannot say where it falls on that distribution because it does not track it [Source: operations-analyst-brief].

3. **MWAA writes its own procurement law, which means roughly 90 percent of any reform is a Board vote, not a legislative act.** Section 1.7 exempts the Authority from the Federal Acquisition Regulation and from Virginia and D.C. procurement codes [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief, §1.7; regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. The most common excuse in public procurement — *the state code won't let us* — is unavailable here. The binding constraints are only two: the federal strings on grant money, and political memory.

4. **The controls are scar tissue from a 2012 corruption finding, and that is the durable constraint.** The DOT Inspector General found MWAA had limited competition on roughly two-thirds of its contracts over $200,000 — 117 of 190 contracts, about $225 million — and spent an estimated $83.6 million without the Board approval its own rules required [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]; a member of Congress called the Authority "a poster child for corruption" [Source: regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. The $200,000 and $3,000,000 Board thresholds, the public quarterly report, and the written sole-source justification are direct descendants. Any reform framed as "reduce oversight" reopens that wound; a reform framed as "same accountability, less cycle time" can pass.

5. **The Board threshold has not kept pace with the capital program, and re-indexing it is the highest-impact, most Board-absorbable reform in the document.** The Board reserves approval of any contract at or above $3 million and any less-than-full-and-open award at or above $200,000 [Source: airport-ceo-brief, §1.4.1]. Against a $9 billion capital program — $6.99 billion of it at Dulles, including a 14-gate concourse and a fifth-runway design — and a Dulles master plan reportedly scoped at up to $22 billion through 2034, $3 million is a rounding error that puts the Board in the critical path of routine execution [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief; virtual-chris-brief]. MWAA already fully delegates *all* competitively offered construction to the CEO [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §1.4.2] — proof it trusts delegation where schedule matters most.

6. **The grant chapter is built for the wrong grant.** Chapter 10, the longest in the manual, equips the Federal Transit Administration regime for the nearly complete Silver Line; there is no parallel chapter for FAA Airport Improvement Program or Bipartisan Infrastructure Law money, even as the $6.99 billion Dulles program comes online [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief, Ch. 10]. The manual also cites FTA Circular 4220.1F, superseded by 4220.1G effective January 2025 — thirteen months before this edition issued [Source: regulatory-political-analyst-brief].

7. **The largest operational losses are designed in at award, where the manual goes silent.** Nothing requires total-cost-of-ownership scoring, an operations representative on the source-selection committee, or operational acceptance as a condition of final payment for the asset classes operations will run for 25 years [Source: airport-coo-brief, §§3.6.7, 5.13]. The jet bridge bought on first cost throws faults in year seven; the operator knew it the day the bid was opened.

8. **The technology gap is Tier 1, not artificial intelligence.** The manual defines its "Contract management system" as one that "consists of procurement policies and processes" — a governance binder, not software [Source: technology-scout-brief, §1.2(10)]. Virginia's eVA, operating in MWAA's own region, reports cutting order-processing time up to 70 percent [Source: technology-scout-brief]; the most trustworthy airport-specific evidence is more modest — digital procurement halved turnaround from about eight days to four [Source: technology-scout-brief]. The unglamorous foundation, not the autonomous-AI pitch, is where the evidence lives.

## III. The argument

### The document is not behind. It is asleep.

Read the manual the way an operator reads a new jet-bridge spec — looking for the place it fails at 5:45 a.m., not the place it reads well in a board binder — and the first surprise is how little is missing. Edition 6.0 carries CMAR, bridging, progressive, and two-phase design-build, Multiple Award Construction Contracts, task-order IDIQs, blanket purchase agreements, pilot programs, unsolicited proposals, stipends to keep losing bidders at the table, RFIs, draft solicitations, qualifications-based selection, and best-value-with-tradeoff [Source: airport-coo-brief, §§6.2.1, 3.6.2.2]. Its thresholds are current: micro-purchase is pegged to "the then-current threshold established by the FAR" — now $15,000 after the October 1, 2025 inflation adjustment — and simplified acquisition runs to $350,000, matching the federal Simplified Acquisition Threshold to the dollar [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §§3.2, 3.3]. On thresholds, MWAA sits at the federal frontier, not behind it.

So the framing that the manual is "trapped in compliance" and needs liberating is, on the text, partly backwards. The Authority delegates more before its Board gets involved than at least one peer delegates by an order of magnitude: Tampa's airport authority requires Board approval at $100,000; MWAA's line is $3 million, thirty times higher [Source: slacker-brief]. (That comparison rests on a single clean peer data point and should be read as directional, not as a multi-airport dataset — the slacker brief flags it honestly, and so should this report.) This is not a hamstrung authority. It is a fairly empowered one that has not built the habit of using its own permissions.

The gap, then, is not in the menu. It is in three things the menu cannot supply on its own: a clock, a default, and an instrument.

### The clock

A queue you never time is a queue you cannot manage. The manual instruments none of the intervals that actually accumulate into a nine-month award. The procurement forecast in §1.5.1 is a demand-planning tool — it tells you what is coming, not how long it takes once it arrives [Source: operations-analyst-brief]. The pre-posting phase, where requirements get defined and specifications drafted, is loaded onto the requesting office and is, in most agencies, where the largest and most invisible delays live; federal practice routinely takes three to six months just to get from need to solicitation, before the 57-day posting-to-award clock even starts [Source: operations-analyst-brief].

The cost of not timing the line is not abstract. The cycle-time literature is consistent and unglamorous: collapsing serial approvals into parallel ones delivers a 30 to 50 percent reduction; one public-sector body cut purchase-order cycle time by 50 percent through automated approvals alone, with no other change [Source: operations-analyst-brief]. Harvard's Kennedy School helped Tulsa recover "nearly a month" of cycle time using a standing cross-departmental body to find and remove pain points — no new statute, no new staff [Source: operations-analyst-brief]. The instrument that produces those gains is a single KPI — days from requisition to award, broken out by method and dollar band — plus a quarterly variance review. It costs nothing structural. The manual requires neither.

This matters more at MWAA than at a steady-state buyer because of where the latency lands. A $3 million contract clears the *rule* easily but still waits weeks for the next Board meeting [Source: slacker-brief]. That is a fixed latency tax unrelated to a contract's complexity or risk — a cadence drag that raising or lowering a dollar threshold does nothing to fix, and that a consent-agenda or programmatic-delegation reform fixes entirely. The slacker brief poses the question the structured analysis keeps missing: *is the friction the threshold, or the calendar?* For a large share of mid-size operational work — the carousel motor, the gate-area re-power, the $1.2 million curb re-strip that has to be done before summer peak — the pain is not approval authority, which the CEO already holds. The pain is the evaluation-committee calendar in the $350,000-to-$3-million band [Source: airport-coo-brief]. Miss it by six weeks and the operator holds the peak with the old equipment.

### The default

The single most expensive decision on any capital program is which delivery method to use, and the manual leaves it to undocumented judgment. CMAR and design-build may be used "when the Contracting Officer and COTR determine that such a delivery method is in the Authority's best interests," subject to VP/OSCM approval [Source: chief-engineer-brief, §6.2.1]. That is the entire selection logic. There is no constructability test, no risk-allocation matrix, no schedule-criticality screen. The tool that exists to *save* schedule sits behind a setup step — VP/OSCM sign-off and OGC coordination before the solicitation issues — that *costs* schedule, with no standing pre-approval or template pathway [Source: chief-engineer-brief].

Delivery method is also the variable most correlated with cost and schedule growth. Design-build projects average 3.8 percent less cost growth than design-bid-build and complete construction about 36 percent faster; progressive design-build now accounts for roughly a third of design-build procurements, concentrated on exactly the technically complex, scope-volatile work that describes airside and terminal projects [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. (Those figures come from the design-build trade association and should be read as indicative, not gospel — the chief-engineer brief says so plainly.) The market has voted: 50 to 75 percent of major airport programs now run CMAR or progressive design-build as their baseline, with peers like San Antonio's $1.7 billion Terminal C, St. Louis Lambert's $3 billion consolidated terminal, and Seattle-Tacoma's North Main Terminal already on these vehicles [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. Yet Chapter 3 still treats sealed bid as the construction default and alternative delivery as the exception that must be justified.

The reform is not to add a method. It is to invert the burden of proof — make alternative delivery presumptive for major capital and sealed bid the exception — and to write the one thing the manual lacks: a delivery-method selection framework that weights operations and maintenance among the drivers. The discipline already exists off the shelf in ACRP Research Reports 21 and 267 [Source: airport-coo-brief]. The Dulles concourse, with a scope still moving, is a textbook progressive-design-build candidate: define the program, select the team on qualifications, develop the guaranteed maximum price as design matures, keep the owner's hand on the wheel [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief].

### The instrument

The manual's most revealing line is its definition of its own infrastructure. Section 1.2(10) describes a "Contract management system, for which the OSCM is responsible. This system, which consists of procurement policies and processes…" [Source: technology-scout-brief]. Everywhere else in public procurement, "contract management system" means a digital tool — a searchable repository, clause libraries, obligation tracking, spend analytics. MWAA uses the same words to mean a binder of rules. The few digital touchpoints are thin: solicitations over $50,000 post to "the Airports Authority's website or its managed portal" with no required electronic submission [Source: technology-scout-brief, §4.1.5]; vendors "may register to be included in a list" — an opt-in Planholders List, not a managed supplier database [Source: technology-scout-brief, §4.1.8]; past-performance assessment is two sentences with no central scored repository [Source: technology-scout-brief, §5.15]. And in 2026, protests "sent electronically will not be accepted" — certified mail or hand delivery only — while the Government Accountability Office runs its entire 1,803-protest docket online [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §9.1].

The instinct is to reach for artificial intelligence. The evidence says reach lower. The proven, low-risk wins are Tier 1 and Tier 2 — electronic sourcing, structured supplier registration, spend analytics, procure-to-pay — which carry a longer and more independently verified track record than anything with "AI" in the name [Source: technology-scout-brief]. Virginia runs the most relevant benchmark in MWAA's own backyard: eVA reports more than $280 million in cumulative savings since 2001 and order-processing time cut up to 70 percent, on an Ivalua source-to-pay platform [Source: technology-scout-brief] — figures that are operator- and integrator-reported, not audited, and should be read as direction rather than business-case input. The most trustworthy number is the most modest: a peer-reviewed study of digital procurement at an airport company found cycle time fell from about eight days to four [Source: technology-scout-brief]. Halving cycle time is the realistic prize. The vendors advertising 40 to 60 percent cuts and 300 to 500 percent ROI in 18 months are selling marketing, and the same literature concedes that ERP-class implementations run over budget and over schedule 80 percent of the time [Source: technology-scout-brief]. MWAA does not have an AI problem. It has a Tier-1 problem, and skipping the foundation to chase the roof is how procurement-technology budgets die.

### The freedom MWAA declines to use

Stand all of this against the legal record and a sharper fact emerges. MWAA is, by the DOT Inspector General's own characterization, "not subject to Federal or State laws that govern procurement" — it is a creature of a Virginia–D.C. compact ratified by Congress, bound only by the Airports Act, the federal lease, and grant strings [Source: virtual-chris-brief; regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. It adopts FAR thresholds, FAR-style methods, and FTA discipline *by choice*. The federal government does not require senior sign-off on a sole-source award until $900,000; MWAA pulls in its full 17-member Board at $200,000 — a control 4.5 times more conservative than the FAR, set by resolution and raisable by a single vote [Source: regulatory-political-analyst-brief].

This is the arbitrage almost no peer enjoys. State DOTs are captive to their procurement acts; federal agencies are captive to the FAR; MWAA is captive to neither for non-grant work and can cherry-pick the most flexible mechanism from each regime [Source: virtual-chris-brief]. Right now it spends that freedom imitating the conservative defaults of leagues it does not have to play in. The modernization thesis, at its sharpest, is not "deregulate." It is: stop paying a conservatism tax you were never charged.

## IV. The counter-case, honestly presented

There is a serious argument that this entire diagnosis is naive, and it deserves to be stated at full strength before it is answered.

**Start with the scandal, because everything starts there.** In 2012 the DOT Inspector General did not find a paperwork problem. It found a culture — "questionable contracting practices including initiating work before contract award, awarding sole source and limited competition contracts without proper justification, and providing nonpublic information that gives potential contractors an unfair advantage" [Source: regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. It found competition limited on two-thirds of contracts over $200,000, $83.6 million spent without required Board approval, and contracts steered to former Board members [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief; slacker-brief]. Congress held hearings. A member called the Authority a poster child for corruption. The controls this report wants to streamline — the $200,000 non-competitive Board trigger, the written sole-source justification, the public quarterly report naming every award and the official who made it — are not bureaucratic accretion. They are the specific provisions that exist *because someone abused exactly those paths* [Source: slacker-brief]. They are a settlement with the IG and Congress, and they are priced politically, not operationally. A reform that loosens them does not save cycle time; it reopens a finding.

**Then add the money, because the timing could not be worse.** MWAA is about to lever up harder than at any point in its history. Coverage was a healthy 3.16x in 2024, but Moody's expects the net-revenue debt-service coverage ratio to fall toward roughly 1.3x as the Authority issues about $5.5 billion of new debt between 2025 and 2028 [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. Rating agencies do not reward velocity. They reward predictability of delivery — the "Infrastructure Development and Renewal" dimension Fitch scores explicitly [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. A reform marketed as "faster procurement" that a rating analyst reads as "weakened controls during a $5.5 billion issuance program" can widen spreads. Slow, defensible contracting that delivers on budget is a credit asset. Fast contracting that produces an overrun is a credit event.

**And the overrun has a name.** Denver International signed a $1.8 billion design-build-finance-operate-maintain concession for its Great Hall at roughly 30 percent design and terminated it 14 months later, with the partner seeking about $288 million in change orders and termination costs — much of it for items as mundane as restrooms, door hardware, and wall panels [Source: chief-engineer-brief; airport-ceo-brief]. The after-action verdict cut both ways: immature scope at financial close *and* an owner that "wanted to micromanage the project but lacked the authority to make decisions" [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. The lesson the counter-case draws is blunt: procurement *speed* without procurement *discipline* at the design-maturity gate produces the single most expensive failure a hub can author. More approvals did not cause Great Hall — but neither would fewer have saved it.

**Add the technology cold shower.** The dream that software fixes process has a body count. GAO found that at one agency nearly 40 percent of reverse-auction dollars drew a single bidder, that 28 of 30 contracting officials could not explain the fees they paid, and that there was "a lack of sufficient data available to verify actual cost savings"; GSA shut its own reverse-auction platform in 2018 [Source: technology-scout-brief]. The federal past-performance system, CPARS — far more structured than MWAA's two sentences — is independently criticized as late, inaccurate, and biased toward "satisfactory" because it is too cumbersome to justify anything else [Source: technology-scout-brief]. A tool that produces one bidder is worse than the three-quote rule it would replace. A vendor-scoring database without enforced data discipline reproduces CPARS's failure.

**Finish with governance physics.** Even a flawless manual leaves untouched the two instruments that actually decide MWAA's fate: the Airline Use and Lease Agreement — a 15-year pact effective January 1, 2025, carrying the roughly $9 billion capital program and permitting up to $300 million to be shifted from Reagan to subsidize Dulles — and the federal lease that lets MWAA hold the airports at all [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. The manual states on page one that it "is not applicable to… Airline Use and Lease Agreements" [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. The dominant carriers hold a "majority of flights" sign-off — the functional equivalent of a Majority-in-Interest veto — over the very program the procurement system is being asked to deliver faster [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. And the reform itself must survive a four-principal bistate Board — 7 Virginia, 4 D.C., 3 Maryland, 3 presidential appointees — where no single principal controls a majority but several can veto, all under permanent DOT IG jurisdiction [Source: regulatory-political-analyst-brief]. The honest version of the counter-case is not "don't reform." It is: the manual is the back-office plumbing, the controls were bought with a scandal, the credit window makes execution risk maximally expensive right now, and the contracts that matter most are out of scope. Polish the plumbing if you like. Just don't sell it as transformation.

## V. Why the counter-case is insufficient

Concede the parts that must be conceded, by name, without hedging.

The 2012 scandal is real, and its controls are earned. The $200,000 non-competitive Board trigger, the sole-source public posting, the written justification, the quarterly public report, the evaluation-committee conflict-of-interest signatures — these are not the fat. They are the integrity backbone, and several of them are the controls that *prevent* a protest from landing rather than the ones that merely sequence the work [Source: operations-analyst-brief]. The credit risk is real: a $5.5 billion issuance program makes execution risk maximally expensive, and an overrun now is dearer than an overrun ever was [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. The Great Hall lesson is real: speed at the expense of design maturity is the most expensive failure mode available [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. The reverse-auction and CPARS records are real: technology amplifies a process; it does not fix one [Source: technology-scout-brief]. And the manual genuinely cannot reach the airline agreement or the federal lease — anyone who sells manual reform as a fix for MWAA's commercial position is overpromising [Source: airport-ceo-brief].

None of that rescues the status quo. It only disciplines the reform.

**First, the counter-case quietly conflates two different things — control and pre-approval — and the 2012 record does not support the conflation.** What the IG punished was *uncompeted spend* and *unaccountable spend*: sole-source awards without justification, work begun before award, $83.6 million that never reached the Board it should have [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. What the manual now imposes in response includes a great deal of *sequential pre-review* that has nothing to do with either failure — paper evaluations returned in hard copy [Source: operations-analyst-brief, §3.6.7.3(12)], serial approval chains that could run in parallel, a mail-only protest channel, a monthly Board cadence applied to a $3 million goods buy. None of those prevented the 2012 abuses, because none of them addresses competition or accountability. They address *order of operations*. The honest test the run prompt itself sets — does the value of a control exceed the delay it creates — separates the two cleanly. The sole-source public posting earns its 15 days; it converts an opaque single-source decision into a public market test [Source: operations-analyst-brief]. Hard-copy evaluation and certified-mail-only protests earn nothing; they are dwell. Tiering the controls to risk is not deregulation. It is what the Sarbanes-Oxley regime did after Enron — it scaled and risk-tiered Section 404 testing rather than repealing it, exempting clean routine filers and concentrating scrutiny where fraud actually hides [Source: virtual-chris-brief]. The reform that restored trust elsewhere was tiering, not subtraction. MWAA's move is identical: thicken control on novel, high-dollar, sole-source, conflict-prone actions; thin it on routine, commoditized, cooperative-eligible buys. Net control *quality* rises.

**Second, the credit argument cuts the other way once you separate speed from discipline.** Rating agencies reward predictability of delivery — and the reforms that most threaten predictability are the ones the status quo already tolerates. A $3 million Board threshold that puts the Board in the critical path of routine change orders does not protect delivery; it endangers it. On Silver Line Phase 2, MWAA burned about 62 percent of a $527.7 million contingency, with one package's change orders alone exceeding $41.9 million, and substantial completion slipped roughly four years [Source: chief-engineer-brief]. When change orders consume contingency at that rate, every one that waits on a Board cycle converts cost risk into schedule risk and idles a crew on a live airfield. The credit-protective reform is not slower procurement. It is a design-maturity gate that guarantees MWAA never signs a Great Hall at 30 percent design, plus an expedited in-scope change-order lane and a Dispute Review Board so disputes stay off the critical path [Source: airport-ceo-brief; chief-engineer-brief]. Those are *controls* — they strengthen the agencies' "Infrastructure Development and Renewal" score — and they happen to be faster. The CEO's own reconciliation is exactly this: back the thesis, reframe it as disciplined throughput, and the rating analyst and the airline both reward it [Source: airport-ceo-brief].

**Third, the "technology doesn't fix process" warning is an argument for sequencing, not for paralysis.** The reverse-auction failure was a failure of a *Tier-1 tool bolted onto a thin process*; CPARS fails on *data discipline*, not on the idea of structured past performance. Both cautions point to the same conclusion the technology scout reaches independently: buy the proven foundation — electronic sourcing, structured supplier data, spend analytics — with the data-quality requirement written into the manual, and discount the autonomous-AI pitch entirely [Source: technology-scout-brief]. The benchmark to underwrite is the airport that halved its turnaround, not the vendor promising to cut it 60 percent [Source: technology-scout-brief]. That is not techno-optimism. It is the opposite: it is refusing to buy the roof before the foundation.

**Fourth, the out-of-scope contracts argument proves the thesis instead of defeating it.** Yes, the manual cannot touch the airline agreement or the lease. But cycle time *is* a competitiveness argument with the carriers. Every quarter a concourse slips is a quarter of cost-per-enplanement pressure the dominant carriers will use in the next Majority-in-Interest fight [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. Faster, disciplined delivery is leverage MWAA can spend at the table the manual cannot reach. The boundary the CEO is right to insist on — name it, don't paper over it — does not shrink the reform. It tells you what the reform is *for*.

**Fifth, and most quietly damning: the federal review stack, not the manual, is the long pole — and the manual is built for the wrong half of it.** The honest board-to-notice-to-proceed figure for a major Dulles capital action is 24 to 48 months once NEPA, Section 106 consultation on a Saarinen-era field, FAA grant concurrence, and the manual's own advertising and protest windows run in series [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. The manual is reasonably efficient; the federal stack bolted on top of it is not. And the manual reserves its full procedural rigor — all of Chapter 10, a complete clause library in Appendix C — for the FTA-funded Silver Line, which is nearly finished, while the $6.99 billion AIP- and BIL-eligible Dulles program comes online under a generic grant-clause hook and no dedicated FAA chapter [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. Every federal dollar in that stack imports a restriction the master plan rarely prices: Buy America's 55 percent domestic-content test that constrains equipment specification before design freezes, Davis-Bacon, qualifications-based A/E selection, DBE goals tied to a real availability study, and FAA prior approval for any noncompetitive award [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief]. A reform that polishes the dormant FTA chapter and ignores the live FAA exposure modernizes the part of the system that was already finished. This is not an argument against reform. It is the single largest structural gap a reform must close — and the counter-case never mentions it.

So the thesis holds, under three conditions the counter-case actually supplies. Reform must *substitute, never subtract* — every approval pulled from a routine buy matched by analytics-based monitoring and tighter scrutiny on the genuinely risky transaction. It must be *framed as accountability preserved, not oversight reduced* — the only frame that survives the four-principal Board and the permanent IG. And it must *strengthen control where value and risk concentrate while thinning it where they do not* — the slime-mold rule, where a resource network thickens the channels that carry the most flow and prunes the rest [Source: virtual-chris-brief]. Same accountability. Less cycle time. That argument survives the fishbowl. "Less oversight" does not.

## VI. Implications for MWAA: a ranked agenda

The scores below are analyst judgment, derived from the briefs' evidence and benchmarks; they are a way to sort the agenda, not a measurement. Priority is computed transparently as Impact + Innovation + (10 − Effort), so high-impact, low-effort, high-innovation items rise. Every recommendation is anchored to authority MWAA already holds and to the named facts above.

Three observations specific to MWAA's position frame this agenda. First, the highest-leverage reform is the one the Board can take about *itself*: re-indexing the threshold and moving to a consent agenda is governance the Board votes on directly, and it is the cleanest answer to a $9 billion program implying hundreds of discrete Board actions [Source: airport-ceo-brief; virtual-chris-brief]. Second, the reforms that matter most to credit are *controls*, not their absence — the design-maturity gate, the change-order lane, the Dispute Review Board — which is precisely why they survive a rating review during the $5.5 billion issuance window [Source: airport-ceo-brief]. Third, the structural gap to close before the next major Dulles RFP is the missing FAA chapter; the manual's rigor is pointed at a closing project while the opening program runs under thinner scaffolding [Source: airport-procurement-expert-brief].

### A concluding vision

What would a leading airport-authority procurement system look like at MWAA? Not a thicker manual. A thinner, sharper one that does three things the current document does not. It would *time itself* — publishing cycle-time targets and variance the way it already publishes awards — so that velocity is a managed metric, not an accident. It would *default to capability* — alternative delivery presumed for major capital, lifecycle cost scored, the operator in the room, the controls tiered to risk rather than applied in a uniform sheet — so that the modern tools already in the book become the path of least resistance instead of the brave exception. And it would *spend its freedom on purpose* — using the rare structural latitude of a compact authority to borrow the best of every regime instead of mimicking the conservatism of the one regime it is not even subject to. The integrity the 2012 scandal bought would be untouched, because integrity is not what slows MWAA down. Sequence is.

The manual that emerges from the 2012 finding is a document built to make sure nothing bad ever happens again. The manual MWAA needs for the $22 billion decade ahead is one built to make sure the right thing happens *fast enough to matter* — and provably, auditable to the last signature, so that the watchers in the permanent fishbowl can see for themselves that speed and integrity were never the enemies the old document assumed they were.
