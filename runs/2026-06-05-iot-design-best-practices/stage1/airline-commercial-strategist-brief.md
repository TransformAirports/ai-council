# Airline-Side Evidence Brief: IoT Design Best Practices
## The Governance and Commercial Dynamics That Strand Airport Technology

**Prepared for:** Transform Airports AI Council — Stage 1 Research  
**Council Run:** IoT Design Best Practices  
**Date:** June 5, 2026  
**Author:** Airline Commercial Strategist Agent

---

## Key Findings

1. **Data ownership in gate areas is legally ambiguous by default.** Current airline use agreements — residual and compensatory alike — contain no standard data rights language. The absence is not an oversight; it reflects decades of negotiation focused on rate structures, not information flows. When an airport deploys IoT sensors in an airline-leased gate area, bag room, or boarding bridge, the data produced exists in a contractual vacuum. The airport cannot reliably aggregate it, share it, or build a platform on top of it without creating a dispute with the tenant carrier. The IATA October 2024 Aircraft Operational Data agreement established the principle that data generated by airline-operated aircraft belongs to the airline — and made no provision for airport operators as stakeholders [Source: https://www.iata.org/en/pressroom/2024-releases/2024-10-02-01/].

2. **Majority-in-interest clauses give airlines structural veto power over airport capital programs, including technology infrastructure.** Under affirmative MII provisions, an airport must secure approval from airlines representing a majority of traffic before proceeding with capital projects that raise rates. At hub airports where one carrier controls 60%+ of landed weight — ATL (Delta ~80%), DFW (American ~85%), IAH (United ~80%) — a single carrier effectively controls the MII vote. Technology infrastructure funded through airport capital programs raises Cost Per Enplanement, triggering MII review. There is no technology carve-out in standard MII language [Source: http://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/articles/airport-capital-review].

3. **CPE differentials across US hubs create a carrier response function airports can model.** ATL's CPE sits at approximately $3.94–$6.07 per enplanement — the lowest among large hubs. LAX runs $30.16–$38.10, ORD approximately $29.56–$29.85, SEA $18.24–$22.51. These are not abstractions; they are the numbers that appear in carrier network planning models when scheduling teams evaluate marginal route profitability. A fortress-hub carrier absorbing high CPE can do so because hub economics — connection revenue, yield premiums — offset the cost. But every dollar of airport IoT capital that flows into the rate base narrows that offset. Airport planners who cannot answer "what does this $15M sensor platform do to our CPE trajectory over 5 years" are not speaking a language carriers can act on [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/large-hub/cpe].

4. **Airlines have built proprietary operational data ecosystems that airports cannot access and rarely acknowledge.** Delta's Baggage AI, built entirely in-house, uses real-time RFID scan data, connection minutes, and ramp routing algorithms to manage 100,000+ daily bags at ATL — independently of any airport-provided system [Source: https://news.delta.com/delta-spurs-innovation-worlds-busiest-ramp]. Delta completed a comprehensive cloud migration to AWS covering its operational infrastructure [Source: https://www.constellationr.com/insights/news/delta-air-lines-completes-cloud-migration-focus-ai-and-data-driven-customer]. United migrated hundreds of applications to AWS, saving $2 billion in technology costs [Source: https://aws.amazon.com/solutions/case-studies/innovators/united-airlines/]. When an airport deploys a sensor to measure what an airline already measures internally, the airport does not become the system of record. The airline already has one.

5. **The Southwest–San Antonio gate dispute reveals the structural leverage airport authorities hold — and how it can detonate airline technology investments.** When Southwest refused to sign San Antonio's use and lease agreement, the airport moved to convert Southwest's proprietary gate equipment — kiosks, boarding systems, branding infrastructure — to city-owned Common Use Passenger Processing Systems (CUPPS). Southwest characterized this as "gamesmanship, rather than responsible airport operations." The dispute was resolved only after a settlement guaranteeing Southwest no fewer than six gates [Source: https://paxex.aero/san-antonio-threatens-conversion-of-southwest-gates-to-common-use/]. The mechanism is the same in reverse for IoT: airlines can refuse to grant access for airport-deployed sensors in their leased spaces; airports can threaten common-use conversion. Neither side has clean leverage.

6. **CUTE/CUPPS adoption history documents the pattern.** The transition from exclusive-use to common-use terminal equipment has generated consistent airline resistance for 40 years, with low-cost carriers most vocal. AirAsia's documented objections at klia2 provide the clearest articulation: self-service kiosk utilization dropped from 35% to 10% after the common-use transition, because AirAsia's proprietary systems — built over years of investment — could not function inside the CUPPS environment. The airline paid per-departure fees for infrastructure it did not use and lost operational automation it had built [Source: https://www.futuretravelexperience.com/2014/07/future-airport-common-use-will-one-size-fits-solution-ever-feasible/]. Airport IoT deployments that assume carriers will feed data into a common platform face the same friction.

7. **At MWAA specifically, United Airlines is the dominant commercial force at Dulles and the carrier whose technology posture governs what the airport can realistically build.** United's December 2024 announcement of a $500M+ Concourse E expansion — 14 exclusive-use gates in a 435,000-square-foot facility — defines the physical and operational envelope for Dulles for the next decade [Source: https://united.mediaroom.com/2024-12-03-More-Than-Half-a-Billion-Dollar-Expansion-Modernization-Coming-to-Uniteds-Washington-Dulles-Hub]. The new MWAA use and lease agreement effective January 1, 2025, governs a $9 billion capital program, with $6.99 billion allocated to Dulles [Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-completes-negotiations-new-use-and-lease-agreement]. Any IoT program MWAA deploys in United-occupied spaces at Dulles operates under lease terms that do not contain data rights language and within a capital program United has commercial consent rights over.

8. **The post-consolidation oligopoly has concentrated the MII veto into fewer hands.** Pre-2008, a large hub might have had 6–8 signatory airlines. Post-consolidation, the same hub may have 3–4. A standard MII formulation requiring a numerical majority representing 60% of landed weight means that at a hub where the dominant carrier controls 60%+, that carrier alone satisfies the threshold. Capital approval — including technology capital — is effectively bilateral: the airport and its dominant carrier. The four-carrier oligopoly (American, Delta, United, Southwest) controls well over 70% of US domestic capacity. Their technology postures, data governance positions, and cost-sharing philosophies now determine what airport IoT programs can survive past the pilot phase [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/dwu-ai/airline-mergers-airport-credit-revenue].

## Evidence Section

### Data Ownership Vacuum

The foundational problem is structural: use agreements were not written to address data. The template Airline Use and Lease Agreement available from ACI-NA (RSW/Southwest Florida template) contains no data rights language — not because drafters forgot, but because "data" was not a commercial asset in the framework's era of origin. What the agreements do address is physical premises: what space the airline leases, at what rate, under what cost methodology. The airline's obligation is to pay rates; the airport's obligation is to maintain the premises. Neither obligation creates a data governance framework [Source: https://airportscouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/rsw_airport__airline_use_agreement.pdf].

The IATA October 2024 Aircraft Operational Data (AOD) agreement establishes that airlines control data generated by aircraft they operate — in flight and on the ground. The five principles: Consent (AOD extracted only with airline's written consent), Transparency, Sharing (airlines choose who receives their data), Accessibility, and Responsible Use. The agreement explicitly covers data "whether in flight or on the ground." An airport-deployed sensor in a gate area that captures aircraft turnaround activity — docking time, boarding bridge engagement, ground support equipment positioning — is measuring aircraft-related operational data. Under the IATA framework, the airline arguably controls that data. The agreement does not list airports as stakeholders [Source: https://www.iata.org/en/pressroom/2024-releases/2024-10-02-01/].

This creates a specific IoT failure mode: the airport deploys sensors, builds a platform, begins aggregating data, and then receives a legal notice from the dominant carrier asserting data rights. There is no standard resolution mechanism because no standard framework addresses the question. The stranded deployment is not a technical failure. It is the predictable result of building a data product on top of an asset (the airline's operational data) without establishing property rights first.

### MII Mechanics and Technology Capital

The MII clause is the most underappreciated mechanism in airport-airline commercial relationships. Its function is straightforward: airlines that bear rate risk under residual ratemaking demand governance rights over the capital decisions that drive rates. An airport considering a $20M IoT infrastructure program funded through its capital budget faces a choice: fund through PFC (passenger facility charges), which are legally ring-fenced from MII veto; fund through bonds against airline rates, which are not.

Under residual ratemaking — the dominant structure at most large US hubs — airport operating costs and debt service that cannot be covered by non-airline revenue flow to the airline rate base. Every dollar of IoT capital funded through bonds under residual ratemaking increases airline rates. The MII clause is the airlines' contractual mechanism to stop that.

PFC funding is a partial workaround: airports are barred from exercising MII vetoes over PFC-financed projects per FAA regulations, but airlines can and do oppose PFC applications through the FAA comment process, and the PFC cap of $4.50 per enplanement — unchanged since 2000 despite the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 failing to raise it — sharply limits PFC-available capital [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/articles/passenger-facility-charges].

The post-merger concentration intensifies the MII dynamic. When a merger reduces signatory airlines from four to three and the merged entity controls 60%+ of landed weight, MII approval on capital programs becomes bilateral. At Dulles, United's dominance means that MWAA's technology capital decisions are not made through multi-stakeholder governance. They are made in a bilateral commercial relationship with United Airlines.

### Carrier Proprietary Data Ecosystems

The three legacy carriers have each made nine-figure investments in proprietary operational technology that functions as a closed-loop data system. These investments predate the airport IoT conversation and will not be displaced by it.

**Delta:** RFID baggage tracking deployed at $50M investment beginning 2016, now generating 99.9% scan success rates on 120M+ annual bags [Source: https://www.supplychaindive.com/news/Delta-RFID-airline-logistics-use-case/529024/]. Baggage AI built in-house, delivering ~30% improvement in bag transfers at ATL. Apple AirTag integration (November 2025) feeds passenger-provided location data directly into Delta's proprietary BRS (Baggage Reconciliation System) [Source: https://skift.com/2025/11/24/apple-delta-bag-tracking-share-item-location/]. Full AWS cloud migration completed. Delta's operational data platform is not a gap that airport IoT fills — it is a parallel and more complete system.

**United:** AWS cloud migration of hundreds of applications, generating $2B in technology savings [Source: https://aws.amazon.com/solutions/case-studies/innovators/united-airlines/]. United is investing $500M+ in Concourse E at Dulles — 14 exclusive-use gates — giving United physical and operational control over its primary operating space [Source: https://united.mediaroom.com/2024-12-03-More-Than-Half-a-Billion-Dollar-Expansion-Modernization-Coming-to-Uniteds-Washington-Dulles-Hub].

**American:** Re-banking DFW to a 13-bank structure in 2024, which requires precise operational data systems to manage connection flows. Deploying electronic boarding gates at DFW at scale — the first major US network carrier to do so — using dormakaba hardware [Source: https://www.futuretravelexperience.com/2026/04/american-airlines-to-launch-electronic-boarding-gates-at-dfw-for-a-more-seamless-experience/]. American achieved 50% reduction in DFW missed connections through AI-powered operational systems.

The data layering problem this creates is concrete: if Delta deploys RFID readers at every baggage point in an ATL bag room, and ATL deploys IoT sensors to track the same bag movements, there are two systems generating competing records of the same physical event. The carrier's system has higher fidelity (99.9% scan rate), longer operational history, and deeper integration with downstream systems (gate agents, ramp crews, passenger notification). The airport's system has broader scope (covers all carriers) but shallower accuracy. Who is the system of record for a mishandled bag? In practice, the airline is — because the airline has the regulatory obligation, the customer relationship, and the data infrastructure. The airport's parallel sensor deployment produces data that nobody uses.

### The AODB Vendor Lock Analog

The UK government tender documentation for Manchester Airports Group (MAG) provides a documented example of the long-term vendor lock problem. MAG has been running a variation of the Chroma AODB solution for over 30 years. Ownership of the solution has changed multiple times; MAG airports are now running varying versions with accumulated technical debt. MAG's stated goal is to escape toward a "loosely coupled systems landscape, away from a single vendor, monolithic solution" and an "API-first, decoupled architecture" [Source: https://www.find-tender.service.gov.uk/Notice/028772-2024]. This is the airport equivalent of the stranded IoT deployment: a system chosen in one era, locked in by accumulated integration complexity, requiring a decade-scale migration effort to escape.

The mechanism airports fail to anticipate: when the airport selects a platform vendor for IoT aggregation, and an airline's internal systems use a different data schema, the airline's ground operations team must build translation middleware. That middleware is invisible in the airport's capital plan. It is a real cost to the carrier. When the carrier's network team is evaluating CPE at competing hub alternatives, the translation tax — the engineering overhead of maintaining airport-imposed data format requirements — is a real, if not formally quantified, cost factor.

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: Southwest vs. San Antonio — Technology as Lease Leverage

When Southwest Airlines refused to sign San Antonio International Airport's use and lease agreement, the airport's response was not purely financial. San Antonio moved to convert Southwest's proprietary gate equipment — including CUPPS check-in kiosks, boarding systems, and branded infrastructure — to city-owned common-use configuration.

The mechanism is instructive: the airport's leverage was not the rate structure alone. It was the threat to remove the carrier's physical technology investment in the terminal. Southwest had built a proprietary operational environment in its leased spaces. The airport, as the landlord, had the ability to force that environment into a common-use standard it did not control.

Southwest's documented objection — "gamesmanship, rather than responsible airport operations" — reflects a carrier that had invested in proprietary gate-area technology and found that investment held hostage to a lease negotiation. The settlement restored Southwest's gate control but required the carrier to sign a new agreement that the airport had set the terms of.

The IoT parallel is exact: an airport that deploys sensors in a carrier's leased gate area is creating a potential point of leverage — and contention — in future lease negotiations. If the carrier rejects the airport's IoT platform, the airport cannot compel data sharing without triggering a dispute. If the airport's IoT platform is funded through the capital program, the carrier pays for infrastructure it is not using through higher CPE. [Source: https://paxex.aero/san-antonio-threatens-conversion-of-southwest-gates-to-common-use/]

### Case Study 2: AirAsia at klia2 — CUTE Transition Destroys Carrier-Built Automation

AirAsia's documented experience at klia2 following the transition to common-use terminal equipment is the most precise case study available of what happens when an airport forces a carrier's proprietary operational technology into a shared-platform environment.

Before the transition, AirAsia had built self-service check-in and tagging capability at its primary base. Self-service kiosk utilization: 35%. Self-service tagging: 35%. After the CUPPS transition, kiosk utilization fell to 10%. Self-service tagging dropped to zero.

AirAsia's head of operations made the unit economics explicit: "we now have to pay 35 cents for each departing customer whether or not they use any of this infrastructure." The carrier had spent years building operational automation — reducing staffing, accelerating turnarounds, improving unit costs. The common-use platform could not support the carrier's proprietary workflows. The automation was destroyed. The staffing costs increased. The carrier was paying for infrastructure that degraded its operation while simultaneously being charged for the shared platform.

This is the template for the airport IoT stranded deployment from the carrier side: the airport builds a data platform, funds it through the rate base, and charges all carriers for access through CPE. The dominant carrier, which already has superior data infrastructure, receives no operational benefit from the airport's platform. It is paying higher rates for a system it does not need. Under residual ratemaking, it is absorbing the debt service for an investment it opposed or was never consulted on. [Source: https://www.futuretravelexperience.com/2014/07/future-airport-common-use-will-one-size-fits-solution-ever-feasible/]

### Case Study 3: MSP and Delta — How Dominant Carriers Shape Capital Programs

Minneapolis-St. Paul International Airport's $242M Airside Modernization Program — the largest interior renovation in MSP's history — was structured around Delta's requirements. Delta contributed $60M of the $242M total. MAC invested $182.5M. The project scope: renovation of six of seven concourses (A, B, C, D, F, G) and approximately 75 Delta gate hold rooms.

The sequence matters: MSP officials traveled to Delta's Atlanta headquarters to make the case for investment. They showed Delta what it had done at Seattle, New York, Los Angeles, and Salt Lake City. Delta then committed more than $100M to MSP infrastructure upgrades. In 2022–2023, Delta's operations accounted for 57–62% of total MSP revenue.

The mechanism is bilateral leverage, not unilateral airport authority. Delta's 57–62% revenue share gives it effective MII control at MSP. The airport's capital program is aligned to Delta's operational priorities. Technology investments that Delta cannot use or does not want — that would raise CPE without generating benefit Delta's network planners can quantify — do not survive this bilateral filter. [Source: https://www.startribune.com/msp-airport-delta-bigger-investment-more-money-hub/601127770]

### Case Study 4: MAG's 30-Year AODB Lock and the Decoupling Escape

Manchester Airports Group's documented migration away from its Chroma AODB system — a solution in place for over 30 years — illustrates the long-term cost of single-vendor dependency in airport operational data systems. The stated problems: varying versions across airports, accumulated technical debt, inability to adapt to modern integration requirements.

MAG's stated solution architecture: API-first, loosely coupled, modular. Avoid a new monolithic replacement. The strategic goal is a system that can survive vendor changes — the precise capability the IoT design thesis argues airports must build into their architecture from day one.

The IoT deployment failure this predicts: an airport that selects a single-vendor IoT platform in 2025 will be where MAG was in 2024 by 2045 — running a degraded version of a system whose original vendor has been acquired twice, whose data schemas are incompatible with the airline systems that emerged in the interim, and whose replacement cost is nine figures. [Source: https://www.find-tender.service.gov.uk/Notice/028772-2024]

### Case Study 5: MWAA and United at Dulles — The Bilateral Governance Reality

The Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority's new use and lease agreement, effective January 1, 2025, governs a $9 billion capital program. Dulles receives $6.99 billion of that total. United Airlines is the dominant carrier, controlling the effective MII vote.

In December 2024, United announced $500M+ in Concourse E investment at Dulles — 14 exclusive-use gates. United's statement: the carrier "along with its Dulles competitors — committed to billions of dollars in investment at the airport over the coming years."

The governance implication for IoT: United is building a 435,000-square-foot facility with 14 exclusive-use gates. Those gates, under a standard use agreement, are United's leased premises. Airport-deployed sensors in that concourse would require United's consent to generate data United considers proprietary. United's technology posture — AWS cloud, proprietary operational systems — means United already has the operational data the airport would want to aggregate. The airport's IoT platform in Concourse E would be a parallel system generating duplicate data in spaces where United controls the operational reality.

Any MWAA IoT program that does not address the United consent question before deployment is building on a foundation that United can choose not to participate in. [Source: https://united.mediaroom.com/2024-12-03-More-Than-Half-a-Billion-Dollar-Expansion-Modernization-Coming-to-Uniteds-Washington-Dulles-Hub; Source: https://www.mwaa.com/news/metropolitan-washington-airports-authority-completes-negotiations-new-use-and-lease-agreement]

## Analytical Conclusion

The central claim of this brief is that airport IoT stranded deployments are not primarily a technology architecture problem. They are a governance and commercial relationship problem that technology choices alone cannot solve.

Three dynamics produce the stranded deployment:

**First**, use agreements do not address data rights. The absence creates a dispute waiting to happen. Every airport that deploys IoT in airline-leased spaces without explicit data sharing agreements in place is building a platform whose longevity depends on carrier tolerance rather than contractual entitlement. Carrier tolerance is not durable across three vendor changes, two network upgrades, and one CIO turnover.

**Second**, carriers have built proprietary operational data systems that are superior to what airports can deploy. Delta's Baggage AI, American's re-banked DFW operational platform, United's AWS-migrated operational infrastructure — these are not gaps that airport sensors fill. They are established systems of record. When airports deploy parallel sensors measuring the same phenomena, the result is competing data, not complementary data. The carrier will not adopt the airport's data as its system of record. The airport's platform becomes an orphan.

**Third**, the post-consolidation oligopoly has concentrated MII veto power. At hub airports where one carrier controls 60%+ of traffic, technology capital decisions are bilateral. The airport cannot proceed with rate-funded technology investment over the dominant carrier's objection. The PFC workaround is real but constrained by the $4.50 cap and carrier opposition through the FAA comment process.

The design implication is not subtle: airport IoT architecture must solve the carrier consent problem before it solves the sensor selection problem. A framework that survives three vendor changes, two network upgrades, and one CIO turnover must also survive one airline renegotiation, one use agreement expiration, and one hub carrier threatening to divert growth to a competing airport with lower CPE and a more accommodating technology governance posture.

Any airport strategy that does not model carrier response to each component of its IoT capital program is incomplete. The question is not "what's the best sensor." It is "under what contractual and commercial conditions will the dominant carrier tolerate this sensor generating data the airport controls."
