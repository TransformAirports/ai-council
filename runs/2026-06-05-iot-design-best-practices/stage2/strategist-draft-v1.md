# The Sensor Outlives the System

*Transform Airports AI Council — IoT Design Best Practices*
*Stage 2 Strategist Draft v1 | June 2026*

---

On February 22, 2022, AT&T switched off its 3G network. Verizon followed at the end of the year. Somewhere in the asset-tracking inventory of a dozen large American airports, the consequence was a quiet, distributed death: parking-guidance sensors, environmental monitors, energy meters, and elevator telemetry that had been installed sometime around 2017 simply stopped reporting. The sensors had not failed. The batteries were fine. The mounting brackets held. What had vanished was the radio underneath them — a decision made by a carrier in a boardroom no airport sat in. And because most IoT devices built on 3G cannot be rescued by swapping a SIM card, the fix was not a software push. It was a technician on a ladder at every sensor location, one at a time, at airport labor rates inside a Security Identification Display Area. No airport has ever published what that cost. The Technology Scout's read is the honest one: nobody disclosed the number because the number was embarrassing.

That is the shape of the problem this report is about. Not the sensor. The architecture around the sensor.

The thesis is simple and uncomfortable. Most airport IoT deployments strand within five years, and they strand not because the hardware breaks but because the surrounding decisions — which network carries the data, who owns the schema it is written in, how it integrates with everything else, which vendor holds the keys — were made for a pilot and never re-made for scale. The future-proofing question that matters is not *what is the best sensor.* It is *what is the architecture that survives three vendor changes, two network upgrades, and one CIO turnover.* An airport that cannot answer that question has not bought an operational-intelligence program. It has bought a future write-down with a warranty.

This is not a new failure. It is at least forty years old, and the airport industry has produced a fresh stranded generation in every technology wave since 1982. What is new is the surface area. The next failure will not strand the lighting data in one terminal. It will strand the gates, the baggage, the security queues, the ground-equipment fleet, and the digital twin — all at once, all on the same orphaned schema.

## Executive Summary

**1. The dominant failure mode in airport IoT is not sensor failure — it is platform discontinuation, protocol obsolescence, and schema lock, arriving on a schedule the vendor never put in the sales deck.** Three extinction events landed inside an eighteen-month window: Cisco ended its Kinetic IoT platform (last orders 2021), Sigfox — a low-power network operator with ten million devices across seventy-plus countries — filed for bankruptcy in January 2022, and the US carriers completed their 3G shutdown by December 2022 (Technology Scout; Operations Analyst). None of those three decisions was made by an airport. Each stranded capital equipment that was working perfectly the day before.

**2. The pilot-to-scale gap is the industry's modal outcome, and four of its five causes are architectural governance, not funding.** Cisco's 2017 survey of 1,845 decision-makers found 60% of IoT projects stall at proof of concept and only 26% are judged a complete success; McKinsey put the share stuck in pilot at 74–84% (Operations Analyst; Chief Engineer; Contrarian). The top failure factors — time to completion, internal expertise, data quality, cross-team integration — are governance problems. Budget shows up last, and it shows up *after* the integration failure, not before.

**3. The hidden cost structure, not the license fee, is what bankrupts the program.** Platform license fees are only 20–40% of three-year IIoT total cost of ownership; the rest hides in integration, change management, and the engineering time to maintain custom connectors (Infrastructure Economist). Forrester puts IoT cost underestimation at 40–60%; other industry data puts schedule overrun at 75% of projects taking twice as long as planned. The Chief Engineer's lifecycle math is blunter: a "pilot-priced" 1,000-sensor deployment runs 2.5–4× its Year-0 capital over ten years, and no airport feasibility study reviewed for these briefs modeled it.

**4. The network layer is the highest-leverage decision and the one most often made implicitly — and it is physical infrastructure, not a software choice.** A LoRaWAN gateway needs power, backhaul, and a mounting location above a live baggage conveyor; a private 5G base station runs $100K–$200K and needs licensed spectrum and equipment rooms; Wi-Fi 6E needs a forklift upgrade of every access point (Chief Engineer; Infrastructure Economist; Operations Analyst). Airports that treat the network as a demo-deck selection underestimate physical-plant cost by 40–60%. The durability hierarchy is knowable: private LoRaWAN and 3GPP-family cellular (LTE-M, NB-IoT) survive a ten-year horizon; private 5G is correct only for the high-bandwidth, low-latency workloads that justify it; proprietary narrow-band (Sigfox) has already demonstrated the exit.

**5. Data ownership is the least-discussed and most expensive dimension, and at airports it is legally unowned by default.** Airline use-and-lease agreements contain no standard data-rights language, and the IATA Aircraft Operational Data principles of October 2024 assert that data generated by an aircraft — *in flight or on the ground* — belongs to the airline, with no provision for the airport as a stakeholder (Airline Commercial Strategist; Chief Engineer). An airport that deploys sensors in a leased gate area, aggregates the data, and builds a platform on top is building on property it does not own and may be told to stop using.

**6. Vendor lock is not a decision — it accumulates across five layers and becomes visible only at exit.** Hardware, connectivity, platform, data schema, and application logic each add a quiet dependency, inflating five-year TCO by 20–30% and producing the documented pattern: a 20-sensor pilot becomes 2,000 sensors on an auto-renewing contract whose switching cost no longer pencils (Technology Scout; Operations Analyst). The single question most airport contracts never answer is the one that decides everything: *who owns the device certificates after the contract ends?* When the vendor holds them, migration means replacing hardware.

**7. The regulatory stack now kills IoT programs through means that have nothing to do with sensors, and it is tightening.** TSA's March 2023 directive imposed IT/OT network segmentation retroactively — flat-network deployments built in 2018–2021 are out of compliance by design — and a forthcoming FAA cybersecurity rulemaking (ARC chartered May 2025) will impose a second pass by 2027–2029 (Regulatory/Political Analyst; Chief Engineer). Meanwhile the PFC cap, frozen at $4.50 since 2001 and worth roughly $2.50 in real terms, structurally funds AIP-eligible conduit and equipment rooms while starving the data and integration layers that actually determine durability.

**8. The architecture that survives is not the most open or the most sophisticated — it is the most deliberately decoupled, and that is a narrower, cheaper claim than the vendors selling "platforms" want it to be.** Schiphol is the cleanest proof: a private LoRaWAN network on open-standard gateways (2019), a *managed* Kafka backbone (Confluent) chosen for cost and elasticity but kept on an open, portable schema, and private 5G added on top of the existing layers rather than replacing them (Technology Scout; Operations Analyst). The durable move is to decouple at the two seams that matter — the data schema and the transport broker — keep everything else simple enough that the team you actually have can run it, and write the exit cost into the contract before signing.

## The Argument

### Start with the graveyard, because it is real and it is recent

The most persuasive evidence for this thesis is not a forecast. It is a casualty list, and most of the casualties are less than five years old.

Google launched Cloud IoT Core in 2017, built on MQTT and HTTP with proprietary device SDKs embedded deep in firmware, and shut it down in August 2023 with twelve months' notice. Six years, a hyperscaler product, gone. Sigfox raised more than $300 million, built dedicated low-power networks in seventy-plus countries, signed up European airports and transport operators for perimeter monitoring and equipment telemetry, and filed for bankruptcy protection in January 2022 — €91 million in net loss on €24 million of revenue. Because Sigfox used a non-standard air interface, there was no fallback carrier; when the network wobbled, the only options were continued dependence on the acquirer or hardware replacement. Cisco retired Kinetic, including the "Kinetic for Cities" variant aimed squarely at campus deployments like airports, with no migration path to another Cisco product. IBM sunset Watson IoT Platform in December 2023 after roughly a decade — no direct replacement, MQTT connections terminated, customers left to re-architect. The Operations Analyst's framing is the one to keep: a hyperscaler was a vendor no airport IT department would have flagged as exit-risk at procurement. That is exactly why the risk was underweighted.

Each of these is the same story told in a different layer. Sigfox stranded the network. Google and IBM and Cisco stranded the platform. The 3G sunset stranded the radio. And the failure was never that the airport chose a *bad* vendor. The failure was that the airport coupled itself to that vendor at a layer it could not later decouple from without ripping out hardware.

The historian's contribution is to remove the novelty. This is not a smart-airport problem; it is a forty-year-old airport problem wearing new clothes. After deregulation gutted Braniff, Eastern, Pan Am, and Midway in the 1980s, airports discovered that airline-specific check-in terminals were not transferable — wrong hardware, wrong protocol, wrong vendor contract — and wrote them off carrier by carrier. The industry's answer was CUTE, the common-use standard deployed at LAX for the 1984 Olympics: standardized hardware, airline logic on airline servers. The architecture was correct. But the standard took a decade to reach critical mass and required two more re-platformings — to CUSS for kiosks, to CUPPS in 2009 — each leaving its own generation of stranded installations. The structural lesson, that terminal technology must be vendor-agnostic by architecture rather than by vendor promise, was learned, encoded in a standard, and then selectively forgotten every time a new wave arrived. The IoT question — MQTT or CoAP or proprietary transport — is CUTE's question asked again, one layer down.

And the original stranded IoT is not a metaphor; it is still running. The building-automation systems airports installed through the 1990s terminal-construction wave — HVAC, lighting, access control on LonTalk, Modbus, early BACnet — are physically operating in every legacy terminal in the country, transmitting data on protocols that cannot reach a modern IP platform without gateway hardware already a generation behind. A 2024 Claroty study found LonTalk still live in commercial facilities including airports, with default credentials and open ports that cannot be patched without hardware replacement. Today's pilot is tomorrow's LonTalk: a sensor still faithfully reporting into a void where its platform used to be.

### The number that should end the optimism: the cost is mostly invisible

The reason airports keep walking into this is that the capital request hides the cost. The line items that get board approval are the visible ones — sensors, gateways, a platform license. Those are the small fraction. The Infrastructure Economist's central finding is that license fees are only 20–40% of three-year IIoT TCO. The other 60–80% is integration with the CMMS, the FIDS, the BMS, the airline systems; change management through staff turnover; retraining every time a vendor revs its API; and the engineering hours to keep custom connectors alive. Forrester pegs the underestimation at 40–60%. The MachineCDN benchmark puts a 50-asset enterprise deployment at $700K–$1M over three years and a 1,000-asset airport at $3–10M.

The Chief Engineer runs the ten-year version of that math, and it is worse, because it includes the things a feasibility study omits: battery replacement at labor-dominant rates inside the SIDA; a gateway refresh at year five to seven driven not by hardware death but by protocol end-of-life; a platform renewal that arrives, predictably, at a 20–40% price increase once the airport is locked to the schema; and firmware end-of-life that turns a working sensor into a security liability before the next capital cycle authorizes its replacement. Total ten-year cost of a pilot-priced deployment: 2.5 to 4 times the Year-0 figure. The brief's most damning sentence is that not one airport feasibility study it reviewed included a ten-year TCO model. Every one showed Year-0 capital plus a vague "O&M."

This is the mechanism of pilot purgatory stated in dollars. The pilot works because the systems integrator is still on-site and the gateway is hand-managed. The second terminal surfaces the real cost structure. And then the rational move — for both the airport and the vendor — is to renew rather than migrate, because re-exporting eighteen months of proprietary-schema sensor data costs more than signing again. That is not a vendor conspiracy. It is what the Chief Engineer correctly calls rational economic behavior by both sides when the exit cost was never made explicit at procurement.

### The network decision is concrete, knowable, and usually made by accident

If one decision deserves the most engineering attention, it is the network, because it determines vendor optionality for a decade and because — the Chief Engineer insists on this — it is physical infrastructure pretending to be a software choice. Every LoRaWAN-versus-5G debate eventually becomes a conversation about conduit, power feeds, and where you can mount a gateway above an active conveyor at 3 a.m. inside a secure zone. Airports that skip the infrastructure survey underestimate physical-plant cost by 40–60%.

The good news is that, for once, the durability question has defensible answers rather than preferences. Private LoRaWAN is the strongest choice for high-density, low-bandwidth, facility-owned sensing: unlicensed spectrum, five-to-ten-year battery life, zero recurring per-device fee, and an open LoRaWAN Alliance specification that lets an airport swap gateways or network servers without touching the sensor layer. Schiphol covers its entire footprint — public and non-public — with a handful of Kerlink gateways on exactly this model. LTE-M and NB-IoT are now formally inside the 3GPP 5G family, which ties their lifecycle to cellular evolution rather than a single vendor's solvency; the cost trade is recurring carrier OpEx that compounds, manageable at a thousand sensors ($10–12 per device-year) and a six-figure annual bill at ten thousand, the point where private LoRaWAN wins even after gateway CapEx. Private 5G is genuinely the right answer — but only for the workloads that need it: video analytics, autonomous apron vehicles, mobile assets demanding sub-five-millisecond latency and deterministic handoffs. The Operations Analyst's warning is the one to frame: deploying private 5G because it is "future-proof" and then using it to track restroom occupancy is capital misallocation. Wi-Fi 6E is for passenger devices, not a sensor network; treating it as one inherits security exposure and the regulatory fragility that iOS 14's MAC randomization already demonstrated when it broke triangulation-based people-counting overnight.

What strands a network is not picking the second-best option. It is picking implicitly — running with whatever the first vendor's demo happened to use — and then discovering at the first upgrade that the sensor firmware only speaks the incumbent's proprietary gateway. And the sunsets are not hypothetical. GSMA Intelligence counts 143 global 2G/3G shutdowns scheduled between 2023 and 2030. Several carriers began retiring even LTE-M and NB-IoT in 2023–2024. Every connectivity standard has a finite life. The architecture has to be designed to survive the transition, which means the IoT layer must be decoupled from the transport layer by a standardized broker that outlives the radio beneath it.

### The seam that decides everything: data schema and the integration broker

Here is where the high-performing programs diverge from the rest, and the divergence is narrower than the marketing implies. It is not "buy a platform." It is two deliberate choices about where data lives and how it moves.

First, the schema. When an airport writes its operational history into a proprietary data model, the switching cost compounds every month, because migration is never just buying the new platform — it is running the old one in parallel, building translation layers to prove fidelity, and re-integrating every downstream consumer, several of which are FAA-regulated systems carrying their own change-management overhead. The Infrastructure Economist's rule of thumb is to budget 2× the vendor's integration quote, and to treat that as conservative for airports. The remedy is not exotic. The Operations Analyst's airfield-lighting case proves it in the hardest possible environment: a legacy proprietary airfield-lighting control system, retrofitted with an AFS-to-AMQP gateway translating closed protocols into standard AMQP and REST/JSON, hit end-to-end latencies of 43 milliseconds — comfortably inside the 500-millisecond regulatory limit for stop-bar operations — while preserving the legacy hardware and creating a portable data surface. Open protocol translation is achievable even in safety-critical, regulated infrastructure, and the migration cost came in below the cost of continued lock-in.

Second, the broker. Enterprise IT will recommend its familiar iPaaS tools — MuleSoft, Boomi, Informatica — built for synchronous, moderate-volume API integration between enterprise systems. IoT is asynchronous, high-volume, and latency-sensitive; forcing it through a synchronous iPaaS adds latency, creates a single point of failure, and produces integration contracts that break across firmware releases. The pattern the durable programs actually use is the opposite of what enterprise IT recommends: MQTT at the edge, a lightweight event broker (MQTT or Kafka) for the real-time stream, and downstream consumption by SCADA, BMS, AODB, and analytics. This is Schiphol's documented Kafka-and-Flink backbone. And the load-bearing principle is one sentence from the Operations Analyst: *the event broker is infrastructure, not an application — it must not be owned by the vendor that owns the sensors or the analytics.* When one vendor supplies sensor, integration, and dashboard, the airport has consolidated three dependencies into one contract, and one acquisition strands all three at once.

Schiphol is worth dwelling on because it is the rare program that made these choices on purpose and can show the operational return. Over ten months on 300 conveyor and asset sensors, its anomaly model triggered 42 inspections; 83% found a real problem; 60% of those needed active intervention; and the post-mortem concluded 26% of historical incidents could have been caught before they happened. That return did not come from a clever sensor. It came from owning the data layer — open LoRaWAN underneath, an open Kafka schema in the middle, managed infrastructure on top for elasticity, and private 5G added later as a new layer rather than a rebuild. The architecture is the product. The sensor is a commodity.

### Edge placement and certificates: two unglamorous decisions that quietly decide survival

Two more choices separate the durable from the doomed, and neither makes a good slide.

Edge compute placement follows one rule: processing that must survive a WAN outage belongs at the edge. Stop-bar lighting, HVAC setpoints, access-control triggers — none can accept a cloud round-trip in their decision loop. On-sensor anomaly detection cuts data egress by 90% on high-frequency signals; on-gateway aggregation and inference handle time-sensitive classification; on-premises holds the SCADA/BMS control loops that must run when the internet does not; cloud gets analytics, training, and benchmarking with no operational dependency. An airport that puts operational control logic in a cloud-only deployment has designed for the nominal case and ignored the failure mode — and the failure mode is a Pacific Northwest airport's employees handwriting boarding passes, which is the physical manifestation of misplaced compute.

And then the single contract question that decides whether migration is even possible: who owns the device certificates after termination? When the vendor holds them, the airport cannot move its devices to a new platform without physically replacing hardware. The Technology Scout's six-question portability framework puts this first because it is the question most airport contracts never answer. It belongs in the RFP, not the post-mortem.

## The Counter-Case, Honestly Presented

The strongest argument against everything above is that it is a consultant's product — complexity sold by people whose revenue scales with the complexity they recommend — and that it misdiagnoses the disease. The Contrarian brief makes this case without apology, and it is not a strawman. It is, in several places, correct.

Begin with the word "stranded." Enterprise IT runs on refresh cycles by design: servers four to six years, network gear five to seven, laptops three to four. By 2022 a five-year-plus lifecycle had become the norm. An IoT sensor network significantly upgraded at year five is not a stranded deployment; it is a planned refresh, the ordinary discipline applied to any asset. Define stranding as "eventually superseded" and every technology in history fails the test. The right question is not "did it survive five years?" but "did it return value during its operating life?" A building-management system that optimized HVAC loads for four years before a planned upgrade was not stranded. It was amortized.

Now turn the graveyard around, because it cuts the other way. BACnet was published in 1995. Modbus was developed in 1979. Both still dominate airport building automation — forty-five years for Modbus, three decades for BACnet, running quietly in SCADA and BMS environments that clock ten to twenty years without overhaul. Against that, look at what the architecture-first thesis points airports *toward*: Google Cloud IoT Core, dead at six. IBM Watson IoT, dead at ten with no replacement. Cisco Kinetic, retired. Azure IoT Central, effectively wound down. A Honeywell BMS at Indira Gandhi International — single-vendor, "locked-in," proprietary — has outlived every major cloud IoT platform brought to market in the last decade. The thesis tells airports to flee vendor lock-in into the arms of hyperscalers who have proved *less* durable than the vendors they are fleeing. The empirical record of which protocols actually endure favors the opposite of what the report recommends.

The deeper objection is about cause. If stranding were primarily driven by wrong network choice or proprietary schema, programs would die in years three to five, after deployment, when those decisions bite. Instead, 72% of IoT initiatives never reach production at all. They die before architecture matters — killed by unclear business goals, absent organizational alignment, and no hard-currency link to a measurable outcome. Cisco's own IoT Signals data put successful completion at 26% and named unclear business goals, not integration architecture, as the leading cause. An airport that deploys queue sensors, builds a dashboard, and never connects the data to a gate-staffing decision is not suffering from vendor lock-in. It is suffering from unclear purpose, and a better integration stack would not have saved it. Fixing the architecture does not fix the purpose.

The thesis also smuggles in a cost assumption that does not survive contact with the field. Open protocols, portable schemas, and multi-vendor stacks do not cost roughly the same as proprietary deployments while adding optionality. They cost more. Every seam — separate MQTT brokers, Kafka clusters, schema-translation layers, API gateways — adds infrastructure, operations, and maintenance overhead that scales with fleet size. IIoT projects underestimate TCO by 30–50%, and that gap widens in regulated environments. A single-vendor integrated deployment eliminates most of the seam cost: one logistics operator cut its IoT spend from roughly $38,000 a month to $8,000 by consolidating from a public-cloud IoT service to a single on-premises solution. That is not a lock-in risk. That is a lock-in benefit.

And it assumes a workforce airports do not have. The prescription — architect for three vendor changes and two network upgrades — presumes in-house engineers who can write schemas, govern APIs, run brokers, and execute migrations. Almost no airport has them. The airport that builds a "three-vendor-change-surviving" architecture and then loses its one IoT architect to a tech company in year two has produced a stranded deployment of a different kind: an orphaned architecture too complex for the staff who remain. The benchmark the industry actually admires cuts against the thesis. Changi's Mozart platform coordinates 5,000-plus sensors, 700 cameras, and 500 mobile devices — not as a loosely coupled, schema-portable, multi-vendor mesh, but as a deeply integrated single-platform deployment backed by a large internal technology organization. The airports that do IoT well tend to commit deeply to one integrated platform and build capability around it, not to architect for an optionality they will almost never exercise.

Finally, the thesis skips the physical layer. Before any protocol or schema question, a sensor must be powered, mounted, and able to transmit through metal structures, subsurface baggage tunnels, and RF-hostile apron geometry. Those constraints — power, cable pathways, propagation, mounting access — determine what can be deployed where. An elegant data architecture is worthless if the sensor cannot be installed at the right spot. The thesis treats the physical layer as solved and argues about the data layer. In most airports the physical layer is not solved, and no architectural sophistication above it changes that. The Contrarian's closing position is precise: architecture is not irrelevant; it is fourth on the list, behind purpose, sponsorship, and staffing, and the report should not give it first billing.

The Regulatory and Political brief sharpens the same blade from a different angle. Even an airport that wants to build the durable architecture must fight its own capital structure to do it. The PFC cap has been frozen at $4.50 since 2001 — worth roughly $2.50 today — and AIP eligibility funds runways, taxiways, lighting, conduit, and equipment rooms, not middleware, schema migration, or vendor-neutral integration layers. Rating agencies reward visible, bond-ratable revenue and view operational-technology spend with skepticism unless it models clean OpEx reduction. The result is a structural bias: airports can fund the *hardware* of IoT and not the *durability* of IoT. The exact layers that make a deployment survive are the layers the funding system is built to starve. Telling an airport to invest in integration-layer maintenance is telling it to fight uphill against its own bond covenants.

A reader who came in skeptical should, at this point, feel the case has been made fairly. It has. Now it has to be answered.

## Why the Counter-Case Is Insufficient

Concede the strong points first, by name, because they are real.

The cloud graveyard is real, and it is the thesis's own counterexample. An airport that built a serious pipeline on Google Cloud IoT Core or IBM Watson IoT is stranded today, and "go open" is not, by itself, a defense — because the alternative usually recommended (MQTT brokers, schema registries, multi-vendor stacks) demands precisely the specialized staff airports lack. Concede it. Concede, too, that most programs die before architecture matters: 72% never reach production, and the killer at that stage is unclear purpose, not protocol. Concede that open architecture carries a documented 30–50% TCO premium and that consolidation can genuinely cut cost. Concede that BACnet and Modbus have outlasted every cloud IoT platform. Concede that the physical layer comes first and that staffing is a binding constraint. None of these is a quibble. All of them are true.

And yet the thesis holds, because the Contrarian has mostly refuted a claim the thesis does not make.

The thesis is not "choose open over proprietary." It is not "buy the sophisticated multi-vendor platform." Read the Contrarian's own conclusion and the agreement is startling: *deploy against a clear outcome, staff for maintainability, and treat every hyperscaler managed service as a platform risk.* That last clause is the thesis. "Treat every managed cloud IoT service as a platform risk" is exactly what an airport learns from Google IoT Core and Watson — and it is an architectural conclusion, not an organizational one. The Contrarian and the thesis disagree about billing order. They agree about the substance more than either is comfortable admitting.

Now resolve the BACnet paradox, because it is the counter-case's best shot and it actually proves the point. Why has Modbus survived forty-five years and BACnet thirty while Google IoT Core died at six? Not because they were proprietary and single-vendor. *Because they were open, standardized, and decoupled from any one vendor's solvency.* Modbus endures precisely because no single company's bankruptcy can take it down — any vendor's device can speak it, and the protocol outlives them all. The Honeywell BMS at Indira Gandhi survives not because Honeywell locked the airport in but because it speaks BACnet, a standard that predates and will outlive Honeywell's product roadmap. The durable thing in every one of the Contrarian's own examples is the open, decoupled interface, and the dead thing in every one is the proprietary, vendor-coupled platform. The lesson the counter-case draws — embrace single-vendor lock-in — is the opposite of what its own evidence shows. The correct lesson is the thesis: durability comes from decoupling at the interface, not from choosing a vendor and praying it survives.

This reframes the whole disagreement, and it is where the thesis earns its keep. The Contrarian is right that "maximum optionality" is a consultant's product — expensive, complex, unstaffable, and rarely exercised. But "maximum optionality" is a caricature of the thesis. The actual prescription is *minimal coupling at the two or three seams that matter, and ruthless simplicity everywhere else.* Decouple the data schema, because that is what compounds and that is what FAA-regulated downstream systems depend on. Decouple the transport broker from the sensor vendor, because that is what survives the radio sunset. Keep operational control logic at the edge, because that is what survives the WAN outage. And then — this is the part the Contrarian gets right and the naive version of the thesis gets wrong — make everything above those seams as simple as the team you actually have can run. Schiphol is not a maximal-optionality cathedral. It chose *managed* Confluent over self-hosted open-source Kafka explicitly for cost, elasticity, and the staffing reality, while keeping the schema open so the data stays portable. That is the synthesis: open where it counts, managed where it doesn't, simple by default. Durability is not complexity. The Contrarian conflated the two, and the conflation is the flaw in an otherwise excellent brief.

On the "72% die before architecture matters" point — concede the number, reject the inference. It is true that most programs die at the business-case stage, and it is true that an architecture framework will not save a program with no purpose. But the thesis is not addressed to the program that dies in the pilot. It is addressed to the program that *survives* — that clears the purpose gate, gets sponsorship, deploys, and scales — and then strands in year five because the surviving program made its architecture decisions back when it was a pilot and never re-made them. The Contrarian's own "scenarios where the thesis is correct" concede exactly this: cloud-lock with a platform that fails, network stranding on proprietary narrow-band, BHS schema continuity across a vendor change, multi-terminal phased expansion. Those are not edge cases at a large hub. They are the operating conditions of a large hub. The thesis is precisely scoped to the programs worth saving. You do not write an architecture standard for the deployments that die in the crib. You write it for the ones that live long enough to strand expensively.

The staffing objection is the most serious, and it modifies the prescription rather than defeating it. Airports do lack integration engineers, and an over-architected stack will orphan itself when the one architect leaves — that is the Chief Engineer's "one CIO turnover" risk made concrete, and it is real. But the conclusion is not "give up and lock in." It is "decouple at the few seams, then buy managed simplicity for the rest, and write the architecture down so a competent engineer who wasn't there at year zero can run it at year five." The contract does the work the staff cannot: a data-export clause with liquidated damages, certificate ownership transferring to the airport at termination, sensor procurement separated from platform procurement so competition survives at both layers. The Chief Engineer is right that every vendor promises interoperability and only a contract clause with teeth makes it real. That is not a staffing burden. It is a procurement discipline, exercised once, at signature — and it is far cheaper than the migration it prevents.

Which leaves the capital-structure objection, the one the thesis cannot argue away and should not try. The Regulatory brief is correct that the PFC freeze and AIP eligibility starve the durable layers. But notice what that argument actually establishes: it does not show the architecture is wrong. It shows the architecture is *underfunded* — that airports systematically buy the sensor hardware (AIP-eligible, bond-ratable) and skimp on the schema and integration layers (neither), and then strand. That is not a refutation of the thesis. It is the thesis's causal mechanism, confirmed by an independent lens. The funding system biases airports toward exactly the half of the architecture that does not determine durability. The implication is not to abandon the durable design. It is to fund it deliberately — through operating budget, through the integration line of a capital project, through writing data-layer requirements into the RFP so the integration cost is bid rather than discovered — precisely because the capital structure will not fund it by default. The constraint is real. It makes the discipline more necessary, not less.

So the thesis stands, narrowed and sharpened by the attack: not "build for maximum optionality," but "decouple deliberately at the schema and the broker, keep everything else simple enough to staff, make exit cost contractual, and fund the durable layers on purpose because the system will not fund them for you." The Contrarian was right that the naive version deserved to lose. The narrowed version is stronger for having survived.

## Implications for MWAA and Washington Dulles

This is not an abstract debate for the Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority. It is a decision being poured in concrete right now.

MWAA's new use-and-lease agreement took effect January 1, 2025, governing a $9 billion capital program with $6.99 billion of it at Dulles. The centerpiece for the airline that matters is United's $500-million-plus Concourse E expansion: fourteen exclusive-use gates in a 435,000-square-foot facility, announced December 2024, defining the physical and operational envelope of Dulles for the next decade. Concourse E is the single highest-stakes IoT architecture decision MWAA will make in this cycle, and it is being made *now*, because the cheapest moment to design the data layer is before the slab is poured. The conduit runs, the equipment rooms, the structured wiring, the network backbone — all of it is being specified today under FAA AC 150/5370-10H, and all of it is far cheaper to get right in design than to retrofit. The historian's warning about IIJA-era capital urgency applies directly: the conditions that produced stranded EDS installations in 2002 — capital abundance, compressed timelines, pressure to show progress — are the conditions MWAA is building under in 2026.

Three specific exposures follow from the briefs.

First, the data-rights vacuum is acute at Dulles precisely because United is dominant. Those fourteen gates are United's leased premises. The IATA Aircraft Operational Data principles of October 2024 assert that data generated by an aircraft, on the ground as well as in flight, belongs to the airline — and turnaround activity in a gate area (docking time, bridge engagement, ground-equipment positioning) is exactly that. United has already migrated hundreds of applications to AWS and booked $2 billion in technology savings; it has its own operational data infrastructure and does not need MWAA's. So any sensor MWAA installs in Concourse E is a parallel system generating duplicate data in a space where United controls the operational reality and arguably owns the data — and where United's effective majority-in-interest position means MWAA's rate-funded technology capital is a bilateral negotiation with United, not a multi-stakeholder decision. The AirAsia precedent at klia2 is the cautionary tale: a dominant carrier paying through fees for an airport platform that degraded its own automation. MWAA must resolve the United consent-and-data question in the lease language *before* deploying, not after the legal notice arrives. The architecture problem is downstream of the contract problem.

Second, fund the durable layers on purpose, because the capital structure will not. Concourse E's AIP-eligible elements — conduit, equipment rooms, structured wiring — will get funded; that is what the system funds. The schema design, the integration broker, the vendor-neutral middleware, the certificate-ownership and data-export clauses are the layers that determine whether the deployment survives to 2040, and they are exactly the layers AIP and a frozen PFC do not cover. MWAA should write data-layer requirements into the Concourse E technology RFP so the integration cost is bid and visible rather than discovered at the second terminal. The Chief Engineer's point is the operative one: ACRIS open APIs and FAA SWIM schemas exist to solve airport data portability, and most RFPs do not reference them, because procurement officers do not know they exist and incumbent vendors have no incentive to volunteer the requirement that limits their lock-in. MWAA can close that gap with a sentence in the specification.

Third, the segmentation clock is already running, and Dulles operates under it. TSA's March 2023 directive made IT/OT segmentation mandatory and retroactive; the FAA cybersecurity rulemaking now in the ARC pipeline will impose a second pass by 2027–2029. A flat-network sensor deployment in Concourse E would be born out of compliance and would face two rounds of forced remediation inside its first five years. The SEA-TAC ransomware attack of August 2024 — dry-erase boards, 90,000 breach notifications, three weeks of manual baggage handling at a peer airport — is the demonstration of what the seam costs when a threat actor finds it. The CISO is already a veto voter on this architecture, whether or not the program plan acknowledges it; better to design segmentation in at Concourse E than to have the program paused for "architecture review," which is where airport IoT goes to die quietly.

The concrete recommendation for MWAA is narrow and cheap relative to the $6.99 billion it sits inside. On Concourse E, before the build locks: separate sensor procurement from platform procurement; require open-schema data export and airport ownership of device certificates at termination, with liquidated damages; specify ACRIS/SWIM-compatible APIs; decouple the transport broker from the sensor vendor; segment OT from IT to current TSA standard; and settle the United data-rights question in writing. None of that is a moonshot architecture. It is a procurement discipline exercised once, at the moment MWAA has the most leverage it will ever have — before the contract is signed and the schema starts filling up.

---

The sensors going into Concourse E in the next two years will almost certainly still be bolted to the walls in 2040. The question is whether anyone will still be able to read what they are saying — or whether they will have joined the LonTalk controllers in the old terminals, faithfully reporting, decade after decade, into a platform that no longer exists, in a language no one kept the key to.
