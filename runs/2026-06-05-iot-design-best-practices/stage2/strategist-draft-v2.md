On February 22, 2022, AT&T switched off its 3G network. Verizon followed at the end of the year. No public record says exactly what that did to any one airport's sensor inventory — and that absence is itself the first piece of evidence. So picture it, as a reconstruction faithful to the mechanism if not to a specific invoice: somewhere in the asset-tracking and facilities-telemetry inventory of a large American hub, a population of devices installed before 2019 simply stopped reporting. The sensors had not failed. The batteries were fine. The mounting brackets held. What had vanished was the radio underneath them — a decision made by a carrier in a boardroom no airport sat in. And because most IoT devices built on 3G cannot be rescued by swapping a SIM card, the fix was not a software push. It was a technician on a ladder at every sensor location, one at a time, at airport labor rates, inside a Security Identification Display Area. No airport has published what that cost. The most likely reason nobody disclosed the number is that the number was embarrassing.

That is the shape of the problem this report is about. Not the sensor. The architecture around the sensor.

The thesis is narrow on purpose. Of the airport IoT programs that survive the pilot and actually scale, a large share strand inside their first capital cycle — and they strand not because the hardware breaks but because the surrounding decisions (which network carries the data, who owns the schema it is written in, how it integrates with everything else, which vendor holds the keys, and who inside the airport is personally accountable for keeping it alive) were made for a twelve-month proof of concept and never re-made for a twenty-five-year bond. The future-proofing question that matters is not *what is the best sensor.* It is *what is the architecture — technical and organizational — that survives multiple vendor changes, at least one network upgrade, and the departure of the people who built it.* An airport that cannot answer that question has not bought an operational-intelligence program. It has bought a future write-down with a warranty.

This is not a new failure. The airport industry has produced a fresh stranded generation in every technology wave for forty years. What is new is the surface area. The next failure will not strand the lighting data in one terminal. It will strand the gates, the baggage, the security queues, the ground-equipment fleet, and the digital twin — all at once, all on the same orphaned schema.

## Executive Summary

**1. The dominant failure mode in airport IoT is not sensor failure — it is platform discontinuation, protocol obsolescence, and schema lock, arriving on a schedule the vendor never put in the sales deck.** Three extinction events landed inside an eighteen-month window: Cisco ended its Kinetic IoT platform (final order date May 2021), Sigfox — a low-power network operator with roughly ten million devices across seventy-plus countries — filed for bankruptcy in January 2022, and the US carriers completed their 3G shutdown by December 2022. None of those three decisions was made by an airport. Each stranded capital equipment that was working perfectly the day before.

**2. The pilot-to-scale gap is the industry's modal outcome, but the stranding this report addresses happens *after* the pilot survives.** Cisco's 2017 survey of 1,845 decision-makers found 60% of IoT projects stall at proof of concept and only 26% are judged a complete success; McKinsey's 2018 survey put the share stuck in pilot at 84%. Those numbers measure programs that never scale. This report is about the smaller, more expensive population: the programs that *do* scale, get bonded, and then strand in year five or six because the architecture was set when the program was a pilot and never revisited.

**3. The decision that strands a scaled program is not a technology choice — it is an authorization gap.** When a pilot scales past the operating-budget threshold and below the board-approval threshold, it quietly becomes a capital liability amortized over 25–30 years against debt-service-coverage ratios the rating agencies score. At a residual-cost airport, that liability flows straight to signatory-airline rates and through majority-in-interest clauses. The Denver Great Hall P3 — terminated at 30% design completion, settled for $183.6 million — is the governance failure in its purest form: a long-horizon financial commitment made before the design was defined.

**4. The hidden cost structure, not the license fee, is what bankrupts the program.** Platform license fees are only 20–40% of three-year industrial-IoT total cost of ownership; the rest hides in integration, change management, and the engineering time to maintain custom connectors. Forrester puts IoT cost underestimation at 40–60%. A "pilot-priced" 1,000-sensor deployment runs 2.5–4× its Year-0 capital over ten years — and no airport feasibility study reviewed for this work modeled that ten-year figure. Toronto Pearson is the bill made visible: seven SCADA systems on three platforms accreted over fifteen years, consolidated only after converting 60,000 data tags and running parallel systems for twelve months.

**5. The network layer is the highest-leverage decision and the one most often made implicitly — and it is physical infrastructure, not a software choice.** A LoRaWAN gateway needs power, backhaul, and a mounting location above a live baggage conveyor; private 5G needs licensed spectrum and equipment rooms; Wi-Fi 6E is for passenger devices, not sensors. The durability hierarchy is knowable: private LoRaWAN and 3GPP-family cellular (LTE-M, NB-IoT) survive a ten-year horizon; private 5G is correct only for the high-bandwidth, low-latency workloads that justify it; proprietary narrow-band (Sigfox) has already demonstrated the exit. But durability is bounded by what sits *below* the abstraction layer: a proprietary protocol on a fifteen-year PLC cannot be cleanly abstracted, which is why "survives two network upgrades" is a design target, not a baseline expectation.

**6. Data ownership is the least-discussed and most expensive dimension, and at airports it is legally unowned by default.** Airline use-and-lease agreements contain no standard data-rights language, and IATA's October 2024 Aircraft Operational Data principles assert that data generated by an aircraft — in flight or on the ground — belongs to the airline. An airport that deploys sensors in a leased gate area, aggregates the data, and builds a platform on top is building on property it does not clearly own. ACRP's own research finds airport IoT data streams "frequently fall under vendor control," making airport access to its own data "challenging or costly."

**7. Vendor lock is not a decision — it accumulates across five layers and becomes visible only at exit; and "open" is not free.** Hardware, connectivity, platform, data schema, and application logic each add a quiet dependency, inflating five-year TCO by an estimated 20–30%. The single question most airport contracts never answer is the one that decides everything: *who owns the device certificates after the contract ends?* When the vendor holds them, migration means replacing hardware. But the open, API-rich architecture that fixes lock-in also expands the attack surface — the Seattle-Tacoma ransomware attack of August 2024 came through networked systems, not closed ones — so decoupling and segmentation have to be designed as one discipline, not traded against each other.

**8. The architecture that survives is the most deliberately decoupled *and* the most clearly owned — a narrower, cheaper, and more organizational claim than the vendors selling "platforms" want it to be.** Schiphol decoupled at the two seams that matter (the data schema and the transport broker) while buying *managed* simplicity above them; the Port of Rotterdam survives four simultaneous vendors because it declared the data layer to be port infrastructure and made it stick; Barcelona's open-source Sentilo platform took a decade and sustained civic will to build. The durable move is to decouple where it counts, keep everything else simple enough that the team you actually have can run it, write the exit cost into the contract before signing — and name a single executive accountable for the system's continuity across the leadership turnover that is a certainty.

## The Argument

### Start with the graveyard, because it is real, it is recent, and most of it is at airports

The most persuasive evidence for this thesis is not a forecast. It is a casualty list. The run that commissioned this report asked for a catalog of airport IoT deployments that became orphaned or got ripped out, and the specific decision that doomed each. Here it is.

**Toronto Pearson — seven SCADA systems, fifteen years, one consolidation bill.** Over fifteen years of incremental baggage-system upgrades, each procurement team chose the best available SCADA platform for its specific project. None of them was wrong in isolation. No integration requirement was ever written into a single one of those procurements. By 2019 Pearson was operating seven independent SCADA systems on three different vendor platforms, a system Inductive Automation's case record describes as "very hard and expensive to support, upgrade and maintain," carrying chronic latency. The airport's Baggage 2025 program could not begin modernization until it first consolidated its own supervisory layer: 60,000 data tags converted, more than 100 graphical displays translated, twelve months of parallel operation. The doomed decision was not a bad product. It was the absence, fifteen times running, of the sentence *this system must share a supervisory interface with the last one.* Stranding by accretion.

**Paris Orly — the runway that was closed by Windows 3.1.** DECOR, the system linking Orly's air-traffic control to Météo-France for low-visibility operations, ran on Windows 3.1. It failed in November 2015 and closed a runway. France had three specialists who could work with the system; one was retiring with no identified successor. The remediation was slow because the institutional knowledge of how the thing worked lived in three human beings rather than in documentation and a standards-based architecture anyone could interrogate. The doomed decision was to let the system's continuity depend on people instead of on a self-documenting design — the "one CIO turnover" risk, but for the network engineer and the facilities technician who never wrote anything down.

**The iFlow passenger-flow system — running, and unused.** A major hub deployed more than 400 Wi-Fi access points, 130 people-counters, and 50 Bluetooth sensors across its terminals to measure passenger flow. A peer-reviewed study found the output lacked precision: low triangulation accuracy, infrequent updates, many devices seen only a couple of times an hour. The system was never ripped out. It was deprioritized. The operations team stopped trusting the data, the vendor relationship atrophied, and the deployment now runs into a void where its users used to be. This is the quietest and most common stranding mode of all — not failure, abandonment. It also answers a critique the counter-case will raise: a system can have perfectly adequate architecture and still strand, because nobody trusted what it said. Purpose and architecture are not the same failure, and a program can suffer both.

**Charlotte Douglas — the right sensor, the unanswered architecture.** CLT is embedding 2,000 pavement sensors in its fourth parallel runway, opening September 2027, on a $6.5 million project feeding FAA pavement research and a digital twin. The sensors are specified for a decade of life. The runway will be in service for thirty to forty years. No public statement answers who owns the data pipeline after the university research program concludes, who maintains the integration when FAA's research interest moves on, or what the plan is for sensor generations two through four. This is not a criticism of CLT; it is the standard condition. The sensor decision is made and announced. The architecture decision is neither made nor announced, which is exactly how it strands.

**Dallas Love Field — the network you do not own.** Boingo built the first airport private CBRS network at Love Field in 2018. The infrastructure is owned and operated by Boingo; the airport controls spectrum access within its footprint, but the operating relationship is with a third party. What happens in 2028 if the operating agreement lapses, Boingo is acquired, or CBRS allocation rules change is a question that belongs in a use-and-lease negotiation before the dependency is load-bearing, not after. The doomed-in-waiting decision is the one common to outsourced infrastructure everywhere: the most durable layer of the stack is the one the airport does not control.

**Cisco Kinetic for Cities — vendor mortality, the polite version.** Cisco was not a startup. It folded its Kinetic for Cities smart-infrastructure platform in 2020, stopped sales, extended support through 2024, and offered no replacement product — existing customers could "transfer their data to a Cisco partner." That is the polite form of stranding: the vendor honors the contract and exits the market anyway. Lock-in is being trapped by a living vendor; mortality is being orphaned by an exiting one, and no contract clause prevents it because no clause was breached. The defense against mortality is not a better contract — it is an architecture in which no single vendor is load-bearing.

**Sigfox — the network that went bankrupt under its customers.** Sigfox raised more than $300 million, built dedicated low-power networks in seventy-plus countries, signed European airports and transport operators for perimeter monitoring and equipment telemetry, and filed for bankruptcy in January 2022 — a €91 million net loss on €24 million of revenue, acquired for €25 million, back in court protection by 2025. Because Sigfox used a non-standard air interface, there was no fallback carrier. When the network wobbled, the only options were continued dependence on the acquirer or hardware replacement. The doomed decision was coupling to a proprietary radio with no second source.

**The 3G sunset — the radio with a finite life.** Already told above. The doomed decision was treating a carrier's network as permanent infrastructure when it was a depreciating asset on someone else's balance sheet.

**Denver International Airport's automated baggage system — the original.** The most documented technology failure in US airport history. A $193 million BAE contract scaled toward $360–400 million, delayed the airport's opening by sixteen months, and was largely decommissioned within roughly a decade. A GAO review found the scope had been expanded mid-development from one concourse to three, with integration treated as a residual rather than a first-class design requirement and 72 certification tasks still incomplete at the expected commissioning date. It is not an IoT deployment, but it is the same mechanism: a system with no graceful degradation path and no vendor ecosystem becomes inescapable, and you cannot leave because the cost of leaving exceeds the cost of staying. The bond obligations did not retire when the system did.

Each of these is the same story told in a different layer. Pearson stranded the integration layer through accretion. Orly stranded on human knowledge. iFlow stranded on trust. CLT and Love Field are stranding-in-waiting on ownership. Cisco and Sigfox stranded the platform and the network. The 3G sunset stranded the radio. Denver stranded the whole thing. In not one of them was the failure that the airport chose a *bad* sensor. The failure was coupling to something — a vendor, a protocol, a radio, a single person's memory — at a layer the airport could not later decouple from without ripping out hardware.

History removes the novelty. After deregulation gutted Braniff, Eastern, Pan Am, and Midway in the 1980s, airports discovered that airline-specific check-in terminals were not transferable and wrote them off carrier by carrier. The industry's answer was the common-use standard — CUTE, deployed at LAX for the 1984 Olympics: standardized hardware, airline logic on airline servers. The architecture was correct. But the standard took a decade to reach critical mass and required two more re-platformings — CUSS for kiosks, CUPPS in 2009 — each leaving its own stranded generation. The lesson, that terminal technology must be vendor-agnostic by architecture rather than by vendor promise, was learned, encoded in a standard, and then selectively forgotten every time a new wave arrived. The IoT question — MQTT or CoAP or proprietary transport — is CUTE's question asked again, one layer down. And the original stranded IoT is still running: the building-automation systems installed through the 1990s terminal-construction wave, on LonTalk and Modbus and early BACnet, are physically operating in every legacy terminal in the country, transmitting on protocols that cannot reach a modern IP platform without gateway hardware already a generation behind. Today's pilot is tomorrow's LonTalk: a sensor still faithfully reporting into a void where its platform used to be.

### The decision that strands a scaled program is a bond decision wearing a technology costume

Here is the part the architecture conversation skips, because it happens above the architects' heads. When a pilot succeeds and scales, it crosses a threshold. Below that threshold it was an operating expense, a few sensors a department bought on a discretionary line. Above it, it is capital — and capital at an airport joins the amortization schedule that Moody's, S&P, and Fitch scrutinize, the one scored against a debt-service-coverage ratio that large hubs target around 2.0x. A capital program that reliably produces five-year stranded assets while carrying 25-to-30-year bonds is a story no airport CEO wants to tell in a rating-agency briefing.

The trap is in the seam. A pilot that scales *past the operating-budget threshold but below the board-approval threshold* becomes a permanent financial commitment that no one ever formally authorized. It succeeded, so it grew; it grew, so it got bonded; nobody re-asked the architecture question at the moment it stopped being an experiment and became a liability. At a residual-cost airport, that liability does not even stay inside the airport — it flows to signatory carriers through the rate base, which is exactly what majority-in-interest clauses were built to police. An airport that arrives at an airline partners committee with a $75 million IoT remediation program, because the previous architecture required rip-and-replace, is negotiating from a posture it did not choose.

The Denver Great Hall is the governance failure stripped of its technology. Denver signed a $1.8 billion concession with $650 million of fixed construction at 30% design completion; eleven months into construction the contractor asked for a $288 million change order; the airport terminated and settled for $183.6 million. The root cause its own CEO named — committing a long-horizon financial structure to an incompletely defined program — maps onto IoT exactly. CVG sharpens the credit point from the other side: the airport fell from A1 to Baa1 after Delta de-hubbed in 2013, having done nothing operationally wrong. An external decision by a single dominant counterparty reshaped the credit profile. A network provider's insolvency does the same thing to every device on that network. Single-dependency risk in technology architecture is the credit analog of single-carrier concentration, and the rating agencies price both.

The personnel axis makes it worse than the slogan admits. CIO tenure at large US organizations averages under five years; for state-government CIOs the documented figure is about twenty months, with nearly 40% of CIOs serving two years or less. A five-year IoT contract will therefore see roughly two CIO cycles before its first renewal. The incoming CIO has no incentive to inherit a predecessor's undocumented platform gracefully; the rational move is to declare it legacy and start fresh. So the survival standard is not "one CIO turnover." Plan for two. And the cost of each re-architecture appears in the capital program, gets bonded, and sits on the balance sheet for two decades. The technology officers will argue LoRaWAN versus 5G. The question upstream of all of them is who authorized the pilot architecture to become the permanent financial commitment, and at what threshold it stopped being a procurement decision and became a bond decision.

### The cost is mostly invisible, which is why airports keep walking into it

The reason airports keep making this mistake is that the capital request hides the cost. The line items that get board approval are the visible ones — sensors, gateways, a platform license. Those are the small fraction. License fees are only 20–40% of three-year industrial-IoT total cost of ownership. The other 60–80% hides in integration with the CMMS, the FIDS, the BMS, the airline systems; in change management through staff turnover; in retraining every time a vendor revs its API; and in the engineering hours to keep custom connectors alive. Forrester pegs the underestimation at 40–60%. A standard benchmark puts a 50-asset enterprise deployment at $700K–$1M over three years and a 1,000-asset airport at $3–10M.

The ten-year version is worse, because it includes the things a feasibility study omits: battery replacement at labor-dominant rates inside the SIDA; a gateway refresh at year five to seven driven not by hardware death but by protocol end-of-life; a platform renewal that arrives, predictably, at a 20–40% price increase once the airport is locked to the schema (the documented logistics case saw a 35% per-device increase at renewal, with the vendor holding the device certificates); and firmware end-of-life that turns a working sensor into a security liability before the next capital cycle authorizes its replacement. Total ten-year cost of a pilot-priced deployment: 2.5 to 4 times the Year-0 figure. The most damning finding in the entire research set is the simplest: not one airport feasibility study reviewed for this work included a ten-year TCO model. Every one showed Year-0 capital plus a vague "O&M."

This is pilot purgatory stated in dollars. The pilot works because the systems integrator is still on-site and the gateway is hand-managed. The second terminal surfaces the real cost structure. And then the rational move — for both the airport and the vendor — is to renew rather than migrate, because re-exporting eighteen months of proprietary-schema sensor data costs more than signing again. That is not a vendor conspiracy. It is rational economic behavior by both sides when the exit cost was never made explicit at procurement. Toronto Pearson is the same lesson at the enterprise scale: its prior structure of 150 separate IT vendors was so expensive to manage that consolidating to a single managed-services provider produced a 94% reduction in major outages. The lesson there is not that consolidation is always right — it is that fragmentation cost can grow severe enough to justify a very large check, and that the check always comes due later than the pilot's economics implied.

### The network decision is concrete, knowable, and usually made by accident

If one decision deserves the most engineering attention, it is the network, because it determines vendor optionality for a decade and because it is physical infrastructure pretending to be a software choice. Every LoRaWAN-versus-5G debate eventually becomes a conversation about conduit, power feeds, and where you can mount a gateway above an active conveyor at three in the morning inside a secure zone. A major hub's communications plant is already fragmented across Wi-Fi, cellular DAS, proprietary OT networks, and hardwired control before the first sensor is placed; the 40%-plus sensor dropout rates reported in dense airport deployments are not discovered in production but designed in during procurement and discovered at 5:45 on a Monday when the ops center starts getting alerts.

For once, the durability question has defensible answers rather than preferences. Private LoRaWAN is the strongest choice for high-density, low-bandwidth, facility-owned sensing: unlicensed spectrum, five-to-ten-year battery life, zero recurring per-device fee, and an open LoRaWAN Alliance specification that lets an airport swap gateways or network servers without touching the sensor layer. LTE-M and NB-IoT now sit formally inside the 3GPP 5G family, which ties their lifecycle to cellular evolution rather than a single vendor's solvency; the cost trade is recurring carrier OpEx that compounds — manageable at a thousand sensors (roughly $10–12 per device-year) and a six-figure annual bill at ten thousand, the point where private LoRaWAN wins even after gateway capital. Private 5G is genuinely the right answer for the workloads that need it: video analytics, autonomous apron vehicles, mobile assets demanding sub-five-millisecond latency and deterministic handoffs. Deploying private 5G because it is "future-proof" and then using it to track restroom occupancy is capital misallocation. Wi-Fi 6E is for passenger devices, not a sensor network; treating it as one inherits security exposure and the regulatory fragility that iOS 14's MAC randomization already demonstrated when it broke triangulation-based people-counting overnight — the failure mode underneath iFlow.

Two honest qualifications, because the network decision is where the thesis is most often oversold. First, the sunsets are not hypothetical and they do not spare the "safe" choices: GSMA Intelligence counts well over a hundred global 2G/3G shutdowns scheduled through 2030, and some carriers began retiring even LTE-M and NB-IoT in 2023–2024. Every connectivity standard has a finite life; the architecture has to survive the transition, which means the sensor layer must be decoupled from the transport layer by a standardized broker that outlives the radio beneath it. Second — and this is the operator's correction the vendors will not volunteer — durability is bounded from below. If the underlying hardware speaks a proprietary protocol (Modbus RTU on a fifteen-year PLC, a BACnet profile only one manufacturer's gateway can parse), the abstraction layer sits on top of something that cannot be cleanly abstracted. The realistic standard for most installations is surviving one vendor change cleanly and one network upgrade with moderate pain. "Two clean network upgrades" is a design target to write into specifications, not a baseline to promise a board.

### The seam that decides everything: the data schema and the integration broker

Here is where the durable programs diverge from the rest, and the divergence is narrower than the marketing implies. It is not "buy a platform." It is two deliberate choices about where data lives and how it moves.

First, the schema. When an airport writes its operational history into a proprietary data model, the switching cost compounds every month, because migration is never just buying the new platform — it is running the old one in parallel, building translation layers to prove fidelity, and re-integrating every downstream consumer, several of which are FAA-regulated systems carrying their own change-management overhead. The conservative rule of thumb is to budget twice the vendor's integration quote. The remedy is not exotic, and the proof comes from the hardest possible environment. A legacy proprietary airfield-lighting control system, retrofitted with a gateway translating its closed protocol into standard AMQP and REST/JSON, hit end-to-end latencies of 43 milliseconds — comfortably inside the 500-millisecond regulatory limit for stop-bar operations — while preserving the legacy hardware and creating a portable data surface. Open protocol translation is achievable even in safety-critical, regulated infrastructure, and the migration cost came in below the cost of continued lock-in.

Second, the broker. Enterprise IT will recommend its familiar tools — MuleSoft, Boomi, Informatica — built for synchronous, moderate-volume API integration between enterprise systems. IoT is asynchronous, high-volume, and latency-sensitive; forcing it through a synchronous iPaaS adds latency, creates a single point of failure, and produces integration contracts that break across firmware releases. The pattern the durable programs actually use is the opposite of what enterprise IT recommends: MQTT at the edge, a lightweight event broker (MQTT or Kafka) for the real-time stream, and downstream consumption by SCADA, BMS, AODB, and analytics. The industrial-IoT community has a name for the mature version of this — the Unified Namespace, an event-driven publish/subscribe topic hierarchy that serves as a single source of truth — and it is a design pattern, not a product anyone sells. The load-bearing principle is one sentence: the event broker is infrastructure, not an application, and it must not be owned by the vendor that owns the sensors or the analytics. When one vendor supplies sensor, integration, and dashboard, the airport has consolidated three dependencies into one contract, and one acquisition strands all three at once.

This is where the most dangerous internal contradiction has to be confronted head-on, because the recommended exemplar appears to violate the recommended rule. The durable programs do not run their own broker on bare metal — Schiphol explicitly migrated *away from* self-hosted open-source Apache Kafka to managed Confluent Cloud, for cost, elasticity, and the staffing reality. A managed cloud service is precisely the risk class that killed Google Cloud IoT Core. So why is one acceptable and the other not? The answer is the only thing that matters: Google IoT Core coupled its customers through proprietary device SDKs embedded deep in firmware, so exit meant touching every device. Schiphol kept the Kafka schema open and the data continuously exportable, so the broker product is disposable — if Confluent reprices, gets acquired, or shuts down, the schema moves to AWS MSK or Azure Event Hubs or self-hosted Kafka and the sensors never know. The rule, stated precisely: a managed broker can be disposable *if and only if* the schema is genuinely portable and the data continuously exportable. That single conditional is the difference between buying convenience and buying a future migration. It is not "avoid managed services." It is "never let a managed service own your schema."

### Edge compute: the decision framework, not a preference

The run asked for a framework here, not a list of opinions, and the framework is clean enough to put on one slide and defend in a design review. One rule governs it: processing that must survive a WAN outage belongs at the edge. Everything else follows from where each function sits relative to that line.

- **On-sensor.** Anomaly detection on continuous signals — vibration, temperature thresholds, acoustic signatures. The justification is bandwidth as much as latency: on-sensor inference cuts data egress by more than 90% on high-frequency signals, which is the difference between a battery that lasts five years and one that lasts eighteen months. Put here anything where the raw signal is high-volume and only the exception matters.

- **On-gateway.** Aggregation, protocol translation, and time-sensitive classification — passenger queue depth, equipment-fault categorization, the AMQP/REST translation that gives a legacy controller a portable data surface. This is the tier that buys decoupling: the gateway is where a proprietary southbound protocol becomes a standard northbound stream, and it is the layer that can be re-pointed at a new broker without touching the sensors.

- **On-premises.** SCADA and BMS control loops, real-time operational dashboards, and the data retention regulators require. The test is binary: if the function must keep working when the internet does not, it lives here. Stop-bar lighting, HVAC setpoints, and access-control triggers cannot accept a cloud round-trip in their decision loop. An airport that puts operational control logic in a cloud-only deployment has designed for the nominal case and ignored the failure mode — and the failure mode is a Pacific Northwest airport's employees handwriting boarding passes during the Seattle-Tacoma ransomware attack, the physical manifestation of misplaced compute.

- **Cloud.** Analytics, model training, multi-airport benchmarking, reporting. Everything with no operational dependency — and nothing with one. The cloud is where the value compounds over time precisely because nothing in the running airport waits on it.

The framework also resolves a network argument the vendors muddy. A checkpoint with forty 4K camera feeds appears to demand fiber or 5G to move the video. Process it on-premises with edge GPUs and the network requirement collapses from hundreds of megabits of raw video to a few kilobits of metadata. The edge-placement decision and the network-sizing decision are the same decision, and getting the first one right shrinks the second.

### The two contract sentences that decide whether migration is even possible

Then the contract, because the architecture is downstream of it. The single question most airport IoT contracts never answer is the one that decides everything else: who owns the device certificates after termination? When the vendor holds them, the airport cannot move its devices to a new platform without physically replacing hardware — the vendor's leverage is absolute, and it is invisible until the day the airport tries to leave. The portability framework that surfaces it puts the question first for a reason. It belongs in the RFP, not the post-mortem.

The second sentence is the data-export clause with teeth: not "does your platform support export" (every platform does) but "what is the liquidated-damages penalty if data cannot be exported in a documented, schema-portable format within thirty days of termination?" That question changes the vendor conversation, and it is the kind of clause that requires someone in the negotiating room who understands what "schema portability" means technically — which is exactly the person most airport procurement processes leave out. The SaaS world learned this a decade ago: bad architecture can be refactored; a bad contract runs for seven years. Open standards exist to make these clauses writable — ACI World's ACRIS Semantic Model for aviation data interoperability, FAA's SWIM schemas — and most RFPs reference neither, because procurement officers do not know they exist and incumbent vendors have no incentive to volunteer the requirement that limits their own lock-in.

### The part the architects cannot solve: the architecture sits inside an organization

A decoupled architecture and a watertight contract will still strand if no human owns the system. This is the finding the technical lenses systematically miss, and three other industries learned it the hard way before airports got here.

Hospitals fought the ownership question for twenty years over their embedded medical devices — infusion pumps, monitors, imaging — which are IoT by any functional definition and outlive everything they talk to. IT said "it runs on our network, we own it." Clinical engineering said "it's clinical equipment, we own it." Both were right; the governance gap between them was where devices stranded. Roughly 30% of US hospitals resolved it by moving biomedical engineering under IT with a written governance agreement defining ownership, patching responsibility, and succession before any device went on the network. The other 70% are still fighting, and they are the ones with the most stranded equipment. The airport parallel is exact: the gap between airport IT and the operational department that depends on the sensors — airfield ops, maintenance, terminal ops — is where airport IoT strands. Not because the sensors failed. Because nobody owned the gap, and the ownership question got discovered in year three when something broke and both sides pointed at each other.

The Port of Rotterdam shows the resolved version. Its IoT program runs simultaneously on IBM, Cisco, Esri, and Axians, with the Port Authority explicitly owning the data layer as port infrastructure and operating a "live and let live" model: each stakeholder accesses only its relevant data through authorized APIs, and no single vendor controls the data model. That is not a technology architecture; it is a governance architecture. The program survives vendor failure because no single vendor is load-bearing — redundancy through vendor diversification, a procurement posture, not an engineering trick. Rotterdam can do this because it is a semi-public corporation with the organizational standing to declare "the data is infrastructure" and make it stick across four vendors and a decade of leadership turnover. Most airport authorities have not asserted that standing, which is why the data generated by sensors in a building the airport owns lives in a vendor's cloud, in a vendor's schema, behind a vendor's API that disappears when the contract ends.

This is also where the capital-handoff failure hides, and it is the operator's sharpest contribution. A master-systems-integration analysis found that systems-integration requirements are "frequently omitted entirely" from airport program-definition documents, with each contractor assuming someone else holds the integration safety net. ACRP Report 59 identified the same pattern in 2011; the same analysis found it again in 2024. The thirteen-year gap between those two observations is itself the finding: the industry invokes Denver's baggage system as a cautionary tale and then reproduces its preconditions in every major capital program, because the COO who will have to run the building is brought in at commissioning rather than at program definition, when the integration requirement could still have been written down. Barcelona's Sentilo — an open-source, vendor-neutral municipal IoT platform launched in 2014 and still running — is the proof that the durable design is achievable, and the warning about its cost: it took a city government with sustained civic will and a decade of commitment to build the thing everyone says they want.

The synthesis the architects need to hear: the most durable airport IoT program is not the one with the cleverest protocol. It is the one a named executive fights to keep — someone who can explain the system to an incoming CIO in thirty minutes using plain language, who has a defined successor, and who owns the gap between IT and operations in writing, signed, before the first sensor goes on the wall. People fight to keep what they own and understand. The architecture survives only inside an organization built to carry it.

## The Counter-Case, Honestly Presented

The strongest argument against everything above is that it is a consultant's product — complexity sold by people whose revenue scales with the complexity they recommend — and that it misdiagnoses the disease. That case is not a strawman. In several places it is correct.

Begin with the word "stranded." Enterprise IT runs on refresh cycles by design: servers four to six years, network gear five to seven, laptops three to four. An IoT sensor network significantly upgraded at year five is not a stranded deployment; it is a planned refresh, the ordinary discipline applied to any asset. Define stranding as "eventually superseded" and every technology in history fails the test. The right question is not "did it survive five years?" but "did it return value during its operating life?" A building-management system that optimized HVAC loads for four years before a planned upgrade was not stranded. It was amortized.

Now turn the graveyard around, because it cuts the other way. BACnet was published in 1995. Modbus dates to 1979. Both still dominate airport building automation — decades of service in SCADA and BMS environments that clock ten to twenty years without overhaul. Against that, look at what the architecture-first thesis points airports *toward*: Google Cloud IoT Core, dead at six. IBM Watson IoT, sunset at roughly a decade with no direct replacement. Cisco Kinetic, retired. The single-vendor, "locked-in," proprietary building-management systems running quietly in airports worldwide have outlived every major cloud IoT platform brought to market in the last decade. The thesis tells airports to flee vendor lock-in into the arms of hyperscalers who have proved *less* durable than the systems they are fleeing. The empirical record of what actually endures does not obviously favor the report's recommendation.

The deeper objection is about cause. If stranding were primarily driven by wrong network choice or proprietary schema, programs would die in years three to five, after deployment, when those decisions bite. Instead, most never reach production at all — Cisco's own data put successful completion at 26% and named unclear business goals, not integration architecture, as the leading cause. An airport that deploys queue sensors, builds a dashboard, and never connects the data to a gate-staffing decision is not suffering from vendor lock-in. It is suffering from unclear purpose, and a better integration stack would not have saved it. iFlow is the cautionary example the thesis itself has to concede: the data was technically available and operationally ignored. Fixing the architecture does not fix the purpose.

The thesis also assumes a cost structure that does not survive contact with the field. Open protocols, portable schemas, and multi-vendor stacks do not come for free. Every seam — separate MQTT brokers, Kafka clusters, schema-translation layers, API gateways — adds infrastructure, operations, and maintenance overhead that scales with fleet size. Industry data finds IIoT projects underestimate total cost of ownership by 30–50% across the board, and that estimation gap widens in regulated environments. A single-vendor integrated deployment eliminates most of the seam cost: one logistics operator cut its IoT spend from roughly $38,000 a month to $8,000 by consolidating from a public-cloud IoT service to a single on-premises solution. That is not, on its face, a lock-in risk. It can be a lock-in benefit.

And it assumes a workforce airports do not have. The prescription — architect for multiple vendor changes and network upgrades — presumes in-house engineers who can write schemas, govern APIs, run brokers, and execute migrations. Almost no airport has them. The airport that builds a "vendor-change-surviving" architecture and then loses its one IoT architect to a tech company in year two has produced a stranded deployment of a different kind: an orphaned architecture too complex for the staff who remain. The benchmark the industry admires cuts this way too. Changi's coordination platform runs thousands of sensors, hundreds of cameras, and hundreds of mobile devices as a deeply integrated single-platform deployment backed by a large internal technology organization — not a loosely coupled, schema-portable, multi-vendor mesh. The airports that do IoT well tend to commit deeply to one integrated platform and build capability around it, not to architect for an optionality they will almost never exercise.

The operator's bench adds three corrections the thesis must answer rather than dodge. First, **open architecture is its own attack surface.** The Seattle-Tacoma ransomware attack did not come through a closed proprietary system; it came through networked ones, and the federal systems that stayed up did so because they were segmented, not because they were open. Every open API the thesis recommends is a target. Openness and interoperability are necessary; they are not free, and the thesis cannot cite SEA-TAC as evidence for segmentation while ignoring that its own recommended architecture widens the surface SEA-TAC exploited. Second, **"two network upgrades" is aspirational.** Where the hardware-layer protocol is proprietary, the abstraction layer the thesis proposes sits on something that cannot be cleanly abstracted; the realistic version survives one vendor change cleanly and one network upgrade with moderate pain. Third, **data governance without organizational governance is worthless.** If an airport puts IoT data under the CIO, facilities under the COO, and airline-facing operational data under the VP of Airline Affairs, the data-ownership provisions in the vendor contract are irrelevant — nobody inside the airport has unambiguous authority to act on unified data. The architecture is only as durable as the organizational model it sits inside.

The regulatory and political structure sharpens the same blade. Even an airport that wants the durable architecture must fight its own capital structure to fund it. The PFC cap has been frozen at $4.50 since 2001 — to hold its original value against CPI it would need to be roughly $7.85 in 2026 dollars — and the 2024 FAA reauthorization did not raise it. AIP eligibility funds runways, taxiways, lighting, conduit, and equipment rooms, not middleware, schema migration, or vendor-neutral integration layers. The IIJA Airport Terminal Program, the closest thing to federal co-investment in terminal technology, closed its final application window on January 15, 2026. Rating agencies reward visible, bond-ratable revenue and view operational-technology spend skeptically unless it models clean OpEx reduction. The result is a structural bias: airports can fund the *hardware* of IoT and not the *durability* of IoT. Telling an airport to invest in integration-layer maintenance is telling it to fight uphill against its own bond covenants and a federal funding stack that has just closed the one window that helped.

A reader who came in skeptical should, at this point, feel the case has been made fairly. It has. Now it has to be answered.

## Why the Counter-Case Is Insufficient

Concede the real points, once and by name. The cloud graveyard is real, and an airport that built a serious pipeline on Google Cloud IoT Core or IBM Watson IoT is stranded today. Most programs do die before architecture matters, and the killer at that stage is unclear purpose, not protocol — iFlow proves a system can have architecture and still be abandoned. Open and multi-vendor stacks do add real seam cost, and consolidation can genuinely cut spend. BACnet and Modbus have outlasted every cloud IoT platform. The physical layer comes first, staffing is a binding constraint, open APIs widen the attack surface, "two network upgrades" is aspirational, and organizational governance can render the best contract inert. None of these is a quibble. All are true. The thesis survives all of them — because the counter-case has mostly refuted a claim the thesis does not make.

The thesis is not "choose open over proprietary," and it is not "buy the sophisticated multi-vendor platform." Read the counter-case's own conclusion and the agreement is startling: deploy against a clear outcome, staff for maintainability, and treat every hyperscaler managed IoT service as a platform risk. That last clause *is* the thesis — and it is an architectural conclusion, not an organizational one. The two sides disagree about billing order more than about substance.

Resolve the BACnet paradox, because it is the counter-case's best shot and it actually proves the point. Why has Modbus survived since 1979 and BACnet since 1995 while Google IoT Core died at six? Not because they were proprietary and single-vendor. Because they are open, standardized, and decoupled from any one vendor's solvency — any manufacturer's device can speak them, and the protocol outlives them all. The durable thing in every one of the counter-case's own examples is the open, decoupled interface; the dead thing in every one is the proprietary, vendor-coupled cloud platform. (The draft does not need to claim any specific named building system runs BACnet to make this point, and it does not: the structural fact is enough.) The lesson the counter-case draws — embrace single-vendor lock-in — is the opposite of what its own evidence shows. The correct lesson is the thesis: durability comes from decoupling at the interface, not from choosing a vendor and praying it survives.

That reframes the whole disagreement. "Maximum optionality" really is a consultant's product — expensive, complex, unstaffable, rarely exercised. But "maximum optionality" is a caricature of the thesis. The actual prescription is *minimal coupling at the two or three seams that matter, and ruthless simplicity everywhere else.* Decouple the data schema, because that is what compounds and what FAA-regulated downstream systems depend on. Decouple the transport broker from the sensor vendor, because that is what survives the radio sunset and the platform death. Keep operational control logic at the edge, because that is what survives the WAN outage. And then — the part the counter-case gets right and the naive thesis gets wrong — make everything above those seams as simple as the team you actually have can run. Schiphol is not a maximal-optionality cathedral; it bought *managed* Confluent precisely for the staffing reality while keeping the schema open. That is the synthesis: open where it counts, managed where it doesn't, simple by default. Durability is not complexity. Conflating the two is the flaw in an otherwise serious objection.

On "most programs die before architecture matters" — concede the number, reject the inference. The thesis is not addressed to the program that dies in the pilot. It is addressed to the program that *survives* the purpose gate, gets sponsorship, deploys, scales, and then strands in year five because it made its architecture decisions back when it was a pilot and never re-made them. The counter-case's own list of "scenarios where the thesis is correct" — cloud-lock with a platform that fails, network stranding on proprietary narrow-band, baggage-schema continuity across a vendor change, multi-terminal phased expansion — are not edge cases at a large hub. They are its operating conditions. You do not write an architecture standard for the deployments that die in the crib. You write it for the ones that live long enough to strand expensively.

The three operator corrections do not defeat the thesis; they finish it. On the attack surface: the answer is not to trade openness for security but to design decoupling and segmentation as a single discipline. SEA-TAC is not evidence against open architecture — it is evidence that the OT/IT boundary must be engineered into the same design that opens the schema, which is why TSA's segmentation directive and the data-export clause belong in the same RFP. On "two network upgrades is aspirational": concede it, and reframe the slogan as a design target rather than a promise — the honest version is "survive one vendor change cleanly, one network upgrade with manageable pain, and write the second as an aspiration into the specification." Meanwhile the personnel axis runs the other way and makes the case more urgent, not less: the human side is *worse* than the slogan, because at a twenty-month CIO tenure a five-year contract sees two turnovers, not one. On organizational governance: this is the most serious correction and it modifies the prescription rather than refuting it. Contract clauses are necessary and insufficient; they require a designated internal data owner and a master systems integrator with real authority, named before construction, plus board-level authorization discipline so the pilot cannot scale into a bonded liability without anyone deciding to let it. That is the half of the solution the technical lenses omit, and the thesis is stronger for absorbing it.

Which leaves the capital-structure objection — the one the thesis cannot argue away and should not try. The PFC freeze, the AIP exclusion, and the closed IIJA window are all real, and they starve the durable layers. But notice what that argument actually establishes. It does not show the architecture is wrong. It shows the architecture is *underfunded* — that airports systematically buy the sensor hardware (AIP-adjacent, bond-ratable) and skimp on the schema and integration layers (neither), and then strand. That is not a refutation of the thesis. It is the thesis's causal mechanism, confirmed by an independent lens. The funding system biases airports toward exactly the half of the architecture that does not determine durability. And the timing inverts the easy reading: because the federal terminal-technology window has now closed, there is no grant to fund a re-do. An airport designing into that cliff has *more* reason to get the durable layers right the first time, through the operating budget, through the integration line of a capital project, through writing data-layer requirements into the RFP so the cost is bid rather than discovered. The constraint is real. It makes the discipline more necessary, not less.

So the thesis stands, narrowed and sharpened by the attack: not "build for maximum optionality," but *decouple deliberately at the schema and the broker; keep everything above those seams simple enough to staff; engineer segmentation into the same design that opens the data; name a single accountable owner with a defined successor; make exit cost contractual; and fund the durable layers on purpose, because the system will not fund them for you.* The naive version deserved to lose. The narrowed version is stronger for having survived.

## Implications for MWAA and Washington Dulles

This is not an abstract debate for the Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority. It is a decision being poured in concrete right now.

MWAA's new use-and-lease agreement took effect January 1, 2025, governing a $9 billion capital program with roughly $6.99 billion of it at Dulles. The centerpiece for the carrier that matters is United's $500-million-plus Concourse E expansion: fourteen exclusive-use gates in a 435,000-square-foot facility, announced December 2024, defining the physical and operational envelope of Dulles for the next decade. Concourse E is the single highest-stakes IoT architecture decision MWAA will make this cycle, and it is being made *now*, because the cheapest moment to design the data layer is before the slab is poured. The conduit runs, the equipment rooms, the structured wiring, the network backbone are all being specified today under FAA design standards, and all are far cheaper to get right in design than to retrofit.

The historical rhyme is real but the funding posture has flipped, and the flip makes the argument stronger. The conditions that produced stranded explosive-detection installations in 2002 were capital abundance and compressed timelines. MWAA in 2026 faces the opposite: the IIJA Airport Terminal Program closed its final window on January 15, 2026, so the federal cost-share that might have helped fund Concourse E technology is gone, and a frozen PFC cannot make up the difference. MWAA is building *into the funding cliff*, not riding a wave of it. That is precisely why durable architecture is more urgent here, not less — there will be no federal money to fund a re-do, so the data layer has to be right the first time, on bonded capital that the rating agencies will score.

Three specific exposures follow.

First, the data-rights vacuum is acute at Dulles precisely because United is dominant. Those fourteen gates are United's leased premises. IATA's October 2024 Aircraft Operational Data principles assert that data generated by an aircraft, on the ground as well as in flight, belongs to the airline — and turnaround activity in a gate area (docking time, bridge engagement, ground-equipment positioning) is exactly that. United has migrated hundreds of applications to AWS and booked roughly $2 billion in technology savings; it has its own operational data infrastructure and does not need MWAA's. So any sensor MWAA installs in Concourse E is a parallel system generating duplicate data in a space where United controls the operational reality and arguably owns the data. United's dominance at Dulles makes that technology capital a substantially bilateral negotiation rather than a multi-stakeholder decision — whether or not United alone clears the formal majority-in-interest threshold, the practical leverage points the same way. MWAA should resolve the United consent-and-data question in the lease language *before* deploying, not after a legal notice arrives. The architecture problem is downstream of the contract problem.

Second, fund the durable layers on purpose, because the capital structure will not. Concourse E's AIP-adjacent elements — conduit, equipment rooms, structured wiring — will get funded; that is what the system funds. The schema design, the integration broker, the vendor-neutral middleware, and the certificate-ownership and data-export clauses are the layers that determine whether the deployment survives to 2040, and they are exactly the layers AIP and a frozen PFC do not cover. MWAA should write data-layer requirements into the Concourse E technology RFP so the integration cost is bid and visible rather than discovered at the second concourse: reference ACI World's ACRIS Semantic Model and FAA SWIM schemas explicitly, require open-schema export with liquidated damages, and require airport ownership of device certificates at termination. Most RFPs reference none of this, because procurement officers do not know the standards exist and incumbent vendors have no reason to volunteer the requirement that limits their lock-in. MWAA can close that gap with a paragraph in the specification — and it should pair that paragraph with a named internal data owner and a master systems integrator engaged at program definition, not at commissioning, because the contract clause is inert without someone holding the authority to enforce it.

Third, the segmentation clock is already running, and Dulles operates under it. TSA's 2023 cybersecurity directive made IT/OT segmentation mandatory; a forthcoming FAA cybersecurity rulemaking will impose a second pass later this decade. A flat-network sensor deployment in Concourse E would be born out of compliance and face two rounds of forced remediation inside its first five years. Seattle-Tacoma's August 2024 ransomware attack — dry-erase boards, 90,000 breach notifications, manual baggage handling at a peer hub — is the demonstration of what the seam costs when a threat actor finds it. But the lesson cuts both ways, and MWAA should hold both halves at once: the open, API-rich data layer that keeps Concourse E from stranding is also the layer that widens the attack surface, so segmentation and schema-portability are one design problem, not a trade-off. The airport CISO is already a veto voter on this architecture whether or not the program plan acknowledges it; designing segmentation in at Concourse E is cheaper than having the program paused for "architecture review," which is where airport IoT goes to die quietly.

The concrete recommendation for MWAA is narrow and cheap relative to the $6.99 billion it sits inside. On Concourse E, before the build locks: separate sensor procurement from platform procurement; require open-schema data export and airport ownership of device certificates at termination, with liquidated damages; specify ACRIS/SWIM-compatible APIs; decouple the transport broker from the sensor vendor; segment OT from IT to current TSA standard as part of that same design; name an accountable internal data owner with a defined successor; and settle the United data-rights question in writing. None of that is a moonshot architecture. It is a procurement and governance discipline, exercised once, at the moment MWAA has the most leverage it will ever have — before the contract is signed and the schema starts filling up.

The sensors going into Concourse E in the next two years will almost certainly still be bolted to the walls in 2040. The question is whether anyone will still be able to read what they are saying — or whether they will have joined the controllers in the old terminals and the system at Orly that three people understood, faithfully reporting, decade after decade, into a platform that no longer exists, in a language no one kept the key to.
