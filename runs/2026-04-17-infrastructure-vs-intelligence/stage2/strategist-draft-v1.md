# Infrastructure vs. Intelligence: Why the Next Billion in Airport CapEx Deserves a Harder Test

**Draft v1 — Strategist Synthesis**
**Date:** April 17, 2026
**Run:** infrastructure-vs-intelligence

---

## 1. Thesis

US airports are allocating capital against a physical problem they diagnosed two decades ago, while the binding constraint on throughput has quietly migrated to coordination, staffing, and software. The industry's own numbers — ROIC below WACC, an 86% overrun rate on megaprojects, a PFC that has lost 40% of its purchasing power since 2001 — indicate that the marginal infrastructure dollar is financing value destruction, not value creation. The next billion in airport CapEx should have to clear a bar that current planning frameworks do not ask it to clear: prove it cannot be substituted, in whole or in part, by a small fraction of the same money spent on operational intelligence.

---

## 2. Executive Summary

1. **Airport CapEx is, in aggregate, destroying value.** ACI World's own 2024 figure puts global airport return on invested capital at 6.3% — below the sector's weighted average cost of capital [Economist brief, Finding 1]. An industry that cannot clear its cost of capital in aggregate is, strictly speaking, not earning its infrastructure.

2. **Megaproject overruns are the baseline, not the exception.** Flyvbjerg's cross-sector research finds an 86% overrun rate with a 28% average magnitude; airport-specific cases — Berlin Brandenburg (132% overrun, nine years late), LAX APM (76% overrun, no opening date as of March 2026), Denver Great Hall (roughly 3x original city exposure) — sit above that baseline [Economist brief, Finding 2]. Heathrow Terminal 5 is the counter-case, and its lesson is governance, not feasibility.

3. **The binding delay constraint in the US is not runway length; it is ATC staffing and ground coordination.** FAA OPSNET data show weather drives 74% of system delays and volume 15%, but BTS data at the flight level attribute 39% of delay minutes to late-arriving aircraft and 35% to air carrier causes [Operations brief, Finding 2]. The 19 worst-staffed ATC facilities produce 40% of NAS delays while running 27% of commercial operations [Operations brief, Finding 2, citing Brookings].

4. **Operational interventions have delivered double-digit capacity gains without pouring concrete.** RECAT at Memphis produced a 15%+ capacity increase and nine added movements per hour; Time-Based Separation at Heathrow cut headwind delays 62% in year one and added 2.9 movements per hour in strong wind [Operations brief, Finding 4]. EUROCONTROL's independent A-CDM assessment across 17 airports documented 34,000 tonnes of annual fuel savings (€26.7M) and 250,000 minutes of flow management delay avoided [Technology Scout brief, Finding 4].

5. **Runway expansion often does not hold its value.** A peer-reviewed study of 55 runway expansions at the world's 150 busiest airports between 2000 and 2016 found that more than half were back below pre-expansion capacity or congested again by 2016, and 15 of 55 were operating peak hours below 90% of pre-expansion capacity — meaning the expansion was unnecessary on its own terms [Operations brief, Finding 5].

6. **The financial math at MWAA specifically is tightening fast.** The Authority's 2025–2039 capital program is roughly 5.6x the prior cycle; between 2025 and 2028 it plans to issue about $5.5 billion in new debt, with adjusted debt per O&D enplanement projected to peak near $400 (up from $223 in 2024) and net revenue DSCR projected to fall from 1.63x to roughly 1.3x [Economist brief, Finding 5]. Moody's shifted the US airport sector outlook to negative in May 2025.

## 3. The Argument

### 3.1 The economics don't work at the top of the stack

Start at the top of the industry's own income statement. ACI World's 2024 return on invested capital came in at 6.3% — and ACI itself concedes that this figure remains below the sector's weighted average cost of capital [Economist brief, Quotable 1]. Total 2024 airport revenue of $194.9 billion was 2.1% below pre-pandemic levels in real terms even though passenger traffic was 4% above 2019 [Economist brief, Evidence: Global CapEx Trajectory]. Traffic recovered. Revenue did not.

The explanation is structural, not cyclical. Airports raised capital aggressively during the pre-pandemic boom and again to bridge the pandemic gap. Interest expense rose 18% year-over-year in 2023 as that debt repriced against higher rates. Meanwhile the capital program did not pause: 2023 global airport capital costs hit $40 billion — 1% above pre-pandemic levels [Economist brief, Evidence: Global CapEx Trajectory]. ACI's own long-run outlook, prepared with Oxford Economics, projects $2.4 trillion in global airport CapEx between 2021 and 2040. That is a trajectory that presupposes the industry can, at some point, clear its cost of capital. The current evidence says it cannot.

This matters for a specific reason: when an industry's aggregate ROIC falls below WACC, you cannot average your way out of it with "good" projects. You can only outperform it with projects that earn materially above the mean — and the margin for execution error on those projects is small.

### 3.2 Overruns are the baseline

Flyvbjerg's 258-project transport infrastructure dataset finds 86% of projects experience cost overruns, with a 28% average magnitude [Economist brief, Finding 2]. That is the backdrop. Airport megaprojects sit above it.

- Berlin Brandenburg: €2.8 billion budget, €6.0–6.5 billion final cost, nine years late, total value destruction estimated near €11.9 billion including interest and unrealized revenue [Economist brief, Evidence: Cost Overrun Case Studies].
- LAX Automated People Mover: $1.9 billion contract in 2018, $3.34 billion as of July 2024, no opening date announced as of March 2026 [Economist brief, Evidence].
- Denver Great Hall: roughly $770 million original city exposure, now $2.1–2.35 billion across multiple phases. A January 2025 city audit found DIA "still is not following procurement procedures or requiring contractors to submit documentation of actual costs" [Economist brief, Evidence].
- Denver original automated baggage system (1995): $560 million in overruns, deployed to one concourse for one airline's outbound flights [Economist brief, Evidence].

The temptation is to read this as a procurement problem. GAO's GAO-20-298 report makes the procurement problem concrete: airport infrastructure cost estimates for FY2019–2023 rose 19% over the prior five-year estimate, and terminal projects specifically rose 51% [Economist brief, Evidence: US Federal Framework]. That is not inflation. It is a systematic under-estimation pattern embedded in the planning phase.

The counter-case exists. Heathrow Terminal 5 opened on schedule at £4.3 billion after BAA conducted a two-year pre-construction study explicitly designed to avoid megaproject failure modes [Economist brief, Evidence]. T5 demonstrates that airport megaprojects can hit their targets. Its lesson is not that the sector is capable of this as a matter of course; its lesson is that hitting targets required an extraordinary pre-commitment to governance most authorities do not make. The base rate remains what it is.

### 3.3 The delay data does not say what the planning data assumes

Walk into a US airport planning meeting and you will hear about runway throughput, gate counts, and peak-hour aircraft movements. Walk over to the FAA's delay data and you find a different picture.

FAA OPSNET data over the six years ending May 2023 attribute 74.26% of system-impacting delays (>15 min) to weather, 14.92% to volume, 5.96% to runway unavailability, 0.61% to equipment, and 4.25% to other factors [Operations brief, Finding 1]. Read quickly, that looks like an argument that nothing is tractable: weather is weather, and there is no runway extension that changes it. Read carefully, and the argument is different. "Volume" delays are not physical runway limits — they are the gap between instantaneous demand and the *operationally realized* capacity of an airport at that moment. That gap is where coordination lives.

Then look at BTS flight-level data, which records what airlines experience rather than what the NAS produces. From January–April 2023, late-arriving aircraft accounted for 38.99% of total delayed minutes, air carrier delays 35.43%, and NAS delays the smaller remainder [Operations brief, Finding 2]. Late-arriving aircraft is entirely a scheduling and turnaround phenomenon. It has no physical solution. You cannot concrete your way out of an aircraft that landed at LGA 20 minutes behind and now has to push back late at EWR.

Then look at the ATC data. The FAA has approximately 11,000 certified professional controllers against a model requirement near 14,000 [Operations brief, Finding 6]. Nineteen of the 30 largest facilities operate below 85% of staffing targets, and those 19 facilities alone account for 40% of all NAS delays [Operations brief, Finding 2, citing Brookings]. At peak dysfunction, DOT Secretary Duffy reported controller staffing accounted for half of all flight delays versus a normal baseline of 5% [Operations brief, Finding 6].

None of this changes with a new concourse.

### 3.4 The operational-intelligence evidence base is real, not theoretical

This is where the conversation usually degenerates into vendor slides. The honest version of the case requires citing only the data points that survive scrutiny.

**RECAT at Memphis (2012).** The FAA reclassified aircraft wake-turbulence categories. No construction. The result: an FAA-estimated 15%+ increase in airport capacity and nine additional aircraft movements per hour, with FedEx reporting 4.18 million gallons of fuel saved annually, monthly fuel cost savings of $1.8 million, and a three-minute cut in average taxi time [Operations brief, Finding 4]. The change took months, not a decade of environmental review.

**Time-Based Separation at Heathrow (2015).** NATS replaced distance-based arrival separation with time-based separation, neutralizing the headwind penalty that collapses runway throughput in strong winds. First-year result: 62% reduction in headwind delays, 2.9 additional landings per hour in strong-wind conditions, and more than 80% of landing aircraft separated more closely than under the old system [Operations brief, Finding 4]. Over ten years: 1.5 million minutes of airborne holding removed, 100,000+ tonnes of fuel saved, ~$70 million in fuel cost savings [Operations brief, Evidence]. The third-runway debate — and its vast expenditure of political, legal, and planning resources — has proceeded in parallel. TBS delivered meaningful throughput without moving earth.

**A-CDM across 17 European airports.** EUROCONTROL's independent 2016 impact assessment — not vendor-reported, compiled from network operations data — documented 34,000 tonnes of annual fuel savings (€26.7M at then-current prices) and approximately 250,000 minutes of flow management delay avoided (€15.5M) [Technology Scout brief, Evidence: A-CDM and DMAN]. Munich's SESAR DMAN trials produced the most granular airport-level numbers: 9% taxi time reduction, 14.6 kg fuel savings per flight, ATFM slot adherence up from 76% to 81%, off-block time predictability improved 7.8% [Technology Scout brief, Finding 4].

**FAA TFDM at Charlotte.** Testing over four years saved more than 275,000 gallons of fuel per year, cut greenhouse gas emissions by 8 tonnes of CO2 daily, and eliminated 916 hours of delay per year at a single airport [Operations brief, Evidence: FAA TFDM Surface Metering]. At national rollout, the FAA projects 7 million gallons saved annually and 75,000 tonnes of CO2 eliminated.

These are single-digit to mid-double-digit percentage improvements. They are not the 40–80% figures vendors put on slides. They are what independent, disclosed-methodology studies produce — and they are large enough to change a capital plan if you take them seriously.

### 3.5 The price tag comparison

Global airport IT spending reached $8.9 billion in 2024. Global airport capital costs were approximately $40 billion. That is a ratio of roughly 1:4.5 between the operational intelligence stack and the physical CapEx stack for the entire industry [Economist brief, Finding 6].

Look at it at project level. The LAX APM alone — the one project, with its $3.34 billion total cost [Economist brief, Evidence] — consumes more capital than roughly 37% of global airport IT spending in a year. DFW's five-year digital twin contract with Willow and Parsons, covering Runway 18R/36L and Terminal D, was initially scoped at approximately $2.9 million [Technology Scout brief, Evidence: Digital Twins]. Full large-hub digital twin deployments, per DWU Consulting's airport-analyst benchmarks, run $15–40 million over 24–36 months, with 10–20% annual recurring costs [Technology Scout brief, Evidence]. The entire top-of-range estimate for a full large-hub digital twin is roughly 1.2% of the LAX APM's current cost overrun alone.

The point is not that airports should spend $40 million on operational intelligence instead of $3 billion on a people mover. Those projects are not substitutes for each other in the simple sense. The point is that the relative scale of the two line items is so extreme that even small adjustments to the allocation ratio would represent step-changes in operational intelligence investment — and the operational intelligence evidence base, as Section 3.4 shows, is built on the premise that those investments compound.

### 3.6 Induced demand and the capacity that evaporates

A peer-reviewed empirical analysis of 55 runway capacity expansions at the world's 150 busiest airports between 2000 and 2016 found that by 2016, more than half of the expanded airports were back below pre-expansion capacity or congested again. Fifteen of the 55 were operating peak hours below 90% of their pre-expansion capacity — meaning expansion was unnecessary for them on its own terms. Nineteen of the 55 were already operating at 90%+ of their post-expansion capacity within a decade — meaning construction bought only temporary relief [Operations brief, Finding 5].

Read that carefully. In 34 of 55 cases (62%), the expansion either was not needed or did not hold. In the remaining 21, the value depends on demand projections that, at airport timescales, nobody can underwrite with confidence.

This is the induced-demand problem applied to aviation. Capacity begets traffic, which re-congests the capacity. It does not argue that infrastructure never pays — Frankfurt Runway 18 West raised movements per hour from 83 to 126, a 52% increase, because the prior runway geometry did not permit simultaneous independent operations and no procedural change could replicate that [Operations brief, Counterexamples]. It argues that infrastructure pays only where the binding constraint is genuinely physical and where demand is genuinely inelastic over the asset's 30–50 year life.

### 3.7 MWAA's balance sheet, specifically

Now apply this to the specific entity. MWAA's 2025–2039 Capital Construction Program is valued at approximately $9 billion, replacing a 2015–2024 program of roughly $1.6 billion — a 5.6x step-up between consecutive cycles [Economist brief, Evidence: MWAA-Specific Financial Trajectory]. The Authority plans to issue approximately $5.5 billion in new debt between 2025 and 2028. Adjusted debt per O&D enplanement is projected to nearly double, from $223 in 2024 to approximately $400 in 2028 [Economist brief, Finding 5]. Net revenue debt service coverage is projected to fall from 1.63x in 2024 to approximately 1.3x over five years — still above bond covenant minimums, but the cushion is shrinking. At Dulles specifically, DSCR is projected to deteriorate from 2.79x in FY2024 to 1.70x by FY2030 even under favorable traffic assumptions [Economist brief, Finding 5].

Moody's shifted its US airport sector outlook from stable to negative in May 2025 [Economist brief, Finding 5]. That is the environmental frame for this capital program.

The PFC backdrop compounds the issue. The statutory PFC cap has been $4.50 since 2001. A RAND study commissioned by Congress concluded that the purchasing power of that $4.50 had declined to the equivalent of $2.72 in year-2000 dollars indexed to construction prices — a 40% real loss — and the 2024 FAA Reauthorization left the cap unchanged [Economist brief, Finding 3]. Airports are being asked to finance an expanding capital envelope from a passenger-level revenue stream whose real value is shrinking.

### 3.8 The opportunity cost is not recoverable on airport timescales

The deepest argument against the marginal dollar of airport CapEx is not that the project will fail. It is that the capital is committed for 30–50 years whether the project succeeds or not. Bond covenants require debt service regardless of utilization. The airport has to generate aeronautical revenues to cover principal and interest whether its gates are full or empty. A terminal built in 2026 for a demand projection that proves wrong in 2035 cannot be un-built.

The structural warning case is CVG. After Delta's dehubbing, Cincinnati's CPE rose from under $5 to over $10 on collapsing enplanements [Economist brief, Finding 7]. The infrastructure was fine. The airlines left. The capital stack had no flexibility.

Compare the optionality of an operational intelligence investment. A-CDM implementation, an APOC, a surface metering system, a computer-vision turnaround monitoring deployment — these are measured in single-digit-million to low-tens-of-million commitments, deployable in months to a few years, and reconfigurable as the operating environment changes. Assaia's 2025 report on AI turnaround monitoring across 15 airports and 450,000+ turns found median departure delay cut from 4 to 3 minutes (a 25% reduction), with North American airports saving roughly $100 per turnaround per minute of delay reduction [Technology Scout brief, Finding 7]. Vendor-published, caveated, but mechanistically sound and corroborated by multiple independent deployments [Technology Scout brief, Section 4]. A bad operational intelligence investment is embarrassing. A bad concourse is a balance sheet problem for 30 years.

### 3.9 The false-precision trap in on-time performance data

One more brick in the argument, which is subtle but load-bearing. Over 2012–2023, average US airline schedule padding increased 27%, adding roughly 3 minutes of buffer time per flight. Research cited by FinanceBuzz finds that a 10% increase in scheduled block times produced a 20-percentage-point artificial improvement in measured on-time performance over five years [Operations brief, Finding 7]. That is the scheduling literature consensus — Zhang, Salant, and Van Mieghem's 2018 work documents this as a systematic competitive response to chronic operational variance.

This matters because many airport capital programs are justified, in part, by recent improvements in on-time performance and passenger experience metrics. Those metrics are partially accounting. The underlying operational variance — the true gap between the airport's realized and potential capacity — is larger than the reported numbers suggest. If you strip the padding out, the case for operational intelligence gets stronger, not weaker: the hidden variance is what A-CDM, TBS, and surface metering are designed to compress.

### 3.10 Summary of the case

- Industry ROIC is below WACC.
- Megaproject overruns are the baseline, not the exception, and terminal projects are the worst offenders.
- The dominant delay drivers are weather resilience, ATC staffing, and carrier-side coordination — none of which are runway or terminal problems.
- Operational interventions have delivered independently validated double-digit capacity gains in hours-to-months, at percentages of megaproject cost.
- Runway expansion fails to hold its value in the majority of documented cases.
- MWAA's specific debt trajectory is moving toward a DSCR that leaves little room for a demand or rate shock.
- The opportunity cost of misallocated airport capital is locked in for decades.

That is the argument. It is not that airports should stop building. It is that the current allocation ratio — roughly 1:4.5 IT-to-CapEx globally, and closer to 1:10 or worse at many large US hubs pursuing aggressive capital programs — is the wrong ratio for the constraints that actually bind throughput. The next billion dollars should have to prove it belongs on the physical side of that ledger.

## 4. The Counter-Case, Honestly Presented

The strongest version of the pro-infrastructure argument is not a defense of current practice. It is a specific set of claims that, taken together, describe conditions under which operational intelligence cannot substitute for physical investment.

### 4.1 Physical ceilings are real, and software is not geography

Runways, taxiways, gate frontage, and airspace sectors have mathematically fixed throughput maxima. LaGuardia is capped by the FAA at 71 scheduled operations per hour not as administrative preference but because its two-runway geometry produces effective single-runway operation during peak hours in a slot-saturated New York corridor [Contrarian brief, Part I-1]. DCA handles 25 million annual passengers on infrastructure designed for 15 million, with the Potomac, federal facilities, and residential neighborhoods forming a hard perimeter; the FAA has stated that no more flights can be added [Contrarian brief, Part I-1].

No algorithm changes the number of runways from two to three. The software case concedes this in principle and then quietly ignores it when costing alternatives.

### 4.2 O'Hare is the cleanest infrastructure ROI case in modern commercial aviation

The O'Hare Modernization Program — converting an intersecting-runway airfield to six parallel east-west runways — produced a documented 64% reduction in system-impact delays comparing 2003–2008 to 2009–2020, a 79% reduction in flight delay rates, and a 50% increase in peak operational capacity [Contrarian brief, Part I-2; Operations brief, Counterexamples]. These are measured FAA outcomes over a decade, not modeled projections.

The mechanism is simple and cannot be replicated in software: intersecting runways force mandatory sequencing between operations. Parallel runways dissolve that dependency. The constraint wasn't the scheduling of operations; it was the geometry that made certain sequences impossible. Software can optimize within a constraint. It cannot eliminate the constraint.

### 4.3 NextGen is the empirical indictment the operational-intelligence case has not answered

This is the single most damaging data point in the debate, and any honest strategist has to address it. The FAA's NextGen program — the largest operational intelligence initiative in the history of commercial aviation — has consumed over $15 billion in federal investment (combined public and private investment through 2030 exceeding $35 billion) and, per the DOT OIG, delivered approximately 16% of its projected benefits [Contrarian brief, Part I-5]. The original $199 billion benefit projection from 2013 was revised to $63 billion by 2024. The NAS Voice System was terminated after $160 million with minimal benefits, then required an additional $274 million to sustain legacy systems through 2030. The Terminal Flight Data Manager program was delayed three years, cost 20% more than budgeted, and had its scope cut from 89 to 49 sites [Contrarian brief, Part I-5; Part II].

The DOT OIG's language is direct: "narrower in scope, more expensive, and later than the version originally promised." [Contrarian brief, Part I-5] Benefit-to-cost ratio for the 2010–2018 measurement period was below 1.0 [Contrarian brief, Part I-5].

A proposal to reallocate airport capital from physical infrastructure to operational intelligence needs to explain why the largest operational intelligence program in aviation history delivered a benefit-to-cost ratio below one, and what is structurally different this time. "New AI is better" is a claim, not an answer.

### 4.4 The base rate for AI project success in infrastructure and operations is low

A Gartner analysis published in April 2026 found that only 28% of AI projects in infrastructure and operations fully succeed and meet expectations; 20% fail outright [Contrarian brief, Part I-7]. Among leaders reporting failure, 57% cited "expecting too much, too fast." Data quality issues and skill gaps are the other leading causes — and the aviation data environment is particularly hostile on both dimensions: inconsistent formats across carriers and ground handlers, legacy system encoding, real-time latency challenges, and no common data standard across stakeholders.

Between 60–85% of enterprise AI projects fail overall, with 68% of failed implementations attributable to data quality issues [Technology Scout brief, Finding 5]. SITA's 2024 Air Transport IT Insights survey — from the industry's own trade body — identifies fragmented data as the primary barrier to realizing value from the $8.9 billion already being spent annually on airport IT. The data backbone that any operational intelligence program requires as its pre-condition does not exist at most airports. SEA spent $13.4 million integrating seven vendors to get basic ramp situational awareness, and the case study does not report a single quantified performance outcome [Technology Scout brief, Quotable 3].

### 4.5 Infrastructure is accountable; operational intelligence, characteristically, is not

A runway, a concourse, a gate — these are inspectable, auditable, and produce observable outcomes. When they fail, everyone can see the failure. When a $15 billion operational intelligence program delivers 16% of promised benefits, the vendor community's response is not to refund the money but to argue for more funding and longer timelines [Contrarian brief, Part I-8]. Infrastructure projects that fail are visible. Software projects that fail are re-scoped and re-branded.

### 4.6 Software creates new fragility; infrastructure creates durable redundancy

The July 2024 CrowdStrike incident grounded Delta for multiple days. A single failed hardware component at a data center grounded Alaska Airlines for hours in 2025, producing over 150 cancellations [Contrarian brief, Part I-6]. The GAO found that 40 of 41 airports it surveyed are actively investing in physical electrical resilience — backup power, microgrids, redundant distribution — precisely because operational intelligence requires physical substrate to function [Contrarian brief, Part I-6]. A runway does not crash. Concrete does not experience an unplanned outage.

### 4.7 Demand is real and growing faster than optimization can absorb

The FAA's 2025–2045 Aerospace Forecast projects US airline enplanements surpassing 1.28 billion annually by 2038. IATA's 20-year forecast projects global air travel reaching 7.8 billion passengers by 2036 [Contrarian brief, Part I-4]. Denver International — built for 50 million — processed 82.4 million in 2024 and is planning four new concourses to reach 125 million. Operational intelligence can improve throughput at the margin. It cannot serve 82 million passengers through a terminal designed for 50 million. The physics do not negotiate.

### 4.8 The $151 billion backlog is not discretionary

ACI-NA's 2023 Infrastructure Needs Report puts US airport infrastructure need at $151 billion over five years, up more than 30% from $115 billion two years prior [Contrarian brief, Part I-3]. A majority of that is mandatory safety, security, and accessibility compliance — not discretionary amenity investment. More than 50% of Airport Improvement Program funding goes toward constructing or rehabilitating runways, taxiways, and aprons, because that is what FAA safety standards require. A runway with a deteriorated Pavement Condition Index score must be rehabilitated before other capital projects can be approved for AIP funding [Contrarian brief, Part II].

### 4.9 Synthesizing the counter-case

Put together, the pro-infrastructure argument is not "build everything." It is:

1. Physical ceilings exist and are absolute where they bind.
2. Where the ceiling is genuinely physical, infrastructure has a measured track record of large and durable gains (O'Hare, Frankfurt).
3. The largest operational intelligence experiment in aviation history delivered 16% of its promises.
4. Base rates for AI success in infrastructure and operations are low; the aviation data environment is harder than average.
5. A large share of current infrastructure spending is mandatory safety and asset-depreciation investment, not discretionary build.
6. Demand growth is real and physical.

That is a serious argument. It deserves a serious answer.

## 5. Why the Counter-Case Is Insufficient

The counter-case has to be conceded where it is right. The thesis still holds. The task of this section is to do both honestly.

### 5.1 What must be conceded

**Physical ceilings are real.** The thesis does not survive contact with an airport operating at 98% of a genuine physical cap. Heathrow is such an airport. DCA, by multiple measures, is approaching such a state. LGA is such an airport. The Operations Analyst brief concedes this directly [Operations brief, Counterexamples]: where the binding constraint is physical geometry with active safety conflicts, infrastructure is the right answer.

**O'Hare is a real data point.** The O'Hare Modernization Program produced large, durable, measured gains. Any framework that would have deferred this investment needs to explain what would have replaced it. Nothing would have. Intersecting runways created mandatory sequencing conflicts that no ATC procedure could fully resolve, and the Operations brief makes that concession on its own terms [Operations brief, Counterexamples].

**NextGen's failure is real.** The $15 billion, 20-year, 16%-of-promised-benefits record is the single hardest fact the operational intelligence case has to answer, and hand-waving does not answer it. The program's failure was not primarily technical; it was governance, scope creep, and the fact that the FAA's contracting apparatus is not built to deliver complex software systems on commercial timelines. But the failure is real, it is documented, and it happened in the exact institutional environment a US operational intelligence expansion would inherit.

**Safety and depreciation spending is not substitutable.** A cracked runway cannot be offset by a surface metering system. The 50%+ share of AIP funding that goes to pavement rehabilitation is baseline compliance, not discretionary capacity investment [Contrarian brief, Part II]. Any rebalancing framework has to segregate this spend from the discussion.

**Demand growth is real.** 82.4 million passengers did pass through DEN in 2024 [Contrarian brief, Part I-4; Part IV]. That is not an accounting artifact. At some airports, in some markets, demand will exceed what optimization can serve. The thesis has to accept this.

### 5.2 Why the thesis still holds

Here is where the work is. The counter-case wins on specific cases. It does not win on the aggregate allocation ratio — which is what the thesis is actually about.

#### 5.2.1 The NextGen indictment is strong. It is also about the wrong thing.

NextGen was an attempt to modernize the national air traffic control system via a federal program that spanned two decades, multiple administrations, and dozens of contractors — reworking a safety-critical distributed system used by every commercial flight in US airspace. Its closest comparables are not airport operational intelligence investments. Its closest comparables are other federal IT modernizations: the IRS modernization program, DoD's defense business systems, the VA's electronic health record rollout. Those programs, collectively, are the empirical basis for the thesis that large federal IT modernizations underperform.

The operational intelligence investments this paper argues for are categorically different in three ways.

First, they are airport-level, not national. A Seattle or a Dulles deploying A-CDM operates inside a defined organizational perimeter with accountable executives. NextGen was a systems-of-systems national modernization whose stakeholders spanned the FAA, every commercial airline, every controller local, and Congress. Governance complexity is not linear; it compounds. The EUROCONTROL A-CDM evidence base — 34,000 tonnes of fuel saved annually across 17 airports [Technology Scout brief, Evidence] — is what airport-level deployments actually produce. That number is not 16% of its promise. It is an independently compiled network-level result.

Second, they are commercially off-the-shelf. A-CDM, DMAN, computer-vision turnaround monitoring, queue prediction, surface metering are productized by multiple vendors with multi-year operational track records at European hubs [Technology Scout brief, Section 4]. NextGen was a custom federal development effort. The base rate for custom federal IT is not the base rate for commercial airport IT.

Third, they are scoped in years, not decades. Munich's SESAR DMAN trials ran, produced measured results, and fed into iterative deployment [Technology Scout brief, Finding 4]. Heathrow's A-CDM was live and delivering results within timeframes orders of magnitude shorter than NextGen's 20-year arc. An airport that commits to A-CDM in 2026 will know by 2028 whether it worked. A terminal expansion that breaks ground in 2026 will not reveal its verdict until 2040.

NextGen is a warning about how federal modernization programs fail. It is not a warning about whether commercially productized operational intelligence works at the airport level. The Contrarian's brief acknowledges this possibility — "a fair reading of the evidence acknowledges that the infrastructure advocate is pointing at a past failure to discredit a category of investment whose current-generation capabilities may genuinely differ" [Contrarian brief, Part III, Scenario 4] — but does not press it. It should be pressed.

#### 5.2.2 The Gartner base rate is a ceiling, not a floor, and it describes the wrong denominator.

The 28%-fully-succeed, 20%-fail-outright figures [Contrarian brief, Part I-7] describe AI projects across infrastructure and operations broadly. That denominator includes every speculative machine-learning project at every enterprise in every sector. The thesis is not that airports should commission speculative ML projects. It is that they should adopt proven, productized systems whose evidence base has been compiled across dozens of deployments and, in the European case, independently audited.

A-CDM is not an AI project in the Gartner sense. It is a deterministic information-sharing and sequencing protocol. TBS is not an AI project. It is a redefinition of separation standards paired with a supporting tool. RECAT is not an AI project at all. Surface metering is barely an AI project — it is a queuing model. The categories the thesis recommends are not, for the most part, AI projects. They are operational technology with known mechanisms and measured returns.

The strongest AI-in-the-loop categories — computer-vision turnaround monitoring, queue prediction, predictive maintenance — do fall into the Gartner denominator. And for those, the thesis should concede: the success rate will be lower, the data backbone is a prerequisite, and the ROI figures will be rough. The Technology Scout brief is blunt about this: vendor claims need aggressive haircutting, the 40% IATA One ID processing-time figure came from a two-passenger pilot, and the $13.4M SEA integration yielded no published performance metrics [Technology Scout brief, Findings 3 and 5, Section 4]. But even a heavily discounted version of the airport-AI investment envelope is an order of magnitude smaller than the infrastructure spend it would partially substitute for.

#### 5.2.3 Physical ceilings bind at a minority of airports

LGA, DCA, Heathrow at 98% of cap, and pre-modernization O'Hare are the canonical physical-ceiling cases. They are also a small fraction of US hub airports. The Operations Analyst brief is explicit about the broader pattern: in 55 documented runway expansions at the world's 150 busiest airports, 15 of 55 airports (27%) were operating peak hours below 90% of their *pre-expansion* capacity by 2016 [Operations brief, Finding 5]. Those expansions were not absorbed; they were not needed. Another 21 of 55 (38%) were either congested again or below pre-expansion levels. Only the remainder — at most 19 airports out of 55 — operationalized the expansion into sustained high utilization, and most of those are in Asia.

The pro-infrastructure argument from physical ceilings is correct where the ceiling is real. The operational intelligence argument is that the ceiling is not real at most US hubs most of the time. The empirical evidence — congested at peak, substantially underutilized off-peak, with delay driven by coordination failures documented in the BTS and OPSNET data — supports the latter framing for the broad middle of the system.

#### 5.2.4 The accountability argument cuts both ways

The Contrarian's argument that infrastructure is accountable while software is not is rhetorically powerful and empirically selective. Denver's DIA Great Hall was inspectable, auditable, and still produced a ~3x cost overrun, a terminated public-private partnership, and a January 2025 city audit finding that DIA "still is not following procurement procedures or requiring contractors to submit documentation of actual costs" [Economist brief, Evidence]. The LAX APM was inspectable, auditable, and has no announced opening date after $3.34 billion spent against a $1.9 billion contract [Economist brief, Evidence]. Berlin Brandenburg was the most inspectable airport project of its generation and opened nine years late at roughly €11.9 billion in total value destruction [Economist brief, Evidence].

Infrastructure is visible when it is complete. During construction — where most of the risk lives — it is not meaningfully more accountable than a software program. The accountability argument is an argument for T5-style governance, not for concrete-over-code.

#### 5.2.5 The fragility argument is real but asymmetric

The CrowdStrike incident grounded Delta. It did not ground the NAS [Contrarian brief, Part I-6]. The Alaska Airlines hardware failure produced 150 cancellations at a carrier with approximately 1,300 daily flights — a bad day, not a systemic failure. Software fragility is real and has to be designed around. But the asymmetric case cuts the other way too: a runway that cannot be built cannot ground flights it was not yet serving. A terminal that was canceled at the planning stage does not trap 30 years of capital. An operational intelligence investment that fails costs tens of millions; a terminal that fails costs billions and encumbers the balance sheet for decades.

The correct response to software fragility is redundancy, backup protocols, and failover design — all of which cost a fraction of physical redundancy. It is not to build concrete instead.

#### 5.2.6 The conditions under which the thesis holds

The thesis is not a universal claim. It holds under specific conditions, and those conditions apply at a large majority of US hub airports in 2026:

- The airport is not at a physical ceiling. Peak-hour utilization exceeds 90% of declared capacity no more than a few hours per day, and substantial off-peak capacity is demonstrably underused.
- Delay attribution data shows significant contribution from air carrier, late aircraft, ATC staffing, or ground coordination causes, rather than overwhelmingly from genuine physical bottlenecks.
- The airport has not yet implemented core commercially productized operational intelligence categories: A-CDM, surface metering, computer-vision turnaround monitoring, queue prediction.
- The capital program's debt trajectory is moving toward DSCR levels below 1.5x within five years.
- The demand forecasts underpinning the capital program assume sustained growth that would be disrupted by airline hub strategy changes, fuel price shocks, or post-pandemic demand pattern shifts.

Under those conditions — which describe a supermajority of the US large-hub system — the thesis holds: the next marginal billion should be pressure-tested against a much smaller operational intelligence alternative. It does not replace all infrastructure spending. It does change the order and composition of what gets built first.

### 5.3 The honest disposition of the argument

Concede: genuine physical ceilings, safety-and-depreciation spending, O'Hare-class legitimate infrastructure cases, and NextGen as a real warning about federal IT governance. Do not concede: the aggregate allocation ratio, the false equivalence between federal-scale modernization and airport-level operational technology, the assumption that demand growth is physical when 39% of delay minutes come from late-arriving aircraft and 40% of NAS delays come from 19 understaffed ATC facilities. The case for an allocation shift is not a case against all concrete. It is a case against unexamined concrete, and for treating the operational intelligence stack as the first option to defeat rather than the last.

## 6. Implications for MWAA

MWAA is not a generic airport. It is a two-airport authority operating one airport that is physically constrained by geography, federal policy, and regulatory cap (DCA), and one airport that has generations of physical room to grow but is fighting for a durable hub identity (IAD). Those two facts shape what this argument means for the Authority specifically.

### 6.1 DCA: the physical ceiling case

DCA is, by the pro-infrastructure brief's own framing, a paradigm case of physical constraint. The airport handles 25 million annual passengers on infrastructure designed for 15 million, hemmed in by the Potomac, federal facilities, and residential neighborhoods, with FAA slot caps and perimeter rules preventing additional movements [Contrarian brief, Part I-1]. The $2.4 billion DCA Project Journey program (including the Terminal B/C redevelopment under the 2025–2039 capital plan) is a passenger-side investment — concourse, gate, and passenger-flow capacity — that is genuinely responsive to the overload condition [Economist brief, Evidence: MWAA-Specific].

But observe the structure carefully. DCA's binding constraint is not the terminal — it is the slot cap. A bigger concourse does not add flights; it handles the passengers associated with the flights the airport is already allowed to operate. That is a legitimate investment. It is also, critically, a throughput-per-passenger investment, not a throughput-per-aircraft investment. And throughput-per-passenger is precisely the domain where operational intelligence — queue prediction, biometric processing, predictive gate resource allocation, ground coordination — produces its most corroborated gains. Copenhagen Airport's security throughput improvements (38,900 items/FTE/year to 55,200 items/FTE/year, a 42% improvement) and queue prediction systems documented across 40+ airports (30–50% reduction in peak wait times, 10–15% reduction in staffing costs) are vendor-reported but mechanistically sound [Technology Scout brief, Evidence: Queue Prediction]. At a facility designed for 15 million handling 25 million, the per-passenger throughput improvement is compounding against every incremental passenger.

The specific implication: DCA's Project Journey capital spend should be coupled with, not serially followed by, a committed operational intelligence deployment — A-CDM, predictive passenger flow, queue management, biometric processing. The pattern to avoid is the pattern at SEA, where $13.4 million was spent integrating seven vendors without published operational outcomes [Technology Scout brief, Quotable 3]. The operational intelligence component should be scoped as a first-class program with published before/after metrics and accountable executive ownership — not as an IT appendix to the concrete.

### 6.2 IAD: the demand-exposed hub

Dulles is the harder case. The airport has physical room. It has an active Concourse E expansion inside the broader $9 billion 2025–2039 capital program. It is a primary international gateway with legitimate long-haul hub economics. And — the uncomfortable fact — it is the exposed flank of MWAA's balance sheet. DWU Consulting's analysis projects Dulles DSCR deteriorating from 2.79x in FY2024 to 1.70x by FY2030 under favorable traffic assumptions [Economist brief, Finding 5]. Adjusted debt per O&D enplanement at the system level peaking near $400 in 2028 (up from $223 in 2024) implicates Dulles most [Economist brief, Finding 5].

The CVG warning case applies to IAD more than to DCA. CVG's CPE rose from under $5 to over $10 on collapsing enplanements after Delta dehubbed [Economist brief, Finding 7]. IAD is not CVG, but it is more structurally exposed to airline network decisions than DCA is. A capital program sized to a traffic forecast that assumes continued hub commitment from United carries asymmetric risk: if the hub holds, the investment earns; if the hub thins, the investment encumbers the balance sheet with bond obligations that follow the Authority regardless of who is flying.

That asymmetry is the specific reason operational intelligence matters at IAD. Operational intelligence investments are reversible in ways that concrete is not. A-CDM, DMAN, surface metering, and computer-vision turnaround monitoring at IAD would produce measurable throughput and cost gains at current traffic, and those gains compound through whatever traffic environment emerges. The total investment envelope — even at the upper end of the Technology Scout brief's digital twin range ($15–40 million for a full large-hub deployment, 10–20% annual recurring) plus adjacent operational intelligence categories — is a small fraction of any single major Concourse E sub-project.

The specific implication: Dulles's capital program should be staged to separate high-confidence mandatory investments (pavement, asset renewal, code compliance, Concourse E core infrastructure) from demand-contingent additions. The demand-contingent tranche should not proceed without an operational intelligence deployment whose results shift the throughput baseline — because a higher baseline is what changes the demand-coverage calculation.

### 6.3 The financial envelope question

The Authority is at a specific decision point that makes the allocation question concrete rather than abstract. The 2025–2039 plan is roughly 5.6x the prior cycle; $5.5 billion of new debt is scheduled for issuance 2025–2028; Moody's sector outlook is negative; the PFC cap is frozen at $4.50 [Economist brief, Finding 5, Finding 3]. In that environment, the marginal bond is more expensive, more scrutinized, and more consequential than at any point in the Authority's recent history.

The operational intelligence allocation within this envelope should not be framed as a subtraction from infrastructure. It should be framed as an uplift to the revenue base that supports the infrastructure. A 9% taxi time reduction (Munich SESAR DMAN benchmark) [Technology Scout brief, Finding 4] or a 0–3 minute per-departure taxi-out saving (EUROCONTROL A-CDM benchmark) [Technology Scout brief, Finding 4] across 400,000+ annual operations translates into fuel savings, gate availability, and delay reductions that feed both airline economics (which shapes CPE tolerance) and passenger experience (which shapes non-aeronautical revenue). These are not rhetorical connections; they are the same dollars.

### 6.4 Governance

The Heathrow T5 lesson applies to MWAA directly. The airports that hit their targets did so because of governance structures built specifically to resist megaproject failure modes — two-year pre-construction discipline, ring-fenced scope, accountable executive ownership with contractual clarity on risk allocation. Most airport authorities do not implement those structures. The Denver Great Hall audit finding — "still is not following procurement procedures or requiring contractors to submit documentation of actual costs" [Economist brief, Evidence] — is the cautionary tale.

The specific implication for MWAA: the 5.6x step-up in program scale is precisely the threshold at which the Authority's governance architecture, procurement practices, and program controls need to be stress-tested by someone other than the people who wrote them. T5-class governance is not a marketing claim. It is an auditable set of practices around risk registers, scope freeze protocols, independent cost validation, and contingency reserve discipline. A $9 billion program run with $1.6 billion program governance will underperform even if every individual project manager is excellent.

### 6.5 The APOC question

SFO opened the first serious US Airport Operations Center in January 2026 — a 22,000-square-foot facility with 67 workstations [Technology Scout brief, Section 4]. Documented operational results are not yet published. The facility is a container, not a capability.

MWAA has neither a DCA APOC nor an IAD APOC in the European sense. The question is not whether to build one. The question is whether the organizational, data, and governance architecture that makes an APOC coherent exists — a unified AODB, real-time feeds from all major operational stakeholders, formal agreements with airlines and ground handlers on data sharing, and accountable executive ownership of operational outcomes. Without that architecture, an APOC is a room with screens. With it, an APOC is the organizational container that makes every other operational intelligence investment compound.

An honest audit of MWAA's current posture on those prerequisites — not a vendor conversation, an internal audit — is the specific first step before any APOC capital commitment.

### 6.6 What this piece is not

It is not a recommendation that MWAA pause Project Journey, halt Concourse E, or cancel any specific committed project. The pavement, the asset renewal, the code compliance, and the capacity components responsive to documented demand are legitimate investments. It is a recommendation that the operational intelligence allocation inside the current capital envelope be scoped, resourced, governed, and measured as a first-class program — not as an IT subline — and that the demand-contingent tranche of the capital plan be subjected to a harder defeat test against a fraction-of-cost operational alternative.

The thesis, applied to MWAA: the next billion dollars in MWAA CapEx is going to get built. It should be built knowing what a hundred million dollars of operational intelligence would have done first — and knowing that the bond market, the rating agencies, and the Authority's own DSCR trajectory now require that question be answered on the record, not assumed away.
