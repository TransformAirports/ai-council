## 1. Key Findings

1. **Global airport CapEx is on a trajectory that the industry's own return metrics cannot justify.** Global airport capital costs reached US$40 billion in 2023—1% above pre-pandemic levels—while global ROIC came in at 6.3% in 2024, a figure that ACI World itself acknowledges remains *below the sector's weighted average cost of capital (WACC)*. An industry whose aggregate return on invested capital fails to clear its cost of capital is, in the strict sense, destroying value even as it builds. [Source: https://aci.aero/2026/03/17/policymakers-must-support-airport-investment-as-global-air-travel-demand-rises/]

2. **The historical overrun rate on airport megaprojects is not a bug—it is the baseline.** Bent Flyvbjerg's research across 258 transport infrastructure projects found that approximately 86% experience cost overruns, with an average of 28% over initial budget. For airports specifically: Berlin Brandenburg opened 9 years late at €6.5 billion against an original estimate of €2.8 billion—a 132% cost overrun. The Denver International Airport Great Hall renovation, originally a ~$770 million city commitment, is now projected to cost approximately $2.1–2.35 billion. Denver's original automated baggage system (1990s) cost roughly $560 million more than budgeted and delivered a fraction of its design scope. The LAX Automated People Mover, originally contracted at $1.9 billion, has ballooned to $3.34 billion with no opening date confirmed as of March 2026—a 76% overrun and multiple years behind schedule. [Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport; https://airlineweekly.skift.com/2022/01/denver-approves-airport-project-with-1-billion-cost-overrun/; https://laist.com/news/transportation/lax-people-mover-delay-grand-jury-report]

3. **US airport capital needs are growing faster than available funding, and the gap is widening.** The FAA's NPIAS 2025-2029 identifies $67.5 billion in AIP-eligible capital needs over five years—the highest ever in any NPIAS cycle, representing an 8% increase from the prior estimate. The American Society of Civil Engineers projects the 10-year need at $310 billion (2024-2033) against projected funding of $168 billion from all sources—a structural shortfall of roughly $142 billion. ASCE grades US aviation infrastructure at D+ in 2025. Yet the statutory PFC cap—at $4.50 since 2001—had lost an estimated 40% of its construction-cost purchasing power by 2018 (declining to an equivalent of $2.72 in year-2000 dollars indexed to construction prices, per a RAND Corporation study commissioned by Congress). The 2024 FAA Reauthorization left the cap unchanged. [Sources: https://www.faa.gov/airports/planning_capacity/npias; https://infrastructurereportcard.org/cat-item/aviation-infrastructure/; https://enotrans.org/article/study-commissioned-by-congress-recommends-increasing-airport-pfc-cap-by-3-per-passenger/]

4. **Cost-per-enplanement at capital-intensive US hubs has reached levels that threaten airline economics at the margin.** As of 2024, CPE at Newark (EWR) stood at $31.67, Los Angeles (LAX) at $30.16, and Chicago O'Hare (ORD) at $29.56—all driven primarily by active capital programs financed by revenue bonds. LAX projects its CPE rising to $51.82 by 2030; San Francisco projects $46.01. At the low end, Atlanta (ATL) sits at $3.94 and projects to $11.50 by 2030 as its own capital program ramps. The CPE spread between the highest and lowest large-hub airports—nearly 8-to-1 in 2024—reflects less about operational quality than about who committed to a capital program when, and how they financed it. [Source: https://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/large-hub/cpe]

5. **Debt-per-enplanement is now the dominant financial risk vector at US airports pursuing aggressive capital programs.** MWAA's adjusted debt per O&D enplanement was $223 in 2024 at the system level; it is projected to peak near $400 per enplanement in 2028 as the authority deploys approximately $5.5 billion in new debt between 2025 and 2028. Net revenue debt service cover is projected to decline from 1.63x in 2024 to approximately 1.3x over the next five years—still above bond covenant minimums (typically 1.10x–1.50x depending on the indenture), but with shrinking margin for error. At Dulles specifically, the debt service coverage ratio is already projected to deteriorate from 2.79x in FY2024 to 1.70x by FY2030 even under favorable traffic assumptions. Moody's shifted its US airport sector outlook from stable to *negative* in May 2025. [Sources: https://dwuconsulting.com/airports/iad; https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-going-to-market]

6. **IT and operational technology spending at airports is trivially small relative to physical CapEx—yet the demand for it is accelerating.** Global airport IT spend reached $8.9 billion in 2024 against global airport capital costs of approximately $40 billion annually—a ratio of roughly 1:4.5. The asymmetry is more pronounced at the project level: the LAX APM alone consumes more capital than the entire annual IT budget of the global airport industry by a factor of approximately 0.37x. Meanwhile, AI-powered predictive maintenance is reported to generate $2–8 million in annual savings at mid-size airports, and airports using AI queue management report 18–25% shorter wait times with no staffing increase. These are directionally interesting but the underlying studies are from vendor-adjacent sources and should be treated as indicative rather than rigorous. [Sources: https://www.sita.aero/resources/surveys-reports/air-transport-it-insights-2024/; https://copenhagenoptimization.com/blog/the-role-of-ai-and-machine-learning-in-airport-resource-optimization/]

7. **The opportunity cost of a misallocated capital program is not recoverable on airport timescales.** A terminal built in 2026 for a demand projection that proves wrong in 2035 cannot be un-built. Bond covenants require debt service regardless of utilization—the airport must generate aeronautical revenues to cover principal and interest whether or not its gates are full. The Denver Great Hall illustrates the dynamic: a project that should have been a $770 million targeted intervention has consumed management bandwidth and public credit capacity for nearly a decade while the airport's operational systems—staffing, flow management, wayfinding, concessions technology—were not the primary institutional focus. History at CVG offers a structural warning: following Delta's dehubbing, Cincinnati's CPE rose from under $5 to over $10 on collapsing enplanements, demonstrating that capital commitments financed against traffic forecasts are not naturally de-risked by infrastructure quality. [Sources: https://dwuconsulting.com/airport-finance/articles/cost-per-enplaned-passenger; https://airlineweekly.skift.com/2022/01/denver-approves-airport-project-with-1-billion-cost-overrun/]

## 2. Evidence Section

### Global CapEx Trajectory

ACI World's Global Outlook of Airport Capital Expenditure (commissioned with Oxford Economics) projects $2.4 trillion in global airport CapEx between 2021 and 2040—$1.7 trillion for brownfield and $731 billion for greenfield. The historical anchor is a global average of $7.83 annual CapEx per passenger during 2015–2019, totaling approximately $63.8 billion per year. COVID-19 cut that by an estimated 33% (~$28 billion) in 2020. By 2023, global capital costs had recovered to $40 billion, with interest expenses alone rising 18% year-over-year as the debt load from pandemic-era bond issuances repriced upward. [Source: https://blog.aci.aero/airport-capacity/airports-need-significant-capital-expenditure-to-alleviate-slot-constraints/; https://aci.aero/2025/04/28/airports-face-financial-challenges-despite-air-traffic-rebound-aci-world-economics-report-reveals/]

In 2024, airports globally served 9.4 billion passengers—4% above 2019—yet total global airport revenue of $194.9 billion remained 2.1% below pre-pandemic levels in real terms. Aeronautical revenues were 3% below 2019; non-aeronautical revenues were 9% below. The traffic-to-revenue disconnect is not fully explained by fare mix or concession model shifts—it reflects the structural fact that airports over-leveraged during the boom and are now funding debt service from a revenue base that has not kept pace. [Source: https://aci.aero/2026/03/17/policymakers-must-support-airport-investment-as-global-air-travel-demand-rises/]

### US Federal Framework

The FAA's NPIAS 2025-2029 identifies $67.5 billion in AIP-eligible development costs: $25 billion at large hubs, $9.3 billion at medium hubs, $6.6 billion at small hubs, and $8 billion at nonhubs. Terminal projects account for $22.2 billion of the total—the single largest category. The AIP was funded at $3.35 billion annually through the pre-IIJA baseline; the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law added $15 billion over five years for airport infrastructure plus $5 billion for terminals (the Airport Terminal Program). The 2024 FAA Reauthorization increased AIP annual funding to $4 billion. Even at $4 billion per year, federal AIP funding covers roughly 30 cents of every dollar of identified capital need. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/airports/planning_capacity/npias]

The GAO (GAO-20-298) documented that airports' planned infrastructure costs for FY2019–2023 averaged $22 billion annually—a 19% increase over prior estimates. Terminal projects specifically increased 51% versus the prior five-year estimate, a pattern that suggests systematic optimism bias in the cost estimation phase. [Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-20-298]

### Cost Overrun Case Studies

**Berlin Brandenburg (BER):** Construction began 2006, first opening canceled in 2012 after fire protection system failures. Commercial service began October 31, 2020—nine years behind schedule. Original budget: €2.8 billion. Final construction cost: €6.0–6.5 billion. Including interest and unrealized revenue, the total value destruction has been estimated near €11.9 billion. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport]

**LAX Automated People Mover:** Contracted at $1.9 billion in 2018, the project reached $3.34 billion by July 2024 when the Los Angeles World Airports Commissioners approved an additional $400 million to resolve legal disputes with the LINXS consortium. A Los Angeles County Civil Grand Jury report attributed delays to the relationship breakdown between LAWA and the contractor plus political pressure around regional events. As of March 2026, no opening date has been announced. [Source: https://laist.com/news/transportation/lax-people-mover-delay-grand-jury-report]

**Denver Great Hall:** The Great Hall renovation was structured as an approximately $770 million city commitment under a public-private partnership with Ferrovial. After cost disputes, the city terminated the contractor in 2019 with work incomplete. The total project cost, across multiple phases now expected to conclude by 2027–2028, is approximately $2.1–2.35 billion—roughly three times the original city exposure. A January 2025 city audit found that DIA "still is not following procurement procedures or requiring contractors to submit documentation of actual costs." [Source: https://airlineweekly.skift.com/2022/01/denver-approves-airport-project-with-1-billion-cost-overrun/; https://www.flydenver.com/app/uploads/2023/09/greathallconstruction_Auditapril2023-1.pdf]

**Denver DIA Original Baggage System (1994–1995):** The automated baggage handling system contributed approximately $560 million in overruns to the airport's $4.8 billion final cost (versus an original $1.7 billion budget for the airport). The system was deployed to only one concourse for one airline's outbound flights. The airport itself opened 16 months late. [Source: https://medium.com/@ketan.keshav7/the-400-million-lesson-what-the-denver-airport-baggage-fiasco-taught-us-about-escalation-and-c6f0bbc37305]

**Heathrow Terminal 5:** A notable counter-case. The £4.3 billion terminal opened on schedule in March 2008—within budget—after BAA conducted a two-year pre-construction study explicitly designed to avoid megaproject failure modes. T5 is frequently cited as one of the only large airport megaprojects to meet both cost and schedule targets. Its lesson is not that large airport projects can't be managed; it is that they require extraordinary pre-commitment to governance structures that most airport authorities do not implement. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heathrow_Terminal_5]

**IAD AeroTrain:** The 3.78-mile underground people mover at Washington Dulles cost approximately $1.4–1.5 billion and opened in January 2010, a few months behind its fall 2009 target. No primary source documentation of the original budget versus final cost was located for this analysis. The delay was attributed to reliability testing requirements. This is flagged as a data gap.

**JFK New Terminal One:** The $9.5 billion privately-financed terminal is a component of the Port Authority's $19 billion JFK transformation program. The 2025 Green Bond issuance of $1.37 billion followed a 2024 issuance of $2.55 billion—described as the largest-ever municipal bond financing for an airport project. Phase A is targeted for 2026 opening. No material cost overrun relative to original budget has been documented in publicly available sources to date; the project is mid-execution. [Source: https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/the-new-terminal-one-at-jfk-successfully-concludes-1-37-billion-2025-green-bond-issuance-302512038.html]

### MWAA-Specific Financial Trajectory

MWAA's 2025-2039 Capital Construction Program is valued at approximately $9 billion, replacing a 2015-2024 program estimated at $1.6 billion—a roughly 5.6x increase in capital program scope across consecutive 10-15 year cycles. Current capital projects include the DCA Terminal B/C redevelopment (part of a $2.4 billion Reagan National program) and Dulles Concourse E expansion. MWAA plans to issue approximately $5.5 billion in new debt between 2025 and 2028. Adjusted debt per O&D enplanement is projected to nearly double from $223 in 2024 to approximately $400 in 2028. Net revenue DSCR, currently 1.63x, is projected to decline to approximately 1.3x over five years—a level that, while above covenant minimums, leaves little cushion against a demand shock, an interest rate spike, or another black-swan event. [Sources: https://wjla.com/news/local/reagan-dulles-airports-authority-approves-9-billion-dollar-contract-multiple-projects; https://www.bondbuyer.com/news/mwaa-goes-to-market; https://dwuconsulting.com/airports/iad]

## 3. Caveats and Limitations

**Data currency and access:** ACI World's most granular airport economics data (operating expenditure per passenger, ROIC by region) is behind a paywall. The figures cited here are drawn from ACI press releases, blog posts, and third-party coverage of paid reports. The precise 2023 ROIC figure and regional breakdowns could not be independently verified against the primary report.

**CPE data interpretation:** DWU Consulting's CPE figures reflect airline payments to airports per departing passenger—they are not equivalent to airport operating cost per passenger or CapEx per passenger. CPE is a contractual construct that varies significantly by ratemaking methodology (residual vs. compensatory). A high CPE airport is not necessarily over-investing; it may have a different cost-sharing structure with airlines.

**Overrun causation:** The case studies cited (BER, LAX APM, Denver Great Hall, Denver baggage) attribute delays and overruns to a mix of governance failure, contractor disputes, scope changes, and political interference. They should not be read as evidence that all airport CapEx is doomed to overrun—Heathrow T5 demonstrates that disciplined programs can hit targets. The Flyvbjerg average (86% overrun rate, 28% average magnitude) is derived from transport infrastructure broadly, not airports specifically.

**MWAA financial projections:** The debt-per-enplanement and DSCR projections cited are drawn from bond rating agency analyses and DWU Consulting's airport finance database, not MWAA's own published documents. MWAA's actual ACFR was not directly accessed. Projections are scenario-dependent and may not reflect current authority modeling.

**IT savings figures:** Claims of $2–8 million in predictive maintenance savings and 18–25% queue time reductions derive from vendor-adjacent sources (Copenhagen Optimization, industry white papers) without peer-reviewed validation. These numbers are plausible directionally but cannot be treated as authoritative benchmarks for opportunity cost analysis.

**IAD AeroTrain original budget:** No primary source establishing the original budget for the AeroTrain project relative to its $1.4–1.5 billion final cost was located. This is an explicit data gap.

## Analyst's Note

The evidence above does not make the case that airports should stop building. It makes the case that the economics of airport building are systematically worse than the planning documents suggest—and that the industry's own aggregate ROIC-to-WACC relationship confirms this is not an episodic problem but a structural one. The question "should we build this?" is different from "should we build only this?" The opportunity cost argument is most compelling not where it claims infrastructure is bad, but where it establishes that a dollar committed to concrete is a dollar unavailable for compounding operational returns—and that the compounding period for airport infrastructure runs 30–50 years, during which the locked-in CPE obligations follow the airlines regardless of what demand does. That is the constraint a strategist needs to force planners to address on the merits.
