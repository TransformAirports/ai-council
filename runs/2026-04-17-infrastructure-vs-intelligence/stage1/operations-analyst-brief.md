# Evidence Brief: Infrastructure vs. Operational Coordination as the Primary Airport Bottleneck

**Prepared by:** Operations Analyst Agent  
**Date:** April 17, 2026  
**Classification:** Stage 1 Research — Independent Evidence Collection

## Key Findings

1. **Weather and volume account for roughly 74% and 15% of NAS-level OPSNET delays respectively, but "volume" delays are operationally tractable.** FAA OPSNET data for the six years ending May 2023 show weather caused 74.26% of system-impacting delays (>15 min), volume 14.92%, runway unavailability 5.96%, equipment 0.61%, and other factors 4.25%. [Source: https://www.aspm.faa.gov/aspmhelp/index/Types_of_Delay.html] The critical nuance: "volume" delays are not infrastructure-determined — they emerge when demand exceeds the *operationally realized* capacity at a given moment, not the physical maximum. That gap is the argument.

2. **Carrier-side operational failures, not physical runway limits, dominate delay minutes at the flight level.** BTS data from January–April 2023 show late-arriving aircraft accounted for 38.99% of total delayed minutes and air carrier delays 35.43%. NAS delays — the category most tied to ATC, runway, or airport physical constraints — were a smaller share. [Source: https://www.bts.gov/explore-topics-and-geography/topics/airline-time-performance-and-causes-flight-delays] Critically, ATC staffing shortfalls at the 19 largest underfunded facilities — those operating 15% or more below target — account for 40% of all system delays while representing only 27% of commercial operations. [Source: https://www.brookings.edu/articles/air-traffic-controllers-and-why-there-arent-enough-of-them/]

3. **Turnaround time variance is large enough to be a system-level threat, and it is not a concrete problem.** In March 2023, none of the US major carriers turned aircraft within planned timeframes. JetBlue ran 19% over schedule on narrowbody turns (12 extra minutes per cycle), while United — the best performer — still ran 10% over (6 minutes). At Orlando, JetBlue posted a 25% variance. [Source: https://www.oag.com/blog/science-aircraft-turnarounds] The 2023 Turnaround Benchmark Report found an average ground delay of eight minutes even among the best-performing short-haul airlines globally. A five-minute improvement compounded across a carrier's daily schedule is the operational equivalent of building a new gate — without building anything.

4. **Operational interventions have delivered capacity gains measured in the double digits, without concrete.** RECAT implementation at Memphis in November 2012 — reclassifying aircraft wake turbulence categories — produced an FAA-estimated 15%+ increase in airport capacity and nine additional aircraft movements per hour. FedEx reported saving 4.18 million gallons of fuel and reducing emissions by nearly 40 metric tons, with monthly fuel cost savings of $1.8 million. [Source: https://www.aero-news.net; https://skybrary.aero/articles/recat-wake-turbulence-re-categorisation] NATS' Time-Based Separation (TBS) system at Heathrow, deployed in March 2015, achieved a 62% reduction in headwind delays in its first year and averaged 2.9 additional movements per hour on strong-wind days. Over ten years, TBS cut airborne holding by approximately 1.5 million minutes and saved over 100,000 tonnes of fuel. [Source: https://nats.aero/blog/2025/03/the-history-of-time-based-separation-tbs-celebrating-10-years-since-tbs-landed-at-heathrow/]

5. **Airport infrastructure expansion induces demand that rapidly re-congests expanded facilities.** A peer-reviewed empirical analysis of 55 runway capacity expansions at the world's top 150 airports between 2000 and 2016 found that by 2016, over half of the expanded airports were back below pre-expansion capacity or congested again. Of those 55, 15 were operating peak hours below 90% of their pre-expansion (pre-construction) capacity — meaning expansion was entirely unnecessary for them. And 19 expanded airports were already operating at 90%+ of their new post-expansion capacity within a decade — meaning construction bought only temporary relief. [Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0969699719304314]

6. **ATC staffing — a coordination and policy problem — is currently causing a larger share of delays than any single physical constraint.** The FAA employs approximately 11,000 certified professional controllers, roughly 3,000 below model requirements. During the most acute staffing shortfalls, DOT Secretary Sean Duffy reported that controller staffing problems "accounted for half of all flight delays compared to the norm of 5%." [Source: https://www.brookings.edu/articles/air-traffic-controllers-and-why-there-arent-enough-of-them/] The 19 understaffed large facilities alone account for 40% of all flight delays in the NAS. This is a coordination and personnel-pipeline problem. It cannot be solved by building a terminal.

7. **Schedule padding has inflated on-time performance statistics by 20 percentage points, masking the true delay picture and reducing pressure for genuine operational reform.** From 2012 to 2023, average airline schedule padding increased 27%, adding roughly 3 minutes of buffer time per flight. Research cited by FinanceBuzz found that a 10% increase in scheduled flight times translated to a 20-percentage-point artificial improvement in measured on-time performance over five years. [Source: https://www.mightytravels.com/2024/09; https://financebuzz.com/airlines-padding-scheduled-flight-times] Block time inflation disguises operational inefficiency as reliability, removing incentive to close the gap through coordination improvements.

8. **Airport Collaborative Decision Making (A-CDM) at European airports has improved departure predictability by up to 85% in adverse conditions and cut taxi times by 0–3 minutes per departure.** A EUROCONTROL impact assessment across 17 fully-implemented CDM airports documented take-off time predictability improvements of up to 85% during adverse conditions, with average taxi-out time savings of 0–3 minutes per departure. A-CDM is now live at 34 European airports. Heathrow's A-CDM implementation produced a reported 50% reduction in departure delays. [Source: https://www.eurocontrol.int/concept/airport-collaborative-decision-making; https://www.haisenglobal.com/blog/airport-collaborative-decision-making-complete-handbook]

## Evidence

### FAA OPSNET and ASPM Delay Attribution

The FAA's Operations Network (OPSNET) is the official source for air traffic operations and delay data. OPSNET categorizes delays as: Weather, Volume, Equipment, Runway, and Other. Over the six years from June 2017 to May 2023, weather caused 74.26% of system-impacting delays greater than 15 minutes, volume caused 14.92%, runway unavailability 5.96%, equipment 0.61%, and other factors 4.25%. [Source: https://www.aspm.faa.gov/aspmhelp/index/Types_of_Delay.html]

The FAA's Aviation System Performance Metrics (ASPM) database tracks airport-level throughput, capacity utilization, and delay causes for the 77 designated ASPM airports. The Airport Adjusted Capacity (AAC) metric measures arrival, departure, and total operations capacity adjusted when actual values exceed facility-reported rates — providing a picture of how much capacity airports actually realize versus their physical maximum. [Source: https://aspmhelp.faa.gov/index/ASPM_Efficiency__Airport_Adjusted_Capacity_Report.html]

### BTS Delay Cause Breakdown

The Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS) collects delay cause data from airlines under the ASQP program. Airlines categorize delays into: Air Carrier, Late Aircraft, NAS (National Aviation System), Extreme Weather, and Security. From January–April 2023, late-arriving aircraft accounted for 38.99% of total delayed minutes; air carrier delays 35.43%; NAS delays (which include ATC, runway, and heavy traffic volume) a smaller share. Full-year 2023 data confirms 24.1% of US domestic flights were delayed or cancelled. [Source: https://www.bts.gov/explore-topics-and-geography/topics/airline-time-performance-and-causes-flight-delays]

Notably, "late aircraft" delay — the single largest category — is entirely an operational and scheduling phenomenon. An aircraft arrives late because an earlier rotation was delayed. No runway construction addresses this. The fix is turnaround time management, schedule discipline, and buffer strategy.

### EUROCONTROL CODA Annual Data

EUROCONTROL's Central Office for Delay Analysis (CODA) publishes annual digest reports on all-causes delay across the European Civil Aviation Conference (ECAC) region. The 2023 annual report records an average delay per flight of 17.5 minutes — down marginally from 17.8 minutes in 2022 despite a 9.8% increase in total ECAC flights. Major contributing factors in 2023 included ATC staffing shortfalls (Copenhagen, Greek islands), ATC equipment issues (Athens), and aerodrome-level capacity constraints — not runway length or terminal square footage. [Source: https://www.eurocontrol.int/publication/all-causes-delays-air-transport-europe-annual-2023]

### ATC Staffing as a Systemic Bottleneck

A Brookings Institution analysis drawing on FAA data and DOT OIG findings establishes that the FAA employs approximately 11,000 certified professional controllers against a model requirement of ~14,000 — a gap of roughly 3,000. From 2013 to 2023, the FAA hired only two-thirds of the controllers required by its staffing model, producing a 13% net decline in controller headcount from 2010 to 2024. By fiscal year 2024, nearly a third of ATC facilities had fallen 10% below model standards. Nineteen of the 30 largest facilities operate below 85% of staffing targets. Those 19 facilities account for 40% of all flight delays in the NAS. [Source: https://www.brookings.edu/articles/air-traffic-controllers-and-why-there-arent-enough-of-them/]

The DOT OIG's 2024 report on FAA controller staffing confirms that at the worst moments of the current staffing crisis, controller shortfalls "accounted for half of all flight delays compared to the norm of 5%." [Source: https://www.oig.dot.gov/sites/default/files/FAA%20Controller%20Staffing%20and%20Training%20at%20Critical%20Facilities%20Final%20Report-06-21-23.pdf]

### Wake Turbulence RECAT at Memphis

The FAA's RECAT program restructures aircraft into six wake-turbulence categories based on wingspan and wake-encounter tolerance, replacing the legacy Heavy/Large/Small classification. At Memphis International Airport — North America's busiest cargo hub — RECAT was implemented in November 2012. The FAA estimated a 15%+ increase in airport capacity and nine additional movements per hour. FedEx reported 4.18 million gallons of fuel saved, $1.8 million per month in fuel cost reduction, and nearly 40 metric tons of emission reductions. Average FedEx taxi time was cut by three minutes per aircraft. [Source: https://www.aero-news.net/index.cfm?do=main.textpost&id=ae5f50f3-a94c-4b76-bc24-d58ee5e6e0ea; https://skybrary.aero/articles/recat-wake-turbulence-re-categorisation]

The critical point: Memphis built nothing. It reclassified aircraft on paper, updated separation tables, and retrained controllers. The 15% capacity gain took months, not a decade of environmental review.

### Time-Based Separation at Heathrow

NATS deployed TBS at Heathrow in March 2015. Traditional distance-based separation expands when headwinds push slower aircraft speeds, collapsing runway throughput. TBS fixes arrival intervals in time rather than distance, neutralizing the headwind penalty. Results over the first year: 62% reduction in headwind delays; average of 2.9 additional landings per hour in strong-wind conditions; more than 80% of landing aircraft were separated more closely than under the old system. Over a ten-year period (2015–2025), TBS accumulated: airborne holding reduced by approximately 1.5 million minutes; fuel savings of over 100,000 tonnes; CO2 reductions of over 300,000 tonnes; estimated fuel cost savings of approximately USD 70 million. [Source: https://nats.aero/blog/2025/03/the-history-of-time-based-separation-tbs-celebrating-10-years-since-tbs-landed-at-heathrow/]

Heathrow's runway debate — a multi-decade political and legal saga over a third runway that has consumed enormous planning resources — has proceeded in parallel with TBS. TBS delivered meaningful capacity gains without moving a single cubic yard of earth.

### FAA TFDM Surface Metering

The FAA's Terminal Flight Data Manager (TFDM) program modernizes airport surface management through electronic flight strips, surface scheduling, and departure metering. Testing at Charlotte Douglas International Airport over four years saved more than 275,000 gallons of fuel annually, reduced greenhouse gas emissions by 8 tonnes of CO2 daily, and cut delay by 916 hours per year. At full national deployment, the FAA projects annual savings of more than 7 million gallons of fuel and elimination of more than 75,000 tonnes of CO2. [Source: https://www.faa.gov/air_traffic/technology/tfdm; https://www.aerotime.aero/articles/29040-faa-surface-data-management-tech-save-aircraft-fuel]

The TFDM program's surface metering function meters departure releases from the gate to the runway, reducing the queue of aircraft burning fuel while waiting in line. It is software eating what used to look like a concrete problem.

### A-CDM: Operational Coordination as Capacity

Airport Collaborative Decision Making (A-CDM) establishes a shared information framework across airlines, ground handlers, air traffic control, and airport operators. Rather than each stakeholder optimizing independently, A-CDM creates a common operational picture for each flight's 16-stage lifecycle from inbound approach to take-off. EUROCONTROL's impact assessment across 17 fully-implemented CDM airports found: departure predictability improved up to 85% during adverse conditions; average taxi-out time savings of 0–3 minutes per departure; average schedule adherence improvement of 0–2 minutes per flight. Heathrow's A-CDM implementation produced a reported 50% reduction in departure delays. A-CDM is now live at 34 European airports. [Source: https://www.eurocontrol.int/concept/airport-collaborative-decision-making; https://www.haisenglobal.com/blog/airport-collaborative-decision-making-complete-handbook]

### Turnaround Time Variance

OAG's March 2023 analysis of major US carrier narrow-body operations found consistent overruns across the industry: Southwest planned 52 minutes and delivered 59; United planned 62 and delivered 68. United Airlines at San Francisco posted the best narrowbody performance at just 4 minutes over plan (5% variance); United at Newark was 10 minutes over (15%). JetBlue ranged from 17–25% variance across bases. [Source: https://www.oag.com/blog/science-aircraft-turnarounds]

These are not runway problems. They are coordination problems — fueling delays, cleaning sequencing, gate conflicts, catering logistics. They propagate forward into the next rotation and the one after that. The FAA's Delay Propagation data show that five minutes at one gate can disrupt up to 15 downstream flights in a busy day-of-operations schedule. [Source: https://www.aspm.faa.gov/aspmhelp/index/Delay_Propagation.html]

### Schedule Padding: The Hidden Distortion

A 27% increase in schedule padding from 2012 to 2023 — adding roughly 3 minutes to average flight block times — means on-time performance has been artificially inflated. Research documented by FinanceBuzz found that a 10% increase in scheduled block times produced a 20-percentage-point improvement in measured on-time performance over five years. [Source: https://www.mightytravels.com/2024/09/the-truth-about-airline-schedule-padding; https://financebuzz.com/airlines-padding-scheduled-flight-times]

This matters because it obscures the actual operational efficiency gap. When airports and airlines report strong on-time performance, some fraction of that improvement is accounting — they've expanded the definition of "on time" by adding buffer time to the denominator. The real throughput problem remains. The schedule-padding literature (e.g., Zhang, Salant & Van Mieghem, SSRN 2018) documents this as a systematic, competitive response to chronic operational variance, not a sign of system health. [Source: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.3238457]

## Counterexamples: When Infrastructure Was the Right Answer

The argument for operational intelligence over infrastructure is strong, but it is not absolute. Intellectual honesty requires confronting where operations cannot substitute for concrete.

**London Heathrow — Genuine Physical Capacity Ceiling**  
Heathrow operates at 98% of its 480,000 annual movement cap. With only two runways serving over 1,300 daily aircraft movements, there is no operational trick that creates a third arrival stream. A-CDM, TBS, and collaborative scheduling have all been implemented and have delivered meaningful gains — but Heathrow's queue of airlines seeking slots and its structural inability to serve more long-haul routes is a genuine infrastructure problem. The constraint is real. [Source: https://mediacentre.heathrow.com/pressrelease/detail/4139]

**Frankfurt Runway 18 West (2011)**  
Frankfurt International Airport's fourth runway, opened in October 2011, increased aircraft movements per hour from 83 to 126 — a 52% increase. The airport's previous parallel runway configuration did not allow simultaneous independent operations because runways were too close together. No operational procedure could replicate the simultaneous independent approach capability that physical separation provided. Punctuality demonstrably improved in the post-expansion period. [Source: https://www.airport-technology.com/news/newsfraport-opens-fourth-runway-at-frankfurt-airport/]

**O'Hare Modernization — Intersecting Runways as a Hard Physical Constraint**  
Chicago O'Hare's legacy airfield had 8 runway intersections, creating conflicts that no ATC procedure could fully resolve. The O'Hare Modernization Program's reconfiguration to parallel runways increased peak capacity by 50% and reduced system-impact delays by an estimated 64% when comparing the 2003–2008 period to 2009–2020. [Source: https://www.v-hr.com/blog/insight-chicago-ohare-airport-runway-capacity/] This is a legitimate case where physical runway geometry — not ATC staffing, not surface metering — was the binding constraint. However, note: O'Hare's expansion also included significant operational and technology upgrades (Terminal Sequencing & Spacing, Time-Based Flow Management), making it difficult to isolate the pure infrastructure contribution.

**Düsseldorf — Infrastructure Built but Not Used**  
A counterexample to both arguments: Düsseldorf Airport was expanded to two runways in 1993 but environmental restrictions prevent simultaneous use during approximately half of weekly operating hours. The airport operates at single-runway capacity for much of the day. This illustrates that infrastructure without the surrounding operational and regulatory framework delivers little. The bottleneck shifted from construction to governance. [Source: https://www.dus.com/en/corporation/company/infrastructure/runway-system]

**The Induced-Demand Problem**  
A peer-reviewed study of 55 runway expansions at the 150 busiest global airports between 2000 and 2016 found that over half were congested again or underperforming relative to pre-expansion capacity by 2016. Of the 19 airports operating at 90%+ of their new post-expansion capacity, most are in Asia — markets with structurally constrained supply and genuinely growing demand. For North American and European airports, the return on construction investment in capacity terms was frequently poor. [Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0969699719304314]

The pattern suggests infrastructure is the right answer when: (a) the airport is a genuine hub with no practical demand substitutes (Heathrow, JFK, ORD pre-modernization); (b) current physical geometry creates conflicts that cannot be managed procedurally (intersecting runways); and (c) the market is in sustained structural demand growth without feasible demand management alternatives.

It is the wrong answer when: (a) the binding constraint is ATC staffing, surface coordination, or schedule compression; (b) new capacity will primarily be absorbed by induced demand; and (c) the delay cost could be addressed by operational improvements that cost orders of magnitude less and can be deployed in months rather than decades.

## Synthesis: Where the Bottleneck Actually Lives

The evidence does not support a simple "operations, not infrastructure" conclusion. It supports a more precise claim: the *ratio* of infrastructure to operational investment is systematically wrong, and the reason is structural bias in how airport systems make capital allocation decisions.

When 74% of OPSNET delays are weather-related, the productive question is not "how do we add runway length?" but "how do we make our operation more resilient to weather-driven capacity reduction?" The answers — A-CDM, surface metering, time-based separation, collaborative departure sequencing — are operational intelligence investments, not construction projects. They are also faster (months vs. decades), cheaper (millions vs. billions), and reversible in ways that poured concrete is not.

When 38.99% of delay minutes in a given period trace back to late aircraft — a pure artifact of scheduling compression and turnaround variance — the answer is not additional gate construction. It is tighter operational standards, better ground coordination technology, and airline incentive structures that reward actual on-time discipline rather than schedule-padded illusions of it.

The genuine infrastructure cases are real but narrow: airports running at 98% of physical capacity with no realistic demand-management alternative (Heathrow), legacy airfield geometries with active safety conflicts (pre-modernization O'Hare), or new-market greenfield facilities where no infrastructure exists at all. For the broad middle of the US hub system — airports that are congested at peak but demonstrably underutilized off-peak, where delay is driven by coordination failures more than physical limits — the infrastructure-first instinct is the expensive wrong answer.

The hidden capacity argument is not a theory. It is documented in the NATS TBS data, the FAA RECAT results, the EUROCONTROL A-CDM assessments, and the TFDM Charlotte pilot numbers. What those results share is that they found capacity that was already there, locked behind procedural, informational, or staffing constraints — and they unlocked it. The next question airports and their planners should ask is not "what should we build?" but "what have we already built that we haven't yet learned to use?"
