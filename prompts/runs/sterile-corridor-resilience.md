# Run: Sterile Corridor Design as a Resilience Problem

## Thesis

Sterile corridor design is treated as a code-compliance problem when it should be treated as a throughput and resilience problem. The difference between a sterile circulation system that scales and one that becomes the terminal's permanent bottleneck is set 10+ years before passengers ever walk through it — in decisions about width, redundancy, vertical circulation, and what happens when one segment fails. MWAA needs a framework for evaluating sterile circulation not against TSA minimums but against the operational reality of recovery from disruption.

## Audience

MWAA leadership, IAD and DCA planning staff, terminal designers, and the airport industry's planning and operations community. Assume sophistication. Assume the reader has signed off on or pushed back against a sterile corridor design in the last five years and knows how thin the design conversations usually are. Assume they are tired of LOS A/B/C/D/E framings that produce false comfort.

## Tone

Analytical sharp — direct, evidence-dense, intellectually honest about the counter-argument. Slightly provocative but not polemical. Model: Matt Levine on aviation, not a consultant thought-leadership piece. The reader should feel that the author has actually walked these corridors at peak, watched cleaning crews close one side of a bidirectional walkway, and seen what an escalator outage does to a midfield concourse at 6:45 a.m.

## Length

8,000–10,000 words for the full report; ~1,100-word executive summary. This is a substantive analytical piece, not a code-consultant deliverable.

## What this is NOT

- A generic ADA/IBC code summary or a restatement of FAA AC 150/5360-13 minimums
- A TSA checkpoint design piece — checkpoint queuing and screening throughput are a separate problem and belong in a separate report
- A code consultant's deliverable, formatted as a series of "shall comply with" bullets
- A vendor pitch for any specific moving-walkway, escalator, or wayfinding system
- A polemic against in-place renovation — recommissioning is sometimes the only option and the piece must take that seriously

## What this IS

- An identification of the 4–6 design variables that actually predict sterile-corridor outcomes, separating high-performing systems (DEN A-bridge, ATL T-concourse spine, SIN T3) from chronically congested ones (LGA pre-redevelopment, ORD T2, IAD midfield corridors at peak)
- An empirical treatment of width-to-throughput curves: where does a given corridor width transition from comfortable to degraded to failing, cited to actual measurement, not LOS letter grades
- A serious treatment of redundancy and failure modes: when one sterile segment closes for cleaning, an incident, or active construction, what design patterns preserve operations and what patterns cascade into shutdown
- A specific treatment of vertical transitions — escalators, elevators, moving walkways — as the most common choke points, with sizing rules that scale and sizing rules that don't
- A playbook for recommissioning sterile space in occupied concourses (DCA Project Journey, IAD Tier 2), maintaining sterile integrity without killing throughput
- A direct application to IAD: the midfield concourses, the AeroTrain-to-concourse handoffs, and the specific vulnerabilities in the current sterile circulation system that capital decisions in the next decade will either fix or lock in

## Operator-specific framing

This run is anchored to MWAA, specifically IAD. The IAD midfield concourses (A/B, C/D) and the AeroTrain stations that feed them are the empirical heart of the piece. The AeroTrain-to-concourse handoffs — the vertical circulation between platform and sterile concourse level, the corridor segments between the station and the gates, and the choke points where two streams merge — are the case study that anchors the general framework. DCA Project Journey supplies the in-place renovation case. Where peer-airport evidence is more illustrative (DEN A-bridge, ATL plane train and T-concourse spine, SIN T3), the piece uses it freely, but the test of the framework is whether it tells MWAA planners something useful about the next decade of capital decisions at IAD.

## Success criteria

- A sophisticated airport planner cannot dismiss the piece in the first 500 words — the framing has to land as obviously correct and obviously underused
- The 4–6 design variables identified are specific enough to apply to a real corridor on a plan set, not abstract enough to apply to everything
- Width-to-throughput claims trace to empirical sources (measured pedestrian flow studies, post-occupancy evaluations, observed peak-hour conditions), not to LOS letter grades or designer rules of thumb
- The IAD application is concrete enough that a MWAA planning staffer reads it and says "this changes how we should evaluate the next Tier 2 segment"
- The counter-case — that sterile corridors are already over-designed, that the real bottleneck is checkpoints or gates, that operational fixes are cheaper than capital ones — is presented strongly enough that the reader feels its force before the piece answers it

## Research agent overrides

- **infrastructure-economist:** Focus on the lifecycle cost of corridor-segment failure (closures for repair, replacement of undersized vertical circulation mid-life, the cost of recommissioning vs. greenfield) rather than headline capex per square foot.
- **operations-analyst:** Lead with empirical pedestrian flow data and post-occupancy throughput observations. Pull from Fruin, Transportation Research Board pedestrian studies, ACRP reports on terminal flow, and any published post-occupancy evaluations of the named comparator concourses.
- **technology-scout:** Treat sensing and real-time flow management (LiDAR people-counting, computer-vision flow analytics, dynamic wayfinding) as instrumentation that exposes corridor performance — not as a substitute for it. Identify what's actually deployed at the comparator airports.
- **contrarian:** Build the strongest possible case that this is a non-problem — that LOS-based design has worked for decades, that the marginal cost of wider corridors and redundant vertical circulation is not justified by the marginal benefit, and that operational interventions (staffing, timing of cleaning, incident response) close the gap more cheaply than capital ones.
- **chief-engineer:** Center constructability and lifecycle realities of in-place sterile-side renovation. The DCA Project Journey and IAD Tier 2 sequencing constraints are load-bearing for the recommissioning section.
- **airline-commercial-strategist:** Lighter touch — flag where airline use-and-lease constructs (preferential vs. common use, gate hold-rooms vs. sterile circulation as common space) shape who pays for corridor capacity and who benefits.
- **regulatory-political-analyst:** Cover the FAA, TSA, and ADA constraints that bind sterile corridor design, plus the political dynamics around in-place renovation (airline pushback, contractor sequencing risk, congressional scrutiny at IAD specifically).
- **aviation-historian:** Trace the arc from the 1960s-70s finger pier era, through the 1990s midfield-concourse generation (including IAD's own midfield), to the current generation. The historical pattern where a corridor sized for the traffic of its decade becomes the permanent bottleneck of the next is the spine of the piece.
- **airport-ceo:** Frame this through the lens of how a board, a bond-rating agency, and an airline use-and-lease counterparty will read a capital request for sterile-side capacity that doesn't show up in headline TSA wait times or gate counts.
- **airport-coo:** Lead voice on what actually happens when a sterile segment closes — the operational playbook, the cascade behavior, the staff and contractor coordination, the things that look fine on a plan and fail in execution.
- **virtual-christian:** This is exactly the kind of run where the operator's intuition about what fails at IAD specifically is load-bearing. Use heavily.
- **slacker:** Optional. Worth seating to surface adjacent connections (stadium concourse design, transit station flow, hospital corridor planning) that the structured agents won't reach for.
