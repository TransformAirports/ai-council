# Run: If Saarinen Were Designing Dulles Today

## Thesis

Eero Saarinen's IAD encoded a set of design principles — about modal separation, monumental civic architecture, flexible mid-field expansion, and a deliberate passenger experience — that were the deeper intent of the 1958–62 design, separable from its visual signature. Some of those principles have aged into operational liabilities: the original mobile lounges, the fixed terminal geometry, the sterile-to-non-sterile transitions. Others remain quietly correct and have been violated by every subsequent expansion: modal clarity, the single-arrival-experience, mid-field flexibility. A 2026 reinterpretation faithful to Saarinen's design philosophy — not his aesthetic — would make materially different choices than the current MWAA master plan in specific, identifiable ways.

## Audience

MWAA leadership, IAD master-planning staff, the design community working on airport terminals, and the policy-and-planning readership that follows large hub redevelopment. Assume sophistication. Assume the reader has seen the renderings of every recent terminal program in the country and is tired of them. Assume the reader is open to a serious argument about design philosophy but allergic to nostalgia, hagiography, and renderings dressed up as conclusions.

## Tone

Analytical sharp with a measured architectural literacy. Direct, evidence-dense, intellectually honest about where Saarinen was wrong. Model: a long-form architectural essay in *Places Journal* or *Harvard Design Magazine* written by someone who has read every contemporary review of the 1962 opening — but with the strategist's eye for what actually binds a 2026 capital decision. Not a McKinsey deck, not a fan-letter to mid-century modernism, not a designer's pitch. The reader should finish the piece thinking the questions are sharper than they were going in, not nodding at the beautiful sentences.

## Length

8,000–10,000 words for the full report; ~1,100-word executive summary. This is a substantive position paper, not a design pitch.

## What this is NOT

- A nostalgia piece. Saarinen got real things wrong; the piece must say so by name
- A rendering-driven design proposal. The output is a position document about principles and the choices they imply, not a master plan
- A pretense that Saarinen would have loved current airport trends. He likely would have hated several of them and the piece should say which and why
- An aesthetic argument. The thesis is about design philosophy — operational and experiential intent — not about the visual signature of the 1962 building
- A polemic against MWAA's current Tier 2 or $22B revitalization concept. Where current plans violate Saarinen's principles, the piece names it. Where they honor them, the piece says so

## What this IS

- A catalog of Saarinen's actual 1958–62 design principles, drawn from the documentary record (program brief, FAA correspondence, contemporary press, Saarinen's own writing and that of his collaborators) — separated cleanly from the visual aesthetic
- A principle-by-principle assessment: still valid in 2026, partially valid (and under what conditions), or obsolete
- A historical accounting of which subsequent expansions (1996, midfield concourses A/B/C/D, AeroTrain retrofit, Tier 2 planning, the $22B concept) honored vs. violated those principles, with specifics
- A scan of contemporary airport projects worldwide that reflect comparable design philosophy (not just adaptive reuse like the TWA Hotel) and an honest assessment of who is designing this way now and why most are not
- A 2026 reinterpretation — what changes, specifically, about modal access, the security/sterile transition, the airside-to-aircraft connection, terminal expansion strategy, and the civic/arrival experience if MWAA reinterpreted Saarinen's principles today
- The honest constraint question: is faithful Saarinen-philosophy design even compatible with 2026 TSA, FAA, ADA, and airline-commercial requirements? Where does it require fighting constraints worth fighting, and where does it require letting go?

## Operator-specific framing

This is an MWAA-anchored run. The Saarinen building at IAD is the empirical case; the current MWAA master plan, the Tier 2 program, and the $22B DOT-RFI revitalization concept (December 2025, no confirmed federal funding mechanism as of May 2026) are the live framing the piece argues against, with, or around — explicitly. The piece should land specific implications for how MWAA evaluates the next master-plan decision against a Saarinen-philosophy frame, not generic ones. Where peer airports (DEN as a Fentress monumental-civic-experience comparator; Kansas City's 2023 new terminal as a modal-clarity comparator; SFO Harvey Milk Terminal 1 as a civic-experience example; Munich's modular expansion strategy as a flexibility comparator) sharpen the analysis, the piece uses them.

## Success criteria

- A sophisticated reader cannot dismiss the piece as nostalgia in the first 500 words — the catalog of principles has to be specific, sourced, and separable from aesthetic
- At least one Saarinen principle the piece concludes is obsolete must be argued at full strength — no hagiography
- The contrarian's case (that the entire framing romanticizes a single architect and a single moment, and that current best-practice airport design has already absorbed what is true and discarded what is not) is presented strongly enough that the reader feels its force
- The 2026 reinterpretation lands as five or six specific design-philosophy moves that a MWAA staffer reading it can hold against the current master plan and identify which decisions are at stake
- The constraint-compatibility question (Saarinen-philosophy design vs. 2026 TSA/FAA/ADA/airline-commercial reality) is answered concretely — naming which constraints are worth fighting and which require letting go, not punting

## Research agent overrides

- **infrastructure-economist:** Lighter touch. Touch the financial framing of "what would the Saarinen-philosophy reinterpretation cost relative to current master plan" only insofar as it informs which fights are realistic. The primary economic frame is opportunity cost on the $22B program scope, not parametric cost estimation of a hypothetical building.
- **operations-analyst:** Modal access flow patterns at the curb-to-gate sequence, terminal volume and circulation logic, and where the original Saarinen design's operational assumptions held up vs. broke. Less pedestrian-flow rate work; more architectural/operational coupling.
- **technology-scout:** Cover the question of whether biometric / contactless / IoT instrumentation could enable a return to Saarinen-style modal clarity — fewer hard physical barriers, more integrated experience — without giving up TSA compliance. This is genuinely a 2026 question Saarinen could not have asked.
- **contrarian:** Build the strongest possible case that the entire framing is romanticism — that Saarinen's design failed in operational reality (the original mobile lounges were the canonical case), that the architectural-philosophy frame is a category error for what is fundamentally an infrastructure-and-airline-commercial problem, and that current airport design practice has absorbed Saarinen's genuine contributions while correctly discarding what didn't work.
- **chief-engineer:** Load-bearing. Cover the constructability and asset-management reality of Saarinen's actual building (the original terminal as a piece of mid-century concrete-and-steel construction with specific durability and adaptive-reuse implications), the AeroTrain retrofit as a 2010-era engineering response to Saarinen's modal-access concept, and what is and is not possible to renovate inside the inherited 1962 envelope.
- **airline-commercial-strategist:** Cover what United (and the secondary carriers at IAD) need from a 2026 terminal that Saarinen's program brief explicitly did not address — connecting-bank flexibility, premium-cabin product space, common-use vs. preferential gate flexibility, baggage system architecture. The honest version of what an airline-commercial-strategist would tell Saarinen if he were sitting in the room today.
- **regulatory-political-analyst:** Cover the TSA, FAA, and ADA framework that constrains any 2026 reinterpretation of Saarinen's principles. Specifically: the post-9/11 sterile-area paradigm and whether it can coexist with Saarinen's preferred single-arrival-experience; ADA modal-access requirements relative to the original mobile-lounge concept; FAA terminal/landside guidance and where it presumes design patterns Saarinen would not have chosen.
- **architectural-historian:** Load-bearing — the heaviest brief in this run. Carries the primary burden of cataloging Saarinen's actual 1958–62 design principles from documentary sources (program brief, FAA correspondence, Saarinen's letters to Roche and Pelli, contemporary architectural press from *Architectural Forum*, *Architectural Record*, *Progressive Architecture*), separating intent from aesthetic. Also responsible for the architectural-philosophical lineage that frames the piece: the canonical mid-century US generation (TWA, Lambert, LAX), the European and Asian arc (Foster Hong Kong, Piano Kansai, Rogers Madrid T4), the post-9/11 architectural inflection, and the contemporary generation (Kansas City, SLC, JFK T1, Munich modular). Identify where the architectural-philosophical record contradicts the thesis — Saarinen reversed himself on key points in his late writing, and that reversal matters.
- **aviation-historian:** Carries the long-cycle industry arc that frames the architectural conversation: how the FAA's program brief for IAD in 1958–62 reflected the regulated era's planning assumptions, how the 1996 expansion sat inside the post-deregulation hub-and-spoke moment, how the AeroTrain retrofit landed inside the post-9/11 capital cycle, and how the current Tier 2 / $22B revitalization sits inside the post-pandemic capital wave. The aviation historian's job is to tell the strategist what *industry-historical* moment each generation of IAD design was responding to, complementing the architectural historian's account of *design-philosophical* intent.
- **airport-ceo:** Frame this through how a current CEO would respond to a position paper that proposes specific changes to the current master plan informed by 1962 design philosophy. The political reality of "we're going to fight the TSA on sterile-area architecture because Saarinen would have wanted us to" is its own kind of CEO conversation, and the piece needs that voice.
- **airport-coo:** What the operator's chair experiences at IAD today as the consequences of decisions made in 1962, in 1996, in 2010, and in the current Tier 2 program — and where the operator's intuition agrees or disagrees with Saarinen's original principles about how the building should run.
- **virtual-christian:** This is exactly the kind of run where the operator's intuition about IAD specifically, and the connect-the-dots reach across other monumental civic buildings (Saarinen's Washington Dulles, Yamasaki's old WTC concourse, McKim Mead & White's old Penn Station, Saarinen's own GM Tech Center, the TWA terminal at JFK), is load-bearing. Use heavily.
- **slacker:** Adjacent civic-monumental design fields — Grand Central, the Moynihan Train Hall, the LA Union Station rebuild, Calatrava's transit hubs (the Oculus as a cautionary tale), Foster's Hong Kong airport, Munich's modular expansion, KCI's 2023 new terminal as the modal-clarity comparator the structured agents may not reach for. Surface the cross-field patterns the structured agents will miss.
