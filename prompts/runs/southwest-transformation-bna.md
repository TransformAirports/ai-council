# Run: Southwest Transformation at BNA

## Thesis

Southwest Airlines is completing a decade-long transformation from an operationally-efficient point-to-point carrier to a legacy-style revenue-optimization carrier. Open seating is going. Checked bag fees are in. Basic economy exists. Premium cabins and airport lounges are on the roadmap. Nashville International Airport — where Southwest is the dominant tenant and whose BNA Vision capital program was scaled to the carrier's historical operating model — is planning for the airline Southwest used to be, not the one it is becoming. The next tranche of BNA capital decisions needs to be pressure-tested against the Southwest that will actually be flying through Nashville in 2030.

## Audience

Executives and planners at the Metropolitan Nashville Airport Authority, the MNAA board, airline commercial analysts who cover Southwest, and airport planning peers who operate heavily-Southwest-exposed airports (Midway, Hobby, Orlando, Las Vegas). Assume sophistication. Assume skepticism. Some readers will know Southwest's internal politics and network decisions better than the Council does — the piece must survive their scrutiny. Readers will also have read whatever the trade press has written about Southwest's transformation; they do not need a primer on what changed. They need to know what it means.

## Tone

The default narrative voice — a beautifully written long-form essay in the John McPhee / Michael Lewis / Tracy Kidder tradition. A reader should finish the piece not because they had to but because they wanted to. Embed the argument in story: a specific morning on Concourse D at BNA, a specific Southwest 737 pulling off a quick turn, a specific gate agent watching the board, a specific passenger figuring out why the boarding process just changed. Use character, place, and scene. The analytical backbone is rigorous; the prose carries the reader.

Avoid consultant-report voice. Avoid purple prose, stacked adjectives, and the reflex to summarize what was just said. The piece should read as if it could appear in a long-form business publication — The Atlantic's business section, a well-turned piece in The New Yorker, a serious long-form feature in Air Transport World — with the specificity of trade-press analysis but the narrative economy of the magazine form.

Rhythm matters. Short sentences for emphasis. Longer sentences where the careful work of distinguishing two similar ideas needs to happen. No paragraph that reads as five identical declaratives in a row.

## Length

8,000-10,000 words for the full report; approximately 1,100 words for the three-page executive summary. This is a substantive piece, not a blog post and not a thought-leadership memo.

## What this is NOT

- Not a hit piece on Southwest. The carrier's transformation is economically rational from its own perspective, and the piece should take that seriously.
- Not a defense of BNA's current capital program. Whether the Authority has fully internalized this shift is a genuinely open question; the piece should examine it rather than pre-answer it.
- Not a vendor pitch dressed in narrative clothes. No product recommendations. No "adoption" or "leverage" language.
- Not a piece that treats Southwest and BNA as interchangeable with any other carrier-airport pair. The specificity of this relationship — the scale of Southwest's presence, the historical operational alignment between carrier and airport, the size of BNA Vision — is the subject.

## What this IS

- A rigorous argument that Southwest's operational assumptions at BNA are changing in ways the airport's capital program has not yet fully accounted for.
- A piece that names specific BNA projects and asks whether each still makes sense under the Southwest-as-evolving-hybrid assumption.
- A demonstration that what looks like continuity — Southwest remains BNA's dominant carrier — masks a structural change in the economics and operational character of that relationship.
- A piece that makes the carrier transformation vivid through narrative and specific cases (airports, gates, turns, schedules) rather than through abstract assertion or a 20-bullet list.
- An honest engagement with the opposing view: that the transformation is cosmetic, that Southwest will preserve its operational DNA where it matters, that BNA's capital program was scaled to passenger growth and not to any particular carrier behavior.

## Operator-specific framing

Nashville International Airport (BNA), operated by the Metropolitan Nashville Airport Authority (MNAA). The piece should engage specifically with:

- **BNA Vision.** The Authority's multi-year, multi-phase capital program, which substantially reshaped the terminal, concourses, and landside connections. Include the program's headline cost, the major completed and in-progress elements, and any remaining tranches not yet committed.
- **Southwest's footprint at BNA.** Gate count, share of total operations, share of enplanements, departure concentration by hour, and the concourse geography Southwest occupies.
- **Southwest's historical operating model at Nashville.** High-frequency point-to-point, short turns, the role of BNA as a crew and aircraft base or transit node rather than a banked hub.
- **The specific changes Southwest has announced or implemented.** Assigned seating, checked bag fees, basic economy fares, premium cabin plans, airport lounges. Engage with public statements, earnings calls, and investor day materials — not press summaries of them.
- **The airport's competitive context.** BNA as one of the fastest-growing US large hubs of the last decade, the non-Southwest carrier presence (Delta, American, United, the ultra-low-cost carriers), and the degree to which BNA's capital program outcomes depend on Southwest's ongoing commitment at its current scale.

## Success criteria

- A Southwest network planner could read the piece and not find material factual errors — the carrier's current and announced strategy is described correctly.
- A BNA capital planner could read the piece and come away with three specific questions to raise at their next capital planning meeting.
- A reader who cares about prose could read the piece for its own sake, even without any airport-industry stake in the outcome.
- The counter-case — "the transformation is cosmetic, Southwest will stay operationally Southwest where it matters, BNA's plan is fine" — is presented strongly enough that a reader who holds that view recognizes it as their own before the rebuttal arrives.
- Every numerical claim traces to a primary source, and every analyst-derived estimate carries an UNVERIFIED tag.

## Research agent overrides

The defaults work for most of the roster. Two targeted asks.

- **airline-commercial-strategist:** This run is primarily in your domain. Go deep on Southwest — its financial trajectory, its public justifications for the transformation, the shift from operational efficiency toward revenue optimization as expressed in CASM, PRASM, and ancillary revenue metrics, the carrier's gate productivity and turn-time history, and how the transformation maps to fleet and network decisions. Compare explicitly with how the legacy carriers (Delta, United, American) built their current revenue architectures over the last twenty years. Document Southwest's BNA footprint specifically — gates, share, frequency profile, departure banking or lack of it.
- **aviation-historian:** Frame Southwest's current transformation inside the broader history of US carrier identity shifts: Continental's 1990s reinventions, US Airways' post-America-West repositioning, Delta's post-Northwest integration, America West's migration toward legacy operations. What happened to airports whose capital was scaled to a carrier's prior identity when that identity shifted? Cincinnati and Pittsburgh are partial analogies, but the mechanism here is different — Southwest is not dehubbing BNA, it is rebranding the operational character of its presence. Distinguish the two carefully. Be honest where no clean historical precedent exists.

Leave the other six agents on default.
